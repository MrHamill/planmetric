5 SIGNS
YOU'RE
OVERTRAINING

More isn't always more. Here's how to tell when your body is asking you to back off.

(your body's been trying to tell you)

— Sign 1 of 5

Your easy pace
feels hard.

If your normal Zone 2 pace suddenly feels like tempo effort — and your heart rate is 10-15 bpm higher than usual for the same speed — your body hasn't recovered from the last block of training.

What to do
Take 2-3 easy days. If it persists after a week, take a full rest week. Don't push through — you're digging a deeper hole.
— Sign 2 of 5

You're tired
but can't sleep.

Overtraining jacks up your cortisol levels. You feel exhausted all day, then lie in bed wired at midnight. Your resting heart rate creeps up. You wake up feeling like you didn't sleep at all.

What to do
Cut training volume by 40-50% for a week. No intervals. Prioritise sleep hygiene — screens off by 9pm, cool dark room, consistent wake time.
— Sign 3 of 5

Every niggle
becomes an injury.

A tight calf that used to loosen up after 2km now sticks around for days. That knee twinge is getting worse, not better. Your body can't repair itself because you won't give it the chance.

What to do
If something has been niggling for more than 10 days, it's not going away on its own. Rest it. See a physio. Missing 5 days now beats missing 5 weeks later.
— Sign 4 of 5

You dread
the session.

Not the normal "I don't feel like it" on a cold morning. This is deeper — a total loss of motivation for something you usually love. Training feels like a chore. You resent the plan.

What to do
Swap a structured session for something unplanned — a trail run, a social ride, a swim with no watch. If the joy doesn't come back after a deload week, you need a proper break.
— Sign 5 of 5

Your performance
is going backwards.

You're training more than ever but your times are getting slower. Intervals feel impossible. Race results are worse than 3 months ago. More volume without recovery = regression, not progression.

What to do
Stop chasing volume. A well-structured plan builds in recovery weeks every 3-4 weeks. If yours doesn't, that's the problem — not your fitness.
