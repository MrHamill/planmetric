timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–3

Foundation building phase focusing on aerobic development through high-volume, low-intensity training. All sessions are in Zone 1-2 with emphasis on technique, consistency, and gradual volume increases. No racing or high-intensity work - we're building your aerobic engine broadly and safely.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 4–7

Introduction of race-specific intensities and volume increases. Training becomes more focused with threshold sessions, tempo runs, and longer intervals. The 80/20 rule shifts to approximately 65% easy / 35% moderate-hard. One or two lower-priority races may be included for experience and fitness testing.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 8–10

Highest training stress phase with race simulations and sport-specific intensities. Sessions become shorter but more intense, with race-pace efforts every 3-4 days. This phase generates the highest physical and mental stress as we fine-tune your race fitness and practice execution under fatigue.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 11–12

Planned volume reduction while maintaining intensity to allow adaptation and recovery before competition. Training volume drops 20-50% while keeping some sharpening work to maintain neuromuscular readiness. Focus shifts to race preparation, strategy refinement, and building confidence for peak performance.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Weeks 1–3

Week 1 — BASE
3.3h 13 Apr–19 2026
Welcome to your half marathon training plan! This first week establishes your base with comfortable pacing and introduces tempo work. Focus on running by feel—easy runs should be fully conversational, while your tempo run should feel comfortably hard. Your body is adapting to the training load, so consistency matters more than speed.

Tue - Easy Base Run (6:00am–6:35am)

RUN Z1-Z2 35min
Warm-up: 5 minutes easy walk, then 5 minutes gentle jogging
Main Set: 25 minutes steady running at 6:15-6:30/km pace (Z1-Z2). Should feel conversational throughout—you should be able to chat easily with a running partner.
Cool-down: 5 minutes easy walking with gentle stretching
Total: 35 minutes
Coach's Note:
This is your first easy run of the plan—nail the pacing here and you'll set yourself up for success. If you find yourself breathing hard or unable to hold a conversation, slow down immediately. Your easy pace might be slower than you think, and that's perfectly normal. Focus on landing with a slight forward lean and aim for around 170-180 steps per minute. Remember, easy days build your aerobic base without adding unnecessary fatigue.

Wed - Tempo Run (6:00am–6:40am)

RUN Z3 40min
Warm-up: 10 minutes easy jogging, then 4 × 30-second accelerations with 30-second easy jogs between
Main Set: 20 minutes at tempo pace (Z3)—comfortably hard effort around 5:40-5:50/km. You should be working but still in control.
Cool-down: 10 minutes easy jogging followed by 5 minutes walking
Total: 40 minutes
Coach's Note:
Your first tempo session! This should feel like a pace you could maintain for about an hour in a race—challenging but sustainable. If you're gasping for air, you're going too fast; if it feels too easy, you can pick it up slightly. This effort teaches your body to clear lactate efficiently and builds mental toughness. Focus on smooth, rhythmic breathing and maintaining good form even when working hard. This pace will feel more comfortable as the weeks progress.

Thu - Easy Recovery Run + Strength (6:00am–6:40am)

RUN Z1-Z2 40min
Warm-up: 5 minutes easy walking, then 5 minutes gentle jogging
Main Set: 30 minutes relaxed running at 6:20-6:35/km pace (Z1-Z2). Keep this genuinely easy—it's active recovery from yesterday's tempo work.
Cool-down: 5 minutes easy walking with light stretching
Total: 40 minutes
Coach's Note:
Perfect day for your first strength supplement! Schedule your strength session at least 3-6 hours after this run, or do it before running if preferred. Focus on single-leg movements (squats, glute bridges), core stability (planks, dead bugs), and hip strengthening (clamshells with resistance band). Keep it to 15-20 minutes and stop each exercise 2-3 reps before failure. This easy run should feel like a gentle shake-out—your legs might feel a bit heavy from yesterday, which is normal. Stay relaxed and enjoy the movement.

Sat - First Long Run (7:00am–8:25am)

RUN Z1-Z2 85min
Warm-up: 10 minutes easy walking and gentle jogging to ease into the session
Main Set: 70 minutes at a comfortable, conversational pace (6:15-6:30/km). This builds to about 11-12km—your aerobic foundation session. Stay hydrated throughout.
Cool-down: 5 minutes walking with gentle stretching, focusing on calves and hamstrings
Total: 85 minutes
Coach's Note:
Welcome to the most important session of your week! Your long run builds aerobic capacity, mental resilience, and teaches your body to burn fat efficiently. Start conservatively and aim to finish feeling like you could have continued for another 10-15 minutes. Bring water or plan a route with drink stops—staying hydrated is crucial for efforts over 60 minutes. If you feel any unusual pain or discomfort, don't push through it. This pace should allow you to enjoy the scenery and think clearly. You're building the foundation for everything that comes next.

This session is excellent mental training for your half marathon, teaching you to sustain discomfort while staying in control. If you start to struggle, focus on your breathing rhythm and imagine yourself running strongly in your target race. Remember, tempo pace should feel like something you could hold for about an hour.

The key is consistency rather than complexity. This easy run should feel smooth and comfortable, helping your body recover from yesterday's tempo session while maintaining your weekly mileage. Sat - Extended Long Run (7:00am–8:45am)

RUN Z1-Z2 105min
Warm-up: 10 minutes easy walking and jogging to gradually warm up
Main Set: 90 minutes at a comfortable, conversational pace (6:15-6:30/km). This builds to about 14-15km. Stay hydrated and fuel if needed in the final third.
Cool-down: 5 minutes walking with comprehensive stretching routine
Total: 105 minutes
Coach's Note:
Your longest run yet! This 20-minute increase is substantial, so start conservatively and focus on negative splits—running the second half at the same pace or slightly faster than the first half. Plan your hydration strategy and consider carrying water or mapping a route with fountains. Around the 75-minute mark, you might feel a slight dip in energy—this is normal and excellent training for race day. Stay patient, maintain your form, and remember that you're building incredible aerobic strength. If you feel good in the final 15 minutes, you've paced it perfectly.

Start conservatively and aim for even effort throughout. The final 30 minutes are where real fitness gains happen, so stay patient and trust your training. flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb breakfast (80-100g carbs) with familiar foods. Avoid high fiber or new foods. Include 400-500ml fluid.
2-3 hours before: Arrive at venue, set up gear, walk the course if possible. Continue sipping fluids (200-300ml/hour).
60-90 minutes before: Begin dynamic warm-up routine. 10 minutes easy jogging, leg swings, high knees, butt kicks.
30-45 minutes before: Optional fast-acting carbs (20-30g) - banana, sports drink, or gel. Complete gear check.
15 minutes before: Final bathroom stop, remove extra layers, apply sunscreen if needed.
5-10 minutes before: 3-4 strides at race pace, positive self-talk, get to start position early.
Your Race Strategy

Pacing Strategy

First 5km: Start 5-10 seconds per km slower than goal pace. Resist the urge to go with faster runners - your race happens in the second half.

Middle 10km (5-15km): Settle into goal pace rhythm. Focus on smooth, efficient form. This is your 'cruise control' phase - steady effort, controlled breathing.

Final 6km: Assess how you feel at 15km. If feeling strong, gradually increase effort. Break this section into smaller chunks - next aid station, next km marker. Push hard in final 2km.

Fueling & Hydration

Take water at every aid station (every 2-3km). Slow down if needed to drink properly. If using sports drink, alternate with water to avoid GI issues. No fueling needed for half marathon distance unless specifically trained.

Mental Strategy

Mantras & Self-Talk

When feeling good: 'Strong and smooth', 'I've trained for this', 'Relax and flow'

When struggling: 'I can do hard things', 'One step at a time', 'This is temporary'

Chunking Strategy

Break the race into three 7km sections: Section 1: Get into rhythm, stay controlled. Section 2: Maintain effort, stay patient. Section 3: Time to compete, give what you have left.

Difficulty Management

Expect to feel challenged around km 15-16. This is normal - your training has prepared you for this. Shorten your focus to the next km marker or aid station. Use positive self-talk and remember why you're here.

school Glossary

Zone 1-2 (Z1-Z2)

Easy aerobic intensity where you can maintain a conversation. Heart rate typically 65-75% of maximum. Forms the foundation of endurance training.

Zone 4-5 (Z4-Z5)

Hard to very hard intensity. Z4 is threshold pace (comfortably hard), Z5 is VO2max pace (hard breathing, 3-8 minute efforts). Used for speed and power development.

Taper

The planned reduction in training volume before a race while maintaining intensity. Allows the body to recover and peak for competition, typically 1-3 weeks depending on race distance.

Cadence

The number of steps per minute while running. Optimal cadence is typically 170-180 steps per minute, which reduces injury risk and improves efficiency.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race faster than the first half. A smart pacing strategy that conserves energy early and allows for a strong finish.

Strides

Short 80-100 meter accelerations at about 90% effort, used in warm-ups to prepare the neuromuscular system for faster paces. Include full recovery between each.

Race Pace

The target pace you plan to maintain during your goal race. Should be practiced regularly in training to develop pacing awareness and confidence.

Easy Pace

A comfortable aerobic pace where you can hold a conversation. For most runners, this is 1-2 minutes per km slower than race pace. Should form 80% of your training.

Threshold Pace

Comfortably hard pace you can sustain for 45-60 minutes in a race. Corresponds to lactate threshold - the point where lactate accumulation begins to exceed clearance.

VO2max Pace

Very hard pace corresponding to your maximum oxygen uptake. Typically sustainable for 6-8 minutes in trained runners. Used for short, intense intervals.

Active Recovery

Light exercise (easy walking, gentle jogging, or swimming) performed on rest days to promote blood flow and recovery without adding training stress.

Lactate Threshold

The exercise intensity at which lactate begins to accumulate faster than it can be cleared. Improving this threshold is key to better endurance performance.

Aerobic Base

The foundation of endurance fitness built through easy-paced, high-volume training. Improves the body's ability to use oxygen efficiently and burn fat for fuel.

Pickup

A short surge in pace during an otherwise easy run. Used to practice changing gears and add variety to training without significant fatigue.

psychology Coach's Tips

schedule

Trust Your Taper

Feeling sluggish or heavy-legged during taper is completely normal. Your body is adapting and storing energy for race day. Resist the urge to add extra training - less is more in these final weeks.

self_care

Prioritize Sleep

Aim for 8-9 hours of sleep per night, especially in the final 2 weeks. Poor sleep increases perceived effort and reduces performance more than missed training sessions.

psychology

Practice Race-Day Visualization

Spend 5-10 minutes daily visualizing your race strategy, including how you'll handle difficult moments. Mental rehearsal improves confidence and performance under pressure.

restaurant

Stick to Familiar Foods

Never try anything new in the final 2 weeks before your race. Stick to foods and drinks you've used successfully in training to avoid digestive issues on race day.

directions_run

Perfect Your Cadence

Focus on 170-180 steps per minute during easy runs. Count steps for 15 seconds and multiply by 4. Higher cadence reduces impact forces and injury risk while improving efficiency.

sports

Practice Positive Self-Talk

Develop 2-3 mantras for different race situations: when feeling good ('smooth and strong') and when struggling ('I can do hard things'). Practice these during harder training sessions.

water_drop

Hydrate Consistently

Maintain consistent hydration throughout taper - aim for pale yellow urine as a simple indicator. Don't dramatically increase fluid intake; just maintain good habits you've developed.

fitness_center

Keep Strength Work Light

If doing strength training during taper, reduce intensity to 60-70% of normal loads and stop sets 2-3 reps before failure. Focus on movement quality and injury prevention, not building strength.
