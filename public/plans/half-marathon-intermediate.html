Plan Focus

You've finished a half marathon. Now you're going to race one. This plan shifts from completion to performance — introducing structured tempo work, threshold intervals, VO2max sessions, and long runs with half marathon-pace segments. Volume builds progressively to ~55 km/week before tapering for race day. The aerobic engine you already have is the foundation. This plan sharpens it, adds top-end fitness, and teaches your body to hold pace when it wants to slow down. Two key sessions per week do the heavy lifting. Everything else supports them.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Identify the two key sessions — these are non-negotiable. Build your schedule around protecting them. Everything else can flex.
  2. Miss a quality session? Don't double up. Move on. Cramming two hard sessions into consecutive days creates fatigue without fitness. One missed session in 12 weeks changes nothing. Two blown weeks from overcompensation changes everything.
  3. Recovery weeks are where fitness consolidates. Weeks 4 and 8 are deliberately lighter. Your body supercompensates during rest, not during intervals. Trust the process — you'll feel sharper the week after.
  4. RPE is your primary intensity guide. Cross-reference heart rate if you have it, but perceived effort accounts for sleep, stress, heat, and fatigue in ways a heart rate strap cannot. If RPE and HR disagree, trust RPE.

Week 1 — Base: Establish Rhythmexpand_more
Week 1 of 12

Set the weekly template. Four sessions at moderate volume. The goal is to establish the rhythm you'll carry for 12 weeks — easy runs easy, quality days with purpose, and the long run as your anchor.

MondayRest
REST

Complete rest. Start the week recovered.

TuesdayEasy Run + Strides
RPE 3–48 km

10 min warm-up jog → 5 km easy → 6 × 20-sec strides with 40-sec walk recovery → 5 min cool-down jog.

Coach's NoteStrides activate the neuromuscular system without adding fatigue. Smooth acceleration to ~85% effort, hold briefly, decelerate. Not sprints — controlled speed.
WednesdayEasy Run
RPE 36 km

Continuous easy run. Conversational pace throughout. This is an aerobic maintenance session — nothing more.

ThursdayTempo Run
RPE 5–610 kmKEY

2 km warm-up → 20 min steady tempo at T3 → 2 km cool-down.

Coach's NoteTempo effort = comfortably hard. You can speak in short sentences but wouldn't choose to. This is the bread-and-butter session for half marathon fitness. Don't start too fast — settle in and hold.
FridayRest
REST

Complete rest before the weekend block.

SaturdayParkrun or Easy Run
RPE 4–55 km

Parkrun at controlled effort (RPE 5, not racing) or 5 km easy run. Use parkrun as a weekly fitness marker — not a race.

SundayLong Run
RPE 3–414 kmKEY

Easy pace throughout. Run by feel, not by watch. If the last 3 km feel the same as the first 3, you paced it right.

Coach's NoteThe long run is the anchor of every week. It builds aerobic endurance, fat oxidation, and musculoskeletal resilience. Protect this session above all others.

star Key Workouts — Thursday Tempo & Sunday Long Run

The Thursday tempo establishes your lactate threshold work. The Sunday long run builds endurance. These two sessions form the backbone of every training week in this plan. Everything else exists to support them.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 7–9 hours. Post-run nutrition within 60 minutes — carbs + protein (e.g., banana + yoghurt, toast + eggs). Stretch calves, hip flexors, and quads after every session. Foam roll if you have access.

psychology Weekly Check-In

"Did I run my easy days genuinely easy? Could I have held a full conversation the entire time — or was I kidding myself?"

Race Day Protocol

Your Half Marathon Race Day Execution

Night Before

Carb-rich dinner: pasta, rice, or potatoes with a lean protein. Nothing new — eat what you've eaten before long runs. Hydrate well but don't overdrink. Lay out all race kit, pin your number, charge your watch. Early bed — accept that sleep may be lighter than usual, and that's okay.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before start)

1–2g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, porridge, or a bagel with honey are reliable choices. Sip water or sports drink. Use the toilet before leaving the house. Take your gels with you — 3 gels minimum.

Arrival (60–75 min early)

Collect race number if not already done. Use toilets. Walk the start/finish area. Check bag drop. Give yourself time to settle nerves and avoid rushing.

Warm-up (15–20 min before start)

10 min easy jog → 4 × 20-sec strides with walk recovery. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles and an elevated heart rate. Seed yourself honestly in the correct corral.

Pacing Strategy

Start 10 seconds per kilometre slower than goal pace for the first 3 km. The adrenaline will make goal pace feel easy — that's a lie. Settle in by km 4. Even pace through km 5–15. If you feel strong at km 16, begin a controlled push. The race doesn't truly start until km 15 — save your cards for when they matter.

Nutrition During Race

2–3 gels: km 7, km 12, km 17. Take with water, not sports drink. Fluid at every aid station — small sips, don't skip. If you trained with gels on long runs, your gut is prepared. Do not try anything new on race day.

Post-Race

Walk 10–15 min to cool down. Drink electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 7–10 days of easy running before resuming structured training. Celebrate — you earned this.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

Protect the Key Sessions

Every week has 2–3 key sessions. If life gets in the way, sacrifice easy runs first. Never sacrifice the long run. If you can only do two sessions in a week, make them the long run and one quality session.

Easy Means Easy

80% of your running should be at conversational pace. Most intermediate runners train their easy days too hard and their hard days too easy. If you can't hold a full conversation, slow down. Your easy pace is slower than you think.

Don't Double Up

Missed a quality session? Move on. Don't cram two hard sessions into consecutive days to "catch up." One missed session changes nothing. Two weeks of accumulated fatigue from overcompensation derails a plan.

Recovery Weeks Are Non-Negotiable

Weeks 4 and 8 exist for a reason. Your body supercompensates during recovery — muscle repair, mitochondrial biogenesis, and glycogen replenishment happen when you rest. Skipping recovery weeks is the fastest way to plateau.

Train Your Gut

Practice race-day nutrition on every long run from Week 5 onward. Gels, fluids, timing — rehearse it all. Your stomach needs training just like your legs. Never try anything new on race day.

Sleep Is Your Best Training Tool

7–9 hours per night is the target. A bad night of sleep affects your next session more than a missed session does. Sleep is when growth hormone peaks and tissue repair happens.

RPE Over Pace

Conditions change. Fatigue accumulates. Heat, hills, wind, and life stress all affect pace. RPE accounts for all of it. If RPE and pace disagree, trust RPE. Your body is smarter than your watch.

The Long Run Is the Anchor

Everything else in the week supports the long run. If you have to rearrange your schedule, rearrange around Sunday. The long run builds the endurance, the fat oxidation, and the mental resilience that the half marathon demands.

Trust the Taper

You may feel anxious, restless, or sluggish during the taper. This is normal. Reduced training volume with maintained intensity is the evidence-based approach. You'll feel the benefit on race morning.

One Bad Session Is One Session

Every runner has bad days. If a session feels terrible, cut it short. Come back next time. Consistency over 12 weeks matters infinitely more than any single session.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Zone 1 (T1)
Recovery effort. Very easy. Barely above walking. Used for warm-ups, cool-downs, and active recovery.
Zone 2 (T2)
Aerobic base. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training happens. Builds fat oxidation and endurance.
Zone 3 (T3)
Tempo/threshold. Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. The primary quality zone for half marathon training.
Zone 4 (T4)
VO2max. Hard, laboured breathing. Used for 1 km interval repeats to raise the aerobic ceiling.
Tempo Run
Sustained effort at T3 (RPE 5–6). Develops lactate threshold — the pace you can hold for ~60 minutes.
Threshold Intervals
Repeated bouts at T3 with short recoveries (e.g., 3×10 min). Accumulates more time at intensity than continuous tempo.
VO2max Intervals
High-intensity repeats at T4 (e.g., 5×1 km). Develops maximum oxygen uptake — the ceiling of your aerobic system.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 sec) at ~85% effort. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
HM-Pace Segment
A portion of the long run at half marathon goal pace. Teaches the body what race effort feels like on tired legs.
Negative Split
Running the second half faster than the first half. The ideal half marathon pacing strategy for an intermediate runner.
Taper
Planned reduction in training volume (20–40%) with maintained intensity in the final 1–2 weeks before race day. Allows full recovery and supercompensation.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress week over week. How fitness is built safely and sustainably.
Supercompensation
The body's response to recovery after training stress — rebuilding to a higher level of fitness than before. Why recovery weeks exist.
