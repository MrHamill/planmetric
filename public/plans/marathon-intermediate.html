timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–5

Foundation building phase focused on aerobic development through easy-paced running. All sessions should be conversational with emphasis on building weekly volume gradually. No speed work or racing during this phase.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 6–11

Introduction of race-specific intensities and threshold work. Mix of easy runs, tempo efforts, and interval training to develop speed and lactate clearance. May include tune-up races for race practice.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 12–14

Highest intensity phase with regular race-pace efforts and simulation workouts. Sharpening phase that prepares your body for the specific demands of marathon racing through targeted training.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 15–16

Strategic reduction in training volume while maintaining some intensity to arrive at race day fresh and strong. Focus shifts to recovery, mental preparation, and race logistics while preserving fitness gained in previous phases.

RUN Z1-Z2 40min
Warm-up: 5 min walk, 5 min easy jog
Main Set: 30 min steady running at 6:15/km pace (Z1-Z2), fully conversational effort

Coach's Note:

Coach's Note:
Tempo pace should feel 'comfortably hard'—you could speak a few words but not hold a conversation. This effort builds your lactate threshold, teaching your body to clear metabolic waste more efficiently. Stay relaxed and maintain consistent effort rather than chasing specific splits. Remember to stay hydrated throughout this session.

flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: Final meal - familiar high-carb foods (oatmeal, banana, toast). 500-750ml fluid.
90 minutes before: Arrive at race venue, collect bib, light dynamic warm-up.
60 minutes before: Final bathroom visit, apply sunscreen, check gear one last time.
30 minutes before: Optional small carb snack (30g) if nervous stomach allows.
15 minutes before: Move to start area, positive self-talk, controlled breathing.
5 minutes before: Final gear check, settle into start position, focus on first 5km strategy.
Your Race Strategy

First 10km (Discipline Phase): Start conservatively at 6:25-6:30/km - 10-15 seconds slower than goal pace. Focus on smooth, relaxed form and settling into rhythm. Resist the urge to go with faster groups.

10-30km (Rhythm Phase): Gradually build to goal marathon pace (6:15/km). This is your steady cruising section - maintain even effort, not even pace. Fuel every 5km with sports drink or gel plus water.

30-42km (Character Phase): The real race begins here. Expect discomfort but trust your training. Break it into smaller chunks - next aid station, next kilometer marker. This is where mental strength and race-day nutrition pay dividends.

Mental Strategy

Chunking Strategy: Divide the race into four 10.5km segments, each with its own mini-goal. Focus only on completing the current segment, not the entire distance remaining.

Positive Mantras: 'Strong and steady', 'I've trained for this', 'Smooth is fast'. Practice these during your long runs so they feel natural on race day.

3 R's for Difficult Moments: Rest (accept the discomfort), Regroup (remember your training), Refocus (narrow attention to the next kilometer). Negative thoughts are normal - acknowledge them and return to your mantras.

school Glossary

Z1-Z2 (Easy Zone)

Aerobic base training zone where you can hold a full conversation. Heart rate typically 60-75% of maximum.

Conversational Pace

A running intensity where you can speak in full sentences without gasping for breath.

Taper

The final 2-3 week period before a race where training volume decreases to allow full recovery and peak performance.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race faster than the first half, a proven pacing strategy for marathons.

Fueling Window

The optimal 30-60 minute period after hard training when muscle glycogen replenishment is most efficient.

Cadence

Steps per minute while running. Optimal range is typically 170-180 spm for efficiency and injury prevention.

Glycogen

The stored form of carbohydrate in muscles and liver, your body's primary fuel source for endurance exercise.

Lactate Threshold

The exercise intensity where lactate begins to accumulate faster than it can be cleared from the blood.

RPE

Rate of Perceived Exertion - a 1-10 scale measuring how hard exercise feels subjectively.

Bonking/Hitting the Wall

The sudden onset of fatigue and loss of energy when muscle glycogen stores are depleted.

Carb Loading

Increasing carbohydrate intake in the days before a race to maximize muscle glycogen stores.

Heart Rate Drift

The gradual increase in heart rate during steady exercise, typically due to dehydration and rising core temperature.

Aerobic Base

The foundation fitness built through easy-pace training that improves fat burning and endurance capacity.

Recovery Run

Very easy-paced running designed to promote blood flow and active recovery between harder training sessions.

schedule

Trust Your Taper

Feeling restless or sluggish is normal during taper weeks. Your body is absorbing training and preparing for peak performance. Resist the urge to add extra sessions.

Hydration Strategy

Start hydrating well 2-3 days before race day, not just the morning of. Aim for pale yellow urine as your hydration indicator.

restaurant

Fuel Early and Often

Begin taking in carbs within the first hour of your marathon. Don't wait until you feel tired - prevention is better than trying to recover from depletion.

psychology

Mental Rehearsal

Practice your mantras and race strategy during long runs. Visualization and positive self-talk are skills that improve with repetition.

pace

Conservative Start

The first 10km should feel almost too easy. You cannot win a marathon in the first half, but you can definitely lose it there.

bed

Prioritize Sleep

Sleep is your most powerful recovery tool. Aim for 8-9 hours per night, especially in the final weeks before your race.

monitor_heart

Listen to Your Body

Unusual aches, persistent fatigue, or elevated resting heart rate are signals to ease back. Better to arrive at the start line slightly undertrained than injured.

check_circle

Race Day Preparation

Nothing new on race day. Practice your morning routine, test all nutrition products, and wear only proven gear during your training runs.
