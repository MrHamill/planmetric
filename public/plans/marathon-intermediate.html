Plan Focus

You've finished a marathon. Now you're going to race one. This plan assumes a solid aerobic base and experience managing 42.2km. It builds marathon-specific fitness through tempo runs, threshold intervals, VO2max work, and progressively longer long runs with marathon-pace segments. Volume peaks around 70km per week before a structured three-week taper. Every workout exists for a reason. Recovery weeks are non-negotiable. The long run is the anchor. And the race starts at kilometre 30 — everything before that is just getting there.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Know which sessions are the priorities so you can structure your schedule around them — if something has to give, protect the long run and the key interval session.
  2. Don't rearrange the hard days back-to-back. The spacing between quality sessions is deliberate — Tuesday intervals and Thursday tempo are separated by an easy day for a reason. If you must move a session, keep at least one easy day between hard efforts.
  3. Complete every recovery week as written. Weeks 5, 9, and 13 reduce volume deliberately. Skipping recovery is the most common intermediate mistake — you feel fit but the fatigue is accumulating beneath the surface. Adaptation happens during rest.
  4. Run by effort and feel, not by pace alone. This plan uses RPE zones. Your GPS watch doesn't know about yesterday's poor sleep, the headwind, or the hill you're on. If the plan says RPE 3–4, you should be able to hold a full conversation — regardless of what your watch says.
  5. Practise race-day fuelling on every long run from Week 6 onward. Your gut needs training just like your legs. Whatever you plan to eat on race day, rehearse it in training. No surprises on the start line.

RPE 3–46 km

SundayLong Run
RPE 3–416 kmKEY

Easy long run. Conversational pace the entire way. No surging, no finishing fast. Just time on feet.

Coach's NoteThe long run is the anchor of every week. Protect it above all other sessions. If something has to give midweek, it's never the Sunday long run.

star Key Workout — Sunday Long Run

16 km at conversational pace. This is your baseline long run. It should feel comfortable and controlled. If you finish feeling like you could have gone further, you ran it perfectly.

nightlight Recovery

Establish your recovery routine this week: post-run nutrition within 60 min (carbs + protein), 8+ hours sleep, and 10 min of stretching or foam rolling after each session. These habits compound across 16 weeks.

psychology Weekly Check-In

"Did I finish every session feeling like I had more in the tank? If yes, the pacing is right. If I felt depleted from easy runs, I'm going too hard."

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

The 80/20 Rule

80% of your running should be easy (T1–T2). 20% should be quality (T3–T4). If you're running your easy days too hard, you won't recover for the sessions that actually build fitness.

Protect the Long Run

If something has to give in a training week, it's never the Sunday long run. The long run is the most important session in marathon training — it builds the aerobic engine, musculoskeletal durability, and mental resilience you need for 42.2 km.

Fuelling Is a Skill

Practise race-day nutrition on every long run from Week 6. Your gut needs training. Aim for 30–60g carbs/hr during the marathon — gels, chews, or sports drink. If it causes GI distress in training, change it before race day.

Sleep Is Non-Negotiable

8–9 hours per night. A single bad night affects your next session more than a missed session. Prioritise sleep above all other recovery strategies.

Don't Race Your Training

Tempo runs and intervals have prescribed effort levels. Running them harder doesn't make you fitter — it makes you tired. Save your racing for race day.

Recovery Weeks Are Sacred

Weeks 5, 9, and 13 reduce volume deliberately. Don't add extra sessions because you "feel good." Feeling good during recovery is the point — your body is adapting.

Nothing New on Race Day

Shoes, socks, shorts, gels, breakfast — everything should be tested in training. Race day is not the time to try new equipment or nutrition.

Consistent Splits Win Marathons

The fastest marathon times come from even or slight negative splits. Starting too fast costs you more time in the last 10 km than it gains you in the first 10.

Monitor Fatigue Signals

Elevated resting heart rate, poor sleep, persistent soreness, irritability — these are signs of accumulating fatigue. If multiple signals appear, take an extra rest day. One day off now is better than three weeks off with an injury.

The Race Starts at km 30

Everything before km 30 is pacing and fuelling. The race — where mental toughness, preparation, and fitness converge — begins when the glycogen starts running low. That's what you've been training for.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Zone 2 (T2)
Aerobic base. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training happens. Builds fat oxidation, mitochondrial density, and endurance.
Zone 3 (T3)
Tempo/threshold. Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. Sustainable for 40–60 minutes. Improves lactate clearance.
Zone 4 (T4)
VO2max. Hard, laboured breathing. Sustainable for 3–8 minutes. Raises your aerobic ceiling.
Marathon Pace (MP)
The pace you plan to sustain for 42.2 km on race day. Sits at the top of T2 or bottom of T3. Should feel controlled and sustainable.
Threshold Intervals
Sustained efforts (8–12 min) at T3–T4 with recovery jog between reps. Improves lactate threshold — the pace you can sustain before fatigue accelerates.
VO2max Intervals
Shorter, faster intervals (1 km reps) at T4. Raises your maximum aerobic capacity, which makes marathon pace feel easier by comparison.
Tempo Run
Continuous effort at T3 (RPE 5–6) for 20–35 minutes. Builds sustained effort capacity and mental endurance.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at ~85% effort. Develops running economy and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
Negative Split
Running the second half faster than the first half. The gold-standard marathon pacing strategy.
Carb Loading
Increasing carbohydrate intake to 8–10g/kg body weight in the 48 hours before a marathon. Maximises muscle glycogen stores.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity, volume) week over week. How fitness is built safely and sustainably.
Taper
Reducing training volume 2–3 weeks before race day while maintaining intensity. Sheds fatigue and allows supercompensation.
Dress Rehearsal
A long training run that simulates race-day conditions: same fuelling, same pacing, same kit. Tests your entire race plan before the real thing.
Race Day Protocol

Your Marathon Race Day Protocol

48 Hours Before — Carb Loading

Increase carbohydrate intake to 8–10g per kg of body weight across the day. Rice, pasta, bread, potatoes, oats — familiar foods, nothing exotic. Reduce fibre slightly to minimise GI risk. Hydrate with water and electrolytes. This is not about eating more — it's about shifting the ratio toward carbs.

Night Before

Eat a normal carb-rich dinner. Nothing new. Prepare your race kit — lay out everything including gels, race number, timing chip, and backup items. Get to bed early. You may not sleep well — that's normal and won't affect your race.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before start)

2–3g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, white rice, banana, porridge — whatever you've tested in training. Sip water — don't overdrink. One coffee is fine if that's your routine.

Warm-up (20 min before start)

10 min easy jog → 4 × 20-sec strides. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles and an elevated heart rate. Take a last gel or sports drink 15 min before the gun.

Pacing Strategy

Start 10–15 seconds per km slower than your goal pace for the first 5 km. The adrenaline, the crowd, and the fresh legs will make goal pace feel too easy — resist the urge to bank time. Settle into marathon pace from km 5. Run even splits through the half. The race starts at km 30 — everything before that is just getting there. If you have anything left at km 35, that's when you race.

Fuelling During the Race

30–60g carbohydrates per hour from km 5 onward. Gels, chews, or sports drink — use exactly what you've tested in training. Take fluids every 15–20 minutes. In warm conditions, add sodium tablets or electrolyte mix. Don't wait until you're thirsty to drink or until you're bonking to fuel.

Post-Race

Walk for 10–15 min. Change into warm, dry clothes. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 10–14 days of easy walking or light jogging before structured training. Celebrate — you've earned it.
