Plan Focus

This plan takes you from base fitness to your first marathon finish line. The first four weeks build your aerobic foundation with easy runs and long runs up to 18 km. Every fourth week is a recovery week — non-negotiable. Strides arrive in Week 6 to sharpen form. Easy tempo work appears from Week 10 to build mental toughness at moderate effort. Your longest run peaks at 30–32 km in Weeks 14–15 before a structured taper into race day. Walk breaks are allowed and encouraged throughout — even on race day. The marathon rewards patience, not speed. 80% of your training happens at easy, conversational effort. That is the engine.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Know what's coming so you can plan your schedule around the key sessions — especially the Sunday long run.
  2. Don't skip ahead. If you miss a session, continue from where you are. If you miss a full week, repeat the previous week rather than jumping forward — your body doesn't know what week number it is.
  3. Complete every recovery week as written. Weeks 5, 9, and 13 are deliberately light. Skipping recovery is the most common mistake beginner marathoners make — adaptation happens during rest, not during the session.
  4. Run by effort, not by pace. This plan uses RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) instead of pace targets. Your GPS watch doesn't know how your legs feel. If the plan says RPE 3–4, you should be able to hold a full conversation.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

The 10% Rule

Never increase weekly running volume by more than 10%. This plan follows this rule strictly. If you repeat a week because life got in the way, don't skip ahead to "catch up."

Easy Means Easy

Zone 2 is the most underused zone in amateur athletes. Most people train their easy days too hard. If you can't hold a full conversation during an easy run, slow down.

The Long Run Is Sacred

If you can only do one session in a week, make it the long run. It builds fat oxidation, musculoskeletal durability, and mental resilience. Nothing else replicates it.

Walk Breaks Are Strategy

Planned walk breaks reduce impact, delay glycogen depletion, and keep your heart rate in the aerobic zone. Many experienced marathoners use run/walk strategy on race day. It's not a sign of weakness.

Practise Your Fuelling

Never try new food, gels, or drinks on race day. Every long run from Week 8 onward should practise your exact race-day nutrition. Your gut needs to be trained.

Sleep Is Your Best Training Tool

8–9 hours per night is the target. Sleep deprivation impairs recovery, increases injury risk, and reduces performance more than a missed session does.

The Race Begins at KM 30

The first 30 km is pacing. The last 12.2 km is racing. If you go out too fast, you'll pay for it. Start conservative. The marathon rewards patience.

Recovery Weeks Are Non-Negotiable

Weeks 5, 9, and 13 are deliberately light. Skipping recovery doesn't make you fitter — it makes you injured. Adaptation happens during rest.

Nothing New on Race Day

Shoes, socks, shorts, shirt, fuel, hydration — everything should be tested in training. Race day is not the time to experiment.

One Bad Run Is One Run

If a long run feels terrible, walk home. Come back next session. Consistency over perfection. One bad run doesn't undo 15 weeks of training.

RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Zone 1 (T1)
Recovery effort. Very easy. Barely above walking. Used for warm-ups, cool-downs, and active recovery.
Zone 2 (T2)
Aerobic base. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training happens. Builds fat oxidation and endurance.
Zone 3 (T3)
Tempo/threshold. Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. Introduced from Week 10 in this plan.
Conversational Pace
A pace where you can speak in full sentences without gasping. The gold standard for easy running.
Long Run
The anchor session of every week. Builds endurance, fat oxidation, musculoskeletal durability, and mental resilience. Always run at easy effort.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints. Introduced from Week 6.
Tempo
A sustained effort at T3 (RPE 5–6). Comfortably hard. Builds lactate clearance and mental toughness. Introduced from Week 10.
The Wall
The point (~30–35 km) where glycogen stores deplete and running becomes extremely difficult. Proper fuelling and pacing delay or avoid it.
Carb Loading
Increasing carbohydrate intake to 8–10g/kg body weight in the 24–48 hours before the race to maximise glycogen stores.
Glycogen
Stored carbohydrate in muscles and liver. The primary fuel source for marathon running. Depletes after ~90–120 minutes of exercise.
Taper
A planned reduction in training volume (30–50%) in the final 2–3 weeks before race day. Maintains fitness while allowing full recovery.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a race faster than the first half. The ideal marathon pacing strategy for beginners.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity) week over week. How fitness is built safely.
Race Day Protocol

Your Marathon Race Day Protocol

48 Hours Before — Carb Loading

Increase carbohydrate intake to 8–10g per kg body weight. Pasta, rice, bread, potatoes, fruit, juice. This is not about eating more — it's about eating more carbs. Reduce fat and fibre to make room. Hydrate well. Stay off your feet. This is a fuelling strategy, not a celebration.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before)

Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, porridge, or white rice are reliable choices. 1–2g carbs per kg body weight. Sip water — don't overdrink. Sip a coffee if you normally do.

Arrival (60–90 min early)

Collect race number if not done the day before. Use toilets. Walk the start area. Settle nerves. Pin gels to your shorts or belt. Know where the aid stations are.

Warm-up (15 min before start)

10 min easy walk/jog → 4 × 20-sec strides. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles and an elevated heart rate. Not too much — save it for 42.2 km.

Fuelling During the Race

30–60g carbohydrates per hour from km 5. Gels, chews, banana, dates — whatever you practised in training. Drink fluid every 20 minutes. If it's hot, add sodium (electrolyte tablets or salted water at aid stations). Don't wait until you're thirsty — by then you're already dehydrated.

Pacing Strategy

Start conservatively. The race begins at km 30. The first 10 km should feel almost too easy. If it feels comfortable at km 15, you're doing it right. The marathon punishes early aggression more than any other distance. Target even pace or negative split. Walk breaks at aid stations are a valid and effective strategy. Seed yourself honestly — don't go out with the front pack unless that's your level.

Run/Walk Strategy

Walking is not failure. A planned run/walk strategy (e.g., walk 1 minute every 3 km, or walk through every aid station) can deliver a faster finish time than continuous running for many first-time marathoners. It reduces cumulative impact, delays glycogen depletion, and keeps your heart rate manageable. If you trained with walk breaks, race with walk breaks.

Post-Race

Walk for 10 minutes to cool down. Rehydrate with water and electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 minutes. Allow 2–4 weeks of easy walking and gentle movement before any structured training. You are a marathoner.
