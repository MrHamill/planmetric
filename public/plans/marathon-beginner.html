timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–5

Foundation building phase focused entirely on aerobic development through easy-paced running. All sessions are conversational with emphasis on building weekly volume gradually. This phase develops your aerobic engine, capillary density, and mitochondrial capacity - the fundamental fitness that supports all future training.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 6–11

Progressive intensity phase where we introduce threshold runs, intervals, and race-pace work alongside continued aerobic base building. This phase develops your lactate threshold, VO2max, and race-specific fitness. Weekly volume continues to grow while maintaining the 80/20 easy-to-hard ratio.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 12–14

Highest intensity phase featuring race simulations, tempo runs, and speed work at reduced volume. Sessions become more race-specific with practice of pacing, fueling, and mental strategies. This phase sharpens your fitness and prepares your body and mind for race-day demands.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 15–16

Volume reduction phase (typically 2-3 weeks) designed to remove accumulated fatigue while maintaining fitness sharpness. Easy runs dominate with occasional short pickups at race pace. This phase allows your body to absorb training adaptations and arrive at the start line fresh and confident.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Week 1 — BASE
3.9h 13 Apr–19 2026
Welcome to your marathon journey! This first week establishes your aerobic base with mostly easy running. Focus on running at a truly conversational pace - you should be able to chat comfortably throughout your easy runs.

Tue - Easy Run (6:00am–6:40am)

RUN Z1-Z2 40min
Warm-up: 5 min easy walk, then 5 min very gentle jog
Main Set: 30 min steady running at 7:00/km pace (Z1-Z2) - conversational effort
Cool-down: 5 min walk
Total: 40 min
Coach's Note:
This is your first session - focus on finding your easy rhythm where you could hold a conversation throughout. Your easy pace should feel almost too slow initially, but this builds your aerobic engine efficiently. Land midfoot with a cadence around 170-180 steps per minute if possible. Remember, easy days must be genuinely easy to allow your body to adapt and recover properly.

Wed - Tempo Run (6:00am–6:50am)

RUN Z3 50min
Warm-up: 10 min easy jog at comfortable pace
Main Set: 30 min tempo running - comfortably hard effort where you could speak 3-4 words at a time (Z3)
Cool-down: 10 min easy jog and walk
Total: 50 min
Coach's Note:
Your first tempo session develops your lactate threshold - the pace you can sustain for roughly an hour. This should feel comfortably hard, not all-out racing. You're building your body's ability to clear lactate efficiently. Focus on smooth, controlled breathing and maintaining consistent effort rather than chasing a specific pace. This effort teaches your body to run efficiently at faster speeds.

Thu - Easy Run + Strength (6:00am–6:45am)

RUN Z1-Z2 45min
Warm-up: 5 min walk, then 5 min gentle jog
Main Set: 35 min easy running at 7:00/km pace - fully conversational effort
Cool-down: 5 min walk
Total: 45 min
Coach's Note:
Another easy-paced session to build aerobic fitness without stress. Schedule your strength work at least 3 hours after this run, or better yet, on a different day entirely. Focus on compound movements like single-leg squats, glute bridges, and planks - these build the stability and power that prevent running injuries. In the base phase, aim for 2 strength sessions per week, stopping 2-3 reps before failure to avoid excessive soreness.

Sat - Long Run (7:00am–8:40am)

RUN Z1-Z2 100min
Warm-up: 10 min easy jog building gradually
Main Set: 80 min continuous running at easy conversational pace (Z1-Z2) - approximately 11.5km at 7:00/km
Cool-down: 10 min walk
Total: 100 min
Coach's Note:
Your most important session of the week! This long run builds aerobic endurance and teaches your body to burn fat efficiently. Stay hydrated with 500-750ml per hour, especially if conditions are warm. The pace should feel comfortable enough that you could chat throughout - resist the urge to go faster. Practice your fueling strategy if the run extends beyond 90 minutes, taking in 30-60g carbs per hour. These long runs build the foundation for your marathon success.

flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb breakfast (oatmeal, banana, toast with honey). Aim for 200-300g carbs. Include familiar foods only.
2-3 hours before: Finish eating. Begin sipping water regularly. Final gear check and mental preparation.
60-90 minutes before: Arrive at race venue. Use bathroom, collect race number, find start area.
30-45 minutes before: Begin dynamic warm-up: 10 min easy walk-jog, leg swings, gentle stretching.
15-20 minutes before: Optional: 20-30g fast carbs (gel or sports drink). Final bathroom stop.
10 minutes before: Move to start corral. Practice deep breathing and positive mantras.
5 minutes before: Final mental check: race pace confirmed, fueling plan ready, positive mindset engaged.
Your Race Strategy

Pacing Strategy: Start conservatively at 7:10-7:15/km (5-15 seconds slower than goal pace) for the first 10km. This will feel easy - that's perfect! Move to goal pace (7:00/km) for the middle 20km. Save your push for the final 12km where you can gradually increase effort.

Fueling Plan: Take gel or sports drink every 5-6km starting at 8km mark. Don't wait until you feel you need it. Aim for 30-60g carbs per hour. Use aid stations for water and electrolytes, especially in the second half.

Mental Segments: Break the race into four 10.5km segments. Each segment has its own focus: establish rhythm, settle in, work phase, and victory charge. Use the crowd energy but don't get pulled into faster paces early.

Mental Strategy

Mantras: 'Strong and steady', 'I've trained for this', 'One kilometer at a time'. Practice these during your easy runs this week.

When it gets tough (it will around 30km): Use the 3 Rs - Rest (let go of any negative thoughts), Regroup (remember your training), Refocus (just get to the next kilometer marker). Break the remaining distance into small chunks.

Positive self-talk: Remind yourself that every training run has prepared you for this moment. When you feel strong, acknowledge it. When you feel challenged, remind yourself 'I can do hard things' and that discomfort is temporary.

Final push: At 35km, you have just 7km left - shorter than your typical easy runs. This is where all your training pays off. Trust your fitness and embrace the challenge of the final kilometers.

school Glossary

Z1-Z2 (Zone 1-2)

Easy conversational pace where you can speak in full sentences comfortably. Heart rate typically 60-75% of maximum.

Conversational Pace

A running intensity where you can maintain a normal conversation without becoming breathless.

Taper

A planned reduction in training volume (typically 2-3 weeks) before a race to allow the body to recover while maintaining fitness.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race faster than the first half, a strategy that often leads to better overall performance.

Carb Loading

Increasing carbohydrate intake in the days before a long race to maximize muscle glycogen stores.

Fueling

The strategic intake of carbohydrates and fluids during exercise to maintain energy levels and performance.

Aid Station

Designated points along a race course where water, sports drinks, and sometimes food are provided to athletes.

Race Pace

The target speed or effort level you plan to maintain during your goal race.

Shakeout Run

A short, easy run performed 1-2 days before a race to activate muscles without creating fatigue.

Glycogen

The stored form of carbohydrates in muscles and liver, serving as the primary fuel source for endurance exercise.

The Wall

The point in a marathon (typically around 30-35km) where glycogen stores become depleted and the pace dramatically slows.

Electrolytes

Essential minerals (sodium, potassium, magnesium) lost through sweat that need replacement during long exercise.

Dynamic Warm-up

Movement-based preparation involving leg swings, walking lunges, and gradual pace building rather than static stretching.

Cadence

The number of steps taken per minute while running, with an optimal range typically between 170-180 steps per minute.
