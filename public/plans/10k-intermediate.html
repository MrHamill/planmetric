timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–2

Foundation phase focusing entirely on aerobic development through easy-paced running. All sessions are in Zone 1-2 with emphasis on building weekly volume safely. No intensity work or racing — we're developing the aerobic engine that powers all future speed. Include technique work, strength training, and establish consistent training rhythms.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 3–6

Introduction of quality sessions while maintaining aerobic base. Tempo runs and longer intervals develop lactate threshold and VO2max. Training becomes more race-specific with 80% easy effort and 20% harder work. One lower-priority race permitted to practice pacing and race skills. Volume continues to build progressively.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 7–9

Highest training loads with maximum combination of volume and intensity. Sharp interval sessions develop race-specific speed while long runs maximize aerobic capacity. Race simulations every 3-4 days prepare body and mind for race demands. This is where we build the final layer of fitness before tapering.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 10–10

Dramatic volume reduction (40-60%) while maintaining neuromuscular sharpness with short, easy efforts. Body absorbs training adaptations and recovers fully for peak race performance. Focus shifts to rest, nutrition, mental preparation, and race logistics. Fitness is already built — now we let it emerge.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Week 1 — BASE
2.8h 13 Apr–19 2026
Welcome to your 10K training journey! This first week focuses on building your aerobic base with comfortable running at conversational pace. The tempo run introduces controlled effort work - aim for 'comfortably hard' where you could speak a few words but wouldn't want to hold a conversation.

Tue - Easy Tuesday (6:00am–6:30am)

RUN Z1-Z2 30min
Warm-up: 5 min easy walk, then 5 min gradual build to easy running pace
Main Set: 20 min steady running at 6:15/km pace - should feel conversational and comfortable throughout
Cool-down: 5 min easy walk with light stretching
Total: 30 min
Coach's Note:
This is your foundation pace - resist the urge to go faster even if you feel good. Focus on relaxed shoulders, landing midfoot, and maintaining a cadence around 170-180 steps per minute. Your breathing should be easy enough to hold a conversation throughout the entire run.

Wed - Tempo Wednesday (6:00am–6:35am)

RUN Z3 35min
Warm-up: 10 min easy running building gradually to tempo pace
Main Set: 20 min tempo running at comfortably hard effort (approximately 5:45-5:55/km) - this should feel like a pace you could maintain for 45-60 minutes
Cool-down: 5 min easy jogging and walking
Total: 35 min
Coach's Note:
Your first tempo session - this effort should feel controlled and sustainable, not breathless. You should be able to speak 3-4 words at a time but wouldn't want to chat. Focus on maintaining even effort rather than hitting exact splits, especially as you learn this intensity.

Thu - Easy Thursday + Strength (6:00am–6:30am)

RUN Z1-Z2 30min
Warm-up: 5 min easy walk, then 5 min gradual build to easy running pace
Main Set: 20 min steady running at 6:15/km pace - keep this truly easy as recovery from yesterday's tempo effort
Cool-down: 5 min easy walk with light stretching
Total: 30 min
Coach's Note:
Today's run should feel easier than Tuesday as your legs recover from yesterday's tempo work. Consider adding 15-20 minutes of strength work focusing on single-leg squats, glute bridges, and core stability 3-6 hours after this run or later in the evening. Base phase is ideal for building strength that will support your running through the training cycle.

Sat - Long Saturday (7:00am–8:10am)

RUN Z1-Z2 70min
Warm-up: 10 min easy walking and jogging to settle into rhythm
Main Set: 50 min continuous running at 6:15/km pace - focus on maintaining steady, relaxed effort throughout
Cool-down: 10 min easy walking with gentle stretching
Total: 70 min

Your most important session of the week! This long run builds your aerobic engine and mental resilience. Stay hydrated throughout and consider carrying water for sessions over 60 minutes. flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb breakfast (60-80g carbs): oatmeal with banana and honey, or toast with jam. Include 20-30g protein. Hydrate with 500ml water.
2 hours before: Stop eating solid food. Continue hydrating (200-300ml per hour). Begin mental preparation and visualization.
60 minutes before: Arrive at venue. Complete registration and course reconnaissance. Optional: 20-30g fast carbs (sports drink or gel) for events over 60 minutes.
30 minutes before: Begin warm-up routine: 10 min easy jog + 4 × 20 sec strides. Use bathroom. Final gear check.
10 minutes before: Move to start area. Final mental preparation. Practice positive self-talk mantras. Remove excess clothing.
5 minutes before: Focus on breathing and race strategy. Visualize strong start and first kilometer pacing. You're prepared and ready.
Your Race Strategy

Start Strategy: Begin 5-10 seconds per km slower than goal pace for the first 2km to settle into rhythm and avoid early lactate buildup. The crowd energy will make this feel too easy — trust your pacing discipline.

Middle Phase (3-7km): Gradually build to goal pace, maintaining smooth form and controlled breathing. Focus on efficient stride and relaxed shoulders. This is where your tempo run training pays dividends.

Final Push (8-10km): From 7km, begin progressive acceleration. At 8km, shift to slightly faster than goal pace. Final 1km: give everything you have — your interval training has prepared you for this lactate tolerance challenge.

Pacing Checkpoints: 1km (conservative), 3km (build to goal), 5km (halfway check), 7km (begin push), 9km (final acceleration). If ahead of schedule early, resist going faster. If slightly behind at 5km, stay patient and make up time in the final 3km.

Mental Strategy

Race Mantras: Practice these during training — 'Strong and steady,' 'I've trained for this,' 'Smooth is fast.' Use different mantras for different race phases: early patience, middle rhythm, final determination.

Chunking Strategy: Break the race into four 2.5km segments, each with its own mini-goal. Focus only on getting to the next marker rather than the full 10km distance. This makes the race feel more manageable when fatigue builds.

Adversity Management: When it gets hard (around 6-7km), remember that everyone else is suffering too — your training gives you the advantage. Use the '3 Rs': Rest (let go of negative thoughts), Regroup (refocus on form and breathing), Refocus (next checkpoint goal).

Positive Self-Talk: Replace 'This hurts' with 'I'm getting stronger.' Replace 'I can't maintain this' with 'I've done this pace in training.' Reframe pain as progress and evidence of your effort.

school Glossary

Zone 1-2 (Z1-Z2)

Easy aerobic effort where you can maintain full conversation. Heart rate typically 65-75% of maximum. This is your aerobic base-building pace.

Zone 3-4 (Z3-Z4)

Tempo/threshold effort that feels 'comfortably hard.' You can speak 3-4 words but not full sentences. Heart rate 80-90% of maximum.

Zone 4-5 (Z4-Z5)

Hard interval effort at or above lactate threshold. Speaking is very difficult. Heart rate 90-95%+ of maximum. Used for VO2max development.

Tempo Run

Sustained effort at comfortably hard pace, typically 15-40 minutes. Improves lactate clearance and teaches race pace discipline.

Interval Training

Repeated hard efforts with recovery periods. Develops VO2max, speed, and lactate tolerance. Key session for 5K-10K performance.

Long Run

Extended easy-paced run building aerobic capacity and mental toughness. Primary session for endurance development in all race distances.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race or training run faster than the first half. Optimal pacing strategy for most distances.

5K Pace

The speed you can sustain for a 5K race — typically 15-30 seconds per km faster than 10K goal pace. Used for interval training.

VO2max

Maximum oxygen uptake capacity. Higher VO2max enables faster sustained speeds. Improved through interval training at 5K pace or faster.

Lactate Threshold

The exercise intensity where lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared. Training at this intensity improves racing efficiency.

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume (20-50%) while maintaining intensity. Allows body to recover and peak for race day performance.

Recovery Week

Planned low-volume week (50-60% of normal load) every 3-4 weeks. Essential for adaptation and preventing overtraining.

Strides

Short 15-20 second accelerations to near-maximum speed with full recovery. Used in warm-ups to activate neuromuscular system.

Race Pace

Target speed for your goal race distance. Should be practiced regularly in training to develop pacing confidence and physiological adaptation.

Conversational Pace

Easy running speed where you can maintain full conversation without breathing difficulty. Forms 80% of total training volume.

schedule

Master Your Pacing

Start every run and race 5-10 seconds per km slower than goal pace. It always feels too easy early, but this discipline prevents early fatigue and enables strong finishing.

water_drop

Hydrate Before Thirst

Drink 150-200ml water every 15-20 minutes during runs over 60 minutes. Monitor urine color post-run — pale yellow indicates proper hydration.

local_fire_department

Fuel Your Long Runs

Start consuming 30-60g carbs per hour after 60 minutes of running. Practice with race-day nutrition products to train your gut and prevent gastric distress.

chat

Talk Test for Easy Days

Easy runs should allow full conversation throughout. If you can't speak in complete sentences, you're running too fast and compromising recovery.

psychology

Chunk Your Hard Sessions

Break long intervals and races into smaller segments. Focus only on reaching the next marker rather than the total distance — makes efforts feel manageable.

hotel

Prioritize Sleep

Aim for 8-9 hours per night, especially during build and peak phases. Sleep is when adaptation occurs — poor sleep reduces training gains and increases injury risk.

fitness_center

Strength Train Smart

Include 2 sessions per week during base phase, reducing to 1 during peak. Focus on single-leg movements and hip stability — schedule 3-6 hours away from hard runs.

self_improvement

Practice Mental Skills

Rehearse positive mantras during training: 'Strong and steady,' 'I've trained for this.' Mental toughness is a trainable skill that improves race performance.
