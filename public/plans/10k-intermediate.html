From finisher to racer.

Plan Focus

You can finish a 10K. This plan is about racing one. Over 10 weeks you will develop your lactate threshold through tempo runs, build VO2max capacity with structured intervals, and sharpen race-specific fitness with parkrun efforts and long runs with tempo finishes. The aerobic base stays strong with easy volume between quality sessions. Two recovery weeks ensure you absorb the training rather than accumulate fatigue. The final taper strips volume while maintaining intensity so you arrive at the start line sharp, fresh, and ready to set a new personal best.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. The session order matters — quality days are separated by easy days for a reason. Don't rearrange the week unless you understand the load pattern.
  2. Respect the recovery weeks. Weeks 4 and 8 are deliberately reduced. If you feel great during a recovery week, that means the taper is working — it does not mean you should add volume.
  3. Run easy days genuinely easy. At this level, the biggest performance limiter is running easy days too fast. If Tuesday's intervals were hard, Wednesday must be truly easy or you will compromise Thursday's tempo session.
  4. Use parkrun strategically. Saturday parkrun appears as a quality session in build weeks. Run it at controlled effort, not all-out — save the full race effort for Week 10.
  5. Run by RPE unless you have tested zones. This plan uses RPE-based intensity. If you know your threshold pace or heart rate, use it — but RPE remains the final arbiter when conditions or fatigue shift.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

Easy Means Easy

The most common mistake intermediate runners make is running easy days too fast. If your easy runs are creeping above T2, you are compromising your quality sessions. Slow down to speed up.

Respect the Recovery Weeks

Weeks 4 and 8 are not optional. Adaptation happens during rest, not during sessions. Skipping recovery weeks leads to accumulated fatigue and plateaus — or worse, injury.

Pacing Your Intervals

VO2max intervals should be at 10K race pace, not 5K pace. If you can't complete all reps at the same pace, you started too fast. Even pacing across every rep is the goal.

Tempo Is Not Threshold Max

T3 should feel "comfortably hard" — sustainable for 40–60 minutes in a race. If you are gasping during a tempo run, you are in T4. Back off slightly. Controlled effort beats heroic effort.

The Long Run Is Sacred

The Sunday long run is the anchor of every week. Never sacrifice it for a midweek session. If you have to skip something, skip anything else first.

Parkrun Is Practice, Not a Race

In this plan, parkrun is a controlled effort session at 10K race pace. Save the all-out 5K effort for a dedicated 5K race. Running parkrun at max effort compromises Sunday's long run.

Fuel Your Quality Sessions

Eat carbohydrates 2–3 hours before interval and tempo sessions. Post-session: carbs + protein within 60 minutes. For runs over 60 minutes, consider carrying a gel or energy drink.

Sleep Over Everything

8–9 hours per night. A bad night of sleep affects your next session more than a missed session does. If you must choose between an early alarm for a run and an extra hour of sleep, choose sleep.

Nothing New on Race Day

No new shoes, no new nutrition, no new clothing. Race day is not for experiments. Use your training to test everything — then execute on race day with familiar kit.

Listen to Your Body

Fatigue is expected. Pain is a warning. If something feels like an injury — sharp, localised, or worsening — take an extra rest day. One missed session is nothing. One injury can cost you weeks.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
VO2max
The maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during exercise. VO2max intervals (T4) improve this ceiling, making race pace feel easier.
Lactate Threshold
The intensity above which lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared. Tempo runs at T3 push this threshold higher, allowing you to sustain faster paces for longer.
Tempo
A sustained effort at T3 (RPE 5–6). "Comfortably hard" — roughly the pace you could sustain for 60 minutes in a race. The core quality session for 10K runners.
Fartlek
Swedish for "speed play." Unstructured variations in pace during a run — surges, lifts, and recoveries based on feel rather than prescribed intervals. Useful for developing pace awareness.
Zone 1 (T1)
Recovery effort. Very easy. Barely above walking. Used for warm-ups, cool-downs, and active recovery days.
Zone 2 (T2)
Aerobic base. Easy and conversational. Where the majority of your weekly volume sits. Builds fat oxidation and aerobic endurance.
Zone 3 (T3)
Tempo/threshold. Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. The primary quality zone in this plan.
Zone 4 (T4)
VO2max. Hard. Laboured breathing. Used for 1 km interval reps at 10K race pace.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a race faster than the first half. The ideal 10K pacing strategy. Requires patience in the early kilometres.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at ~85% effort. Develops running form, neuromuscular coordination, and leg speed without adding fatigue.
Threshold Blocks
Multiple sustained efforts at T3 with brief recovery jogs between. Accumulates more total time at threshold than a single continuous tempo while maintaining better form.
Tempo Finish
A long run where the final segment is run at T3 tempo pace. Simulates the effort of racing on tired legs — highly specific to 10K performance.
Taper
A planned reduction in training volume before race day. Maintains intensity while reducing volume so the body can absorb accumulated training and arrive fresh.
Race Day Protocol

Your 10K Race Day Execution

Night Before

Eat a normal meal with carbohydrates — pasta, rice, potatoes. Hydrate well but don't overdrink. Prepare your kit: race number, shoes, watch, clothing, nutrition. Visualise the race — your warm-up, start position, pacing, the finish. Early to bed.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before)

1–2g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, porridge, or a bagel with honey are reliable choices. Sip water or sports drink. Coffee if it's part of your routine.

Arrival (45–60 min early)

Collect race number if not collected. Use toilets. Walk the start/finish area. Identify km markers if visible. Give yourself time to settle nerves and prepare mentally.

Warm-up (20 min before start)

15 min easy jog → 4 × 20-sec strides with 40-sec walk. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles and an elevated heart rate. Dynamic stretches: leg swings, high knees, butt kicks.

Pacing Strategy

Negative split. KM 1–2: Controlled start at RPE 7. Settle into the pace. Do not go out with faster runners. KM 3–7: Find your rhythm. RPE 7–8. Even effort, even splits. This is where the race is run. KM 8–9: Begin to push. RPE 8–9. Access the threshold fitness you have built. KM 10: Everything you have left. This is why you trained.

Post-Race

Walk for 10 minutes. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 minutes. Stretch gently. Allow 5–7 days of easy running before any quality sessions. Reflect on the race — what went well, what to improve next time.
