How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Your schedule weaves triathlon around soccer — plan pool access, bike routes, and run sessions at the start of each week so nothing clashes.
  2. Train by HR and pace, not just feel. This plan gives you specific heart rate zones and pace targets for every session. Use your GPS watch and HR monitor. If your HR is above the prescribed zone, slow down — ego is the enemy of aerobic development.
  3. Complete every recovery week as written (Weeks 4, 8, 12, 16, 20). You love intensity and hate rest — but adaptation happens during recovery, not during the session. Skipping recovery is the fastest way to plateau or get injured. Rest is performance-enhancing, not laziness.
  4. Soccer counts as training load. Tuesday and Thursday afternoons add ~4 hours of running per week to your body. This plan accounts for that — do not add extra run sessions on soccer days.
  5. Practise nutrition on the bike from Week 9. You have never fuelled during racing. Your gut needs 10–12 weeks of progressive training to absorb 70–80 g/hr of carbohydrates without GI distress. Start at 40 g/hr and build.
  6. Sleep is your biggest performance gap. You are averaging 6–7 hours. Every additional 30 minutes of sleep measurably improves recovery, HRV, and training adaptation. Aim for 7.5–8 hours. This single change will have more impact than any workout in this plan.

MondaySwim — CSS Test
SWIM1800mKEY

Warm-up: 400m easy (200 free / 100 back / 100 choice) at Recovery pace (2:10+/100m). 4 × 50m build (start easy, finish strong) with 15s rest. Coach’s NoteThis is the most important swim session of the entire plan. Your CSS (Critical Swim Speed) is calculated as: CSS = (400m time − 200m time) / 2. Example: if you swim 400m in 7:00 and 200m in 3:20, your CSS = (420 − 200) / 2 = 110 sec/100m = 1:50/100m. This number calibrates every swim interval for the next 24 weeks. Give maximum effort on both time trials — they should hurt. Pace the 400m evenly; don’t fly the first 100m and die. Warm up thoroughly. Record both times in your training log. If your CSS differs from the estimated 1:55/100m in the zones table, adjust all swim paces accordingly.

Glossaryexpand_more
CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
Your sustainable threshold pace in the pool — the fastest pace you can hold for about 20 minutes. Calculated from a 400m + 200m time trial. Your estimated CSS: ~1:55/100m.
Karvonen Formula
Heart rate zone calculation using resting HR and max HR: Training HR = Resting HR + (% × (Max HR − Resting HR)). More accurate than simple % max HR.
Brick Session
A workout combining two disciplines back-to-back, typically bike then run. Trains the body to perform the second discipline on fatigued legs.
T1 / T2
T1: transition from swim to bike. T2: transition from bike to run. Practise both in training so race day feels familiar.
Z2 / T2 (Aerobic Base)
Your primary training zone. Easy, conversational, sustainable for hours. HR 120–151 (run) / 112–143 (bike). Where 80% of your training happens.
Z3 / T3 (Tempo)
Race effort zone. Comfortably hard, short sentences only. HR 151–167 (run) / 143–159 (bike). Your race-day intensity.
Cadence
Pedal revolutions per minute (RPM). Target 85–95 RPM for triathlon — higher cadence spares leg muscles for the run.
Sighting
Lifting your eyes briefly while swimming to navigate in open water. Crocodile sighting: eyes only above water, every 8–10 strokes.
Gut Training
Progressively increasing carbohydrate intake during training to improve absorption during racing. Your protocol: 40 g/hr (Week 9) → 70–80 g/hr (race day).
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at ~85% effort. Develop running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
Over-Unders
Alternating efforts above and below threshold. Trains lactate clearance — simulates race surges on the bike.
Negative Split
Completing the second half faster than the first. The ideal pacing strategy for all three disciplines.
Sweet Spot
88–93% of FTP on the bike. High adaptation stimulus with manageable fatigue. The most efficient training intensity.
Taper
Planned reduction in volume before race day. Volume drops 20–50%, intensity maintained. Allows your body to absorb training and arrive fresh.
NHE1 Upregulation
Speed endurance training increases NHE1 protein expression (acid clearance in muscles) by 30–75%. Improves recovery between efforts.
Active Recovery
Very light movement (Z1 / T1) to promote blood flow without adding training stress. HR <120 (run) / <112 (bike).
