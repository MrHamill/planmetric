A 70.3 is a half-distance Ironman — also called a Half Ironman. The typical race-day cutoff is 8 hours 30 minutes. Most beginner athletes finish in 5:30–7:30 depending on the course. The swim takes 35–50 min, the bike 2:45–3:30, and the run 2:00–2:45. You will transition twice: T1 (swim→bike) and T2 (bike→run). The bike leg is the longest and most important discipline to train — it accounts for roughly half of your total race time. Nutrition on the bike determines how your run feels. This plan prepares you for all of it.

Plan Focus

This plan takes you from little or no triathlon experience to crossing the 70.3 finish line. Weeks 1–5 build basic competence in all three disciplines — you will learn to swim with structured sets, ride for 90+ minutes, and run easy off the bike. Week 4 is your first recovery week. Weeks 6–10 introduce brick sessions (bike→run), longer rides building toward race distance, and open water swimming. Week 8 is another recovery week. Weeks 11–13 are race-specific — your longest bike ride, a race-simulation brick, and transition rehearsals. Weeks 14–16 taper you into race day fresh, confident, and ready. Every session has a purpose. Rest days are training days. The long bike ride is the anchor session of every week. 80% of your development happens at easy intensity — that is the engine.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Triathlon has three disciplines and transitions to manage — you need to plan your week around pool access, bike routes, and run sessions.
  2. Don’t skip ahead. If you miss a session, just continue from where you are. If you miss a full week, repeat the previous week rather than jumping forward — your body doesn’t know what week number it is.
  3. Complete every recovery week as written. Weeks 4, 8, and 12 are deliberately light. Adaptation happens during rest, not during the session — skipping recovery is the most common mistake beginners make.
  4. Train by effort, not by pace. This plan uses RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) instead of pace targets. If the plan says RPE 3–4, you should be able to hold a full conversation.
  5. Practise transitions. T1 (swim→bike) and T2 (bike→run) are the “fourth discipline.” Rehearse them in training so race day feels familiar, not frantic.
  6. Practise nutrition on the bike. For a 70.3, you need 60–80 g of carbohydrates per hour on the bike leg. Your gut needs to be trained for this — start practising from Week 6 onwards on every long ride.

Base Phase— Weeks 1–5 — Build aerobic fitness across all three disciplines
Week 1 — Foundationexpand_more
Week 1 of 16

The only goal this week is to start all three disciplines. Get in the pool, get on the bike, get out for a run. Don’t chase distance or speed. Build the habit of training across swim, bike, and run.

MondayRest
REST

Complete rest.

TuesdaySwim — Technique Intro
SWIMRPE 3–41400m

200m easy warm-up (mix of freestyle and backstroke) → 4 × 50m catch-up drill with 15s rest (one arm stays extended until the other hand touches it — teaches patience and glide) → 6 × 100m freestyle at Aerobic zone with 20s rest (focus on exhaling fully underwater, inhaling to the side) → 200m easy cool-down (choice of stroke).

Coach’s NoteIf you can’t swim 100m freestyle without stopping, use 50m reps instead. The goal is smooth, rhythmic breathing — exhale underwater, inhale to the side. Holding your breath is the most common mistake and causes rapid fatigue. Begin exhaling through nose and mouth as soon as your face enters the water so your lungs are partially empty by the time you rotate to breathe.
WednesdayBike — Easy Endurance
BIKERPE 3–460 min

10 min easy spin warm-up at Z1, gradually raising cadence to 85+ RPM → 40 min steady at Z2 on a flat route → 10 min easy spin cool-down at Z1. Focus on smooth pedalling and staying relaxed in the shoulders. Cadence target: 80–90 RPM.

Coach’s NoteThis is about time on the bike, not distance. If you only have a stationary trainer, that works perfectly — indoor sessions produce similar training stress with no coasting or traffic lights. Keep it conversational. If you can’t talk comfortably, ease off the pedals.
ThursdayRun — Easy Run
RUNRPE 3–430 min

5 min walk warm-up → 20 min easy run at T2 → 5 min walk cool-down. Conversational pace throughout. If you need walk breaks, use run/walk intervals of 3 min run / 1 min walk.

Coach’s NoteRunning is the highest injury-risk discipline in triathlon. Start conservative. Walk breaks are always allowed and are part of the strategy, not a sign of failure. Run off-road where possible to reduce impact stress on your joints.
FridayRest
REST

Complete rest. At least one full rest day per week is non-negotiable.

SaturdayBike — Long Ride
BIKERPE 3–475 minKEY

10 min easy spin warm-up at Z1 → 55 min steady at Z2 (flat to gently rolling route) → 10 min easy spin cool-down. Practise drinking from your bottle while riding — aim for one sip every 15 min.

Coach’s NoteThe long ride is the anchor session of every week for the next 16 weeks. Do not skip it. It builds the aerobic engine that carries you through all three disciplines on race day. The bike is the best discipline for accumulating aerobic volume safely because it has the lowest impact stress. If you finish feeling like you could have done more, you rode it perfectly.
SundayRun — Easy Long Run
RUNRPE 3–435 min

5 min walk warm-up → 25 min easy run at T2 → 5 min walk cool-down. Longest run of the week. Easy effort throughout. Walk breaks encouraged.

Coach’s NoteRunning on Sunday after a long bike on Saturday simulates mild fatigue — a small preview of how your legs will feel on race day after the bike leg. Go slow. The purpose is time on feet, not speed.

star Key Workout — Saturday Long Ride

75 minutes of easy cycling builds your aerobic base more than any other session this week. Stay in Z2. The bike accounts for roughly half your race time — every minute invested on the bike pays off on race day. If you can only do one session this week, do this one.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 8+ hours. Hydrate throughout the day. Stretch calves, quads, hip flexors, and shoulders for 5 minutes after each session. Your shoulders may be sore from swimming — that’s normal if you haven’t swum recently. Soreness is expected; sharp pain is not.

psychology Weekly Check-In

“Did I complete a swim, a bike, and a run this week? If not, what got in the way — and can I solve it before next week?”

Week 2 of 16

Same template, slightly longer sessions. You’re building a weekly rhythm across all three disciplines. Swim technique is still the focus in the pool — efficient strokes save enormous energy over 1.9 km on race day.

TuesdaySwim — Technique + Endurance
SWIMRPE 3–41600m

Week 3 — Consistencyexpand_more
Week 3 of 16

Volume increases slightly. The swim adds more continuous freestyle. The long ride extends to 90 min. This is the last build week before your first recovery week — your body needs the load before it can absorb it.

MondayRest
REST

TuesdaySwim — Endurance Build
SWIMRPE 3–41800m

300m easy warm-up (100 free / 100 back / 100 choice) → 4 × 50m single-arm drill with 15s rest (one arm stays at your side while the other completes full strokes — develops body rotation and catch awareness) → 3 × 200m freestyle at Aerobic zone with 25s rest → 4 × 50m easy with 10s rest → 200m cool-down.

Coach’s NoteThe 200m reps build continuous swimming endurance. Focus on a steady stroke count per length. If your stroke count creeps up, you’re losing efficiency — slow down and lengthen your stroke. WednesdayBike — Easy Ride + Cadence
BIKERPE 3–465 min

10 min easy spin warm-up → 3 × 5 min high cadence spins at 95–105 RPM in Z2 with 3 min normal cadence between → 20 min steady Z2 → 10 min cool-down. Include gentle hills if available. Stay seated on climbs, keep cadence above 70 RPM.

Coach’s NoteHigher cadence (90–100 RPM) is more cardiovascular than muscular. For triathletes, this matters because a higher cadence on the bike spares your leg muscles for the run. Many age-groupers default to 70–75 RPM because it feels powerful — but it costs them on the run.

BIKERPE 3–490 minKEY

Week 6 — First Brickexpand_more
Week 6 of 16

A landmark week: your first brick session. You will ride the bike and immediately run — no break. Your legs will feel strange. That is the point. You also swim 2000m+ for the first time in a structured set. This is where triathlon training begins to feel real.

MondayRest
REST

TuesdaySwim — CSS Introduction
SWIMRPE 3–52100m

300m easy warm-up → 4 × 50m catch-up drill with 15s rest → 8 × 100m at CSS pace (comfortably hard — breathing every 3 strokes, controlled) with 15s rest → 4 × 50m easy kick with 10s rest → 200m cool-down.

Coach’s NoteCSS stands for Critical Swim Speed — roughly the fastest pace you can sustain aerobically. For now, swim these 100m reps at a pace that feels “comfortably hard” — you should be able to maintain the same pace across all 8 reps. If the last reps are significantly slower, you started too fast. Consistency across reps matters more than the first few being fast.

SaturdayBrick — Bike + Run
BRICKRPE 3–490 min bike + 15 min runKEY

Bike: 10 min easy spin warm-up → 70 min steady Z2 with nutrition practice (aim for 50 g carbs/hr) → 10 min cool-down spin. Transition: Immediately dismount, rack bike, change shoes (practise race-day sequence). Run: 15 min easy run at T2. Walk the first 2 min if needed — your legs will feel heavy and strange. This is “brick legs” and it diminishes with practice.

Coach’s NoteYour first brick session. The purpose is neuromuscular adaptation to the discipline transition — teaching your body to switch from cycling to running. The “brick legs” sensation (heavy, wooden feeling) is caused by the sudden change in muscle recruitment pattern. It improves dramatically with repeated practice. Do not chase pace on the run — just run easy and let the sensation settle. By kilometre 2–3, your legs will begin to feel more normal.
SundayRest or Easy Walk
RPE 1–230 min

Easy walk if desired. No running. Let your body process the brick session.

star Key Workout — Saturday Brick Session

Your first bike-to-run transition. This is the defining workout of triathlon training — nothing else replicates the sensation of running on “bike legs.” Research shows you need at least 2–4 brick sessions in the 6 weeks before race day. This is the first. The heavy-leg feeling is normal and it gets better every time you practise.

nightlight Recovery

Brick sessions are more fatiguing than their individual parts suggest. Post-session: carbs + protein within 60 min. Foam roll quads, hamstrings, and calves. Elevate legs for 10 min. Sleep 8+ hours. Take Sunday genuinely easy.

“How did the brick feel? Did the heavy-leg sensation ease after a few minutes of running? Did I fuel adequately on the bike?”

Week 7 — Build IIexpand_more
Week 7 of 16

Volume builds. Long ride extends to 2 hours. Swim pushes to 2200m. Sunday run reaches 50 min. You are now training at volumes that require attention to nutrition, sleep, and recovery between sessions.

TuesdaySwim — Endurance + Sighting
SWIMRPE 3–42200m

300m easy warm-up → 4 × 50m fingertip drag drill with 15s rest → 6 × 150m freestyle at Aerobic zone with 20s rest (every 10 strokes, lift eyes to sight an object at the end of the pool — “crocodile sighting”) → 4 × 50m easy with 10s rest → 200m cool-down.

Coach’s NoteSighting is the most valuable open water skill. In open water, even a small sighting error compounds over 1900m into significant added distance — a 1° error adds ~25m. Full head lift drops your hips, increases drag, and wastes energy. “Crocodile sighting” means eyes only — chin stays at water level.

Coach’s NoteFirst structured intensity on the bike. Tempo blocks sit at Z3 — above easy but below threshold. They develop aerobic efficiency and teach your body to sustain moderate effort. Race Day Protocol

Your 70.3 Race Day

Night Before

Eat a normal meal with carbohydrates — pasta, rice, potatoes. Familiar foods only. Hydrate well but don’t overdrink. Prepare all gear in transition order: swim kit (wetsuit, goggles, cap), bike kit (helmet, shoes, sunglasses, nutrition), run kit (shoes, race belt, hat). Check tyre pressure. Pin race number to race belt. Set two alarms. Get to bed early — the sleep 2 nights before matters more than the night before.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before start)

Pre-race meal: 1–2 g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, porridge, or a bagel with honey. Sip water — don’t overdrink. Take a gel or energy chew 15–20 min before the swim start. Use the toilets before heading to transition.

Arrival (60–90 min early)

Set up transition: bike racked, helmet on bars (open and ready), shoes positioned, nutrition taped to bike frame or in bento box, run gear laid out in order. Walk the transition area — know your entry and exit points for T1 and T2. Use a landmark to find your rack position. Put on wetsuit 15–20 min before start.

Warm-Up (10–15 min before)

If the swim warm-up area is open, swim 200–300m easy to settle breathing. If no warm-up is available, splash cold water on your face and neck to reduce the cold shock response. Do arm swings and light jogging. Take 10 slow nasal inhales to calm your nervous system.

Swim — 1.9 km

Start conservative. Do not sprint the first 200m. The first 200m is the highest risk for panic — adrenaline spike + cold shock + body contact. Start at the side or back of your wave to avoid the central “washing machine.” Settle into your rhythm by 300m. Sight every 10 strokes using a fixed landmark. Breathe bilaterally if comfortable. If you feel panicky: roll to your back, float, breathe slowly, then resume. Breaststroke is acceptable mid-race for recovery. Target: even pace or slight negative split. Do not chase faster swimmers.

T1 — Swim to Bike

Run out of the water. Unzip wetsuit at the waist while jogging. At your rack: pull arms out, push suit to ankles, step out. Helmet on (buckle it!), sunglasses on, bike shoes on. Grab bike. Run to mount line. Mount and go. Don’t rush — 30 seconds of smooth transition beats 15 seconds of flustered chaos.

Bike — 90 km

Don’t go too hard. Ride at Z2–Z3. You still have a half marathon after this. Begin eating and drinking within the first 15 min. Target: 60–80 g carbs/hr using gels, bars, or sports drink. Drink 500–750 mL/hr. Electrolytes (300–700 mg sodium/hr) if conditions are warm. Hold steady effort on climbs — don’t surge. Maintain legal drafting distance. Cadence 85–95 RPM. If you feel strong at km 60, resist the urge to accelerate — save it for the run.

T2 — Bike to Run

Dismount before the line. Run to your rack. Rack bike, remove helmet, change to run shoes, grab race belt and hat/visor. Go. Stay calm. Smooth is fast.

Run — 21.1 km

The first 3 km will feel strange. Your legs are transitioning from cycling to running. Start very slowly. Walk through the first aid station. Sip water or sports drink. Take a gel at km 3. By km 5, your legs will settle. Then find a rhythm you can hold to the finish. Walk the aid stations if needed — it’s smarter than blowing up. Target: 30–60 g carbs/hr on the run (gel every 30–40 min + water from aid stations). Aim for even effort or slight negative split. Save something for the last 3 km and finish strong. You have earned this finish line.

Post-Race

Walk for 10 min. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 10–14 days of easy movement before any structured training. Enjoy the finish line feeling — you are a 70.3 finisher.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Brick Session
A workout combining two disciplines back-to-back, typically bike then run. Trains the body to perform the second discipline on fatigued legs — simulating race conditions.
T1 (Transition 1)
The transition from swim to bike. Includes exiting the water, removing wetsuit, putting on helmet and bike shoes, and mounting the bike.
T2 (Transition 2)
The transition from bike to run. Includes dismounting, racking the bike, removing helmet, changing to run shoes, and heading out on the run course.
CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
Your sustainable threshold pace in the pool — the fastest pace you can hold for about 20 minutes. Used for swim interval training. Calculated from a 400m + 200m time trial.
FTP (Functional Threshold Power)
The highest power output you can sustain for approximately 1 hour on the bike. The gold standard for setting cycling training zones.
Cadence
Pedal revolutions per minute (RPM) on the bike. For triathlon, 85–95 RPM is optimal — higher cadence spares leg muscles for the run.
Sighting
Lifting your eyes briefly while swimming to navigate in open water. Lift only the eyes (“crocodile sighting”) every 8–10 strokes to stay on course.
Zone 2 (T2 / Z2)
Aerobic base intensity. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training happens. Builds fat oxidation and endurance.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace during a run. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
Negative Split
Completing the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The ideal pacing strategy for all three disciplines in triathlon.
Gut Training
Progressively increasing carbohydrate intake during training rides to improve the gut’s ability to absorb fuel during racing. Essential for 70.3 and Ironman distances.
Conversational Pace
A pace where you can speak in full sentences without gasping. The gold standard for easy training across all three disciplines.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity) week over week. How fitness is built safely. Maximum ~10% increase per week.
Taper
The planned reduction in training volume before race day (typically 2–3 weeks). Volume drops 20–50% but intensity is maintained. Allows the body to absorb training and arrive fresh.
Active Recovery
Very light movement (walking, easy swimming, easy spinning) to promote blood flow without adding training stress.
Coach Tips

Nutrition Is a Discipline

For a 70.3, nutrition is as important as swim, bike, and run. Practise your race-day fuelling on every long ride from Week 6 onwards. Target 60–80 g carbs/hr on the bike. Train your gut — start low and build.

Nothing New on Race Day

Every piece of gear, every gel, every drink should have been tested in training. Race-day GI issues are almost always caused by untested nutrition. Use training to find what works for you.

Easy Days Are Easy

80% of your training is at conversational effort. Most beginners train easy days too hard and hard days too easy. If you can’t hold a full conversation, slow down. The aerobic engine is built at low intensity.

Consistency Over Volume

Missing one session is fine. Missing one week sets you back. Missing one month requires rebuilding. Show up consistently, even for short sessions. Frequency matters more than any single long workout.

Sleep Is Recovery

Sleep is the highest-ROI recovery tool available. 8–9 hours per night during training. No supplement, ice bath, or compression garment comes close to the recovery power of quality sleep.

Trust the Taper

You will feel restless, sluggish, or anxious during the taper. This is normal. No fitness is gained in the final 2 weeks — only lost through overtraining or injury. Rest is the final ingredient.
