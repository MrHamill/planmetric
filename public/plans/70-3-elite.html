timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–6

Foundation phase focusing on building aerobic capacity and movement efficiency. All training stays in Z1-Z2 with emphasis on technique development across all three disciplines. No racing or high-intensity work — this phase builds the engine that will support all future training.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 7–11

Progressive phase introducing race-specific intensities and durations. Training shifts to approximately 80% easy, 20% hard with threshold and VO2max work included. Brick sessions become regular, and lower-priority races may be included for experience and fitness testing.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 12–14

Highest training stress phase with frequent race simulations and sport-specific intensity work. Sessions become shorter but more race-specific. This phase teaches your body to perform at race pace under accumulating fatigue. Recovery between sessions becomes critical.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 15–16

Strategic reduction in training volume (20-50%) while maintaining intensity to allow supercompensation. Your fitness is already built — this phase optimises recovery, sharpens neuromuscular pathways, and builds confidence for race day performance.

Welcome to your 70.3 journey! This first week establishes your aerobic base with technique-focused sessions. All efforts should feel comfortable and conversational - resist the urge to go hard.

Tue - Swim Technique (35 min) (5:30am–6:05am)

SWIM Z1-Z2 35min
Warm-up: 200m easy freestyle, 4 × 50m build (25m easy/25m faster)
Main Set: 6 × 100m at CSS+10 sec pace with 20 sec rest, focus on catch and rotation. 4 × 50m drill (catch-up drill) with 15 sec rest
Cool-down: 200m easy choice of stroke
Total: 1200m
Coach's Note:
Focus on technique over speed today. The catch-up drill will help you feel your stroke timing and balance. If you feel breathless during the 100s, slow down - we're building aerobic base, not testing fitness. Every stroke should feel controlled and purposeful.

Wed - Bike Endurance (35 min) (5:30am–6:05am)

BIKE Z1-Z2 35min
Warm-up: 10 min easy spinning at 85-95 RPM
Main Set: 20 min steady at Z2 (65-70% FTP), maintaining 90-95 RPM cadence throughout
Cool-down: 5 min easy spinning
Total: 35 min
Coach's Note:
Cadence is crucial for triathlon success - higher cadence saves your legs for the run. If 90+ RPM feels unnatural, practice it now in base phase. Stay relaxed through your shoulders and focus on smooth, circular pedaling. This should feel like a pace you could maintain for hours.

Your Race Strategy

Swim Strategy

Start conservatively — first 200m at 85% effort to settle in. Sight every 10-12 strokes using crocodile technique (eyes only). Draft when possible but prioritise clean water and your own rhythm. If you feel rushed or panicked, roll to backstroke briefly to reset breathing.

T1 Strategy

Take your time — 30 seconds here saves 2-3 minutes on the bike. Dry feet thoroughly, apply anti-chafe if needed, helmet and glasses on before touching bike.

Bike Strategy

First 10km: ride at 75-80% FTP while body adapts from swim. Middle section: settle into race pace (80-85% FTP). Start fuelling within 20 minutes. Maintain 90+ RPM cadence throughout — this preserves your run legs.

T2 Strategy

Mental reset time. Use the 3 Rs: Rest (let go of bike performance), Regroup (prepare for run), Refocus (first km strategy).

Run Strategy

First 2km: run 10-15 seconds per km slower than target pace. Your legs will feel heavy initially. Middle section: find your rhythm and settle into race pace. Final 5km: this is where your training pays off — stay mentally strong and push when others fade.

Mental Strategy

Pre-Race Nerves

Reframe nervousness as readiness — your body is primed to perform. Use positive self-talk: 'I am prepared', 'I belong here', 'I can do hard things'.

During the Race

Break the race into chunks: swim to T1, first half bike, second half bike, T2, first half run, final push. Focus only on the current chunk. When things get tough, use mantras: 'Strong and steady', 'I've trained for this', 'Smooth is fast'.

When It Gets Hard

Remember that everyone is suffering — the difference is who manages it best. Focus on process goals (technique, nutrition, pacing) rather than outcome. Use the 'Yes, and...' technique: 'Yes this hurts, and I am strong enough to handle it.'

Finishing Strong

The final 10% of any race is where mental toughness matters most. Remind yourself of all the hard training sessions you've completed. You've prepared for this exact moment of discomfort.

school Glossary

CSS

Critical Swim Speed — your threshold pace for swimming, calculated as (400m time - 200m time) ÷ 2. Used to set training zones.

FTP

Functional Threshold Power — the highest power you can sustain for approximately 60 minutes. Used to set cycling training zones.

Z1-Z2

Training zones 1-2 — easy, conversational intensity where you can speak in full sentences. Forms 80% of total training volume.

Z3-Z4

Training zones 3-4 — tempo to threshold intensity. Comfortably hard to hard effort that can be sustained for 20-60 minutes.

Brick Session

Training session combining bike and run with no break between disciplines, simulating race day transition stress.

Negative Split

Completing the second half of a race or training session faster than the first half. Optimal pacing strategy for most distances.

Cadence

Steps per minute (running) or pedal revolutions per minute (cycling). Optimal running cadence is 170-180 SPM.

Taper

Period of reduced training volume while maintaining intensity, allowing the body to recover and peak for race day.

T1/T2

Transition areas in triathlon. T1 is swim-to-bike, T2 is bike-to-run. Efficient transitions can save significant time.

Open Water Swimming

Swimming in natural bodies of water (ocean, lake, river) rather than pools. Requires different techniques like sighting and drafting.

Drafting

Positioning behind another cyclist or swimmer to reduce wind/water resistance, saving 20-30% energy expenditure.

Sighting

Lifting the head to navigate while swimming in open water. Should be done every 10-12 strokes using minimal head movement.

Race Pace

The target intensity/speed you plan to maintain during your race. Should be practiced extensively in training.

Fuelling

Consuming carbohydrates during exercise to maintain blood glucose. Target 60-80g/hour for races over 90 minutes.

HRV

Heart Rate Variability — measure of recovery and readiness to train. Higher HRV generally indicates better recovery.
