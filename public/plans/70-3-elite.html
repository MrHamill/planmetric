A 70.3 is a half-distance Ironman. Competitive age-group athletes finish in 4:00–5:00 depending on the course. The swim takes 25–35 min, the bike 2:15–2:45, and the run 1:25–1:50. At this level, the race is won on the run — and the run is determined by how intelligently you ride the bike. Power management, nutrition execution, and pacing discipline separate the podium from the pack. This plan assumes you have completed at least one 70.3 or equivalent and have an established training base of 8–12 hrs/week.

Plan Focus

This plan operates at higher intensity from Week 1. Weeks 1–3 (Base) sharpen your existing aerobic engine with CSS swim sets, sweet spot bike work, and tempo running. Week 4 is a recovery week. Weeks 4–7 (Build) introduce VO2max intervals in all three disciplines, over-under threshold work on the bike, and race-pace brick sessions. Week 8 is a recovery week. Weeks 8–11 (Peak) are the highest volume and specificity — race simulation rides with embedded sweet spot blocks, 1900m race-pace continuous swims, and long runs finishing at half-marathon pace. Weeks 12–14 (Taper) drop volume while maintaining intensity through short, sharp openers. Double sessions appear on select days. Nutrition targets 80–90 g carbs/hr on the bike. Every session has specific numbers. Every number has a reason.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Identify which days have double sessions and plan logistics around pool access, route selection, and recovery windows.
  2. Calibrate your zones. Run a CSS test (400m + 200m TT), an FTP test (20 min bike TT, multiply average power by 0.95), and establish your running threshold from a recent 10 km or 30 min TT. Re-test every 6–8 weeks.
  3. Hit the key sessions. If life forces a compromise, protect the key workout (marked KEY). Drop a recovery swim or easy run before cutting a threshold or brick session.
  4. Recovery weeks are reduced frequency, not zero. Week 4 and Week 8 drop volume by 40–50% but keep one quality session to maintain sharpness. Elite athletes recover differently — active recovery is more effective than full rest.
  5. Practise race nutrition on every long ride and every brick session. Target 80–90 g carbs/hr using 2:1 glucose:fructose products. Gut training is non-negotiable at this level.
  6. Monitor readiness. If morning HRV is suppressed or perceived fatigue is high, convert the day’s quality session to Z2 volume. Rigid execution of a plan underperforms flexible, data-responsive training.

Week 1 — Sharpenexpand_more
Week 1 of 14

Establish the training rhythm. CSS intervals in the pool from day one. Sweet spot work on the bike. Tempo running. You have the base — now we sharpen it. This week is about calibration: confirming your zones and locking in the weekly structure.

MondaySwim — CSS Intervals
SWIMCSS2800m

400m easy warm-up (200 free / 100 back / 100 choice) → 4 × 50m catch-up drill with 10s rest → 8 × 200m at CSS pace with 20s rest (hold consistent splits, aim for less than 2 sec variation between reps) → 4 × 50m easy kick with 10s rest → 200m easy cool-down.

Coach’s NoteCSS intervals from Week 1 because your aerobic base is already established. The 200m reps are long enough to demand pacing discipline — if you go out 3 sec/100m too fast on rep 1, you will pay for it by rep 6. Aim for a send-off that gives you 20s rest. If you cannot hold pace with 20s rest, extend to 25s and note it — that is your current ceiling. Exhale fully underwater and rotate through the hips, not just the shoulders.
TuesdayBike — Sweet Spot
BIKEZ3–Z475 min

15 min easy spin warm-up at Z1–Z2, building cadence to 90+ RPM → 3 × 15 min at 88–93% FTP (sweet spot) with 5 min Z1 recovery spin between → 10 min easy spin cool-down at Z1. Cadence target: 85–95 RPM throughout efforts.

Coach’s NoteSweet spot delivers the best watts-per-hour-of-fatigue ratio in cycling training. It sits just below threshold — high enough to force adaptation, low enough to recover from within 24 hours. Do not chase power in the first 3 minutes of each block. Let HR stabilise and settle into the effort. If you are on a trainer, use resistance mode rather than ERG — it teaches real pacing discipline.
WednesdayRun — Tempo Intervals
RUNT350 min

10 min easy jog warm-up at T2 → 4 × 100m strides → 3 × 8 min at T3 (tempo/threshold pace — roughly 15–20 sec/km slower than 10 km race pace) with 2 min jog recovery → 10 min easy jog cool-down.

Coach’s NoteTempo pace should feel comfortably hard — you can speak in short sentences but not hold a conversation. These 8-minute blocks build lactate threshold, the single most important predictor of half-marathon performance. First rep should feel controlled. If the third rep falls apart, you started too fast. Consistency across reps matters more than any single split.

SaturdayBike — Long Endurance
BIKEZ22 hr 30 minKEY

15 min easy spin warm-up at Z1 → 2 hr steady Z2 on a rolling route. Cadence 85–95 RPM. Practise race nutrition: aim for 60–70 g carbs/hr using gels or sports drink. Hydrate 500–750 mL/hr. Final 15 min easy spin cool-down.

TuesdayBike — Threshold Intervals
BIKEZ480 minKEY

15 min easy spin warm-up at Z1–Z2 → 2 × 20 min at 95–105% FTP (threshold) with 8 min Z1 recovery spin → 15 min easy spin cool-down. Cadence 85–95 RPM. HR should stabilise at ~90–95% of threshold HR by the end of each interval.

Coach’s NoteThese are FTP blocks — the gold standard for raising your functional threshold power. Do not chase power from the first minute. Let it build over the first 3–4 minutes. The second 20 min block should be within 3–5 watts of the first. If power drops by more than 10% in the second block, your FTP is set too high — re-test. This session is demanding. Protect the recovery day that follows.
WednesdayRun — Hill Repeats
RUNT450 min

10 min easy jog warm-up → 4 × 100m strides → 8 × 90s hard uphill effort at T4 (5–8% gradient) with walk-back-down recovery (~2.5 min) → 10 min easy jog cool-down.

Coach’s NoteHill repeats build running-specific strength and power while reducing impact stress compared to flat intervals. The effort is hard but repeatable — 8/10 RPE. Drive your knees, lean slightly into the hill, and maintain a quick cadence. Walk the recovery — do not jog. These repeats develop the force production that makes race-pace running feel sustainable later in the plan.

Race Day Protocol

Your 70.3 Race Execution Plan

Night Before

High-carb meal: pasta, rice, potatoes. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Final carbohydrate loading — 8–10 g carbs/kg across the day. Hydrate with electrolytes. Check tyre pressure (90–110 PSI), chain, brakes, gears. Prepare all nutrition for the bike: gels taped to top tube, bottles filled, salt capsules ready. Lay out transition kit in order. Pin race number to race belt. Set two alarms.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before start)

Pre-race meal: 1–2 g carbs/kg body weight. Familiar foods — toast with jam, banana, porridge, or a bagel with honey. Sip water with electrolytes. Caffeine: 3–6 mg/kg body weight 45–60 min before start (improves TT performance by 2–4%). Final gel 15–20 min before the swim start.

Transition Setup (60–90 min early)

Rack bike. Helmet on bars, open and ready. Shoes positioned. Nutrition taped to bike. Run gear laid out: shoes, race belt, hat/visor, gel for km 3. Walk T1 and T2 — know the entry/exit points. Count rack rows. Use a landmark. Double cap method: base cap, goggles, race cap. Put wetsuit on 15–20 min before start.

Warm-Up (10–15 min before)

Swim 300–400m easy including 4 × 30s at race pace. If no warm-up swim: splash cold water on face and neck. Arm swings, light jogging, 10 slow nasal inhales. Arrive at the start ready to go, not cold and stiff.

Swim — 1.9 km

Controlled start. Seed yourself appropriately. Start at race pace, not sprint pace. The first 200m is the highest-risk window for panic — control breathing, settle into rhythm. Sight every 10 strokes using a landmark. Draft behind faster swimmers (30–50 cm behind feet saves 20–30% energy). Negative split: first 1000m at CSS + 2 sec/100m, final 900m at CSS or faster. Approach T1 with momentum — begin unzipping wetsuit while standing.

T1 — Swim to Bike

Run to your rack. Wetsuit arms off while running. At rack: strip suit to ankles, step out. Helmet on, buckle. Shoes on. Bike out. Run to mount line. Mount and go. Target: under 90 seconds.

Bike — 90 km

Race power, not race ego. Z2–Z3, 78–85% FTP. Begin fuelling within the first 10 min. Target: 80–90 g carbs/hr (2:1 glucose:fructose). Hydrate: 600–750 mL/hr. Electrolytes: 300–700 mg sodium/hr in warm conditions. Cadence 85–95 RPM. Hold steady on climbs — do not surge above Z4 except briefly. If you feel strong at km 60, resist the urge to accelerate. You are saving the run. The last 20 km of the bike determines the first 10 km of the run.

T2 — Bike to Run

Dismount before the line. Rack bike. Helmet off. Run shoes on. Race belt on. Go. Smooth is fast. Target: under 60 seconds.

Run — 21.1 km

The race starts at km 15. First 3 km: settle into stride. Take a gel at km 3. By km 5, find T3 pace and commit. Walk through aid stations for fluids — the 10-second cost saves 2+ minutes later by preventing GI distress. Fuel: 30–40 g carbs/hr (gel every 30–40 min). Electrolytes from aid stations. At km 15, the race becomes about who has paced the bike correctly and who has fuelled properly. If you have done both, this is where you pass people. Final 3 km: commit everything. You have trained for this exact moment — 20 min at T3 on fatigued legs. Execute.

Post-Race

Walk for 10 min. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 10–14 days of easy movement before structured training. Review your race data: pacing, nutrition timing, where you gained or lost time. Record it while the memory is fresh.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
Your sustainable threshold pace in the pool — the fastest pace you can hold for about 20 minutes. Calculated from a 400m + 200m time trial. The foundation of structured swim training.
FTP (Functional Threshold Power)
The highest power output you can sustain for approximately 1 hour on the bike. Tested via a 20-min TT (multiply average power by 0.95). The gold standard for cycling zones.
Sweet Spot
88–93% of FTP. Sits just below threshold — high enough to force adaptation, low enough to recover from within 24 hours. The most efficient training zone for building cycling fitness.
Over-Unders
Intervals alternating above threshold (110% FTP) and below threshold (92% FTP). Trains lactate clearance under sustained load. Simulates race-day surges and recovery.
VO2max
Maximum oxygen uptake — your aerobic ceiling. Trained via 3–5 min intervals at 110–120% FTP (bike) or T4 pace (run). Raises the upper limit of sustainable performance.
Brick Session
Two disciplines back-to-back, typically bike then run at race intensity. Trains the neuromuscular transition and pacing discipline required for the T2–run sequence.
T1 / T2
Transition 1 (swim to bike) and Transition 2 (bike to run). The “fourth discipline.” Practise in training so race day feels automatic. Target: T1 under 90s, T2 under 60s.
Negative Split
Completing the second half faster than the first. The ideal pacing strategy for all three disciplines. Requires discipline to start conservatively.
Cadence (RPM)
Pedal revolutions per minute on the bike. 85–95 RPM is optimal for triathletes — higher cadence spares leg muscles for the run by shifting load to the cardiovascular system.
Sighting
Lifting eyes briefly while swimming to navigate in open water. “Crocodile sighting”: eyes only above water, chin at surface. Every 8–10 strokes. A 1° error over 1900m adds ~25m.
Gut Training
Progressively increasing carbohydrate intake during training to improve absorption during racing. Essential for 80–90 g carbs/hr targets. Start at 40 g/hr and build by 5–10 g/hr per week.
NHE1 Adaptation
Speed endurance training (maximal sprints with full recovery) upregulates NHE1 protein, which clears acid from muscle cells faster. Improves repeat-effort capacity and recovery between hard efforts.
HRV (Heart Rate Variability)
A measure of autonomic nervous system status. Suppressed morning HRV indicates incomplete recovery. Used to guide daily training intensity decisions.
Taper
Planned reduction in training volume (20–50%) while maintaining intensity in the 2–3 weeks before race day. Allows the body to absorb training and arrive at the start line fresh and sharp.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress week over week. Maximum ~10% increase per week. How fitness is built safely over time.
Polarised Training
~80% of volume at low intensity (T1–T2), ~20% at high intensity (T4–T5). Minimal time in the moderate “grey zone” (T3). Consistently observed in elite endurance athletes.
Coach Tips

Nutrition Is the Fourth Discipline

At elite level, 80–90 g carbs/hr on the bike is non-negotiable. Use 2:1 glucose:fructose products. Train your gut from Week 1. GI issues in a 70.3 are almost always preventable — they are a preparation failure, not bad luck.

The Race Is Won on the Run

Specifically, in the final 10 km. Every decision on the bike — power, cadence, nutrition — determines whether you can hold T3 when it matters. Resist the urge to ride above race power. Discipline on the bike is speed on the run.

Monitor, Don’t Just Execute

Rigid plans underperform flexible, data-responsive plans. Use morning HRV, perceived fatigue, and resting HR to guide daily intensity. When recovery is compromised, drop intensity. When you feel sharp, execute the plan as written.

Re-Test Your Zones

CSS and FTP should be re-tested every 6–8 weeks. Training on stale zones means under-training at threshold and over-training at VO2max. Accurate zones are the foundation of accurate training.

Sleep Is Non-Negotiable

Sleep is the highest-ROI recovery tool. 8–9 hours per night. No supplement, ice bath, or compression garment comes close. Poor sleep suppresses HRV, impairs glycogen resynthesis, and reduces training quality the next day.

Trust the Taper

You will feel restless, sluggish, or anxious. This is the taper working. No fitness is gained in the final 2–3 weeks — only lost through overtraining or injury. Rest is the final ingredient in a race performance.
