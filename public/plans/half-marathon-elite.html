Plan Focus

The half marathon sits at the intersection of aerobic power and lactate threshold. This plan treats it as what it is: a sustained effort at or near threshold that demands both the engine of a distance runner and the top-end speed of a 10K racer. Two key sessions per week are non-negotiable — VO2max intervals and threshold blocks. Everything else supports those sessions. The long run progressively integrates HM-pace segments until your body no longer distinguishes between training and racing. Recovery weeks are programmed deliberately, not as afterthoughts. If you execute this plan honestly, you will arrive at the start line knowing exactly what your body can do over 21.1 kilometres.

How To Use This Plan (Elite)
  1. Two key sessions per week are non-negotiable. Tuesday (VO2max) and Thursday (threshold/hills) are the sessions that move the needle. Everything else is support structure. If life forces a choice, protect these two.
  2. Recovery weeks are programmed deliberately — not earned by breaking down. Week 5 is a planned reset. Do not skip it, do not fill it with junk miles, do not treat it as weakness. Adaptation consolidates during recovery, not during the session that triggered it.
  3. RPE is your primary intensity gauge. Trust feel over data. If the plan says RPE 7–8 and your watch says you're 10 seconds per km slower than expected, run by feel. Conditions, fatigue, and cumulative load all shift the pace-to-RPE relationship. The effort is what matters.
  4. Answer the weekly check-in questions honestly. They exist to catch drift — overtraining, underrecovery, mechanical issues. If the honest answer is "no," adjust. The plan serves you; you don't serve the plan.

Race Day Protocol

Your Half Marathon Race Day Execution

48 Hours Before — Carb Loading

Begin carbohydrate loading 24–48 hours before race start. Target 8–10g carbs per kg body weight per day. Rice, pasta, bread, potatoes, oats — familiar foods, nothing experimental. Reduce fibre and fat. Hydrate consistently. This is not optional at elite level — it's the difference between hitting the wall and running through it.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before)

2–3g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods: toast with honey, white rice, banana, energy drink. Low fibre, low fat. Sip water or electrolyte drink — don't overhydrate. Caffeine if tolerated (3–6 mg/kg, taken 45–60 min before start).

Warm-Up (20 min before start)

15 min easy jog, progressive → 4 × 20 sec strides at race pace → stand, stay warm, stay loose. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles and an elevated heart rate. You should be lightly sweating.

Fuelling During Race

2–3 gels over 21.1 km. First gel at 30–35 min, second at 55–60 min, third at 75–80 min if racing over 85 min. Take with water. Practice this in training long runs — never try a new gel on race day.

Pacing Strategy

Start at goal pace. Not faster. Even splits through 15 km. The first 5 km should feel comfortable — if they feel hard, you're going too fast. Hold discipline through the middle kilometres where the crowd thins and the excitement fades. From 18 km, begin to push. Final 3 km — everything you have. The negative split is the dream; even splits are the floor. Do not go out with the lead pack unless that is genuinely your level.

Post-Race

Walk 10 min. Rehydrate immediately — electrolyte drink, not just water. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Ice bath or cold water immersion within 2 hours if available. Allow 5–7 days of easy movement before structured training. Full training resumes after 10–14 days.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

Two Key Sessions, Non-Negotiable

Tuesday (VO2max) and Thursday (threshold/hills) are the sessions that drive adaptation. Everything else supports them. If life forces a choice, protect these two above all others.

Easy Means Easy

At elite volume, the polarity is critical. Easy days must be genuinely easy — T2 or below. Running your easy days at moderate effort is the fastest path to overtraining. If you can't hold a full conversation, slow down.

Recovery Is Training

Sleep 8+ hours. Post-session nutrition within 60 min of every key workout. Foam roll, massage, compression — these aren't luxuries at 75 km/week, they're requirements. The fitter you are, the harder you can push, and the more recovery you need.

RPE Over Pace

RPE is your primary intensity gauge. Conditions, fatigue, terrain, and cumulative load all shift the pace-to-RPE relationship. A "bad" VO2max session where the pace is off but the effort was right is a good session. Trust feel over data.

The Long Run Is Sacred

The Sunday long run with HM-pace finish is the most important session in this plan. It teaches your body to find race pace on tired legs. Never sacrifice it for a midweek session. Never run it too fast in the early kilometres.

Fuel Like An Athlete

At 65–75 km/week, you need 40–60g carbs per hour during key sessions and long runs. Practice your race-day fuelling strategy during training. Gel timing, hydration volume, electrolyte intake — dial it in during Weeks 6–8, not on race day.

Trust The Taper

The taper will make you feel restless, anxious, and undertrained. That's normal. The fitness is banked. Reduced volume with maintained intensity is the evidence-based approach. Do not fill the taper with junk miles.

Listen To Warning Signs

Persistent joint pain, disrupted sleep, elevated resting HR, or loss of motivation are overtraining signals. One bad session is one session. A bad week is a pattern. Address it before it becomes an injury. The plan serves you — you don't serve the plan.

Strength Is Non-Negotiable

Monday strength work protects you from the volume. Single-leg stability, calf raises, hip strength, and core work prevent the breakdowns that end elite training blocks prematurely.

Race Day Is Execution, Not Exploration

Nothing new on race day. Same shoes, same kit, same gels, same warm-up. The race is a controlled expression of fitness, not an experiment. Every variable should be tested during training.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
T1 (Recovery)
Very easy. Barely above walking. Active recovery, warm-ups, cool-downs.
T2 (Aerobic Base)
Easy and conversational. Where the majority of your volume lives. Builds fat oxidation, mitochondrial density, and aerobic endurance.
T3 (Tempo/Threshold)
Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. Sustainable for 40–60 min. Your half marathon race effort sits here.
T4 (VO2max)
Hard. Laboured breathing. Sustainable for 3–8 min intervals. Develops maximal aerobic power — the ceiling of your endurance.
T5 (Anaerobic)
Very hard. Unsustainable beyond ~2 min. Used sparingly — race kick, late-race surges.
VO2max Intervals
Repeated efforts at T4 (typically 1 km reps) with controlled recovery. Develops maximal oxygen uptake — the single most important predictor of endurance performance.
Threshold Blocks
Sustained efforts at T3–T4 (10–12 min) with short recovery. Raises lactate threshold — the intensity you can sustain for a half marathon.
Tempo Run
Continuous effort at T3 (30–40 min) without breaks. Simulates race-day sustained effort.
Hill Repeats
Repeated uphill efforts (60–90 sec) at T4. Builds muscular power and running economy without the joint impact of flat speed work.
HM-Pace Finish
The final segment of the long run executed at half marathon goal pace. Teaches the body to find race pace on fatigued legs.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 sec) at fast pace. Maintains neuromuscular sharpness. Not sprints — smooth, controlled, fast.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a race faster than the first. The optimal half marathon pacing strategy.
Even Splits
Running each segment of the race at the same pace. The realistic floor for elite half marathon pacing.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (volume, intensity) across weeks. How fitness is built safely and sustainably.
Taper
A planned reduction in training volume (20–50%) while maintaining intensity. Allows the body to supercompensate before race day.
