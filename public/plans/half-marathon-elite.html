timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–3

Foundation building phase focusing exclusively on aerobic development through easy-paced running. All sessions are conversational (Z1-Z2) with emphasis on gradually building volume, improving running economy, and establishing consistent training habits. No racing or hard intervals - this is pure aerobic base building.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 4–7

Progressive phase introducing race-specific intensities while maintaining aerobic base. Includes threshold runs, race pace intervals, and tempo work alongside continued easy volume. The intensity distribution shifts to include structured hard sessions while building toward peak training loads.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 8–10

Highest intensity phase with race simulation sessions every 3-4 days. Training becomes very specific to race demands with shorter, more intense sessions. Peak weekly volume combined with race-pace practice and mental preparation. Final preparation for target event.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 11–12

Planned volume reduction (20-50%) while maintaining intensity to allow recovery and supercompensation. Body absorbs months of training adaptations while fatigue dissipates. Focus shifts to race preparation, mental rehearsal, and arriving at start line fresh and confident.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Week 1 — BASE
3.3h 13 Apr–19 2026
Welcome to your half marathon journey! This first week establishes your base training rhythm with 80/20 intensity distribution. Focus on running by feel - easy days should be fully conversational, tempo day comfortably hard. Your body is adapting to the training stimulus, so prioritise consistency over speed.

Tue - Easy Run (5:30am–6:00am)

RUN Z1-Z2 30min
Warm-up: 5 min walk, then gradual transition to easy running pace
Main Set: 20 min continuous running at 5:10-5:20/km (conversational pace)
Cool-down: 5 min walk with gentle stretching
Total: 30 min
Coach's Note:
This should feel genuinely easy - you should be able to chat comfortably throughout. Focus on smooth, relaxed form with a natural cadence around 175-180 steps per minute. If you're breathing hard, slow down. Remember, easy days build your aerobic base and promote recovery.

Wed - Tempo Run (5:30am–6:05am)

RUN Z3 35min
Warm-up: 10 min easy running, building gradually to tempo effort
Main Set: 20 min sustained tempo at 4:30-4:40/km (comfortably hard effort)
Cool-down: 5 min easy jogging
Total: 35 min
Coach's Note:
Your first quality session! Tempo pace should feel comfortably hard - you could speak in short phrases but not hold a conversation. This effort builds your lactate threshold and race-specific fitness. Stay relaxed through your shoulders and maintain steady breathing rhythm.

Thu - Easy Run + Strength (5:30am–6:00am)

Another conversational-paced run focusing on consistency. Schedule your strength session 3-6 hours after this run or on a separate day entirely. flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb breakfast (1-2g carbs/kg bodyweight) - oatmeal with banana, toast with honey, or familiar race-day fuel. Hydrate with 500-750ml fluid.
2-3 hours before: Complete race preparation checklist. Lay out all gear, check weather conditions, and mentally rehearse your race plan and pacing strategy.
1-2 hours before: Light dynamic warm-up and mobility routine. Final bathroom stop. Begin sipping on sports drink if race duration >90 minutes.
30-60 minutes before: Arrive at race venue. Easy 10-15 minute warm-up jog with 4×20 second pickups at race pace. Practice positive self-talk mantras.
10-15 minutes before: Final preparation - remove warm-up layers, apply race fuel/gels to belt, set watch, and get in starting corral. Deep breathing and visualization.
5 minutes before: Final mental reset. Focus on your first 2km pacing goal. Remember: 'smooth is fast' and trust your fitness. Execute your plan with confidence.
Your Race Strategy

First 5km: Start 10-15 seconds per km SLOWER than goal pace. Let faster runners go - your patience here wins the race. Focus on smooth rhythm, relaxed shoulders, and efficient cadence (175+ spm). Settle into conversational effort.

Middle 10km (5-15km): Gradually build to goal pace. This is your rhythm section - stay relaxed, maintain consistent splits, and practice positive self-talk. Monitor effort rather than obsessing over pace. Take fluid at every aid station if warm conditions.

Final 6km (15-21km): Time to race. If feeling strong at 15km, begin a controlled progression. Focus on maintaining form as fatigue builds. Break this section into 2km chunks. Save your kick for the final 2km when you can see the finish.

Pacing targets: First 5km: Goal pace +15 sec/km | Middle 10km: Goal pace | Final 6km: Goal pace -10 to -5 sec/km if feeling strong, or hold goal pace if struggling.

Mental Strategy

Pre-race nerves: Reframe nervousness as readiness. Use the mantra 'I'm prepared, not nervous.' Channel that energy into focused execution rather than anxiety.

Early race patience: When others surge past you in the first 5km, remind yourself: 'My race starts at 15km.' Trust your negative split strategy - most runners start too fast and fade.

Mid-race focus: Break the race into four 5km segments. Each segment has one simple goal: maintain smooth rhythm. Use mantras like 'Strong and steady' or 'I've trained for this moment.'

Managing difficulty: When fatigue hits (usually 15-17km), use the '3 Rs' - Rest (accept the discomfort), Regroup (focus on form), Refocus (next 1km only). Remember: everyone feels this - your training prepared you to push through it.

Final push: At 18km, tell yourself 'Just a parkrun left.' Focus on process goals: maintain cadence, stay tall, pump your arms. The finish line will come to you.

school Glossary

Z1-Z2 (Zone 1-2)

Easy aerobic pace where you can hold full conversations. Heart rate typically 65-80% max HR. This should feel comfortable and sustainable.

Z4-Z5 (Zone 4-5)

Hard intensity from threshold to VO2max pace. Z4 is comfortably hard (like half marathon pace), Z5 is hard (like 5-10K pace).

Race Pace

Your target pace for half marathon distance. Should feel comfortably hard but sustainable for 21km when fresh.

Cadence

Steps per minute. Optimal running cadence is 170-180 spm, which reduces injury risk and improves efficiency.

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume (20-50%) while maintaining intensity, allowing the body to recover and peak for race day.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race faster than the first half. Requires discipline early but leads to stronger finishes.

VO2max

Maximum rate of oxygen consumption. Training at VO2max pace (roughly 5K race pace) improves aerobic power and running economy.

Threshold Pace

Comfortably hard pace you could sustain for 45-60 minutes. Slightly faster than half marathon pace for most runners.

RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)

Subjective measure of effort on a 1-10 scale. Easy runs should feel 3-4/10, threshold 7-8/10, intervals 8-9/10.

Active Recovery

Very easy movement to promote blood flow and recovery. Should feel effortless and leave you more refreshed than when you started.

Supercompensation

The body's adaptation response where fitness peaks after a period of reduced training load. The principle behind effective tapering.

Neuromuscular

The connection between brain and muscles. Race pace intervals maintain this connection, helping your body 'remember' target pace.

Progressive Run

Starting easy and gradually building pace throughout the run. Excellent for teaching pacing discipline and negative split execution.
