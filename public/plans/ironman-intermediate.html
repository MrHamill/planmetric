A full-distance Ironman — 3.8 km swim, 180 km bike, 42.2 km marathon run. The race-day cutoff is 17 hours. As an intermediate athlete targeting improvement, your goal window is 10–13 hours. The swim takes 60–80 min, the bike 5:00–6:30, and the run 3:30–5:00. The bike leg is where races are won or lost — pacing discipline and fuelling execution on 180 km determine whether you run a marathon or walk a death march. This plan builds the fitness, the fuelling, and the race-day execution to go meaningfully faster than your first Ironman.

Plan Focus

You have done an Ironman. You know the distance. Now it is time to race it. Weeks 1–5 (Base) rebuild aerobic volume with CSS swim intervals from day one, structured endurance rides, and progressive long runs. Week 4 is your first recovery week. Weeks 6–10 (Build) introduce threshold bike work, tempo runs, open water swimming, and your first brick sessions. Week 8 is another recovery week. Weeks 11–16 (Peak) are where the real work happens — race-pace brick sessions, 5.5–6 hour rides with sweet spot blocks, long runs with marathon-pace finish sections, and advanced gut training at 80–90 g/hr. Recovery weeks at 12 and 16 keep you absorbing the load. Weeks 17–20 (Taper) strip volume while maintaining intensity, delivering you to the start line sharp, fresh, and confident. Every session has a specific purpose. 80% of your training stays aerobic. The other 20% is where your speed comes from.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Plan your pool, bike, and run sessions around your schedule. Key sessions (marked KEY) are non-negotiable — protect them.
  2. Recovery weeks (4, 8, 12, 16) are sacred. Adaptation happens during rest. Skipping recovery is the fastest route to overtraining and stale performance on race day.
  3. This plan uses RPE and zone-based effort targets. If you have a power meter, use FTP-based zones on the bike. If you have a CSS test result, use CSS-based pacing in the pool. RPE works if you don’t have devices.
  4. Practise race nutrition on every long ride from Week 5. Build from 60 g/hr toward 80–90 g/hr by Week 14. Your gut is trainable — but only if you train it consistently.
  5. Brick sessions simulate race-day transitions. The heavy-leg sensation diminishes with practice. You need a minimum of 6–8 bricks before race day.
  6. If you miss a session, move on. If you miss a full week, repeat the previous week. Your body does not know what week number it is — it only knows accumulated training stress.

Reset week. You know the distance — this week is about re-establishing rhythm across all three disciplines with aerobic integrity. CSS intervals in the pool from day one. Easy volume on the bike and run. Resist the urge to go hard. Build the engine first.

SWIMRPE 4–62800m

400m easy warm-up (200 free / 100 back / 100 drill) → 4 × 50m catch-up drill with 15s rest → 8 × 200m at CSS pace with 20s rest (hold consistent splits across all 8 reps) → 4 × 50m easy backstroke with 10s rest → 200m cool-down.

Coach’s NoteCSS intervals from the first session. You have done an Ironman — your swim base exists. Coach’s NoteThe aerobic endurance ride is the foundation of your bike fitness. Higher cadence (85–95 RPM) spares muscular glycogen and preserves run legs for the marathon. Most age-groupers default to 70–75 RPM because it feels powerful, but it costs them on the run. Start training cadence discipline now.

SWIMRPE 4–63000m

400m easy warm-up → 4 × 50m fingertip drag drill with 15s rest → 5 × 200m at CSS pace with 20s rest → 4 × 100m descend 1–4 (each faster than the previous, final rep at VO2max effort) with 20s rest → 4 × 50m easy kick with 10s rest → 200m cool-down.

Coach’s NoteCadence drills identify your optimal pedalling range and build efficiency at RPMs outside your default. Most triathletes self-select 75 RPM when 85–95 would spare their legs for the run. A landmark week. Sweet spot work appears on the bike for the first time. Tempo running is introduced. The first brick session of the programme. Volume returns to Week 3 levels with the addition of structured intensity. This is where your Ironman gets faster.

TuesdaySwim — CSS Intervals

WednesdayBike — Sweet Spot Introduction
BIKERPE 5–790 min

15 min easy spin warm-up → 2 × 15 min at sweet spot (88–93% FTP / Z3–Z4 RPE 6–7) with 5 min Z1 recovery between → 40 min Z2 → 10 min cool-down. Cadence: 85–95 RPM during sweet spot efforts.

Coach’s NoteSweet spot training sits just below threshold — it delivers the highest adaptation per unit of fatigue. The sensation is “comfortably hard” — you can speak in short sentences but not hold a conversation. Do not chase the upper end of the range on your first session. Let the effort build within each 15-min block. Sweet spot work will grow to 3 × 20 min by the peak phase.
ThursdayRun — Tempo Introduction
RUNRPE 5–655 min

10 min easy jog warm-up → 3 × 8 min at T3 (tempo — comfortably hard, short sentences) with 3 min easy jog recovery → 10 min easy jog cool-down.

Coach’s NoteFirst tempo work. T3 pace is roughly 15–20 sec/km slower than your 10 km race pace. It should feel like a controlled push, not an all-out effort. The 3-min recoveries are important — jog them, do not walk. Tempo work improves your lactate threshold, which directly determines your marathon pace off the bike.
FridaySwim — Aerobic + Sighting
SWIMRPE 3–42000m

200m easy warm-up → 6 × 50m sighting drill (sight a target at the end of the lane every 8–10 strokes without breaking rhythm) with 15s rest → 4 × 250m at Aerobic pace with 20s rest → 200m cool-down.

Coach’s NoteSighting is the most valuable open water skill. Lift only your eyes above water — not your whole head. A full head lift drops your hips, increases drag, and wastes energy. Practise the “crocodile sight” rhythm: sight every 8–10 strokes until it becomes automatic. Even a 1-degree error over 3800m adds significant distance.
SaturdayBrick — Bike + Run
BRICKRPE 3–53 hrs bike + 20 min runKEY

Bike: 15 min easy spin warm-up → 2 hrs 30 min steady at Z2 with nutrition practice (60 g carbs/hr, 500–700 mL fluid/hr) → 15 min cool-down. Coach’s NoteYour first brick session of the programme. The “brick legs” sensation is caused by the sudden shift in muscle recruitment from cycling to running. It improves dramatically with practice — you need 6–8 bricks before race day. Do not chase pace on the run. The transition practice is as important as the run itself. Time your transition — on race day, T2 should take less than 3 minutes.

Race Day Protocol

Pacing Strategy

Swim: Start conservatively. First 200m at CSS + 5 sec/100m to manage the adrenaline spike. Settle to CSS pace by 500m. Draft whenever possible. Target: 60–75 min.
Bike: First 30 km at Z2 (easy). Race effort from km 30: Z2–Z3 (sweet spot at most). Cadence 85–95 RPM. Do not chase others. Ride your own race. Target: 5:00–6:00.
Run: Walk through T2. Start at T2 pace for the first 3 km. Find Ironman marathon pace by km 5. Even splits or negative split. Walk every aid station. Target: 3:30–4:30.

Nutrition Plan

Bike: 80–90 g carbs/hr (gut-trained athletes can push to 120 g/hr). 2:1 glucose-fructose products. 600–750 mL fluid/hr. 500–700 mg sodium/hr. Eat every 20–30 min, drink every 15 min. Do not skip feeds during sweet spot efforts.
Run: 30–60 g carbs/hr. Cola + gels work well. 500 mg sodium/hr. Walk aid stations to drink. If GI distress occurs, reduce intake briefly but do not stop completely.
Electrolytes: 500–700 mg sodium/hr across the entire race. Individual sweat rate varies — if you have done a sweat test, match sodium to your results.

Mental Strategy for km 30+ of the Marathon

After km 30, your brain will start telling you to stop. This is not a sign that something is wrong — it is the normal response to deep fatigue. Strategies: (1) Shorten your mental horizon to the next aid station. (2) Count to 100 steps, then start again. (3) Use a mantra: “I trained for this. I am strong.” (4) Focus on form: tall posture, quick feet, relaxed hands. (5) Remember why you started — the finish line is coming. The difference between a finisher and a fast finisher is the ability to hold pace when everything says stop.

Coach Tips

Nutrition Is the Fourth Discipline

For an Ironman, nutrition is as important as swim, bike, and run. Train your gut from Week 1. Build from 50 g/hr toward 80–90 g/hr. Use 2:1 glucose-fructose products. Test everything in training — nothing new on race day.

The Bike Determines the Run

Ride 5% below your target power and you will run well. Ride 5% above and you will walk the marathon. Pacing discipline on the bike is the single biggest differentiator between a good Ironman and a bad one.

Sweet Spot Is Your Best Friend

Sweet spot training (88–93% FTP) delivers the highest adaptation per unit of fatigue. It raises your FTP without destroying your legs. The embedded sweet spot blocks in long rides are the most Ironman-specific sessions in this plan.

Recovery Weeks Are Training

Adaptation happens during rest, not during the session. Recovery weeks are where your body consolidates the fitness from the previous block. Skipping them leads to staleness, overtraining, and race-day underperformance.

Cadence Saves Your Run

Higher cadence (85–95 RPM) on the bike is more cardiovascular, less muscular. It spares the glycogen and muscle fibre recruitment you need for the marathon. Train cadence discipline from Week 1.

Trust the Taper

You will feel restless, sluggish, or anxious during the taper. This is normal. No fitness is gained in the final 3 weeks — only lost through overtraining or injury. Rest is the final ingredient.

Glossary
CSS
Critical Swim Speed — maximum sustainable aerobic swim pace. Calculated from 400m and 200m time trials.
FTP
Functional Threshold Power — the highest power you can sustain for ~60 min on the bike. Zones are based on % of FTP.
Sweet Spot
88–93% of FTP. High-efficiency threshold development with manageable fatigue.
Over-Unders
Alternating intervals above and below threshold that train lactate clearance under sustained effort.
Brick
Back-to-back bike and run sessions with no break — simulates the T2 transition and “brick legs” sensation.
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard the effort feels. Used when no power meter or HR monitor is available.
T1 / T2
Transition 1 (swim to bike) and Transition 2 (bike to run). The “fourth discipline” of triathlon.
Gut Training
Progressively increasing carbohydrate intake during training to improve intestinal absorption and reduce GI distress on race day.
Negative Split
Running or swimming the second half faster than the first — the hallmark of disciplined pacing.
NHE1
Sodium-Hydrogen Exchanger — a membrane protein that clears acid from muscles. Upregulated by sprint/speed endurance training.
