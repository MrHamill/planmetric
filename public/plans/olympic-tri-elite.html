Plan Focus

This is not a plan for finishing. This is a plan for racing. You already have an aerobic base, a history of multisport training, and the discipline to execute structured sessions at high intensity. The next 10 weeks sharpen every system that matters on race day: swim VO2max capacity and CSS pace-lock, bike threshold power and over-under tolerance, run economy at race pace off fatigued legs. Bricks are not optional — they are the centrepiece. T1 and T2 speed is trained, not hoped for. Periodisation follows a sharpen → build → recover → race-specific → taper arc. Every session has an intensity target. Every rest day protects the quality of the next hard day. If you execute this plan with precision, you will arrive at the start line in the best multisport form of your life.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. This plan has 6–8 sessions per week across three disciplines — logistics matter. Book pool lanes, plan bike routes, and schedule brick sessions around weather and recovery.
  2. Execute intensity targets precisely. RPE 7–8 means RPE 7–8, not 9. The difference between a productive VO2max session and a junk session is discipline at the top end. If you cannot hit the prescribed intensity, you are fatigued — back off and protect the next session.
  3. Respect the recovery week. Week 5 exists because Weeks 3–4 are the highest-volume block. Skipping recovery at this level does not demonstrate toughness — it demonstrates poor planning. Adaptation happens during rest.
  4. Prioritise bricks. The bike-to-run transition is where Olympic-distance races are won and lost. Every brick session in this plan simulates race-specific fatigue. Execute them at the prescribed intensity, not harder.
  5. Rehearse transitions under fatigue. T1 and T2 speed drills are programmed into brick sessions. Practise your exact race-day setup: wetsuit removal, shoe placement, helmet routine. Seconds compound across two transitions.

Week 1 — Sharpen Base Iexpand_more
Week 1 of 10

Establish rhythm across all three disciplines. Reintroduce structured intensity after your base block. The sessions are challenging but controlled — the goal is to calibrate your effort levels and confirm your current fitness before the build phase begins.

MondaySwim VO2max + Strength
SWIMRPE 7–875 min

Swim: 400m warm-up (mixed stroke) → 6×100m at CSS–5s / 20s rest → 4×50m kick at RPE 6 / 15s rest → 200m cool-down. Total: ~2,000m. Strength: 30 min full-body circuit — single-leg squats, pull-ups, plank variations, hip stability band work, calf raises.

Coach's NoteThe VO2max set is 6 reps, not 8 — this week is calibration. If you cannot hold CSS–5s pace on reps 5–6, your CSS estimate needs recalibrating. Note your 100m splits.
TuesdayRun VO2max Intervals
RUNRPE 7–860 minKEY

15 min easy warm-up with 4×20s strides → 5×1 km at T4 / 90s jog recovery → 10 min easy cool-down.

Coach's Note5 reps, not 6. Calibration week. Target even splits across all eps. If rep 5 is more than 8 seconds slower than rep 1, you started too fast. Discipline at the front end defines this session.
WednesdayBike Threshold
BIKERPE 6–775 min

15 min spin warm-up → 2×15 min at Z4 / 5 min Z2 between → 10 min Z2 → 5 min cool-down.

Coach's NoteShorter threshold blocks than the build phase. Lock into your target power or heart rate and hold it steady. No surging. Threshold is about sustainability, not heroics.
ThursdaySwim CSS + Easy Run
SWIMRUN90 min

Swim: 300m warm-up → 4×200m at CSS / 25s rest → 4×50m build (easy to fast) → 200m cool-down. Total: ~2,200m. Run: 25 min easy at T2. Conversational pace only.

Coach's NoteThe CSS 200s should feel controlled — hard but repeatable. The run afterwards is genuinely easy. This is not a brick — it is a double session with different energy systems.

SaturdayLong Bike + Tempo Blocks
BIKERPE 4–6100 min

15 min Z2 warm-up → 3×10 min at Z3 / 5 min Z2 between → 20 min Z2 steady → 5 min cool-down.

Coach's NoteThe long ride with tempo inserts. Z3 should feel moderate — sustainable for the full 10 minutes with controlled breathing. This is not a threshold session.
SundayBrick: Bike → Run

star Key Workouts — Tuesday & Sunday

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 8+ hours. Post-session nutrition within 30 minutes of every high-intensity session: 1–1.2g carbs/kg + 0.3g protein/kg. Foam roll quads, IT band, and calves. Ice bath optional but effective after the Sunday brick if ambient temperature is high.

psychology Weekly Check-In

"Can I hit the prescribed intensities without excessive fatigue carry-over? If Tuesday's intervals are still affecting Thursday's swim, my recovery protocol needs tightening — not my training volume."

Race Day Protocol

Your Olympic Triathlon Race Execution Plan

Night Before

Normal dinner with 2–3g carbs/kg. Hydrate well. Prepare all race kit — wetsuit, goggles (with anti-fog), bike (tyres checked, nutrition loaded), run shoes, race belt, timing chip. Lay everything out in transition order. Set two alarms. Sleep early.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before)

1–2g carbs/kg. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, white rice — whatever you have tested in training. Sip 500ml water with electrolytes over 2 hours. Do not overdrink.

Transition Setup (90 min before)

Rack bike. Set up T1 (towel, helmet on bars, bike shoes, glasses) and T2 (run shoes, race belt, gel if needed). Walk the T1 swim-in route and T2 bike-in/run-out route. Know your rack position by landmark.

Swim — 1.5 km

Controlled start. Position yourself based on your ability — front if you can hold the pace, wide if you want clean water. First 200m is chaos — stay calm, find rhythm. Sight every 6 strokes. Draft off a faster swimmer's hip if possible (legal in non-draft races for swim only). Negative split: second half faster than first. Accelerate in the final 100m to prime legs for T1.

Bike — 40 km

Start conservatively. Z3 for the first 5 km while your body transitions from horizontal to seated. Settle into Z3–Z4 race power. Take on 60–90g carbs/hr from KM 10 — gels, bars, or liquid, whatever you trained with. Drink to thirst. Push to Z4 for the final 5 km into T2. Do not coast into the dismount — ride hard until the line.

Run — 10 km

The first kilometre is a lie. Your legs will feel terrible. This is normal — you have trained for this in every brick session. Start at T2–T3, settle into T3 race pace by KM 2. Hold steady through KM 7. If you have practised your fuelling correctly, you will have energy for the final 3 km. Push T3+ and finish hard. Run through the line, not to it.

Post-Race

Walk for 10 min. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Compression garments for 24 hours. 5–7 days of easy movement before any structured training resumes.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

Intensity Distribution

This plan follows a ~70/20/10 distribution: 70% easy, 20% threshold, 10% VO2max. If your easy sessions feel moderate, you are eroding your recovery and compromising the hard sessions that actually build race fitness.

Brick Sessions Are Sacred

The bike-to-run transition is where Olympic-distance races are decided. Never skip a brick session. If life forces a choice between a standalone run and a brick, choose the brick every time.

Transition Speed Is Free Speed

A 90-second T2 vs a 45-second T2 is 45 seconds you did not have to train for. Practise your exact race-day setup in training. Helmet on, shoes on, go. Smooth is fast.

Race-Day Nutrition Is Training

Never try new nutrition on race day. Every long bike and brick session should use your race-day products at race-day timing. Gut training is as important as leg training at this level.

Sleep Outperforms Supplements

8–9 hours per night is the single highest-ROI recovery intervention. No supplement, compression garment, or recovery protocol outperforms consistent sleep.

Monitor, Don't Obsess

HRV, resting heart rate, and training load metrics are useful signals — not absolute rules. If the data says you are fatigued but you feel fresh, trust your body. If the data says you are fine but you feel destroyed, trust your body.

The Taper Will Make You Anxious

Reduced training volume triggers anxiety in competitive athletes. This is normal. You will feel restless, irritable, and convinced you are losing fitness. You are not. You are shedding fatigue while preserving every adaptation.

Race Your Own Race

Olympic distance is long enough that going out with the leaders when you are not one of them will destroy your run. Pace the swim, ride your power, and run your pace. Podiums are built on the run, not the swim.

One Bad Session Is One Session

A terrible Tuesday VO2max set does not erase the fitness from the previous 6 weeks. Note it, recover, and execute the next session. Consistency across months beats any single workout.

Heat and Altitude

If racing in heat, add 200–400ml/hr to your bike hydration plan and consider pre-cooling strategies. If racing at altitude (>1,500m), arrive 48 hrs early minimum and reduce bike power targets by 3–5%.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. Used as the primary intensity measure in this plan.
CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
Your sustainable swim threshold pace, typically derived from a 400m and 200m time trial. The pace you can hold for ~30 minutes continuously.
CSS –5s
5 seconds per 100m faster than your CSS pace. Used for swim VO2max intervals. Hard, repeatable, but not sprint pace.
FTP (Functional Threshold Power)
The highest average power you can sustain for ~60 minutes on the bike. Z4 threshold intervals are calibrated to this number.
Over-Unders
Alternating intervals above and below threshold (e.g., Z4/Z3). Trains your body to clear lactate while maintaining high power output.
Brick Session
Back-to-back training in two disciplines (typically bike → run) with a transition between them. Simulates race-specific fatigue.
T1 (First Transition)
Swim to bike transition. Includes swim exit, wetsuit removal, helmet on, bike mount. Practised under fatigue in this plan.
T2 (Second Transition)
Bike to run transition. Includes bike dismount, helmet off, run shoes on, race belt on. Target sub-45 seconds by race day.
Zone 2 / T2 (Run)
Aerobic base effort. Easy and conversational. The recovery and easy-day intensity that protects hard-session quality.
Zone 4 / Z4 (Bike)
Threshold effort on the bike. Sustainable for 20–40 minutes. The primary race-day intensity for the 40 km bike leg.
Zone 5 / Z5 (Bike)
VO2max effort on the bike. 3–8 minute intervals at very hard effort. Builds the top-end power for surges and climbs.
Tempo / T3 (Run)
Race-pace effort for the 10 km run. Comfortably hard — short sentences only. This is the intensity you will hold for the entire race run.
VO2max / T4 (Run)
Hard interval effort. Used in 1 km repeats to build maximal aerobic capacity. Laboured breathing, few words at most.
Negative Split
Completing the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The optimal pacing strategy for all three disciplines.
Sighting
Lifting your head during open water swimming to check direction. Practised every 6–8 strokes in this plan.
Drafting (Swim)
Swimming in another swimmer's wake or on their hip to reduce drag. Legal in all standard triathlon swim legs.
Race Simulation
A training session that replicates race-day intensity, duration, transitions, and nutrition. The highest-specificity sessions in the plan.
Taper
A planned reduction in training volume (20–50%) while maintaining intensity. Sheds fatigue without losing fitness. Weeks 9–10 of this plan.
Periodisation
The systematic planning of training phases (base → build → recover → race-specific → taper) to peak fitness for a target race.
HRV (Heart Rate Variability)
A measure of autonomic nervous system recovery. Lower-than-normal HRV may indicate accumulated fatigue. Useful but not infallible.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (volume, intensity, or density) week over week. The fundamental principle of all adaptation.
Active Recovery
Very light training (easy swim, spin, walk) to promote blood flow and recovery without adding training stress.
Neuromuscular
Short, high-intensity efforts (<30 seconds) that train the nervous system's ability to recruit muscle fibres rapidly. Strides and sprints.
