timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–4

Foundation phase focused on building aerobic capacity and technique. Training is predominantly easy-paced (Z1-Z2) with high volume across all three disciplines. No racing or high-intensity work - this is about building your engine and preparing your body for harder training ahead.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 5–8

Progressive phase where race-specific intensities are introduced. Training becomes more focused on your goal event with threshold work, race-pace sessions, and brick training. Volume may increase slightly while intensity becomes more demanding. 1-2 lower-priority races permitted for experience.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 9–11

Highest training load phase with race simulations and sport-specific work. Sessions become shorter but more intense, closely mimicking race demands. Regular brick sessions and open water swimming are essential. This phase builds final fitness and confidence before tapering.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 12–12

Final 1-2 weeks where volume drops significantly but intensity is maintained. The goal is to arrive at the start line fresh, recovered, and ready to perform. Focus shifts to race preparation, equipment checks, and maintaining sharpness with short, quality sessions.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Weeks 1–4

Week 1 — BASE
4.4h 13 Apr–19 2026
Welcome to your Olympic triathlon training journey! This first week establishes your aerobic base with gentle, conversational-pace sessions. Focus on consistent movement patterns and building sustainable habits. All sessions should feel comfortable—save your competitive spirit for later phases.

Tue - Swim Technique (5:30am–6:00am)

SWIM Z1-Z2 30min
Warm-up: 200m easy freestyle, focusing on long strokes and relaxed breathing
Main Set: 6 × 50m catch-up drill (pause when hands meet in front), 20 sec rest 6 × 50m fingertip drag drill, 20 sec rest 200m easy freestyle, count strokes per length 4 × 50m build pace (easy to moderate), 30 sec rest
Cool-down: 150m easy freestyle, focus on exhaling fully underwater
Total: 900m
Coach's Note:
Technique is your foundation—perfect form now prevents bad habits later. Count your strokes per 50m and aim for consistency rather than speed. If you feel breathless, you're going too hard. This is about movement quality, not intensity.

Wed - Bike Endurance (5:30am–6:00am)

BIKE Z1-Z2 30min
Warm-up: 5 min easy spinning, cadence 85-90 RPM
Main Set: 20 min steady endurance pace (Z2), maintain 90-95 RPM throughout Focus on smooth pedal stroke and relaxed upper body
Cool-down: 5 min easy recovery, cadence 80-85 RPM
Total: 30 min
Coach's Note:
Higher cadence is crucial for triathlon success—it saves your legs for the run. If 90+ RPM feels unnatural, practice it now during easy rides. Your cardiovascular system should feel gently challenged, but your legs should feel fresh.

Thu - Easy Run (5:30am–6:00am)

RUN Z1-Z2 30min
Warm-up: 5 min walk-jog progression to easy running pace
Main Set: 20 min conversational pace (5:00/km), focus on 175+ steps per minute Land midfoot, slight forward lean from ankles
Cool-down: 5 min walk-jog cooldown with gentle dynamic stretches
Total: 30 min
Coach's Note:
This should feel embarrassingly easy—you should be able to hold a full conversation throughout. Higher cadence reduces injury risk and improves efficiency. Count your steps for 15 seconds and multiply by 4 to check you're hitting 175+ per minute.

Fri - Swim Threshold (5:30am–6:05am)

SWIM Z3-Z4 35min
Warm-up: 300m easy freestyle with bilateral breathing every 3-5 strokes
Main Set: 4 × 100m at CSS+5 seconds (comfortably hard), 30 sec rest 200m easy recovery 6 × 50m at CSS pace (threshold), 20 sec rest 200m easy recovery 4 × 50m build pace, 25 sec rest
Cool-down: 200m easy freestyle, focus on long, smooth strokes
Total: 1400m
Coach's Note:
This introduces your first threshold work—you should feel challenged but controlled. Your CSS pace should feel comfortably hard but sustainable. Focus on maintaining stroke length even as intensity increases, and remember to exhale fully underwater to manage any breathlessness.

Sat - Long Run (5:30am–6:40am)

RUN Z1-Z2 70min
Warm-up: 10 min easy progression from walk to comfortable running pace
Main Set: 50 min conversational pace (5:00/km), stay in Z1-Z2 throughout Focus on relaxed shoulders, midfoot landing, steady breathing rhythm
Cool-down: 10 min walk-jog cooldown with light stretching
Total: 70 min (14km)
Coach's Note:
Your most important session of the week! This builds your aerobic engine and mental toughness for race day. Stay disciplined with your easy pace—it should feel almost boringly comfortable. Hydrate with 150-200ml every 15-20 minutes if it's warm. Your body is learning to burn fat efficiently at this pace.

Sun - Long Ride (5:30am–6:40am)

BIKE Z1-Z2 70min
Warm-up: 10 min easy spinning, gradually increase to endurance pace
Main Set: 50 min steady endurance (Z2), maintain 85-95 RPM throughout Practice aero position for 2-3 minutes every 10 minutes if you have aerobars
Cool-down: 10 min easy recovery spinning
Total: 70 min
Coach's Note:
Build your aerobic base with steady, sustainable effort. Your breathing should be slightly elevated but controlled—you could still maintain a conversation. Practice your race position regularly to build comfort and efficiency. Hydrate 150-200ml every 15-20 minutes and focus on smooth, circular pedaling.

Your Race Strategy

Swim (1500m)

Start conservatively in outer third of pack. First 200m: focus on rhythm, not speed. Sight every 10-12 strokes using 'crocodile' technique. Draft when possible, staying 30cm behind feet ahead. If panic sets in, roll to backstroke briefly and refocus on exhale.

Bike (40km)

First 10km: settle at 75-80% FTP, establish nutrition rhythm. Middle 20km: race effort at 80-85% FTP, 85-95 RPM cadence. Final 10km: slight negative split, prepare legs for run. Fuel: 1-2 gels total, 150-200ml fluid every 15min.

Run (10km)

First 2km: settle 5-10sec/km slower than goal pace, find rhythm post-bike. Middle 6km: target race pace, stay relaxed. Final 2km: progressively increase effort, empty the tank in last 400m.

Mental Strategy

Pre-Race Mindset

Nerves are excitement in disguise - channel that energy positively. Use the mantra 'I am prepared' during final preparations. Visualize smooth execution rather than outcomes.

Race Mantras

Swim: 'Smooth and strong' - focus on technique over speed
Bike: 'Spin to win' - maintain high cadence for run legs
Run: 'I've got this' - trust your fitness when it gets tough

Adversity Management

Use 'Yes, and...' technique: 'Yes, that was challenging, and I have the skills to adapt.' Break the race into segments - just get to the next km marker. Remember: discomfort is temporary, finishing is forever.

school Glossary

CSS (Critical Swim Speed)

Your threshold pace for swimming, calculated from 400m and 200m time trial results. Used to set training zones.

FTP (Functional Threshold Power)

The highest power you can sustain for 1 hour on the bike. Used as the basis for all cycling training zones.

Brick Session

A training session combining two disciplines back-to-back (usually bike-run) to practice race transitions.

Negative Split

Running or racing the second half faster than the first half. A smart pacing strategy for most distances.

Zone 1-2 (Z1-Z2)

Easy aerobic training intensities. You should be able to hold a conversation throughout.

Zone 3-4 (Z3-Z4)

Moderate to hard training intensities. Z3 is 'comfortably hard', Z4 is threshold/race pace effort.

Sighting

Lifting your head during open water swimming to navigate and stay on course.

Cadence

Your pedaling rate on the bike (RPM) or step rate while running (steps per minute).

Drafting

Swimming close behind another swimmer to reduce water resistance and save energy.

T1/T2

Transition 1 (swim to bike) and Transition 2 (bike to run). The 4th and 5th disciplines of triathlon.

80/20 Rule

Training principle where 80% of sessions are easy (Z1-Z2) and 20% are moderate to hard (Z3+).

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume before a race to allow the body to recover and perform optimally.

TSS

Training Stress Score - a measure of training load that accounts for both intensity and duration.

Race Pace

The target intensity you plan to sustain during each discipline on race day.

psychology Coach's Tips

pool

Master Open Water Skills

Practice sighting every 10-12 strokes and swimming in a straight line. Book 2-3 open water sessions before race day to adapt to the different conditions.

directions_bike

Higher Cadence = Better Run

Aim for 85-95 RPM on the bike. Higher cadence uses more cardiovascular system and less leg muscles, leaving you fresher for the run.

nutrition

Practice Race Nutrition

Never try anything new on race day. Practice with the exact gels, drinks, and timing you'll use in competition during your longer training sessions.

schedule

Perfect Your Transitions

T1 and T2 are often called the 4th and 5th disciplines. Practice your transition routine until it's automatic - every second counts.

psychology

Break The Race Into Chunks

Mental strategy: divide the race into smaller segments (swim buoys, bike aid stations, run km markers) and focus on just reaching the next checkpoint.

local_fire_department

Start Conservative

It's better to negative split each discipline than blow up early. Start 5-10% easier than goal pace, then build throughout each leg.

water_drop

Hydration Starts Early

Begin hydrating 2-3 days before the race. Aim for pale yellow urine and drink 150-200ml every 15 minutes during longer sessions.

bedtime

Prioritize Sleep

Sleep is your most powerful recovery tool. Aim for 8-9 hours per night, especially in the final week before racing. Poor sleep increases perceived effort significantly.
