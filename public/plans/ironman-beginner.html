An Ironman is the pinnacle of single-day endurance racing — 3.8 km swim, 180 km bike, and a full 42.2 km marathon, back to back. The typical race-day cutoff is 17 hours. Most beginner athletes finish in 12–16 hours depending on the course and conditions. The swim takes 1:00–1:30, the bike 6:00–8:00, and the run 4:30–6:30. You will transition twice: T1 (swim→bike) and T2 (bike→run). The bike leg is the longest discipline by far — it accounts for roughly half your total race time and determines how the marathon feels. Nutrition on the bike is not optional; it is the fourth discipline. This plan prepares you for all of it.

Plan Focus

This plan takes you from base fitness to the Ironman finish line over 20 weeks. Weeks 1–6 (Base) build aerobic fitness across all three disciplines — you will learn to swim with structured sets, ride for 2+ hours, and run easy and progressively longer. Weeks 7–12 (Build) introduce brick sessions (bike→run), open water swimming, longer rides building toward race distance, and race-specific volume. Weeks 13–16 (Peak) are the highest-volume weeks — your longest bike ride, longest brick, a race-distance swim, and race simulation sessions. Weeks 17–20 (Taper) reduce volume progressively while maintaining intensity, arriving at race day fresh, confident, and ready. Recovery weeks fall on Weeks 4, 8, 12, and 16. Every session has a purpose. Rest days are training days. The long bike ride is the anchor session of every week. 80% of your development happens at easy intensity — that is the engine that carries you 226 km.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Ironman training involves three disciplines, long sessions, and transitions to manage — you need to plan your week around pool access, bike routes, and run sessions.
  2. Don’t skip ahead. If you miss a session, just continue from where you are. If you miss a full week, repeat the previous week rather than jumping forward — your body doesn’t know what week number it is.
  3. Complete every recovery week as written. Weeks 4, 8, 12, and 16 are deliberately light. Adaptation happens during rest, not during the session — skipping recovery is the most common mistake beginners make.
  4. Train by effort, not by pace. This plan uses RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) instead of pace targets. If the plan says RPE 3–4, you should be able to hold a full conversation.
  5. Practise transitions. T1 (swim→bike) and T2 (bike→run) are the “fourth discipline.” Rehearse them in training so race day feels familiar, not frantic.
  6. Practise nutrition on the bike from Week 6 onwards. For an Ironman, you need 80–90 g of carbohydrates per hour on the bike leg. Your gut needs to be trained for this — start at 40 g/hr and build progressively. Under-fuelling on the bike is the single biggest race-day mistake.

Week 1 — Foundationexpand_more
Week 1 of 20

The only goal this week is to start all three disciplines. Get in the pool, get on the bike, get out for a run. Don’t chase distance or speed. Build the habit of training across swim, bike, and run. You are beginning a 20-week journey to the Ironman finish line.

MondayRest
REST

Complete rest.

TuesdaySwim — Technique Intro
SWIMRPE 3–41400m

200m easy warm-up (mix of freestyle and backstroke) → 4 × 50m catch-up drill with 15s rest (one arm stays extended until the other hand touches it — teaches patience and glide) → 6 × 100m freestyle at Aerobic zone with 20s rest (focus on exhaling fully underwater, inhaling to the side) → 200m easy cool-down (choice of stroke).

Coach’s NoteIf you can’t swim 100m freestyle without stopping, use 50m reps instead. The goal is smooth, rhythmic breathing — exhale underwater, inhale to the side. Holding your breath is the most common mistake and causes rapid fatigue. Begin exhaling through nose and mouth as soon as your face enters the water so your lungs are partially empty by the time you rotate to breathe. On race day you’ll swim 3.8 km — efficiency now saves enormous energy later.
WednesdayBike — Easy Endurance
BIKERPE 3–460 min

10 min easy spin warm-up at Z1, gradually raising cadence to 85+ RPM → 40 min steady at Z2 on a flat route → 10 min easy spin cool-down at Z1. Focus on smooth pedalling and staying relaxed in the shoulders. Cadence target: 80–90 RPM.

Coach’s NoteThis is about time on the bike, not distance. If you only have a stationary trainer, that works perfectly — indoor sessions produce similar training stress with no coasting or traffic lights. Keep it conversational. If you can’t talk comfortably, ease off the pedals. On race day the bike is 180 km — patience on the bike starts now.
ThursdayRun — Easy Run
RUNRPE 3–430 min

5 min walk warm-up → 20 min easy run at T2 → 5 min walk cool-down. Conversational pace throughout. If you need walk breaks, use run/walk intervals of 3 min run / 1 min walk.

Coach’s NoteRunning is the highest injury-risk discipline in triathlon. Start conservative. Walk breaks are always allowed and are part of the strategy, not a sign of failure. On Ironman race day, every beginner should walk through aid stations — it is the smart strategy. Run off-road where possible to reduce impact stress on your joints.

REST

Complete rest. At least one full rest day per week is non-negotiable.

SaturdayBike — Long Ride
BIKERPE 3–490 minKEY

10 min easy spin warm-up at Z1 → 70 min steady at Z2 (flat to gently rolling route) → 10 min easy spin cool-down. Practise drinking from your bottle while riding — aim for one sip every 15 min.

Coach’s NoteThe long ride is the anchor session of every week for the next 20 weeks. Do not skip it. It builds the aerobic engine that carries you through all three disciplines on race day. The bike is the best discipline for accumulating aerobic volume safely because it has the lowest impact stress. If you finish feeling like you could have done more, you rode it perfectly.
SundayRun — Easy Long Run
RUNRPE 3–435 min

5 min walk warm-up → 25 min easy run at T2 → 5 min walk cool-down. Longest run of the week. Easy effort throughout. Walk breaks encouraged.

Coach’s NoteRunning on Sunday after a long bike on Saturday simulates mild fatigue — a small preview of how your legs will feel on race day after the bike leg. Go slow. The purpose is time on feet, not speed.

90 minutes of easy cycling builds your aerobic base more than any other session this week. Stay in Z2. The bike accounts for roughly half your Ironman race time — every minute invested on the bike pays off on race day. If you can only do one session this week, do this one.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 8+ hours. Hydrate throughout the day. Stretch calves, quads, hip flexors, and shoulders for 5 minutes after each session. Your shoulders may be sore from swimming — that’s normal if you haven’t swum recently. Soreness is expected; sharp pain is not.

Race Day Protocol

Your Ironman Race Day

Night Before

Eat a normal meal with carbohydrates — pasta, rice, potatoes. Familiar foods only. Hydrate well but don’t overdrink. Prepare all gear in transition order: swim kit (wetsuit, goggles, cap), bike kit (helmet, shoes, sunglasses, nutrition on bike frame), run kit (shoes, race belt, hat). Prepare Special Needs bags: bike bag (extra gels, tube, salt tablets), run bag (dry socks, gels, anti-chafe cream, paracetamol). Check tyre pressure. Pin race number to race belt. Set two alarms. Get to bed early — the sleep 2 nights before matters more than the night before.

Race Morning (4 hrs before start)

Wake 4 hours before swim start. Pre-race meal: 1–2 g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, porridge, or a bagel with honey. Sip water — don’t overdrink. Arrive at transition 90 min before start. Set up transition: bike racked, helmet on bars, nutrition secured. Drop off Special Needs bags. Take a gel or energy chew 15–20 min before the swim start. Use the toilets before heading to the swim start. Apply anti-chafe cream liberally (neck, armpits, inner thighs, feet).

Warm-Up (10–15 min before)

If the swim warm-up area is open, swim 200–300m easy to settle breathing. If no warm-up is available, splash cold water on your face and neck to reduce the cold shock response. Do arm swings and light jogging. Take 10 slow nasal inhales to calm your nervous system.

Swim — 3.8 km

Start conservative. Do not sprint the first 200m. The first 200m is the highest risk for panic — adrenaline spike + cold shock + body contact. Start at the side or back of your wave to avoid the central “washing machine.” Settle into your rhythm by 400m. Sight every 10 strokes using a fixed landmark. Breathe bilaterally if comfortable. If you feel panicky: roll to your back, float, breathe slowly, then resume. Breaststroke is acceptable mid-race for recovery. Target: even pace or slight negative split. Do not chase faster swimmers. The swim is a tiny fraction of your total race time — preserve energy for the bike and run.

T1 — Swim to Bike

Run out of the water. Unzip wetsuit at the waist while jogging. At your rack: pull arms out, push suit to ankles, step out. Helmet on (buckle it!), sunglasses on, bike shoes on. Grab bike. Run to mount line. Mount and go. In an Ironman, T1 speed matters less than in shorter races — take 30 extra seconds to be organised. Apply sunscreen if needed. Sip water.

Bike — 180 km

Z2 ONLY for beginners. You still have a marathon after this. Begin eating and drinking within the first 15 min. Target: 80–90 g carbs/hr using gels, bars, or sports drink. Drink 500–1000 mL/hr depending on conditions. Electrolytes: 500–700 mg sodium/hr in warm conditions. Hold steady effort on climbs — don’t surge. Maintain legal drafting distance. Cadence 85–95 RPM. Collect your Special Needs bag at the designated point and resupply nutrition. If you feel strong at km 120, resist the urge to accelerate — save it for the marathon. The bike is about arriving at T2 healthy, hydrated, and fuelled. Eat even when you don’t feel hungry. Drink even when you don’t feel thirsty.

T2 — Bike to Run

Dismount before the line. Run to your rack. Rack bike, remove helmet, change to run shoes, grab race belt and hat/visor. Apply more anti-chafe cream if needed. Change socks if your feet are wet. Go. Stay calm. Smooth is fast. Take a gel and sip water before leaving T2.

Run — 42.2 km (Marathon)

Walk every aid station. This is not weakness — it is the smartest beginner Ironman strategy. Run/walk from the start: run between aid stations, walk through them. Start very slowly — the first 5 km will feel strange as your legs transition from cycling. By km 8–10, your legs will settle. Target: 30–60 g carbs/hr on the run (gel every 30–40 min + sports drink at aid stations). Collect your run Special Needs bag. In the late marathon (km 30+), cola is your friend — the caffeine and sugar provide a real boost. Flat sports drink and broth at aid stations can help with sodium and warmth if racing into the evening. If you cramp, walk, stretch, take salt, and resume. The finish line is worth every step.

Post-Race

Walk for 15 min. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 3–4 weeks of easy movement before any structured training. No running for at least 2 weeks. Gentle walking and easy swimming are fine. Your body has done something extraordinary — give it the time it needs to recover. Enjoy the finish line feeling — you are an Ironman.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Brick Session
A workout combining two disciplines back-to-back, typically bike then run. Trains the body to perform the second discipline on fatigued legs — simulating race conditions.
T1 (Transition 1)
The transition from swim to bike. Includes exiting the water, removing wetsuit, putting on helmet and bike shoes, and mounting the bike.
T2 (Transition 2)
The transition from bike to run. Includes dismounting, racking the bike, removing helmet, changing to run shoes, and heading out on the run course.
CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
Your sustainable threshold pace in the pool — the fastest pace you can hold for about 20 minutes. Used for swim interval training. Calculated from a 400m + 200m time trial.
FTP (Functional Threshold Power)
The highest power output you can sustain for approximately 1 hour on the bike. The gold standard for setting cycling training zones.
Cadence
Pedal revolutions per minute (RPM) on the bike. For Ironman, 85–95 RPM is optimal — higher cadence spares leg muscles for the marathon.
Sighting
Lifting your eyes briefly while swimming to navigate in open water. Lift only the eyes (“crocodile sighting”) every 8–10 strokes to stay on course.
Zone 2 (T2 / Z2)
Aerobic base intensity. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training happens. Builds fat oxidation and endurance.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace during a run. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
Negative Split
Completing the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The ideal pacing strategy for all three disciplines in triathlon.
Gut Training
Progressively increasing carbohydrate intake during training rides to improve the gut’s ability to absorb fuel during racing. Essential for Ironman — target 80–90 g carbs/hr on the bike.
Conversational Pace
A pace where you can speak in full sentences without gasping. The gold standard for easy training across all three disciplines.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity) week over week. How fitness is built safely. Maximum ~10% increase per week.
Taper
The planned reduction in training volume before race day (typically 3–4 weeks for Ironman). Volume drops 20–50% but intensity is maintained. Allows the body to absorb training and arrive fresh.
Active Recovery
Very light movement (walking, easy swimming, easy spinning) to promote blood flow without adding training stress.
Special Needs Bags
Bags you prepare and drop off before the race, collected mid-course on the bike (usually ~90 km) and run (usually ~20 km). Contain extra nutrition, clothing, and supplies. Plan what goes in each bag carefully.
Aid Station Strategy
Walking through aid stations on the Ironman run is a proven beginner strategy. It allows you to eat, drink, and recover without GI distress. Run between stations, walk through them.
Run/Walk Strategy
A structured approach to the Ironman marathon: alternate running and walking at planned intervals. Common patterns: run 10 min / walk 1 min, or run between aid stations and walk through them. Prevents blowup.
DNF Prevention
The most common causes of DNF (Did Not Finish) in Ironman: under-fuelling on the bike, going too hard too early, GI distress from untested nutrition, dehydration, and heat illness. All are preventable with training and planning.
Coach Tips

Nutrition Is the Fourth Discipline

For an Ironman, nutrition is as important as swim, bike, and run. Practise your race-day fuelling on every long ride from Week 6 onwards. Build toward 80–90 g carbs/hr on the bike. Train your gut — start at 40 g/hr and build over 14 weeks.

Nothing New on Race Day

Every piece of gear, every gel, every drink should have been tested in training. Race-day GI issues are almost always caused by untested nutrition. Use training to find what works for you.

Easy Days Are Easy

80% of your training is at conversational effort. Most beginners train easy days too hard and hard days too easy. If you can’t hold a full conversation, slow down. The aerobic engine is built at low intensity.

Consistency Over Volume

Missing one session is fine. Missing one week sets you back. Missing one month requires rebuilding. Show up consistently, even for short sessions. Frequency matters more than any single long workout.

Sleep Is Recovery

Sleep is the highest-ROI recovery tool available. 8–9 hours per night during training. No supplement, ice bath, or compression garment comes close to the recovery power of quality sleep.

Trust the Taper

You will feel restless, sluggish, or anxious during the taper. This is normal — it is called taper madness. No fitness is gained in the final 3 weeks — only lost through overtraining or injury. Rest is the final ingredient.

Walk the Aid Stations

On race day, walk through every aid station on the marathon. It allows you to eat, drink, and reset. The time you “lose” walking is gained back by avoiding a blowup later. Every experienced Ironman finisher knows this.

The Bike Determines the Run

How you ride the bike determines how you run the marathon. Ride Z2, fuel aggressively, and arrive at T2 healthy. A conservative bike and a good run beats a fast bike and a death march every single time.
