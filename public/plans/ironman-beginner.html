timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–6

Foundation building phase focusing on aerobic development and technique refinement. All training should be at conversational effort (Zone 1-2) with emphasis on consistency and gradual volume increases. This is where you build the aerobic engine that powers everything else.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 7–13

Introduction of race-specific intensities and longer sessions. Training becomes more focused on your target event with threshold efforts and race-pace practice. Volume peaks while maintaining the 80/20 intensity distribution principle.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 14–18

Highest training stress with race-simulation sessions. This is where everything comes together - fitness, technique, and race-specific preparation. Sessions are shorter but more intense, preparing you for race-day demands.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 19–20

Planned reduction in volume while maintaining intensity to optimize freshness for race day. Your fitness is locked in; now we're removing fatigue while keeping your systems sharp and race-ready.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Week 1 — BASE
5.0h 13 Apr–19 2026
Welcome to your Ironman journey! This first week focuses on establishing consistent movement patterns across all three disciplines. Keep everything conversational and relaxed - we're building your aerobic foundation.

Tue - Swim Technique (6:00am–6:35am)

SWIM Z1-Z2 35min
Warm-up: 200m easy freestyle, focusing on long strokes and relaxed breathing
Main Set: 8 × 50m easy freestyle with 15 sec rest - focus on catching water with fingertips, high elbow position, and complete exhale underwater. 4 × 50m kick with board (optional fins) with 20 sec rest
Cool-down: 150m easy freestyle, count strokes per length and try to maintain consistency
Total: 35 minutes
Coach's Note:
Technique is everything in swimming - perfect practice makes perfect performance. Focus on catching water early in your stroke and maintaining a long, smooth stroke rate around 18-20 strokes per 25m length. If you feel breathless, you're going too fast - this should feel completely sustainable.

Wed - Bike Endurance (6:00am–6:35am)

BIKE Z1-Z2 35min
Warm-up: 5 min easy spinning at 85-95 RPM
Main Set: 25 min steady ride in Z1-Z2, maintaining 85-100 RPM cadence throughout. Focus on smooth pedaling and relaxed shoulders
Cool-down: 5 min easy spinning, gradually reducing effort
Total: 35 minutes
Coach's Note:
Cadence is crucial for triathlon success - aim for 85-100 RPM to preserve your legs for the run. This should feel conversational throughout, like you could easily chat with a training partner. Higher cadence reduces muscular strain and improves your transition to running.

Thu - Easy Run (6:00am–6:35am)

RUN Z1-Z2 35min
Warm-up: 5 min walk building to easy jog
Main Set: 25 min easy running at 7:00/km pace - should feel completely comfortable and conversational
Cool-down: 5 min walk with gentle stretching
Total: 35 minutes
Coach's Note:
This is your aerobic base-building run - resist the urge to go faster! You should be able to hold a full conversation throughout. Focus on landing midfoot with a cadence around 170-180 steps per minute. Your body is learning to burn fat efficiently at this pace.

Fri - Long Ride (6:00am–7:15am)

BIKE Z1-Z2 75min
Warm-up: 10 min easy spinning, gradually building cadence to 90+ RPM
Main Set: 55 min steady endurance ride in Z1-Z2, maintaining consistent 85-100 RPM cadence. Practice drinking every 15 minutes
Cool-down: 10 min easy spinning, focusing on smooth pedal stroke
Total: 75 minutes
Coach's Note:
Your first long ride! Stay hydrated with 500-750ml per hour and practice your fueling routine even though it's under 90 minutes. This builds your aerobic engine and teaches your body to use fat as fuel efficiently. Keep the effort controlled - you should finish feeling like you could continue for another 30 minutes.

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before race start: Large breakfast with familiar foods. Target 3-4g carbs per kg body weight. Include moderate protein and minimal fat/fiber. Examples: oatmeal with banana and honey, or toast with jam and a small amount of peanut butter.
2-3 hours before: Begin hydrating with electrolyte drink. Aim for 500-600ml over the next 2 hours. Check and double-check all equipment. Apply sunscreen and anti-chafe products.
60-90 minutes before: Arrive at transition area. Set up your equipment methodically - practice your routine. Take time to visualize your race strategy and positive self-talk.
30-45 minutes before: Light dynamic warm-up. 5-10 minutes easy movement to activate muscles. Optional: consume 20-30g quick carbs (gel or sports drink) if race is over 90 minutes.
15 minutes before: Final equipment check. Put on wetsuit if required. Begin mental race preparation - focus on your first discipline strategy and positive mantras.
5 minutes before: Get to start line. Control breathing and excitement. Remind yourself: 'I'm prepared, I'm ready, I can do this.' Focus on executing your race plan one segment at a time.
Your Race Strategy

Swim Strategy

Start conservatively - the first 200m should feel easier than race pace. Focus on long, smooth strokes and full exhalation underwater. Sight every 10-12 strokes using the crocodile technique (eyes only, not full head lift). If you feel anxious, roll to your back, float briefly, then resume. Look for feet to draft behind - stay 30-50cm behind for 20-30% energy savings.

Bike Strategy

Mount smoothly and find your rhythm gradually. Target 85-95 RPM cadence throughout. Start fuelling within the first 20-30 minutes - aim for 40-60g carbs per hour and 150-200ml fluid every 15 minutes. Keep effort controlled and sustainable - the bike sets up your run. If you have a power meter, stay within your tested race power zones. If using RPE, you should be able to speak a few words but not hold full conversation.

Run Strategy

Expect heavy legs for the first 1-2km - this is normal after the bike. Settle into your target pace gradually. Break the run into smaller segments - aid station to aid station. Continue fuelling every 30-35 minutes with easily digestible carbs. Walk through aid stations to ensure proper hydration and fuelling. Save your hardest effort for the final 25% of the run when you know you can finish strong.

Mental Strategy

Race Day Mental Tools

Chunking: Break your race into small, manageable segments. Focus only on getting to the next aid station, kilometer marker, or transition. Each chunk gets its own mini-goal and celebration.

Positive Self-Talk: Use mantras you've practiced in training: 'Strong and steady,' 'I've trained for this,' 'Smooth is fast,' or 'I can do hard things.' Replace negative thoughts immediately with positive, actionable cues.

The 3 Rs at Transitions: Rest (let go of the previous leg), Regroup (prepare mentally for the next discipline), and Refocus (narrow attention to the immediate task ahead). Use 'Yes, and...' thinking: 'Yes, that was challenging, and I still have the strength to execute my plan.'

When Things Get Tough: Remember that discomfort is temporary and expected. Focus on your breathing, form, and the next small goal. Every athlete feels discomfort - your training has prepared you to work through it. Trust your fitness and stay patient with your race plan.

school Glossary

CSS (Critical Swim Speed)

Your threshold pace for swimming, calculated as (400m time - 200m time) ÷ 2. This is your sustainable pace for longer swim efforts and the basis for setting training zones.

Brick Session

A training session combining two disciplines back-to-back with no break, typically bike-to-run. Teaches your body to adapt to the muscle transition demands of triathlon racing.

Zone 1-2 (Aerobic Base)

Easy, conversational effort level where you can speak in full sentences. This should make up 80% of your training volume and builds your aerobic engine efficiently.

Zone 3 (Threshold)

Comfortably hard effort where you can speak a few words but not hold full conversation. This is typically your race pace for Olympic distance triathlon.

FTP (Functional Threshold Power)

The highest power you can sustain for one hour on the bike. Used to set training zones and race pacing targets. Typically tested with a 20-minute all-out effort.

RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)

Subjective measure of how hard you're working on a scale of 1-10. Useful when heart rate or power data isn't available or reliable.

Negative Split

Racing strategy where the second half is completed faster than the first half. Requires discipline early but typically results in stronger overall performance.

Drafting

Swimming or cycling closely behind another athlete to reduce energy expenditure. Legal in swim and run, illegal in most age-group bike segments.

T1/T2

Transition areas where you change from swim to bike (T1) and bike to run (T2). Efficient transitions can save significant time and mental energy.

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume (but not intensity) before a race to optimize freshness and performance. Typically 1-2 weeks for Olympic distance.

Cadence

Steps per minute while running (target 170-180) or pedal revolutions per minute while cycling (target 85-95). Higher cadence typically improves efficiency.

Sighting

Lifting your eyes to navigate while swimming. Best done with minimal head lift every 10-12 strokes using the 'crocodile' technique.

Fuelling

Consuming carbohydrates during exercise to maintain energy levels. Target 40-60g per hour for efforts over 90 minutes, starting within the first 30 minutes.

Recovery Week

Planned reduction in training volume (typically 50-60% of normal) every 3-4 weeks to allow adaptation and prevent overreaching.

Begin taking in carbohydrates within the first 20-30 minutes of any session over 60 minutes. Don't wait until you feel depleted - prevention is better than rescue.
