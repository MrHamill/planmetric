timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–2

Foundation building phase focusing on aerobic development through easy-paced running and technique work. All runs at conversational effort with emphasis on consistent volume building and injury prevention.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 3–6

Intensity introduction phase combining tempo runs and longer intervals with continued aerobic base development. Begin race-specific pacing work while maintaining high weekly volume.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 7–9

Highest training loads with race-specific interval sessions. Sharp focus on 10K pace work and VO2max development through varied interval lengths. Maximum weekly volume and training stress.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 10–10

Volume reduction while maintaining fitness through short, easy runs with occasional race-pace strides. Focus shifts to race preparation, mental rehearsal, and physical recovery for optimal race day performance.

Base phase introduction - establishing aerobic foundation with conservative volume. Focus on running at conversational pace with one tempo session to develop threshold capacity.

Tue - Easy Run (5:30am–5:55am)

RUN Z1-Z2 25min
Warm-up: 5 min walk/gentle jog
Main Set: 15 min easy running at 5:15-5:30/km pace, fully conversational

Coach's Note:
Welcome to your 10K journey! This first easy run should feel comfortable - you should be able to hold a conversation throughout. Total: 30 min
Coach's Note:
Your first tempo session targets threshold development - the pace should feel comfortably hard, around 85% effort. You should be able to say 3-4 words at a time but not hold a full conversation. This pace teaches your body to clear lactate efficiently while running fast.

RUN Z1-Z2 25min
Warm-up: 5 min walk/gentle jog
Main Set: 15 min easy running at 5:15-5:30/km pace
Cool-down: 5 min walk
Total: 25 min

Keep this run genuinely easy to prepare for tomorrow's session. Consider adding 15-20 minutes of strength work 3-6 hours after this run, focusing on single-leg squats, glute bridges, and core stability. Cool-down: 5 min walk
Total: 25 min

flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb meal (1-4g carbs per kg bodyweight). Avoid high fiber or unfamiliar foods. Include moderate protein and minimal fat. Hydrate with 500-750mL fluid.
90-120 minutes before: Arrive at venue. Complete registration, gear check, and course familiarization. Begin sipping sports drink if using.
45-60 minutes before: Begin warm-up routine: 10 min easy jog + 6 x 20sec strides building to race pace. Stay relaxed and positive.
30-45 minutes before: Final prep: race number, timing chip check. Mental preparation and positive self-talk. Optional: 1 gel if racing >45 minutes.
15-20 minutes before: Move to start line. Final dynamic stretches (leg swings, high knees). Remind yourself of pacing strategy and race goals.
5-10 minutes before: Final mental preparation. Deep breathing, positive visualization. Trust your training and stay calm.
Your Race Strategy

Start Strategy: Position yourself appropriately based on goal time. Begin 5-10 seconds per km slower than target pace for the first 2-3km to avoid going out too fast in race excitement.

Middle Section (3-7km): Settle into target race pace. Focus on smooth, efficient form and controlled breathing. Use aid stations strategically but don't break rhythm.

Final Push (7-10km): Gradually increase effort from 7km onward. Use any speed reserve built from your interval training. Focus on maintaining form as fatigue sets in - shorter stride, quicker cadence.

Pacing Targets: If targeting 40:00 (4:00/km), aim for 4:05-4:10/km in first 3km, 4:00/km for kilometers 3-7, then progressively faster to finish strong.

Mental Strategy

Chunking Strategy: Break the race into four 2.5km segments. Each segment has its own mini-goal - just get to the next milestone without worrying about the full distance.

Positive Self-Talk: Rehearse key mantras: 'Strong and steady', 'I've trained for this', 'Smooth is fast'. Replace any negative thoughts with these practiced phrases.

Discomfort Management: Expect discomfort from 6km onward - this is normal and means you're racing properly. Use your mantra, focus on form cues (relaxed shoulders, quick cadence), and remind yourself this feeling is temporary.

Finishing Mindset: From 8km onward, shift to 'I can do anything for 2km'. Use the crowd energy, pick off runners ahead, and remember - everyone hurts equally in the final kilometers.

school Glossary

Zone 1-2 (Z1-Z2)

Easy conversational pace where you can comfortably hold a conversation. Builds aerobic base with minimal fatigue.

Zone 3-4 (Z3-Z4)

Tempo/threshold effort - comfortably hard but sustainable. Key pace for building lactate clearance ability.

Zone 4-5 (Z4-Z5)

Hard interval pace targeting VO2max development. Race pace and faster efforts that improve oxygen uptake.

5K Pace

The pace you could sustain for a maximum 5km effort. Used for VO2max intervals, typically 15-20 sec/km faster than 10K pace.

10K Race Pace

Your target pace for race day. The fastest pace you can sustain for the full 10km distance.

Tempo Pace

Comfortably hard effort, roughly 15-30 seconds per km slower than 10K pace. Builds lactate threshold.

Strides

Short 20-30 second accelerations to near-race pace. Used in warm-ups to activate fast-twitch muscle fibers.

Conversational Pace

Easy effort where you can speak in full sentences. Essential for aerobic base building and recovery.

Cadence

Steps per minute. Optimal running cadence is typically 170-180 spm for injury prevention and efficiency.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race faster than the first half. Requires disciplined early pacing.

VO2max

Maximum oxygen uptake capacity. Intervals at 5K pace and faster improve this crucial fitness metric.

Lactate Threshold

The exercise intensity at which lactate begins accumulating faster than it can be cleared. Improved by tempo running.

Active Recovery

Easy exercise that promotes blood flow and adaptation without creating additional fatigue.

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume while maintaining intensity to peak for race day.

Brick Session

Training two disciplines back-to-back (bike-run) to practice race transitions and specific adaptations.

psychology Coach's Tips

schedule

Consistency Over Perfection

Missing one session won't hurt your fitness, but consistently missing easy runs will. Prioritize the long runs and easy miles over speed work if life gets busy.

favorite

Easy Days Must Be Easy

The most common mistake is running easy days too hard. If you can't hold a conversation, you're going too fast. These runs build your aerobic engine.

directions_run

Practice Race Day Fueling

If your 10K will take over 45 minutes, practice taking a gel at 5-6km during long runs. Never try anything new on race day.

self_improvement

Master the Mental Game

Break your race into 2.5km chunks with specific goals for each segment. When it gets tough, just focus on reaching the next kilometer marker.

speed

Start Conservatively

Begin 5-10 seconds per km slower than goal pace. It's much easier to speed up in the second half than recover from going out too fast.

fitness_center

Don't Skip Strength Work

Two strength sessions per week in base phase prevent injuries and improve running economy. Focus on single-leg stability, glutes, and core.

local_drink

Hydration Strategy

For runs over 60 minutes, drink 500-750mL per hour. Practice your race day hydration plan during long runs to avoid stomach issues.

bedtime

Prioritize Recovery

Sleep 8-9 hours per night during peak training. Recovery weeks every 4th week are when your body adapts and gets stronger - don't skip them.
