10K Elite.
Every second counts.

An 8-week race-specific programme for competitive runners. Structured VO2max intervals, threshold blocks, and race-pace finishing — built on the principle that elite 10K performance is earned in the final 3km.

Plan Focus

This plan assumes a strong aerobic base and recent racing history. You are not building fitness from scratch — you are sharpening it. The first two weeks hone your threshold and VO2max ceiling. Week 3 is the highest-intensity week in the programme. Week 4 absorbs the load. Weeks 5–6 shift to race-specific work: VO2max at 10K pace, long runs with race-pace finishes. Weeks 7–8 strip volume to a minimum and deliver you to the start line sharp, rested, and dangerous. Every session has a purpose. If you cannot articulate why you are doing a session, re-read the coach's note.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before starting it. Understand the stimulus of each session and how it fits the micro-cycle. Move sessions within the week if needed, but never stack two high-intensity days back-to-back.
  2. Respect the recovery week. Week 4 drops to ~30km. Elite athletes who skip recovery weeks accumulate fatigue that surfaces as injury or plateau in the race-specific block. Absorb the work.
  3. Run easy days genuinely easy. If your T2 pace feels embarrassingly slow, you are doing it right. The purpose of easy days is perfusion and recovery — not fitness.
  4. Nail the key sessions. Each week has one or two sessions marked KEY. These are non-negotiable. If life forces you to drop a session, drop the easy run — never the key workout.

Week 1 of 8

Establish the weekly rhythm. Threshold and VO2max sessions appear immediately — you are not building base, you are sharpening it. ~52km total. Controlled aggression.

45 min easy → 6 × 20-sec strides with 40-sec jog recovery.

Coach's NoteSet the tone for the week. Genuinely easy. Strides at the end are neuromuscular — smooth acceleration to ~90% effort, tall posture, quick turnover. Not sprints.

Coach's NoteFirst key session. Hit the paces but don't exceed them — the goal is 5 consistent reps, not 3 fast ones and 2 death marches. If rep 5 is more than 5 seconds slower than rep 1, you went out too hard.

RPE 340 min~7 km

40 min continuous easy. Flat route preferred. This is recovery disguised as running.

ThursdayThreshold Blocks
RPE 5–655 min~10 kmKEY

15 min warm-up → 3 × 10 min at T3 with 2 min jog recovery → 10 min cool-down.

Coach's NoteThreshold work is the backbone of 10K preparation. These should feel controlled and sustainable — comfortably hard. If you can't complete sentence fragments, you're in T4 territory. Back off.
FridayRest
REST

Complete rest. No cross-training. Sleep.

SaturdayParkrun Race Effort
RPE 7–840 min~8 km

10 min warm-up (easy + 4 strides) → 5K parkrun at controlled race effort (aim for consistent splits) → 10 min cool-down jog.

Coach's NoteParkrun is a weekly race stimulus. Do not treat it as an all-out effort — treat it as a controlled 5K at 95% race pace. Use it to practise pacing discipline and build race-day composure.

RPE 3–570 min~13 km

70 min easy with final 3km at 10K goal pace (T3–T4).

Coach's NoteThe long run anchors the week. Run the first 55 minutes genuinely easy — then close with 3km at race pace. The purpose of the finish is to practise running fast on tired legs. This is where 10K races are won.

star Key Workouts — Tuesday & Thursday

VO2max intervals build your aerobic ceiling. Threshold blocks raise the pace you can sustain for 30–40 minutes. Together they form the two pillars of elite 10K performance. Nail both. If you must choose one, choose Tuesday.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 8+ hours. Post-session nutrition within 30 minutes of key workouts — 1g/kg carbs + 0.3g/kg protein. Foam roll quads, calves, and glutes. Cold water immersion after Tuesday and Thursday if available.

psychology Weekly Check-In

"Were my VO2max reps consistent? Could I complete all 3 threshold blocks without drifting into T4? If the answer to both is yes, the base is sharp."

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

The 80/20 Rule

80% of your training volume should be at easy intensity (T1–T2). The remaining 20% is where the magic happens — VO2max, threshold, race pace. If you violate this ratio, you'll accumulate fatigue that undermines your key sessions.

Easy Means Embarrassingly Slow

If you wouldn't feel comfortable running your easy pace past a group of club runners, it's probably the right pace. Easy days are for recovery and perfusion. They are not fitness sessions.

Consistency Over Heroics

Five consistent reps at target pace beats three fast reps and two death marches. Control the effort. The body adapts to sustained, repeatable stimuli — not one-off maximal efforts.

Sleep Is Non-Negotiable

8–9 hours per night. Growth hormone, muscular repair, neural consolidation — all happen during sleep. A poor night of sleep degrades your next session more than a missed session does.

Fuel the Work

Post-workout nutrition within 30 minutes: 1g/kg carbs + 0.3g/kg protein. Don't restrict calories during training blocks. You are not training to lose weight — you are training to race fast.

Monitor Resting Heart Rate

Check RHR each morning before getting out of bed. If it's elevated by 5+ bpm, your body is still recovering. Convert that day's session to easy running or take complete rest.

Race-Pace Finishes Are Everything

The long run with a 10K-pace finish is the most race-specific session in this plan. It teaches the body to find race pace on tired legs — exactly what happens in km 7–10 of your race.

Don't Race the Training

Tuesday's VO2max session is not a time trial. Thursday's threshold is not a race. Hit the prescribed effort and stop. The race is on Saturday of Week 8 — save it.

Parkrun Is a Tool, Not the Goal

Weekly parkrun provides race stimulus, pacing practice, and fitness markers. Run it controlled. One hard parkrun in Week 3 for benchmarking. Every other week: 95% effort, pacing discipline.

Trust the Taper

Weeks 7–8 will make you feel restless, sluggish, or anxious. That is normal. The taper is not detraining — it's allowing accumulated adaptations to express themselves. Do not add sessions.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of session intensity. The primary effort measure in this plan.
T1 — Recovery
Very easy. Warm-ups, cool-downs, active recovery. Barely above walking pace.
T2 — Aerobic Base
Easy and conversational. The engine of endurance. Where 80% of your volume lives.
T3 — Threshold
Comfortably hard. Sustainable for 20–40 minutes. Short sentences only. The backbone of 10K fitness.
T4 — VO2max
Hard. 3–8 minute efforts. Laboured breathing. Builds your aerobic ceiling.
T5 — Anaerobic
Maximum. Unsustainable beyond ~2 minutes. Accessed briefly via strides and hill repeats.
VO2max Intervals
Repeated efforts at T4 intensity (typically 1km) with controlled recovery. Raises the aerobic power ceiling.
Threshold Blocks
Sustained efforts at T3 for 8–12 minutes. Raises the pace sustainable for 30–40 minutes — directly applicable to 10K racing.
Hill Repeats
Repeated 60–90 second efforts up a moderate gradient. Develops power, running economy, and strength-endurance.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at ~90% effort. Maintains neuromuscular sharpness. Not sprints.
Race-Pace Finish
Closing a long run with 3km at 10K goal pace. Simulates late-race conditions and trains the body to find speed on tired legs.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a race faster than the first. The gold standard of elite pacing strategy.
Taper
A planned reduction in training volume (20–50%) while maintaining intensity. Allows full adaptation expression before race day.
Periodisation
The structured progression of training phases: base sharpening, peak build, recovery, race-specific, taper.
Race Day Protocol

Your 10K Race Execution Strategy

48 Hours Before

Carb-load moderately — 7–10g/kg carbs over the final 48 hours. Reduce fibre and fat. Hydrate well but don't overdrink. No alcohol. Sleep 8+ hours both nights.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before)

1–2g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, or porridge. Caffeine if you use it (3–6mg/kg, 60 min pre-race). Sip water — don't flood the system.

Arrival (45–60 min early)

Collect race number. Use toilets. Walk the start and finish areas. Identify the 1km and 5km markers if possible. Settle nerves. Visualise the race plan.

Warm-up (20–25 min before start)

15 min easy jog → 4 × 20-sec strides at race pace → dynamic drills (leg swings, high knees, A-skips). Arrive at the start line with warm muscles, elevated heart rate, and a clear mind.

Race Execution — KM by KM

KM 1–3: Patience. Start at goal pace or 2–3 seconds per km slower. The adrenaline will make this feel too easy. It is not too easy — it is correct. Settle into rhythm. Control breathing.

KM 4–7: Execution. This is where the race is run. Lock onto goal pace. Stay composed. Focus on form — tall posture, relaxed shoulders, quick turnover. Use other runners to pace off but do not surge or respond to surges. Run your race.

KM 8–10: Everything. This is where the race is won. The threshold work and race-pace finishes prepared you for this. When the pain arrives, maintain form first, then find another gear. The final km should be your fastest or second-fastest. Leave nothing on the course.

Post-Race

Walk 10 min to cool down. Refuel within 60 minutes — carbs + protein. Rehydrate aggressively. Log your splits while you remember them. Allow 5–7 days of easy movement before returning to structured training.
