Plan Focus

This plan takes you from little or no triathlon experience to crossing the finish line of an Olympic-distance triathlon: 1.5 km swim, 40 km bike, 10 km run. The first three weeks build basic competence in all three disciplines. Week 4 is a recovery week. Weeks 5–8 increase durations, introduce brick sessions (bike-to-run), and add open water swim practice. Weeks 9–10 are race-specific with transition rehearsals. Weeks 11–12 taper you into race day fresh and confident. Every session has a purpose. Rest days are training days. The long bike ride is the anchor session of every week. 80% of your development happens at easy intensity — that's why those sessions aren't optional, they're the engine.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Triathlon has three disciplines and transitions to manage — you need to plan your week around pool access, bike routes, and run sessions.
  2. Don't skip ahead. If you miss a session, just continue from where you are. If you miss a full week, repeat the previous week rather than jumping forward — your body doesn't know what week number it is.
  3. Complete every recovery week as written. Week 4 is deliberately light. Skipping recovery is the most common mistake beginners make — adaptation happens during rest, not during the session.
  4. Train by effort, not by pace. This plan uses RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) instead of pace targets. Your GPS watch doesn't know how your legs feel. If the plan says RPE 3–4, you should be able to hold a full conversation.
  5. Practise transitions. T1 (swim-to-bike) and T2 (bike-to-run) are often called the "fourth discipline." Rehearse them in training so race day feels familiar, not frantic.

Week 1 — Foundationexpand_more
Week 1 of 12

The only goal this week is to start all three disciplines. Get in the pool, get on the bike, get out for a run. Don't chase distance or speed. Build the habit of training across swim, bike, and run.

TuesdaySwim — Technique Intro
SWIMRPE 3–41500m

Coach's NoteIf you can't swim 50m freestyle without stopping, use 25m reps instead. The goal is smooth, rhythmic breathing — exhale underwater, inhale to the side. No rush.

Coach's NoteThis is about time on the bike, not distance. If you only have a stationary trainer, that works perfectly. Keep it conversational.

RUNRPE 3–430 min

5 min walk warm-up → 20 min easy run → 5 min walk cool-down. Conversational pace throughout. If you need walk breaks, take them — run/walk 3 min/1 min is fine.

FridayRest or Easy Swim

Complete rest, or 20–30 min easy swim (Recovery zone) if you feel good and want pool time. Kick drills, backstroke, easy freestyle. Nothing structured.

SaturdayBike — Long Ride
BIKERPE 3–475 minKEY

75 min easy Z2 ride. Mostly flat. Practise drinking from your bottle while riding. Aim for one sip every 15 min.

Coach's NoteThe long ride is the anchor session of every week. Do not skip it. It builds the aerobic engine that carries you through all three disciplines on race day.
SundayRun — Easy Long Run
RUNRPE 3–435 min

5 min walk warm-up → 25 min easy run → 5 min walk cool-down. Longest run of the week. Easy effort.

star Key Workout — Saturday Long Ride

75 minutes of easy cycling builds your aerobic base more than any other session this week. Stay in Z2. If you finish feeling like you could have done more, you rode it perfectly.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 8+ hours. Hydrate throughout the day. Stretch calves, quads, hip flexors, and shoulders for 5 minutes after each session. Soreness in the first week is normal — sharp pain is not. Your shoulders may be sore from swimming — that's normal if you haven't swum recently.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

Easy Means Easy

Zone 2 is the most underused zone in amateur athletes. Most people train their easy days too hard and their hard days too easy. If you can't hold a full conversation during an easy session, slow down — in all three disciplines.

The Long Ride Is King

The Saturday long ride is the anchor of this plan. If you can only do one session in a week, do the long ride. It builds the aerobic engine that carries you through all three legs on race day.

Practise Nutrition Early

Your stomach needs to learn to process food while exercising. Start practising on the bike from Week 2. On race day, aim for 30–60g carbs per hour on the bike. If you haven't practised it, don't try it for the first time on race day.

Sleep Is Your Best Training Tool

8–9 hours per night is the target. A bad night of sleep affects your next session more than a missed session does.

Don't Skip Transitions

T1 and T2 are the "fourth discipline." Practise them in training so they feel automatic. Lay out your gear in order. Know the sequence. Rehearse until it's muscle memory.

Swim Technique Over Volume

An efficient 1500m swim uses less energy than a thrashing 1000m. Prioritise drills, breathing rhythm, and body rotation over brute metres. The pool is where you learn to be efficient.

Open Water Is Different

Swimming without a black line, in a wetsuit, surrounded by other swimmers is nothing like the pool. Get at least 3–4 open water sessions before race day. Never swim open water alone.

Brick Sessions Build Confidence

The jelly-leg feeling when you run off the bike is normal. It fades with practice. Brick sessions teach your body and mind what to expect on race day. Don't skip them.

Soreness vs. Pain

Muscle soreness after training is normal. Sharp, localised pain in joints (knees, shoulders, ankles) is a warning sign. Rest and reassess — never train through joint pain.

One Bad Session Is One Session

If a swim feels terrible, get out. If a ride is miserable, ride home. Come back next session. Consistency over perfection.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Brick Session
A workout combining two disciplines back-to-back, typically bike then run. Trains the body to perform the second discipline on fatigued legs — simulating race conditions.
T1 (Transition 1)
The transition from swim to bike. Includes exiting the water, removing wetsuit, putting on helmet and bike shoes, and mounting the bike. Speed here is free time.
T2 (Transition 2)
The transition from bike to run. Includes dismounting, racking the bike, removing helmet, changing to run shoes, and heading out on the run course.
CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
Your sustainable threshold pace in the pool — the fastest pace you can hold for about 20 minutes. Equivalent to lactate threshold for swimming. Used for swim interval training.
Cadence
Pedal revolutions per minute (RPM) on the bike. For endurance cycling, aim for 80–95 RPM. Higher cadence is generally more efficient and saves your legs for the run.
Sighting
Lifting your head briefly while swimming to navigate in open water. Look forward every 8–10 strokes to stay on course. Practise in the pool by looking at a wall marker.
Drafting
Swimming or cycling closely behind another athlete to reduce drag. Legal in the swim (and beneficial). Usually illegal on the bike in age-group triathlon — maintain the required gap.
Aero Position
A low, aerodynamic position on the bike with hands on the drops or aerobars, elbows bent, back flat. Reduces wind resistance. Practise in training — don't try it for the first time on race day.
Zone 2 (T2 / Z2)
Aerobic base. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training happens across all three disciplines. Builds fat oxidation and endurance.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace during a run. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
Conversational Pace
A pace where you can speak in full sentences without gasping. The gold standard for easy training in all three disciplines.
Wetsuit
A neoprene suit worn for open water swimming. Provides buoyancy (makes you faster) and warmth. Usually required or recommended for water below 20°C.
Negative Split
Completing the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The ideal pacing strategy for the bike and run legs of a triathlon.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity) week over week. How fitness is built safely.
Active Recovery
Very light movement (walking, easy swimming, easy spinning) to promote blood flow without adding training stress.
Race Day Protocol

Your Olympic Triathlon Race Day

Night Before

Eat a normal meal with carbohydrates. Hydrate well. Prepare all gear in transition order: swim kit, bike kit, run kit. Check tyre pressure. Pin your race number to your race belt. Set two alarms. Get to bed early.

Race Morning (3 hrs before start)

Pre-race meal: 1–2g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, or porridge. Sip water — don't overdrink. Take a gel or energy chew 15 min before the swim start.

Arrival (60–90 min early)

Set up transition: bike racked, helmet on bars (open and ready), shoes positioned, run gear laid out in order. Walk the transition area — know your entry and exit points for T1 and T2. Use the toilets. Put on your wetsuit 15–20 min before start.

Warm-Up (10–15 min before)

If the swim warm-up area is open, swim 200–300m easy to get your face in the water and your breathing settled. If no warm-up swim is available, do arm swings, light jogging, and splash water on your face.

Swim — 1.5 km

Start conservative. Don't sprint the first 200m with the pack. Find clear water to the side or back. Settle into your rhythm by 200m. Sight every 8–10 strokes. Breathe bilaterally if comfortable, or breathe away from the sun/chop. Stay calm — if you feel panicky, roll on your back, float, breathe, then continue.

T1 — Swim to Bike

Run out of the water. Unzip wetsuit at the waist while jogging. At your transition spot: pull arms and suit to ankles, step out. Put on helmet (buckle it!), sunglasses, bike shoes (or run to mount line barefoot). Grab bike, run to mount line, mount and go.

Bike — 40 km

Don't go too hard. Ride at Z2–Z3. You still have a 10K run after this. Start eating and drinking within the first 15 min. Aim for 30–60g carbs per hour: 1–2 gels plus water. Drink every 15 min. Hold a steady effort on climbs — don't surge. Maintain legal drafting distance.

T2 — Bike to Run

Dismount before the line. Run to your rack spot. Rack bike, remove helmet, change to run shoes, grab race belt and hat/visor. Go. Keep it calm — 30 seconds of smooth transition beats 15 seconds of flustered chaos.

Run — 10 km

The first 2 km will feel strange. Your legs are switching from cycling to running. Start very slowly. Walk through the first aid station. Sip water or sports drink. By KM 3, your legs will settle. Then find a rhythm you can hold. Walk the aid stations if needed — it's smarter than blowing up. Aim for an even effort or negative split. Save something for the last KM and finish strong.

Post-Race

Walk for 10 min. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 5–7 days of easy movement before any structured training. Enjoy the finish line feeling — you've earned it.
