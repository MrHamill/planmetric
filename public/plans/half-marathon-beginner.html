timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–3

Building your aerobic foundation with easy-paced running and gradual volume increases. Focus on consistency over speed — every run should feel conversational. This phase develops fat-burning efficiency and strengthens bones, tendons, and ligaments for harder training ahead.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 4–7

Introducing race-specific workouts while maintaining your aerobic base. You'll add tempo runs, intervals, and race-pace efforts to build lactate clearance and VO2max. Volume continues to increase but intensity is carefully controlled — quality over quantity.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 8–10

Highest training stress with race simulations and specific pace work. Long runs reach maximum distance and interval sessions become more race-specific. This phase teaches your body to handle the demands of race day while building confidence.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 11–12

Strategic volume reduction while maintaining intensity to maximize race day performance. Your body absorbs all the training adaptations while eliminating fatigue. Less is more — trust your preparation and arrive at the start line fresh and eager.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Weeks 1–3

Week 1 — BASE
3.3h 13 Apr–19 2026
Welcome to your half marathon journey! This first week establishes your aerobic base with gentle progression. Focus on running conversationally during easy sessions and finding a comfortably hard rhythm during your tempo run. Your long run is the cornerstone of endurance development.

Tue - Easy Run (6:00am–6:40am)

RUN Z1-Z2 40min
Warm-up: 5 min walk, then 5 min easy jog
Main Set: 30 min steady running at 7:30/km pace (Z1-Z2) - fully conversational effort
Cool-down: 5 min easy walk with gentle stretching
Total: 40 min
Coach's Note:
This is your foundation session - resist the urge to go faster than conversational pace. You should be able to speak in full sentences throughout. If you're breathing hard, slow down. Building your aerobic base requires patience, and easy runs done too fast will compromise your harder sessions later in the week.

Thu - Tempo Run (6:00am–6:50am)

RUN Z3 50min
Warm-up: 10 min easy jog, then 5 min gradual build to tempo effort
Main Set: 25 min tempo running at comfortably hard effort (Z3) - roughly 30-45 sec/km faster than easy pace
Cool-down: 10 min easy jog and walking
Total: 50 min
Coach's Note:
Tempo pace should feel like a controlled, sustained effort that you could hold for about an hour in a race. This isn't a sprint - think 'comfortably hard' rather than all-out. Focus on smooth, relaxed running form and consistent effort rather than chasing specific split times. This session builds your lactate threshold, which is crucial for half marathon performance.

Sat - Long Run (7:00am–8:45am)

RUN Z1-Z2 105min
Warm-up: 10 min easy walk-jog transition
Main Set: 85 min continuous running at 7:30/km pace (Z1-Z2) - stay relaxed and conversational
Cool-down: 10 min walking with dynamic stretching
Total: 105 min
Coach's Note:
Your most important session of the week! This builds the aerobic engine that powers endurance performance. Keep the effort genuinely easy - you should finish feeling like you could have continued. Hydrate well throughout (aim for 150-200ml every 15-20 minutes) and practice your race-day fuelling if the session approaches 90 minutes. Remember, the goal is time on your feet, not speed.

flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: Light breakfast with familiar foods — oatmeal with banana, toast with jam, or cereal. Aim for 1-2g carbs per kg bodyweight. Start hydrating.
2-3 hours before: Finish eating solid food. Continue sipping water. Begin mental preparation and visualization.
1-2 hours before: Arrive at race venue. Complete registration and familiarize yourself with course layout. Use bathroom.
45-60 min before: Begin dynamic warm-up — 10 min easy jogging, leg swings, gentle stretches. Stay warm and loose.
20-30 min before: Final bathroom visit. Check gear one last time. Move to start area.
10-15 min before: 3-4 short strides to activate muscles. Get in position according to pace group.
5 min before: Deep breathing, positive self-talk. You're ready.
Your Race Strategy

First 5km (0-21:07): Start conservatively at 6:50-7:00/km pace — slightly slower than your 6:45/km goal. Let faster runners go; you'll see them later. Focus on smooth rhythm and settling in.

Middle 15km (21:07-1:43:15): Gradually build to goal pace of 6:45/km. Take water at every aid station from km 5 onwards. Break this section into 5km chunks — just get to the next marker. Stay relaxed and controlled.

Final 6.1km (1:43:15-finish): This is where your training pays off. Maintain pace through km 18, then give everything you have in the final 3km. Embrace the discomfort — you've prepared for this.

Fueling: Take small sips at each aid station. Consider energy gel at km 10 if you've practiced with it in training.

Mental Strategy

Chunking: Break the race into four 5km segments. Each segment has one simple goal — get to the next aid station feeling controlled and strong.

Self-Talk Mantras: 'Strong and steady' (first half), 'I've trained for this' (middle section), 'I can do hard things' (final 5km). Practice these during your long runs.

When it gets tough (km 15+): Remember that everyone suffers in the final kilometers. Your consistent training means you suffer less than most. Focus on form cues — relaxed shoulders, quick cadence, forward lean.

Embrace the challenge: The half marathon is meant to be hard. When you hit that wall around km 18, smile — this is where the real race begins and where your preparation shows.

school Glossary

Easy Pace (Z1-Z2)

Conversational pace where you can speak in full sentences — typically 7:30/km for beginners. Should feel sustainable for hours.

Tempo/Threshold (Z4)

Comfortably hard pace you could maintain for 45-60 minutes — around 6:30-6:45/km. Builds lactate clearance and race pace strength.

VO2max Intervals (Z5)

Short, intense efforts at 5K race pace or faster with equal recovery. Improves maximum oxygen uptake and running economy.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race faster than the first half. The gold standard for optimal pacing and energy management.

Taper

2-3 week period before race day where training volume decreases by 40-60% while maintaining intensity to maximize recovery and performance.

Race Pace

Your target pace for race day — 6:45/km for a 2:23:30 half marathon goal. Should feel controlled and sustainable.

Long Run

Weekly cornerstone session building aerobic endurance. Duration matters more than pace — should always feel conversational.

Strides

Short 15-30 second accelerations to near-sprint pace with full recovery. Activates fast-twitch muscles and improves running form.

Cadence

Steps per minute (SPM). Optimal range is 170-180 SPM for injury prevention and efficiency. Count one foot for 30 seconds, multiply by 4.

Progressive Run

Run that starts easy and gradually increases in pace. Teaches pacing discipline and negative split racing strategy.

Recovery Week

Reduced volume week (50-60% of normal training) every 3-4 weeks to allow adaptations to occur and prevent overtraining.

Fartlek

Swedish for 'speed play' — unstructured intervals mixing fast and slow running based on feel rather than strict time/distance.

Hill Repeats

Running intervals up a moderate gradient (4-6%) at threshold effort. Builds power, strength, and mental toughness with lower impact.

Warm-up

Gradual preparation phase starting with walking, progressing to easy jogging, then dynamic movements to prepare muscles for hard efforts.

psychology Coach's Tips

pace

Master Conversational Pace

If you can't speak in full sentences during easy runs, you're going too fast. Slow down — this builds your aerobic base most effectively.

schedule

Consistency Over Speed

Three regular runs per week beats sporadic hard training. Missing a week sets you back more than running every session a bit slower.

healing

Listen to Your Body

Persistent pain, extreme fatigue, or elevated resting heart rate are signs to take an extra rest day. Recovery is when adaptation happens.

local_drink

Practice Race Day Fueling

Never try anything new on race day. Practice your hydration and fueling strategy during long runs to avoid stomach issues.

trending_up

Build Gradually

Increase weekly volume by no more than 10% each week. Sudden jumps in training load are the fastest route to injury.

psychology

Train Your Mind

Practice positive self-talk and race strategy during hard training sessions. Mental preparation is as important as physical fitness.

bedtime

Prioritize Sleep

8-9 hours of quality sleep is the highest ROI recovery tool. Poor sleep increases injury risk and reduces training adaptation.

directions_run

Focus on Form

Aim for 170-180 steps per minute with relaxed shoulders and slight forward lean. Good form prevents injury and improves efficiency.
