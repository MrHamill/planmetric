Plan Focus

This plan takes you from 10K fitness to a confident half marathon finish. The first three weeks consolidate your aerobic base with easy running and a long run that builds from 10 to 12km. Recovery weeks at Weeks 4 and 8 let your body absorb the training. Strides are introduced in Week 5 to sharpen your running economy. An easy tempo effort appears from Week 9 to prepare you for sustained race-pace running. The long run is the anchor of every week — it builds to 18–19km, which means you'll cover 85–90% of race distance in training. Walk breaks on long runs are encouraged, not a sign of weakness. 80% of your training happens at conversational effort. Trust the easy pace — it's building the aerobic engine that carries you to 21.1km.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Know what's coming so you can plan your schedule around the long run — it's the most important session every week.
  2. Don't skip ahead. If you miss a session, continue from where you are. If you miss a full week, repeat the previous week rather than jumping forward — your body doesn't know what week number it is.
  3. Complete every recovery week as written. Weeks 4 and 8 are deliberately light. Skipping recovery is the most common mistake beginner half marathon runners make — adaptation happens during rest, not during the session.
  4. Run by effort, not by pace. This plan uses RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) instead of pace targets. Your GPS watch doesn't know how your legs feel. If the plan says RPE 3–4, you should be able to hold a full conversation.
  5. Walk breaks on long runs are part of the plan. Taking a 1–2 minute walk every 3–4km on your long run is a proven strategy — it delays fatigue and lets you cover more distance with less injury risk.

Race Day Protocol

Your Half Marathon Race Day

Night Before

Eat a carb-rich dinner — pasta, rice, or potato-based. Nothing new or exotic. Hydrate well but don't overdrink. Prepare your kit: race number pinned, gels in pockets, shoes by the door. Early night — aim for 8+ hours of sleep.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before)

1–2g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, porridge, or a bagel with honey are reliable choices. Sip water or sports drink — don't overdrink. Use the toilet before leaving the house.

Arrival (60–90 min early)

Collect race number if needed. Use toilets (queues are long — go early). Walk the start/finish area. Give yourself time to settle nerves. Have a final small sip of water 30 min before start.

Warm-up (15 min before start)

10 min easy walk/jog → 4 × 20-sec strides. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles and a slightly elevated heart rate. Don't overdo it — save energy for the race.

Fuelling During the Race

Take your first gel at km 8 and your second at km 15 — the same strategy you practised in training. Drink at every aid station — small sips of water or sports drink. Don't skip aid stations in the first half because you feel good. You'll need the fluid later.

Pacing Strategy

Start 10–15 seconds per km slower than your goal pace for the first 3km. The adrenaline at the start will make goal pace feel easy — that's a trap. Most beginner half marathon runners go out too fast and pay for it after km 15. Settle into your rhythm by km 5. Run even splits or a slight negative split — the second half should feel harder but not slower. Don't go out with the front pack unless that's your level. Seed yourself honestly. Walk breaks are fine — they're a strategy you've trained with.

Post-Race

Walk for 10–15 min to cool down. Rehydrate. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 5–7 days of easy movement before any hard training. A half marathon takes longer to recover from than you expect — respect it.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

The 10% Rule

Never increase weekly running volume by more than 10%. This plan follows this rule strictly. If you repeat a week because life got in the way, that's fine — don't skip ahead to "catch up."

Easy Means Easy

Zone 2 is the most underused zone in amateur athletes. Most people train their easy days too hard and their hard days too easy. If you can't hold a full conversation during an easy run, slow down.

The Long Run Is Sacred

If you can only do one session in a week, make it the long run. It builds aerobic capacity, fat oxidation, musculoskeletal durability, and mental resilience. Everything else is secondary.

Walk Breaks Are Strategy

Taking planned walk breaks on your long run is a proven half marathon strategy. Walk 1–2 minutes every 3–4km. You'll cover the same distance with less fatigue and lower injury risk.

Practise Your Fuelling

Never try anything new on race day. Practise gels, drinks, and pre-run meals during training. Your stomach needs to adapt to processing fuel while running — that takes weeks, not days.

Sleep Is Your Best Training Tool

8–9 hours per night is the target. A bad night of sleep affects your next session more than a missed session does.

Don't Chase Pace

Consistency across months, not pace, is the measure of long run success. Your GPS watch doesn't know how your legs feel. Run by effort, not by numbers.

Soreness vs. Pain

Muscle soreness after longer runs is normal. Sharp, localised pain in joints (knees, ankles, shins) is a warning sign. Rest and reassess — never train through joint pain.

Warm Up, Always

Every running session starts with a walk warm-up. Cold muscles are injury-prone muscles. 5 minutes of walking before you run is the cheapest injury prevention available.

Rest Days Are Training Days

Adaptation happens during rest, not during the session. Skipping rest days doesn't make you fitter — it makes you injured.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Zone 1 (T1)
Recovery effort. Very easy. Barely above walking. Used for warm-ups, cool-downs, and active recovery.
Zone 2 (T2)
Aerobic base. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training happens. Builds fat oxidation and endurance.
Zone 3 (T3)
Tempo/threshold. Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. Used for tempo efforts in Weeks 9–11.
Conversational Pace
A pace where you can speak in full sentences without gasping. The gold standard for easy running.
Tempo Run
A sustained effort at RPE 5–6 (comfortably hard). Develops lactate threshold — the effort you can sustain for the full half marathon.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
Long Run
The longest run of the week, done at easy effort. The anchor session that builds aerobic capacity, fat oxidation, and mental resilience.
Walk Breaks
Planned walking intervals during long runs. A proven strategy that reduces fatigue and injury risk while covering the same distance.
Active Recovery
Very light movement (walking, easy stretching) to promote blood flow without adding training stress.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The ideal half marathon pacing strategy.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity) week over week. How fitness is built safely.
Taper
A planned reduction in training volume before race day. Maintains fitness while allowing the body to recover and arrive fresh.
Gel
A concentrated carbohydrate supplement taken during long runs and races to maintain energy. Practise during training — never try new gels on race day.
