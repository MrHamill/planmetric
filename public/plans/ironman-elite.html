An Ironman is the pinnacle of single-day endurance racing — 3.8 km swim, 180 km bike, 42.2 km run. Elite target: 9–11 hours. The swim takes 50–65 min for competitive athletes, the bike 4:30–5:30, and the marathon 3:00–4:00. The bike is the decisive discipline — pacing it correctly determines everything that follows. This plan assumes you have a strong aerobic base, race experience at 70.3 or above, and access to power meter, HR monitor, and pool. You are not here to finish. You are here to race.

Plan Focus

This plan is structured in four distinct phases. Weeks 1–4 (Base) sharpen your existing aerobic foundation with high-volume, low-intensity training across all three disciplines, establishing the platform for quality work. Weeks 5–8 (Build) introduce threshold intervals, VO2max sessions, sweet spot bike work, over-unders, and speed endurance training — the intensity that separates competitive from recreational. Weeks 9–12 (Peak) are the highest-volume block, featuring race simulations, 5.5–6 hour long rides with embedded tempo, 2.5+ hour runs with marathon-pace finishes, and full-dress rehearsal bricks. Weeks 13–16 (Taper) progressively strip volume while maintaining the intensity and sharpness that deliver race-day readiness. Recovery weeks at 4, 8, and 12 absorb the accumulated training load. Every session has specific targets. Every rest day is intentional.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the entire week before you start it. Some days include double sessions (AM swim + PM run). Plan logistics around pool access, bike routes, and fuelling needs.
  2. Train by power, pace, and HR — not feel alone. This plan prescribes FTP percentages for the bike, CSS-based paces for the swim, and zone-based paces for the run. Calibrate your zones with field tests before Week 1.
  3. Recovery weeks (4, 8, 12) are non-negotiable. Volume drops to 50–60% but intensity is maintained on select sessions. Adaptation happens during rest. Skipping recovery is how fit athletes get injured.
  4. Nutrition is the fourth discipline. From Week 3 onwards, practise race-day fuelling on every long ride. Target 80–120 g carbs/hr by peak phase. Train the gut systematically — start at 60 g/hr and build.
  5. Monitor recovery with HRV, sleep quality, and perceived readiness. If HRV is suppressed and perceived recovery is low, convert a quality session to Z1/Z2. Rigid plans without adjustment mechanisms underperform flexible ones.
  6. Log everything. Power, pace, HR, RPE, nutrition intake, sleep hours, body weight. The data from Weeks 1–8 calibrates your race-day execution strategy in Weeks 13–16.

Week 1 — Aerobic Sharpenexpand_more
Week 1 of 16

Establish training rhythm and volume baseline. All sessions are aerobic with technique emphasis. This week sets the foundation for the intensity that follows. Confirm your zones are current — if you haven’t tested FTP, CSS, or run threshold in the last 8 weeks, do so before starting.

MondaySwim — CSS Test + Aerobic
SWIM3200m

400m easy warm-up (mix freestyle/backstroke) → 4 × 50m drill (catch-up) with 10s rest → 400m time trial (max effort, record time) → 10 min easy recovery swim → 200m time trial (max effort, record time) → 4 × 200m at Aerobic pace with 20s rest → 200m cool-down.

Coach’s NoteUse the CSS formula: (400m time − 200m time) / 2 = your CSS per 100m. Example: 400m in 5:40 (340s), 200m in 2:40 (160s), CSS = (340−160)/2 = 90s = 1:30/100m. All swim intervals in this plan reference this number. Re-test at Week 8. Warm up thoroughly before the 400m TT — cold muscles produce inaccurate results.
TuesdayBike — Endurance + Cadence
BIKEZ22.5 hrs

15 min easy spin warm-up at Z1 → 2 hrs steady Z2 with cadence pyramid embedded at 60 min mark: 5 min @ 70 RPM → 5 min @ 80 RPM → 5 min @ 90 RPM → 5 min @ 100 RPM → 5 min @ 90 RPM (maintain constant power throughout) → resume Z2 steady → 15 min Z1 cool-down.

Coach’s NoteThe cadence pyramid identifies your optimal RPM range and trains smooth pedalling efficiency across the spectrum. Higher cadence (90–100 RPM) spares muscular glycogen and preserves run legs — critical for Ironman. Most age-groupers default to 70–75 RPM because it feels powerful, but it costs them on the marathon. Start building the 85–95 RPM habit now.
WednesdayRun — Easy Aerobic
RUNT260 min

10 min easy jog warm-up at T1 → 40 min steady at T2 (fully conversational, HR 65–75% max) → 4 × 20s strides at controlled fast pace with 40s easy jog between each → 10 min easy jog cool-down.

Coach’s NoteStrides are not sprints. They are smooth accelerations to roughly 5K effort held for 20 seconds, followed by easy recovery. They develop neuromuscular coordination and running economy without the fatigue cost of true interval work. Run on soft surfaces where possible to reduce impact loading.
ThursdaySwim — Aerobic Endurance
SWIM3400m

300m easy warm-up → 6 × 50m single-arm drill (alternate arms each 50m, 10s rest) → 8 × 200m at Aerobic pace (CSS + 10s/100m) with 15s rest → 4 × 100m at CSS pace with 10s rest → 300m easy cool-down.

Coach’s NoteSingle-arm drill isolates catch mechanics and forces bilateral breathing. Keep the non-working arm extended, pressing lightly down on the water surface. Rotate fully from the hips, not just the shoulders. The 200m aerobic reps are the bread and butter of Ironman swim fitness — sustainable pace, clean technique, consistent splits.
FridayBike — Easy Spin + Strength
BIKEZ1–Z275 min

15 min easy spin warm-up → 4 × 5 min low-cadence force reps at 60–65 RPM in Z3 power with 3 min Z1 easy spin between each → 20 min steady Z2 at 90+ RPM → 10 min Z1 cool-down.

Coach’s NoteLow-cadence force reps at Z3 power develop seated climbing strength and muscular force production. The key cue is full pedal circle engagement — pull through the bottom and push over the top. No stomping. These reps transfer directly to sustained power on hilly Ironman courses. Keep upper body quiet and relaxed throughout.
SaturdayBike — Long Endurance Ride
BIKEZ24 hrsKEY

15 min Z1 warm-up → 3.5 hrs steady Z2 on a rolling course, cadence 85–95 RPM → 15 min Z1 cool-down. Practise race nutrition: target 60 g carbs/hr using gels + sports drink. Drink 500–750 mL/hr. Note what works and what causes GI discomfort.

Coach’s NoteThe long ride is the anchor session of every week for the next 16 weeks. At elite level, this ride is not about surviving the distance — it is about dialling in cadence discipline, nutrition timing, aero position comfort, and pacing restraint. Resist chasing every hill. Hold Z2 on climbs even when it feels easy. The aerobic engine you build here determines your marathon off the bike.
SundayRun — Long Run
RUNT290 min

10 min easy jog warm-up → 70 min steady at T2 (conversational, HR <75% max) → 10 min easy jog cool-down. Run by HR or feel only — no GPS pace watching.

Coach’s NoteRunning on Sunday after a 4-hour bike on Saturday simulates the accumulated fatigue of race day. Your legs will feel heavy for the first 15 minutes — that is the training stimulus. Go slow. Time on feet, not pace, is the measure of success. This long run on pre-fatigued legs is more specific to Ironman than any fresh long run could be.

star Key Workout — Saturday Long Ride (4 hrs)

Four hours of disciplined Z2 riding with race nutrition practice. This single session builds more Ironman-specific fitness than any interval workout. Hold cadence at 85–95 RPM, stay in aero position for at least 50% of the ride, and consume 60 g carbs/hr. If you can only do one session this week, do this one.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 8–9 hours. Post-session: 0.3–0.5 g/kg carbs + 20–30 g protein within 60 min. Stretch hip flexors, calves, shoulders, and lats. Monitor morning HRV — if suppressed on any day, convert the next quality session to Z1/Z2.

Week 2 of 16

Volume increases across all three disciplines. Swim volume rises to 3600m. The long ride extends to 4.5 hrs. All intensity remains aerobic — the base phase is about building the engine, not testing it.

SWIM3600m

Coach’s NoteFingertip drag forces a high elbow recovery, which sets up a better catch underwater. On the descending 100s, the first rep should be comfortable aerobic, the fourth at CSS pace. TuesdayBike — Endurance + ILT
BIKEZ22.5 hrs

15 min Z1 warm-up → 6 × 30s single-leg pedalling each leg (Z1 resistance, 30s both legs between) → 1 hr 45 min steady Z2, cadence 85–95 RPM → 15 min Z1 cool-down.

Coach’s NoteIsolated leg training eliminates the dead spot in your pedal stroke. Most athletes initially struggle above 60 RPM single-leg — that is normal. The cue is “scrape mud off your shoe” at the bottom of the stroke and push over the top. Over 180 km, even a 2% improvement in pedalling efficiency saves significant energy for the marathon.
WednesdayRun — Aerobic + Strides
RUNT265 min

10 min easy jog warm-up → 45 min steady at T2 → 6 × 20s strides with 40s easy jog recovery → 10 min easy cool-down.

Coach’s NoteSix strides this week, up from four. These short accelerations prime the neuromuscular system without creating fatigue. Focus on quick, light turnover with a slight forward lean from the ankles. Arm drive should be relaxed but purposeful. These strides establish the running economy adaptations that compound over 16 weeks.
ThursdaySwim — Endurance Pyramid
SWIM3600m

300m easy warm-up → 4 × 50m kick with board (10s rest) → Pyramid: 100m → 200m → 400m → 200m → 100m all at CSS + 5s/100m with 15s rest between → 6 × 150m at Aerobic pace with 15s rest → 300m easy cool-down.

Coach’s NotePyramid sets prevent the pace drift and technique deterioration that plague long continuous swims. The 400m in the middle of the pyramid is where you practise holding form under moderate fatigue — exactly the skill you need at km 2.5 of the Ironman swim. Keep stroke count consistent across all reps. If your stroke count creeps up, you are losing efficiency.
FridayRun — Easy Recovery
RUNT140 min

40 min very easy jog at T1 (HR <65% max). Flat route. No GPS watching. This is active recovery — if you feel anything more than very easy, slow down.

Coach’s NoteRecovery runs promote blood flow and metabolic waste clearance without adding training stress. The purpose is recovery, not fitness. If your HRV is suppressed this morning, convert this to a complete rest day or a 30 min walk. Never sacrifice recovery for volume at elite level — the training load ahead demands it.
SaturdayBike — Long Endurance Ride
BIKEZ24.5 hrsKEY

15 min Z1 warm-up → 4 hrs steady Z2 on a rolling/hilly course, cadence 85–95 RPM → 15 min Z1 cool-down. Nutrition: increase to 70 g carbs/hr this week. Practise eating at the 45 min mark and every 30 min thereafter. Drink 600–750 mL/hr.

Coach’s NoteThe long ride adds 30 minutes this week. Choose a route with some hills to build climbing patience. On every climb, hold Z2 — do not surge into Z3. Practise aero position for increasing durations — target 60% of ride time in aero by Week 4.
SundayRun — Long Run
RUNT2100 min

10 min easy warm-up → 80 min steady at T2 → 10 min easy cool-down. Stay conversational. Practise run nutrition: take a gel at 45 min and 90 min with water.

Coach’s NoteThis is 100 minutes on pre-fatigued legs after a 4.5-hour bike yesterday. The heavy-leg sensation in the first 20 minutes is the exact stimulus that prepares you for the Ironman marathon. Do not chase pace. Run by feel or HR only. Practise your planned run nutrition — you need 60–90 g carbs/hr during the race marathon, so start training the gut now.

star Key Workout — Saturday Long Ride (4.5 hrs)

Volume steps up to 4.5 hours. Nutrition target rises to 70 g/hr. Increasing caloric intake systematically across the base phase trains gut absorption capacity — the gut can absorb ~60 g/hr glucose + ~30 g/hr fructose via separate transporters. By race day, you need 80–120 g/hr to sustain performance across 9–11 hours.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 8–9 hours. Anti-inflammatory nutrition: emphasise fatty fish, berries, leafy greens, nuts, olive oil. Reduce processed foods and alcohol. Recovery nutrition within 60 min of every session: carbs + protein. Monitor body weight — chronic weight loss during base phase indicates under-fuelling.

ThursdaySwim — Endurance + Sighting
SWIM3400m

300m easy warm-up → 4 × 50m sighting drill (sight every 8 strokes using a target at end of lane, 10s rest) → 3 × 400m at Aerobic pace (CSS + 8s/100m) with 20s rest → 4 × 100m at CSS with 10s rest → 300m cool-down.

Coach’s NoteSighting is the most valuable open water skill. Lift only your eyes above water — crocodile sighting — not your whole head. Full head lift drops your hips, increases drag, and wastes energy. In a 3.8 km Ironman swim, even a 1-degree sighting error adds ~25m of extra swimming. Practise the rhythm: swim 8 strokes, sight, swim 8 strokes. Ingrain it now so it is automatic on race day.

Build Phase— Weeks 5–8 — Threshold, VO2max, race-specific intensity
Week 5 — Threshold Introexpand_more
Week 5 of 16

The build phase begins. Threshold intervals arrive on the bike and run. Speed endurance training (SET) is introduced for NHE1 upregulation. Volume remains high but the character of training shifts — you are now building race-specific fitness, not just aerobic capacity.

MondaySwim — VO2max Intervals
SWIM3600m

300m easy warm-up → 4 × 50m catch-up drill (10s rest) → 8 × 100m at VO2max pace (CSS −5–8s/100m) with 20s rest → 4 × 200m at CSS pace with 15s rest → 4 × 50m easy backstroke → 300m cool-down.

Coach’s NoteVO2max swim intervals raise your aerobic ceiling. At CSS −5–8s/100m, you should be breathing every 2 strokes and unable to sustain this pace for more than 3–4 minutes continuously. The 20s rest is short enough to keep heart rate elevated but long enough to maintain pace quality. If pace drops more than 3s/100m from your target, the set is over — quality, not quantity.
TuesdayBike — Threshold Intervals
BIKEZ42.5 hrs

15 min Z1 warm-up → 5 min Z2 build → 2 × 20 min at Z4 (95–105% FTP), cadence 90 RPM, with 8 min Z1 easy spin between → 60 min Z2 steady → 15 min Z1 cool-down.

Coach’s NoteTwo 20-minute threshold blocks are the gold standard for raising FTP. Do not chase power from the first minute — let it build over 3–4 minutes. HR should stabilise at ~90–95% of threshold HR by the end of each interval. If you cannot complete the second interval within 5% of the first, the session is too hard — reduce power by 3–5%. These intervals teach your body to sustain race-pace effort for extended periods.
WednesdayRun — Threshold Intervals
RUNT370 min

15 min easy jog warm-up → 4 × 100m strides → 3 × 10 min at T3 (comfortably hard, ~threshold pace) with 3 min T1 jog between → 15 min easy cool-down.

Coach’s NoteThree 10-minute threshold reps total 30 minutes at T3 — the pace zone that correlates most strongly with marathon performance. T3 is roughly 15–20 sec/km slower than 10K race pace. You should be able to speak in short sentences but not hold a conversation. Even splits across all three reps indicate correct pacing. If the third rep is significantly slower, start the first rep more conservatively next time.
ThursdayAM: Swim — CSS Endurance / PM: Run — SET Sprints
SWIMRUNDouble Day

AM Swim (3400m): 300m warm-up → 6 × 300m at CSS pace with 15s rest → 4 × 100m descending (Aerobic → VO2max) with 15s rest → 200m cool-down. PM Run (35 min): 10 min easy jog warm-up → 8 × 30s maximal sprints with 3 min complete rest (walk) between each → 10 min easy jog cool-down.

Coach’s NoteSpeed endurance training (SET) begins. The 30-second maximal sprints with full recovery upregulate NHE1 protein expression in skeletal muscle — a membrane protein that clears acid (H+) from muscle cells during intense effort. Research shows 30–75% increase in NHE1 expression over 6 weeks of consistent SET. This translates to faster recovery between efforts and reduced fatigue at matched intensities. The recovery intervals (3 min) are long intentionally — near-complete recovery ensures maximal effort on each sprint. Insufficient recovery converts SET into submaximal threshold work, which produces a lesser adaptation.

Week 6 — VO2max + Open Waterexpand_more
Week 6 of 16

VO2max intervals arrive on the bike. Open water swimming begins. Volume continues climbing. This week has three quality sessions (Tuesday bike VO2max, Wednesday run intervals, Thursday swim VO2max) — manage recovery carefully between them.

MondaySwim — Open Water Session
SWIM3000m OW

First open water session of the plan. 10 min easy warm-up along the shoreline → 4 × 400m at Aerobic pace with 1 min treading water between → 4 × 100m at CSS with 30s rest (sight every 8 strokes) → 10 min easy cool-down with backstroke. Practise sighting to fixed landmarks. Wear race-day wetsuit if water temperature permits.

Coach’s NoteOpen water requires 15–20% more energy than pool swimming at the same pace — no walls, no black line, current, chop. Your first session should be conservative. Sight every 8–10 strokes using a fixed landmark (building, tree) not just buoys. If you feel anxious: roll to your back, float, breathe slowly, resume when calm. Always swim with a partner or group. Complete a minimum of 4–5 open water sessions before race day.
TuesdayBike — VO2max Intervals
BIKEZ52 hrs

15 min Z1 warm-up → 5 min Z2 build → 5 × 4 min at Z5 (110–120% FTP) with 4 min Z1 easy spin recovery between → 20 min Z2 steady → 15 min Z1 cool-down. Cadence: 95–100 RPM during VO2max intervals.

Coach’s NoteVO2max intervals raise your aerobic ceiling — the higher the ceiling, the lower the relative effort at race pace. Race Day Protocol

Ironman Elite — Race Execution Plan

Night Before

High-carb dinner: 8–10 g carbs/kg across the day. Pasta, rice, potatoes — familiar foods. Hydrate well but do not overdrink. Prepare all gear in transition order. Check tyre pressure (race-day pressure, not training pressure). Tape nutrition to top tube. Pack special needs bags. Set two alarms. Get to bed by 9 PM. The sleep 2 nights before matters more than tonight — if nerves affect sleep, that is normal and will not hurt performance.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before start)

Pre-race meal: 1–2 g carbs/kg body weight. Toast with jam, porridge, banana, or bagel with honey. Low fibre, low fat. Sip water — do not overdrink. Caffeine: 3–6 mg/kg (e.g. 75 kg = 225–450 mg caffeine) 45–60 min before start. Take a gel 15–20 min before the swim start. Use the toilets. Arrive 60–90 min early. Set up transition. Walk T1 and T2 paths. Identify sighting landmarks for the swim. Put on wetsuit 15–20 min before start.

Warm-Up (10–15 min before)

Swim 200–400m easy to settle breathing and acclimatise to water temperature. Include 4 × 20s at CSS to prime the cardiovascular system. If no warm-up swim is allowed, splash cold water on face and neck. Arm swings, light jogging. 10 slow nasal inhales. Visualise the first 200m: smooth, controlled, patient.

Swim — 3.8 km

Draft, negative split, exit strong. Position yourself strategically at the start — near the front if you can sustain the pace, slightly wide if you want cleaner water. First 200m: control breathing, settle rhythm. Do not sprint. Settle onto the feet of a faster swimmer and draft (saves 20–30% energy). Sight every 10 strokes using a fixed landmark. Breathe bilaterally. Negative split: second half faster than first. Final 400m: increase effort, sight frequently, build into a strong T1 exit. Target: 55–65 min. Begin unzipping wetsuit as soon as you can stand.

T1 — Swim to Bike

Run out of the water. Wetsuit down to waist while jogging. At your rack: arms out, suit to ankles, step out. Helmet on (buckle!), sunglasses, shoes. Grab bike. Run to mount line. Smooth, fast, rehearsed. Target: under 3 min.

Bike — 180 km

Ride Z2–Z3 only. Resist going harder. Target: 70–78% FTP. Cadence: 85–95 RPM. The bike is where most competitive athletes lose their race by going too hard. Begin eating within the first 15 min. Nutrition: 80–120 g carbs/hr using gels, bars, and sports drink (dual-transport formula: 2:1 glucose:fructose). Drink 600–750 mL/hr. Electrolytes: 500–700 mg sodium/hr, adjusted for conditions. Hold steady on climbs — do not surge. Maintain legal draft distance. Aero position as much as possible. At km 120, you will feel strong and want to push — resist this urge. Save it for the marathon. Check yourself every 30 min: power, cadence, HR, nutrition intake. Target: 4:30–5:30.

T2 — Bike to Run

Dismount before the line. Rack bike, helmet off, run shoes on, race belt, hat/visor. Take a gel immediately. Go. Target: under 2 min.

Run — 42.2 km (Marathon)

Even split or negative split. Walk zero aid stations. The first 5 km will feel strange — brick legs are normal. Start at T2 pace (slightly slower than marathon target). By km 5, settle into marathon pace (T3). Hold this pace through km 30. Nutrition: 60–90 g carbs/hr (gel every 30 min + water/sports drink at aid stations). Sodium: continue 500–700 mg/hr. Cola/caffeine strategy from km 30+: flat cola provides caffeine + sugar when the gut becomes resistant to gels. The race starts at km 30 of the marathon — this is where competitors separate from participants. Km 30–42: maintain pace, maintain nutrition, use mental cues (“I did not come here to walk”, “one km at a time”). Negative split the final 5 km if possible. Attack the last 2 km. Finish line is yours. Target: 3:00–3:45.

Post-Race

Walk for 15 min. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 3–4 weeks of easy movement before any structured training. No quality sessions for at least 2 weeks. Celebrate what you accomplished. You are an Ironman.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
FTP (Functional Threshold Power)
The highest power output you can sustain for approximately 1 hour on the bike. The gold standard for setting cycling training zones. Measured via a 20-minute test (95% of 20-min avg power = FTP).
CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
Your sustainable threshold pace in the pool. Calculated from a 400m + 200m time trial: (400m time − 200m time) / 2 = CSS per 100m. The basis for all swim training zones.
Sweet Spot
88–93% of FTP. Sits just below threshold. Provides the highest adaptation stimulus per unit of fatigue — the most efficient power zone for Ironman bike training.
Over-Unders
Intervals alternating above and below threshold (e.g. 2 min at 110% FTP / 3 min at 90% FTP). Trains the body to clear lactate during sustained race efforts and handle surges.
SET (Speed Endurance Training)
8–12 × 30s maximal sprints with 2–4 min complete rest. Upregulates NHE1 protein expression by 30–75% over 6 weeks, improving acid clearance from muscle cells and enhancing repeat-effort recovery.
NHE1
Sodium-Hydrogen Exchanger Isoform 1. A membrane protein that clears acid (H+) from muscle cells during and after intense exercise. Better NHE1 expression = faster recovery between efforts and reduced fatigue.
Brick Session
A workout combining two disciplines back-to-back, typically bike then run. Trains the neuromuscular transition and the “brick legs” sensation. Essential for Ironman preparation.
T1 / T2 (Transitions)
T1: swim to bike. T2: bike to run. The “fourth discipline” of triathlon. Practised transitions save minutes and reduce stress on race day.
Negative Split
Completing the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The ideal pacing strategy for all three disciplines in Ironman.
VO2max
Maximum rate of oxygen consumption during maximal exercise. Trained via 3–5 min intervals at 110–120% FTP (bike) or 5K race pace (run). Raising VO2max lowers relative effort at race pace.
Gut Training
Progressively increasing carbohydrate intake during training to improve the gut’s absorption capacity. Elite Ironman athletes target 80–120 g carbs/hr on the bike — the gut must be trained to absorb this amount.
Cadence
Pedal revolutions per minute (RPM). For Ironman, 85–95 RPM is optimal. Higher cadence spares muscular glycogen and preserves legs for the marathon. Lower cadence increases muscular fatigue.
Taper
Planned reduction in training volume before race day. Volume drops 20–50% but intensity is maintained. Allows the body to absorb accumulated training and arrive race-ready.
Carb-Loading
Increasing daily carbohydrate intake to 8–10 g/kg body weight in the 2–3 days before an Ironman. Maximises muscle glycogen stores for race-day energy availability.
Drafting (Swim)
Swimming directly behind a faster swimmer (30–50 cm from their feet) saves ~20–30% energy expenditure. Side-drafting off the hip saves ~15%. A critical race strategy for the 3.8 km swim.
Sighting
Lifting only the eyes (“crocodile sighting”) every 8–10 strokes in open water to navigate. Full head lift drops hips and increases drag. A 1-degree sighting error over 3.8 km adds ~65m of extra swimming.
Coach Tips

The Bike Determines the Marathon

Ironman is not a bike race followed by a run. It is a run that happens to have a 180 km warm-up. Ride 70–78% FTP. Save your legs. The athlete who wins km 30–42.2 of the marathon is the athlete who was disciplined on the bike.

Nutrition Is the Fourth Discipline

80–120 g carbs/hr on the bike. 60–90 g/hr on the run. 500–700 mg sodium/hr. These numbers are not optional — they are the difference between racing and surviving. Train the gut. Test every product. Nothing new on race day.

Race Starts at km 30

The first 30 km of the marathon is pacing discipline. The real race begins when everyone around you is slowing down and you are not. The athlete who holds pace from km 30 to the finish has trained specifically for this moment — that is what the tempo finishes on long runs prepared you for.

Protect the Taper

No fitness is gained in the final 2–3 weeks — only lost through overtraining or injury. Taper anxiety is normal. You will feel sluggish, restless, and doubtful. This is your body absorbing 13 weeks of training. Trust the process.

Sleep Is Non-Negotiable

8–9 hours every night during training. Sleep is the highest-ROI recovery tool available. No supplement, ice bath, or compression garment comes close. HRV is suppressed with poor sleep — one bad night can derail two days of training.

Data-Driven, Not Ego-Driven

Train to improve, not to prove fitness in training. Record everything: power, pace, HR, RPE, nutrition, sleep. The data from training calibrates race-day execution. A perfect race is built on 16 weeks of data, not race-day heroics.
