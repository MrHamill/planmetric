Plan Focus

You can finish an Olympic triathlon. This plan teaches you to race one. Over 12 weeks you’ll build threshold capacity in the pool with CSS intervals, develop sustainable bike power through tempo and threshold blocks, and sharpen your run with race-pace segments and structured strides. Brick sessions from Week 5 train your body to run fast on tired legs. Recovery weeks at Weeks 4 and 8 let adaptation happen. Weeks 9–10 are race-specific — full dress rehearsals with transition practice. The taper delivers you to the start line fresh, sharp, and ready to execute a pacing plan, not just survive. 80% of training remains aerobic. The other 20% is where you get faster.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Sessions are ordered intentionally — hard sessions are followed by easy sessions or rest. Moving sessions around disrupts the training stimulus.
  2. Respect the intensities. When the plan says RPE 3–4, it means easy. When it says RPE 6–7, it means uncomfortable. The temptation to make easy sessions moderate is the most common mistake intermediate athletes make — it erodes the quality of your hard sessions.
  3. Complete every recovery week as written. Weeks 4 and 8 are deliberately reduced. Adaptation happens during rest, not during the session. Skipping recovery leads to plateau, not progress.
  4. Practise race-day nutrition on every long ride and brick session. Your fuelling plan should be rehearsed 6–8 times before race day. Aim for 40–60g carbs per hour on the bike.
  5. Transitions are free speed. From Week 5, every brick session includes a deliberate T2 rehearsal. By race day, the sequence should be automatic. Lay out your gear in the same order every time.

Week 1 — Base Iexpand_more
Week 1 of 12

Establish your weekly rhythm across all three disciplines. Every session is aerobic with controlled technique focus. This week is about calibrating your effort levels and settling into the schedule — not testing fitness.

MondayRest
REST

Complete rest.

TuesdaySwim — Technique + Aerobic
SWIMRPE 3–42000m

300m easy warm-up (mix of strokes) → 4 × 50m drill (catch-up, fingertip drag) with 10s rest → 6 × 150m freestyle at Aerobic zone with 15s rest → 4 × 50m easy kick → 200m cool-down.

Coach's NoteThis is a rhythm session. Focus on a high elbow catch, body rotation, and bilateral breathing. Don’t chase the clock — chase form. Count your strokes per 25m and try to lower them by 1–2 over the coming weeks.
WednesdayBike — Easy Endurance
BIKERPE 3–470 min

70 min easy Z2 spinning. Flat to rolling terrain. Cadence 85–95 RPM. Stay relaxed in the shoulders and grip. Drink every 15 min.

ThursdayRun — Easy Run + Strides
RUNRPE 3–440 min

5 min walk warm-up → 28 min easy run at T2 → 4 × 20-sec strides with 40-sec walk recovery → 3 min walk cool-down.

Coach's NoteStrides are controlled accelerations to about 85–90% effort, not sprints. Focus on quick turnover and relaxed shoulders. They prime your neuromuscular system without adding fatigue.
FridaySwim — CSS Introduction
SWIMRPE 3–52200m

300m easy warm-up → 4 × 50m build (easy to CSS pace) with 10s rest → 6 × 100m at CSS with 15s rest → 4 × 100m Aerobic with 20s rest → 200m easy cool-down.

Coach's NoteCSS pace is your sustainable threshold — comfortably hard, breathing every 3 strokes. If you don’t know your CSS pace, swim a 400m time trial this week and use that average pace per 100m as your starting point.
SaturdayBike — Long Ride
BIKERPE 3–490 minKEY

90 min Z2 ride. Rolling terrain if available. Practise drinking from your bottle every 15 min and eating a gel or bar at the 45 min mark. Cadence 80–90 RPM on flats.

Coach's NoteThe long ride is the anchor session of every week. It builds your aerobic engine more than any other session. If you can only do one workout in a week, do the long ride.
SundayRun — Long Run
RUNRPE 3–450 min

5 min walk warm-up → 40 min easy run at T2 → 5 min walk cool-down. Conversational pace throughout. If your legs are heavy from Saturday, slow down — don’t push.

star Key Workout — Saturday Long Ride

90 minutes of easy Z2 cycling. The foundation of your race fitness. Finish feeling like you could have done another 30 minutes — that means you rode it perfectly.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 7–9 hours. Stretch hip flexors, quads, calves, and shoulders for 5–10 minutes after each session. Hydrate throughout the day — aim for pale yellow urine.

psychology Weekly Check-In

“Did I complete all six sessions? Were my easy sessions genuinely easy? Can I identify my CSS pace in the pool? If not, schedule a 400m time trial this week.”

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

Polarised Training Works

80% easy, 20% hard. The most common mistake intermediate athletes make is turning easy sessions into moderate sessions. This blunts the quality of your hard sessions and leads to plateau. Easy means easy. Hard means hard. Nothing in between.

The Long Ride Is King

The Saturday long ride or brick is the anchor of this plan. If you can only do one session in a week, do the long ride. It builds the aerobic engine that carries you through all three legs on race day.

Race Your Plan, Not the Pack

Have a pacing strategy for each discipline: swim at CSS, bike at Z3–Z4, run at T3. Write it down. Tape it to your top tube. The athletes who execute a plan outperform those who race by feel.

Nutrition Is a Discipline

Practise your race-day nutrition on every long ride and brick. Aim for 40–60g carbs per hour on the bike. Test your gels, bars, and hydration in training — never try something new on race day.

Transitions Are Free Speed

A smooth T1 and T2 can save 2–5 minutes versus a flustered one. Practise the sequence: lay out gear in order, rehearse the movements, know exactly what goes on in what order.

CSS Is Your Swim Compass

Critical Swim Speed is the fastest pace you can sustain for about 20 minutes. It’s your threshold in the water. All swim intervals in this plan are calibrated relative to CSS. Retest every 4 weeks.

Open Water Confidence

Get at least 4–5 open water sessions before race day. Practise sighting, race starts, and T1 exits. Swim with a buddy. The pool builds fitness; open water builds race readiness.

Brick Sessions Build Race Legs

The jelly-leg feeling fades with practice. Every brick session teaches your body and mind what to expect on race day. The T2 transition should feel automatic by Week 10.

Sleep Is Your Best Recovery Tool

7–9 hours per night. A bad night of sleep affects your next session more than a missed session does. Prioritise sleep over an extra training session every time.

Negative Split Everything

Start the swim conservative, build through the bike, finish the run strong. The athlete who negative splits outperforms the athlete who goes out hard and fades. Always have something left.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Brick Session
A workout combining two disciplines back-to-back, typically bike then run. Trains the body to perform the second discipline on fatigued legs — simulating race conditions.
T1 (Transition 1)
The transition from swim to bike. Includes exiting the water, removing wetsuit, putting on helmet and bike shoes, and mounting the bike.
T2 (Transition 2)
The transition from bike to run. Includes dismounting, racking the bike, removing helmet, changing to run shoes, and heading out on the run course.
CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
Your sustainable threshold pace in the pool — the fastest pace you can hold for about 20 minutes. Equivalent to lactate threshold for swimming.
Cadence
Pedal revolutions per minute (RPM) on the bike. For endurance cycling, aim for 80–95 RPM.
Sighting
Lifting your head briefly while swimming to navigate in open water. Look forward every 8–10 strokes to stay on course.
Threshold (Z4 / T3–T4)
The intensity you can sustain for 20–40 minutes. Hard but controlled. The effort you race at for Olympic distance bike and run.
Tempo (Z3 / T3)
Comfortably hard. Sustainable for 40–60 minutes. Short sentences only. The middle ground between easy and threshold.
Negative Split
Completing the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The ideal pacing strategy for all three legs of a triathlon.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace during a run. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
Polarised Training
An intensity distribution where ~80% of training is easy (Z1–Z2) and ~20% is hard (Z4+). Minimal time in the moderate “grey zone” (Z3). Produces the best endurance adaptations.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity) week over week. How fitness is built safely. Max ~10% increase per week.
Active Recovery
Very light movement (walking, easy swimming, easy spinning) to promote blood flow without adding training stress.
Aero Position
A low, aerodynamic position on the bike. Reduces wind resistance. Practise in training — don’t try it for the first time on race day.
VO2max Intervals
Short, hard efforts (3–8 minutes) at RPE 7–8 that develop maximum aerobic capacity. Used sparingly in this plan for top-end speed development.
Race Day Protocol

Your Olympic Triathlon Race Plan

Night Before

Eat a normal meal with carbohydrates. Don’t experiment. Hydrate well. Prepare all gear in transition order. Check tyre pressure. Pin race number to race belt. Set two alarms. Get to bed early — you may not sleep well, and that’s fine. One bad night doesn’t affect performance.

Race Morning (3 hrs before start)

Pre-race meal: 1–2g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods only — toast with jam, banana, porridge. Sip water, don’t overdrink. Take a gel 15 min before swim start.

Arrival (60–90 min early)

Set up transition: bike racked, helmet on bars (open and ready), shoes positioned, run gear laid out. Walk the transition area — know your entry/exit for T1 and T2. Use the toilets. Wetsuit on 15–20 min before start.

Warm-Up (10–15 min before)

Swim 200–400m easy if warm-up area is open. Get your face in the water, settle your breathing, find your rhythm. If no swim warm-up, do arm swings and light jogging.

Swim — 1.5 km

Start at CSS pace. Don’t sprint the first 200m. Find clear water, settle into rhythm by 200m. Sight every 8–10 strokes. Draft off a slightly faster swimmer if possible — legal and efficient. Build effort in the last 300m to exit strong.

T1 — Swim to Bike

Run out of the water. Unzip wetsuit at waist while jogging. At your spot: pull suit to ankles, step out. Helmet on (buckle it!), sunglasses, bike shoes. Grab bike, run to mount line, mount and go. You’ve rehearsed this — trust the sequence.

Bike — 40 km

Ride at Z3–Z4. Start conservative (Z3 for first 10 km), build to Z3–Z4. Eat within the first 15 min: 40–60g carbs/hr. Drink every 15 min. Hold steady on climbs — don’t surge. Maintain legal drafting distance. Save your legs for the run — the race is won in the last 5 km.

T2 — Bike to Run

Dismount before the line. Rack bike, remove helmet, run shoes on, race belt, go. Smooth and automatic — you’ve done this dozens of times in training.

Run — 10 km

First 2 km: settle. Start at T2–T3, let the jelly legs pass. By KM 3, settle into T3 race pace. Take water or sports drink at every aid station. KM 7–8: if you feel good, build. Last KM: give everything you have. Negative split the run — that’s how you race smart.

Post-Race

Walk 10 min. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. 5–7 days easy before structured training. Review your race plan vs. execution — that analysis fuels your next block.
