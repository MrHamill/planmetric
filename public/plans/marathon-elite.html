timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–5

Foundation phase focused on building your aerobic engine through easy-paced running. All sessions should be conversational, emphasizing consistency and gradual volume increases. This is where you develop the metabolic efficiency needed for marathon success.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 6–11

Progressive phase introducing marathon-specific workouts including tempo runs, long runs at race effort, and threshold training. You'll practice race pacing and nutrition strategies while continuing to build weekly volume toward peak levels.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 12–14

Highest intensity phase featuring race simulations, marathon-pace segments, and peak weekly volume. This is where you fine-tune race tactics, test your fuelling strategy, and build confidence in your goal pace through specific training stimuli.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 15–16

Recovery phase allowing your body to supercompensate from previous training stress. Volume drops significantly while maintaining movement quality through short, easy runs. Mental preparation and race logistics become the primary focus.

flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb breakfast (60-80g carbs) - oatmeal with banana and honey, or toast with jam. Include familiar foods only.
2 hours before: Finish eating and drinking. Begin mental preparation and gear check.
60-90 minutes before: Arrive at race venue, collect bib, final gear organization.
30-45 minutes before: Begin warm-up - 10-15 minutes easy jogging with 3-4 short accelerations.
15 minutes before: Final bathroom stop, move to start area, activate positive self-talk.
5 minutes before: Deep breathing, final visualization, trust your preparation.
Your Race Strategy

Pacing Strategy

0-10km: Start conservatively at 5:05-5:10/km pace. It should feel easy - resist the temptation to go with faster runners. Focus on smooth rhythm and relaxed form.

10-21km: Settle into marathon pace (5:00/km). Monitor effort level rather than obsessing over splits. Fuel every 30-35 minutes starting around 60 minutes.

21-32km: Maintain marathon pace while managing any discomfort. Use chunking strategy - focus on reaching the next aid station. This is where mental toughness training pays off.

32-42km: If feeling strong, gradually increase effort to 4:55-4:50/km pace. If struggling, focus on maintaining form and just moving forward. Remember: every step is closer to the finish.

Mental Strategy

Mental Race Plan

Chunking: Break the race into four segments of approximately 10.5km each. Focus only on completing the current segment.

Mantras: 'Strong and steady', 'I've trained for this', 'Smooth is fast'. Rehearse these during training runs.

Transition Reset (3 Rs): At each 10km marker: Rest (let go of the previous section), Regroup (assess how you feel), Refocus (concentrate on the next segment).

Discomfort Management: Expect discomfort around 30km - this is normal. Use positive self-talk: 'This is where my training kicks in' rather than 'I can't do this'.

Crowd Energy: Use spectator support to boost energy, but don't let it push you off your pacing plan in the early stages.

school Glossary

Z1-Z2 (Easy Zone)

Conversational pace where you can speak in full sentences. Heart rate typically 65-75% of maximum.

Marathon Pace

Your goal race pace - sustainable for the full 42.2km distance. Should feel 'comfortably hard' during training.

Negative Split

Running strategy where the second half of the race is faster than the first half.

Taper

Period of reduced training volume before a race to allow the body to recover and supercompensate.

Supercompensation

The process where your body adapts to training stress by becoming stronger than before the training began.

Cadence

Steps per minute - optimal running cadence is typically 170-180 spm for injury prevention and efficiency.

Fuelling

Consuming carbohydrates during exercise to maintain energy levels. Target 60-80g per hour for marathon distance.

Chunking

Mental strategy of breaking a long race into smaller, manageable segments to reduce psychological overwhelm.

Threshold Pace

The fastest pace you can maintain for approximately 60 minutes - slightly faster than marathon pace.

VO2 Max

Maximum rate of oxygen consumption during exercise - a key measure of aerobic fitness capacity.

Lactate Threshold

The exercise intensity at which lactate begins to accumulate in the blood faster than it can be cleared.

Brick Session

Training session combining two disciplines back-to-back, typically bike-to-run, to practice race transitions.

RPE

Rate of Perceived Exertion - subjective scale (1-10) for measuring exercise intensity based on how hard it feels.

HRV

Heart Rate Variability - measure of recovery and readiness to train based on beat-to-beat heart rate variation.

Bonking/Hitting the Wall

Sudden energy depletion typically around 30-32km in marathon due to glycogen depletion.

psychology Coach's Tips

schedule

Perfect Your Pre-Race Routine

Practice your race morning routine during long training runs. Same breakfast, same timing, same warm-up. Consistency reduces anxiety and ensures nothing is left to chance on race day.

local_drink

Start Fuelling Early

Begin consuming carbohydrates within the first 60 minutes of your marathon, not when you start feeling tired. Aim for 60-80g per hour using familiar products you've tested in training.

psychology

Master Mental Chunking

Break your marathon into smaller segments - aim for the next aid station or kilometer marker rather than thinking about the full distance. This makes the challenge feel more manageable.

speed

Trust Your Taper

Legs feeling heavy during taper week is completely normal - it's your body supercompensating. Resist the urge to 'test' your fitness with hard efforts. Your marathon fitness is already built.

trending_down

Start Conservatively

Run the first 10km 5-10 seconds per kilometer slower than goal pace. The energy you save early will be invaluable in the final 10km when it really counts.

self_improvement

Practice Positive Self-Talk

Develop and rehearse mantras during training: 'Strong and steady', 'I've trained for this', 'Smooth is fast'. Positive internal dialogue significantly improves performance under stress.

hotel

Plan Your Carb Loading

Focus on familiar high-carb foods in the 2-3 days before your race. Avoid trying new foods or drastically changing your diet. Aim for 8-12g of carbs per kg of body weight daily.

directions_run

Maintain Efficient Form

Focus on 175-180 steps per minute cadence, relaxed shoulders, and avoiding overstriding. Good form becomes even more critical as fatigue sets in during the later stages of your marathon.
