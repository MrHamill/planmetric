Plan Focus

This plan is built for the athlete who already has a marathon under their belt and wants to race the distance properly. You know what 35km feels like. Now you learn what a negative split feels like. The programme peaks at ~95km per week across three structured blocks — base, build, and race-specific — with recovery weeks every fourth week. VO2max intervals sharpen the top end. Threshold blocks extend your lactate ceiling. Marathon-pace segments in the long run teach your body to hold rhythm under fatigue. Strength and plyometric sessions are integrated, not optional. The race begins at km 30. Everything before that is controlled patience.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the entire week before you start it. Map the key sessions to your best training windows — VO2max on fresh legs, long run on a day where you have time and mental space.
  2. Respect the recovery weeks. Weeks 4 and 8 are deliberate deloads at ~60% volume. Skipping them is the fastest route to overtraining syndrome. Adaptation happens during rest.
  3. Run by zone and feel. RPE guides are provided for every session. If your heart rate drifts above zone in a threshold session and RPE is manageable, trust RPE. If RPE is high and pace is slow, you need more recovery — not more effort.
  4. Practise your race-day fuelling in every long run from Week 5 onward. Gels, fluids, sodium — nothing new on race day. Your gut is trainable. Train it.
  5. Strength and plyometric sessions are noted on Mondays. These are not optional at this level — they are injury prevention and force production. 30–40 minutes of targeted work.

Week 1 — Base Iexpand_more
Week 1 of 14

Establish rhythm. ~80km this week. The focus is volume at aerobic intensity with your first VO2max session to set a physiological baseline. Strength work begins. No heroics.

MondayEasy Run + Strength
RPE 3–410 km

10km easy → 30–40 min strength & plyometrics (single-leg squats, calf raises, box jumps, core).

Coach's NoteStrength is non-negotiable at this level. Target posterior chain, single-leg stability, and hip strength. Plyometrics develop ground-contact stiffness — critical for marathon economy.
TuesdayVO2max Intervals
RPE 7–814 kmKEY

3km warm-up → 6 × 1km at T4 (3:30–3:50/km effort) with 90s jog recovery → 3km cool-down.

Coach's NoteFirst VO2max session. Start conservative — the goal is even splits across all six reps, not a fast first rep and a death march. If you fade more than 5s/km by rep 5, the opening pace was too aggressive.
WednesdayEasy Run
RPE 310 km

10km easy. Genuinely easy. Recovery from yesterday's intervals.

ThursdayThreshold Blocks
RPE 5–614 kmKEY

3km warm-up → 3 × 10min at T3 (4:00–4:15/km effort) with 3min jog recovery → 3km cool-down.

Coach's NoteThreshold work extends your lactate ceiling. The effort should be comfortably hard — sustainable but not comfortable. You should be able to speak in short sentences, no more.
FridayRest
REST

Complete rest. Sleep. Eat. Hydrate. The body builds itself today.

SaturdayParkrun + Strides
RPE 6–710 km

2km warm-up → 5km parkrun (controlled race effort, not all-out) → 3km cool-down including 4 × 20s strides.

Coach's NoteParkrun is a training tool, not a test. Run it at 90–95% effort. Practise pacing discipline — the first km should feel too easy.
SundayLong Run
RPE 3–422 kmKEY

22km steady aerobic. Start easy (RPE 3), finish at RPE 4. No surges, no racing. This is pure aerobic volume.

Coach's NoteThe long run is the anchor of every week. At this stage it is pure aerobic — marathon-pace segments begin in the Build block. Fuel with water every 20 min. Begin practising gel timing if you haven't already.

star Key Workouts — Tuesday VO2max & Thursday Threshold

Two quality sessions establish the training rhythm you'll hold for 14 weeks. Tuesday develops the top end. Thursday extends the ceiling. Sunday anchors the volume. Everything else is recovery and support.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 8–9 hours. Post-session nutrition within 30 minutes — 1g/kg carbs + 0.3g/kg protein. Foam roll quads, ITB, and calves for 10 min on quality days. Compression garments overnight if you have them.

psychology Weekly Check-In

"Did I hit both quality sessions at the prescribed effort without fading? If yes, the base is ready for progressive loading. If not, the starting volume may be too high."

Race Day Protocol

Your Marathon Race Day Execution Plan

48 Hours Before — Carb Loading

10g carbs per kg of body weight per day. Rice, pasta, bread, potatoes, white rice, sports drink. Reduce fibre and fat. Spread across 5–6 meals. Hydrate aggressively — urine should be pale yellow. Reduce all non-essential activity. Sleep 8–9 hours.

Race Morning (3–4 Hours Before Start)

2–3g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam and honey, white rice, banana, or porridge are reliable choices. Sip 500–750ml water in the 2 hours before start. Stop drinking 30 min before gun to allow for a final bathroom stop.

Warm-Up (20–30 Min Before Start)

10–15 min easy jog → 4 × 20s strides at marathon pace → dynamic stretches (leg swings, hip circles, ankle circles). Arrive at the start line with warm muscles, elevated heart rate, and a clear head. Seed yourself honestly in the corral.

Fuelling Strategy — 60–90g Carbs/Hr

Begin fuelling from km 5. Gel or sports drink every 25–30 min. Target 60–90g carbs per hour (the upper range for trained athletes with practised gut tolerance). Use the same products you trained with — nothing new on race day. Carry your own gels. Don't rely on aid station nutrition unless you've tested it.

Hydration — Every 15–20 Min

Drink to thirst, not to a fixed schedule — but use every aid station. Small sips of water or electrolyte drink every 15–20 min. Target 500–700mg sodium per hour, especially in warm conditions. Sodium tablets or electrolyte capsules are reliable. Hyponatremia (overdrinking) is more dangerous than mild dehydration.

Pacing Strategy — Even Splits or Negative Split

Start at goal pace. Not faster. KM 1–10: controlled and easy, bank nothing. KM 10–25: sustainable rhythm, hold pace, settle into the pattern. KM 25–30: the patience phase — you will feel good and want to push. Don't. KM 30–42: this is where you race. The race begins at km 30. Maintain form, maintain cadence, maintain fuelling. If you have anything left at km 38, use it. Negative split — the second half faster than the first — is the mark of a perfectly executed marathon.

Post-Race

Walk for 10–15 min. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Compression garments for 12+ hours. Cold water immersion within 2 hours if available. No running for 7 days minimum. Full recovery takes 2–4 weeks.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

The 80/20 Rule

80% of your volume should be at T1–T2 intensity. 20% at T3+. This plan follows that distribution. If your easy runs feel hard, you're running them too fast. Slow down. The easy runs are the engine of your marathon.

Fuelling Is a Skill

Your gut is trainable. Practise your race-day fuelling protocol in every long run from Week 5 onward. 60–90g carbs/hr is achievable with progressive gut training. Start at 40g/hr and increase by 10g/hr each week.

Sleep Is Performance

8–9 hours per night is the target. Sleep debt accumulates and compounds. One bad night costs you two days of recovery. Consistent sleep hygiene is the highest-ROI recovery tool available.

The Long Run Is Non-Negotiable

Sunday's long run is the most important session of the week. If you can only do one session, do this one. The physiological adaptations — fat oxidation, glycogen storage, musculoskeletal durability — come from sustained time on feet.

Strength Is Injury Prevention

Monday strength sessions prevent the injuries that derail marathon campaigns. Single-leg work, hip stability, calf strength, and core control are non-negotiable at 80–95km/week.

Recovery Weeks Are Sacred

Weeks 4 and 8 are ~60% volume for a reason. Skipping them leads to overtraining, illness, and injury. The athletes who arrive at the start line healthy are the ones who respected recovery.

Negative Split Mentality

Every MP long run, every threshold session, every parkrun — practise running the second half faster than the first. The negative split is a skill. It requires patience, discipline, and trust in your fitness. Practise it until it's automatic.

Race Day: Nothing New

Shoes, socks, shorts, gels, hydration, breakfast, warm-up — everything on race day should be tested in training. The marathon is not the place to experiment. Familiarity reduces variables. Reduced variables reduce risk.

The Race Begins at KM 30

Everything before km 30 is controlled patience. The first 30km should feel sustainable and rhythmic. The last 12.2km is where you race. Train for it. Prepare for it. When it arrives, you'll be ready.

Monitor, Don't Obsess

Track HRV, resting heart rate, sleep quality, and mood. If multiple markers trend downward for 3+ days, take an extra rest day. One missed session costs nothing. Overtraining costs weeks.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
T1 (Recovery)
Very easy effort. Shuffle pace. Full conversation. Used for warm-ups, cool-downs, and active recovery.
T2 (Aerobic Base)
Easy and conversational. Sustainable for 2+ hours. Where 80% of your training happens. Builds fat oxidation, mitochondrial density, and aerobic capacity.
T3 (Tempo/Threshold)
Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. Sustainable for 40–60 min in trained athletes. Develops lactate clearance and raises the anaerobic threshold.
T4 (VO2max)
Hard. Laboured breathing. 3–8 min per rep. Develops maximal oxygen uptake — the ceiling on your aerobic performance.
Marathon Pace (MP)
The pace you intend to hold for 42.2km. Sits at the T2/T3 boundary. Should feel controlled and sustainable in training — but hard to hold beyond km 30 on race day.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a race faster than the first half. The optimal marathon pacing strategy. Requires disciplined restraint in the first half.
VO2max Intervals
Repeated efforts at near-maximal aerobic intensity (T4) with recovery jogs between. Develops the oxygen transport system.
Threshold Blocks
Sustained efforts at lactate threshold (T3) for 10–15 min with short recovery. Raises the intensity at which lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace. Develops running form, neuromuscular coordination, and leg speed. Not sprints.
Periodisation
The structured division of training into blocks (base, build, race-specific, taper) with progressive overload and planned recovery.
Carb Loading
Increasing carbohydrate intake to 10g/kg/day for 48 hours before race day. Maximises muscle glycogen stores for endurance performance.
Plyometrics
Explosive jumping exercises that develop ground-contact stiffness and running economy. Integrated into Monday strength sessions.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (volume, intensity, density) week over week within each block. How fitness is built safely and sustainably.
