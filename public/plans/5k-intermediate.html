timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–2

Foundation phase focusing exclusively on aerobic development through easy-paced runs. All sessions in Zone 1-2 with emphasis on consistent volume, proper form, and injury prevention. No speed work or racing - just building your aerobic engine.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 3–5

Progressive phase introducing race-specific training. Combines continued aerobic base work with structured interval sessions and tempo runs. Intensity distribution shifts to include quality sessions while maintaining easy run discipline.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 6–7

Highest training loads and race-specific intensity. Maximum weekly volume with challenging interval sessions and longest runs of the plan. This phase develops peak fitness and race-day confidence through progressive overload.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 8–8

Strategic volume reduction while maintaining intensity touches. Allows full recovery and freshness for race day performance. Focus shifts from building fitness to feeling sharp, loose, and mentally prepared to race.

flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb meal (oatmeal with banana, toast with honey, or familiar pre-workout meal). Aim for 1-3g carbs per kg body weight.
2-3 hours before: Begin hydrating with 400-500ml fluid. Check gear one final time, then stop fiddling with equipment.
60-90 minutes before: Light snack if desired (banana, dates, or familiar pre-exercise fuel). Continue steady hydration.
30-45 minutes before: Begin warm-up routine: 10 min easy jogging, 4-6 strides building to race pace, dynamic stretching.
15 minutes before: Final mental preparation, review race strategy, get to start line feeling calm and confident.
5 minutes before: Final sip of water, positive self-talk, embrace the excitement and trust your training.
Your Race Strategy

Start Strategy: Begin at goal pace (4:45-4:50/km for sub-24min 5K). Resist the urge to go out faster - the race starts at 3K, not the gun. Stay relaxed and find your rhythm within the first 400m.

Middle Kilometers (2-3K): Hold steady at goal pace. Focus on smooth breathing and efficient form. This is where patience pays off - maintain confidence in your pacing strategy even if others surge past.

Final Push (4-5K): Begin gradual acceleration from 3.5K onwards. The final 800m is where your interval training pays off - shift into race mode and embrace the discomfort. Sprint the final 200m with everything you have.

Pacing Checkpoints: 1K in 4:45-4:50, 2K in 9:30-9:40, 3K in 14:15-14:30. If ahead of schedule early, stay patient. If slightly behind, remain calm and focus on negative splitting.

Mental Strategy

Chunking Strategy: Break the race into three sections: steady first kilometer, controlled middle 3K, and aggressive final kilometer. Each section has its own goal and mindset.

Mantras for Each Phase: Start - 'Smooth and controlled', Middle - 'Strong and patient', Finish - 'This is my moment'. Practice these phrases during your interval sessions.

Managing Discomfort: From 3K onwards, reframe pain as progress. 'I've trained for this feeling' and 'Every step is closer to my goal'. The discomfort means you're in the race, not that something is wrong.

Confidence Anchors: Recall your best interval sessions and long runs during tough moments. You've already run race pace multiple times in training - your body knows how to do this. Trust your preparation and execute with confidence.

school Glossary

Z1-Z2 (Easy Zones)

Heart rate zones 1-2, representing 65-75% of max HR. Should feel fully conversational and relaxed - you could maintain this pace for hours.

Z4-Z5 (Hard Zones)

Heart rate zones 4-5, representing 85-95%+ of max HR. These are your interval and race-pace efforts - breathing is labored but controlled.

5K Race Pace

The pace you can sustain for a full 5K race - typically 4:45-4:50/km for sub-24min goal. Should feel comfortably hard, not all-out.

Interval Training

Structured periods of hard effort followed by recovery periods. Builds VO2max and speed - key for 5K performance improvement.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race or run faster than the first half. A smart pacing strategy that prevents early burnout.

Strides

Short 20-30 second accelerations to near-race pace, used in warm-ups to activate fast-twitch muscles and prepare for hard efforts.

VO2max

Maximum oxygen uptake - your aerobic engine's ceiling. Interval training at Z4-Z5 intensity improves this crucial performance marker.

Lactate Threshold

The intensity where lactate begins accumulating faster than clearance. Training at this level improves your ability to sustain hard efforts.

Cadence

Steps per minute while running. Optimal range is 170-180 spm - higher cadence typically means more efficient, injury-resistant form.

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume (maintaining intensity) before race day to ensure full recovery and peak freshness.

Peak Phase

Highest training volume and intensity phase. Where fitness reaches maximum levels before tapering for race day.

Active Recovery

Easy movement sessions between hard training days. Promotes blood flow and recovery without adding training stress.

Race Strategy

Pre-planned approach to pacing, fueling, and effort distribution throughout your race distance.

Training Load

The cumulative stress of training sessions. Must be balanced with recovery to promote adaptation without overreaching.

Periodization

Systematic planning of training phases (base, build, peak, taper) to optimize performance for target race date.
