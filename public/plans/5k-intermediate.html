timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–2

Foundation building phase focusing exclusively on aerobic development through easy-paced running. All sessions are conversational pace with emphasis on building weekly volume gradually. No racing or high-intensity work - this phase develops your endurance engine through patient, consistent training.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 3–5

Introduction of race-specific intensity while maintaining aerobic base. Includes threshold runs, interval training, and tempo work. Training becomes more structured with 2-3 quality sessions per week. Volume continues to increase while introducing the intensity needed for 5K performance.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 6–7

Highest training loads with maximum race-specific work. Features challenging interval sessions, race-simulation efforts, and peak weekly volumes. This phase tests your fitness gains and builds confidence through successfully completing demanding workouts.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 8–8

Strategic reduction in training volume while maintaining some intensity. Allows complete physical and mental recovery before race day. Focus shifts to sleep, nutrition, and mental preparation while preserving fitness through short, sharp sessions.

Your Race Strategy

5K Race Execution

First km: Start at goal pace or 5-10 seconds per km slower. Resist the adrenaline surge and settle into rhythm. Focus on smooth, controlled breathing.

Middle 3km: Hold steady at goal pace. Stay relaxed through shoulders and arms. Break the race into 1km segments mentally - 'just get to the next kilometer marker.'

Final km: This is where your training pays off. Gradually increase effort from 4km mark, then give everything you have from 4.5km to finish. Pump your arms, lift your knees, and embrace the discomfort.

Pacing targets: Even effort throughout with negative split finish. Better to run the second half 10-15 seconds faster than the first half.

Mental Strategy

Mental Race Tools

Pre-race nerves: Reframe butterflies as excitement and readiness. Remind yourself 'I'm prepared for this challenge.' Use box breathing (4 counts in, hold 4, out 4, hold 4).

During the race: Use positive mantras like 'Strong and smooth,' 'I've trained for this,' or 'Embrace the challenge.' When it gets tough around 3km, remind yourself this is exactly when your training kicks in.

When it hurts: Remember that everyone around you is hurting too - the winner is who handles it best. Focus on your form cues: quick cadence, relaxed shoulders, efficient stride.

Final push: Visualize crossing the finish line strong. Think 'This is what I trained for' and use the crowd energy to carry you home.

school Glossary

Zone 1-2 (Z1-Z2)

Easy aerobic pace where you can hold a conversation. Heart rate typically 60-75% of maximum. Forms the foundation of endurance training.

Zone 4-5 (Z4-Z5)

Hard intensity training zones. Z4 is threshold/tempo pace, Z5 is VO2max intervals. Used for building speed and lactate tolerance.

VO2max

Maximum oxygen uptake - the ceiling of your aerobic system. Improved through high-intensity intervals at 5K race pace or faster.

Conversational Pace

An effort level where you could speak in full sentences while running. Key indicator that you're training aerobically.

Cadence

Steps per minute while running. Optimal range is 170-180 spm, which reduces injury risk and improves efficiency.

Strides

Short 15-30 second accelerations to near-maximum speed with full recovery. Used to activate neuromuscular system.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race faster than the first half. Optimal pacing strategy for most distance events.

Lactate Threshold

The exercise intensity where lactate begins accumulating faster than it can be cleared. Corresponds to tempo/threshold training pace.

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume before a race to allow full recovery and peak performance. Maintains intensity but reduces duration.

80/20 Rule

Training principle where 80% of sessions are done at easy intensity (Z1-Z2) and 20% at hard intensity (Z4-Z5).

Active Recovery

Low-intensity exercise used to promote blood flow and recovery. Should feel restorative, not fatiguing.

Race Effort

The specific intensity you plan to hold during your goal race. For 5K, this is a hard but sustainable pace for 15-25 minutes.

Aerobic Base

The foundation fitness built through easy-paced running. Improves fat burning, recovery, and endurance capacity.

Interval Training

Structured periods of hard effort followed by recovery periods. Designed to improve specific energy systems.

Peak Phase

Training phase with highest intensity and specificity. Includes race-pace workouts and simulation sessions.
