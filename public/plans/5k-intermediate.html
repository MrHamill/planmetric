5K Intermediate.
Race it, don't just run it.

An 8-week structured programme built on tempo development, VO2max intervals, and race-specific sessions. You've finished a 5K. Now learn to race one.

Plan Focus

This plan is for runners who have completed a 5K and want to improve their time. The first three weeks rebuild your aerobic base with easy volume and strides while introducing tempo work at lactate threshold intensity. Week 4 is a recovery week. Weeks 5-7 layer in VO2max intervals, parkrun efforts, and long runs with tempo finishes to develop race-specific fitness. Week 8 tapers to race day. Two quality sessions per week drive adaptation. Everything else supports recovery. The 80/20 rule still applies — but the 20% is sharper now.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before starting. Identify the 2 key sessions and protect those in your schedule — everything else can flex.
  2. If you miss a quality session, don't double up. Move on. Two hard sessions back-to-back is worse than one missed session.
  3. Recovery weeks are where fitness consolidates. Cutting them short is the fastest way to plateau.
  4. This plan uses RPE-based guidance. If you have a HR monitor or power meter, use the zones table to cross-reference — but RPE should always be the final authority.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

Polarise Your Training

Easy days easy, hard days hard. The biggest mistake intermediate runners make is running their easy days too fast, which compromises their quality sessions. If your easy runs feel like moderate efforts, slow down.

Protect the Key Sessions

Each week has 2 key sessions. If life gets in the way, protect those and sacrifice the easy runs. Two quality sessions per week drive 80% of your adaptation.

Warm Up for Quality Sessions

Every interval and tempo session starts with a 10-minute warm-up jog and strides. Skipping this reduces the quality of the session and increases injury risk. The warm-up is part of the workout.

Equal Splits on Intervals

The goal for every interval session is equal pace across all reps. If your last rep is significantly slower than your first, you started too fast. Controlled pacing in training translates to controlled pacing in racing.

Threshold Effort Is Not Racing

Tempo runs should feel "comfortably hard" — sustainable for 40-60 minutes in a race. If you can't speak in short sentences, you're above threshold. If you can chat freely, you're below it.

Recovery Runs Are Not Junk Miles

Easy runs between quality sessions flush metabolic waste, maintain aerobic conditioning, and add low-stress volume. But they only work if they're genuinely easy. RPE 3. Full conversation. No exceptions.

Don't Race Every Parkrun

Parkrun is a training tool in this plan, not a weekly race. Some weeks it's easy, some weeks it's controlled, and one week it's a dress rehearsal. Follow the plan's prescribed effort.

Sleep Compounds

One night of poor sleep won't ruin you. Three in a row will. Prioritise 8+ hours consistently. Sleep quality matters more during build weeks than any supplement or recovery gadget.

Taper Anxiety Is Normal

When volume drops in Weeks 7-8, you may feel restless, flat, or sluggish. This is taper madness — it's psychological, not physiological. Your body is supercompensating. Trust the process.

RPE Is the Final Authority

Heart rate drifts with heat, caffeine, stress, and fatigue. GPS pace varies with terrain and signal. RPE accounts for everything. If the plan says RPE 5-6 and your watch says you're slow — trust the RPE.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1-10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
VO2max
Maximum rate of oxygen consumption during exercise. VO2max intervals (T4 zone) improve this ceiling. Higher VO2max = faster sustainable pace at every intensity below it.
Lactate Threshold
The exercise intensity above which lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared. Tempo runs at T3 train your body to sustain higher intensity before crossing this threshold.
Fartlek
Swedish for "speed play." Unstructured intervals where you vary pace by feel — e.g., surging to a lamppost, then recovering. Useful for practising pace changes without rigid structure.
Zone 1 (T1)
Recovery effort. Very easy. Barely above walking. Used for warm-ups, cool-downs, and active recovery.
Zone 2 (T2)
Aerobic base. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training volume happens. Builds fat oxidation and endurance.
Zone 3 (T3)
Tempo/threshold. Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. The most effective zone for 5K performance improvement.
Zone 4 (T4)
VO2max. Hard, laboured breathing. Sustainable for 3-8 minutes. Used for 400m intervals in this plan.
Strides
Controlled accelerations (15-20 seconds) at ~85% effort. Develop running economy and neuromuscular coordination without significant fatigue.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The optimal 5K racing strategy for intermediate runners.
Tempo Finish
A long run that ends with a block of tempo-effort running. Simulates pushing when fatigued — exactly what happens in the final km of a 5K race.
Supercompensation
The process where fitness improves above baseline after a period of training stress followed by adequate recovery. Why recovery weeks exist.
Taper
A planned reduction in training volume before a race, maintaining intensity. Allows the body to fully recover and peak on race day.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (volume, intensity, or both) week over week. The fundamental principle of adaptation.
Race Day Basics

Your 5K Race Day Protocol

Night Before

Eat a normal meal with carbohydrates. Hydrate well but don't overdrink. Prepare your kit — lay out everything including race number, watch, shoes. Get to bed early. Accept that pre-race nerves may disrupt sleep slightly — one night won't affect performance.

Race Morning (2–3 hrs before)

Light pre-race meal: toast with honey, banana, or porridge. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Sip water. Consider a gel or small carb hit 15 minutes before the start if you've practised this in training — nothing new on race day.

Arrival (45–60 min early)

Collect race number. Use toilets. Walk the start/finish area. Identify the 1km marker so you can check your opening split. Give yourself time to settle.

Warm-up (15 min before start)

10 min easy jog → 6 × 100m strides building to 5K pace. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles, an elevated heart rate, and confidence. You've done this warm-up before every quality session — it's routine.

Pacing Strategy

Start controlled. The first 2km should feel easy — almost too easy. This is correct. Settle into race rhythm at km 2-3. From km 4, start to push. Final km — empty the tank. Target even splits or a slight negative split. The race is won between km 3-4 when it gets uncomfortable — that's where your tempo training pays off.

Post-Race

Walk for 10 min to cool down. Rehydrate. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 3-5 days of easy movement before any structured training. Review your splits — even pacing means you executed well.
