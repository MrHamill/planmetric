timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–2

Foundation phase focusing on building your aerobic engine through easy, conversational-pace running. All sessions should feel comfortable as you develop the fitness base that supports faster running later. This is where patience pays off.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 3–6

Progressive phase that introduces tempo runs and moderate intensity work. You'll maintain your aerobic base while beginning to challenge your lactate threshold and race-specific fitness. The balance between easy and moderate effort is crucial.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 7–9

Highest intensity phase featuring challenging interval sessions and peak training volumes. These weeks develop your VO2max and race-specific speed while maintaining endurance. Trust your preparation and embrace the challenge.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 10–10

Strategic reduction in training volume while maintaining intensity to allow your body to peak for race day. You should feel increasingly energized and ready to race. Less is more during this crucial phase.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Coach's Note:
Your first tempo session introduces you to threshold running - this is the pace you could theoretically hold for about an hour in a race. It should feel comfortably hard, where you're working but not gasping for air. Focus on maintaining steady, controlled breathing and consistent pace throughout. This effort teaches your body to clear lactate efficiently and builds your race pace foundation.

flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: Light breakfast with familiar carbs (banana, toast, or oatmeal) and moderate caffeine if used to it
2 hours before: Finish eating and begin sipping water regularly
90 minutes before: Arrive at race venue, collect bib, and familiarize yourself with the course
45 minutes before: Begin dynamic warm-up routine - 10 minutes easy jogging
30 minutes before: 4 x 30-second strides building to race pace, then stay loose with walking
15 minutes before: Final bathroom visit, remove warm layers, and move to start area
5 minutes before: Take position in appropriate pace group, focus on positive mantras and race strategy
Your Race Strategy
Start Conservative (0-3K): Begin 5-10 seconds per km slower than goal pace. Use this time to settle into rhythm and let faster runners go ahead. Focus on smooth form and controlled breathing.

Build Phase (3-7K): Gradually increase to goal pace. This is where your tempo training pays off - you should feel 'comfortably hard' but in control. Stay relaxed and maintain good posture.

Push Phase (7-10K): Time to dig deep! Draw on your interval training and embrace the discomfort. Focus on your breathing, pump your arms, and use positive self-talk. Remember - you've trained for exactly this effort.
Mental Strategy
Chunking: Break the race into 2.5K segments rather than thinking about the full 10K distance. Each segment has its own mini-goal and reward.

Mantras: Use phrases you've practiced in training: 'Strong and steady,' 'I can do hard things,' or 'Smooth is fast.' Repeat these during challenging moments.

Focus Points: When discomfort builds, focus on one technical aspect - breathing rhythm, arm swing, or cadence. This gives your mind something constructive to do rather than dwelling on fatigue.

school Glossary

Conversational Pace

A running intensity where you can speak in complete sentences throughout. This corresponds to Zone 1-2 training and builds your aerobic base.

Tempo Run

A 'comfortably hard' effort that you could sustain for 45-60 minutes. This improves your lactate threshold and race pace endurance.

Intervals

Repeated fast efforts with recovery periods between. These improve your VO2max and ability to run at faster paces.

VO2max

Your maximum oxygen uptake capacity. Higher VO2max allows you to run faster before reaching exhaustion.

Lactate Threshold

The intensity where lactate begins accumulating faster than your body can clear it. Training this improves your sustainable pace.

Zone 1-2

Easy aerobic intensity zones. Zone 1 is very easy recovery pace, Zone 2 is comfortable aerobic pace.

Zone 3-4

Moderate to hard intensity zones. Zone 3 is tempo effort, Zone 4 is lactate threshold to VO2max.

Strides

Short 20-30 second accelerations to near-race pace. These activate your nervous system and improve running form.

Taper

A planned reduction in training volume before race day to allow your body to recover and peak performance.

Aerobic Base

Your body's foundational fitness for using oxygen efficiently. Built primarily through easy, conversational-pace running.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race faster than the first half. This conservative start strategy often leads to better results.

Cadence

Steps per minute while running. An optimal cadence of 170-180 steps per minute can reduce injury risk.

Recovery Week

A planned reduction in training volume (usually every 3-4 weeks) to allow your body to adapt and get stronger.

Race Pace

The target pace you plan to sustain during your 10K race, based on your current fitness and goal time.

psychology Coach's Tips

pace

Master Your Easy Pace

80% of your training should be at conversational pace. If you can't speak in complete sentences, slow down. Easy runs build your aerobic engine and aid recovery.

schedule

Consistency Beats Intensity

Three consistent runs per week will improve your fitness more than sporadic hard efforts. Show up regularly, even if some runs feel slower than planned.

local_drink

Hydrate Strategically

Drink small, frequent sips during runs over 60 minutes. Practice your race-day hydration strategy during long training runs to avoid surprises.

self_care

Listen to Your Body

Some days will feel harder than others. If you feel unusually fatigued, don't hesitate to cut a session short or make it easier. Recovery is when you get stronger.

psychology

Practice Positive Self-Talk

Develop mantras during training that you can use on race day. Phrases like 'strong and steady' or 'I can do hard things' become powerful tools when racing gets tough.

trending_up

Build Gradually

Never increase your weekly running time by more than 10% week to week. Gradual progression reduces injury risk and allows your body to adapt safely.

bed

Prioritize Sleep

8-9 hours of quality sleep is your most powerful recovery tool. Poor sleep increases injury risk and makes training feel harder than it should.

fitness_center

Add Strength Training

2 strength sessions per week during base training can improve running economy by 3-8% and significantly reduce injury risk. Focus on single-leg exercises and core stability.
