10K Beginner.
Twice the distance, same smart approach.

You've already proven you can run 5K. This plan simply extends that fitness — the principles are the same, the distances are longer. 10 weeks of progressive, easy-effort running to get you across the 10K finish line with confidence.

Plan Focus

This plan takes your existing 5K fitness and extends it to 10K over 10 weeks. The first three weeks build your aerobic base with easy runs and a gradually lengthening long run. Week 4 is a recovery week — non-negotiable. Weeks 5–7 introduce strides and an easy tempo session to sharpen your running economy without adding risk. Week 8 is another recovery checkpoint, and Weeks 9–10 bring you to peak fitness and a smart taper into race day. 80% of this plan lives in T1–T2. The long run is the anchor of every week. Rest days are training days.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. Know what's coming so you can plan your schedule around the key sessions.
  2. Don't skip ahead. If you miss a session, continue from where you are. If you miss a full week, repeat the previous week rather than jumping forward — your body doesn't know what week number it is.
  3. Complete every recovery week as written. Skipping recovery is the most common mistake beginners make — adaptation happens during rest, not during the session.
  4. Run by effort, not by pace. This plan uses RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) instead of pace targets. If the plan says RPE 3–4, you should be able to hold a full conversation.

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

The 10% Rule

Never increase weekly running volume by more than 10%. This plan follows this rule strictly. If you repeat a week because life got in the way, that's fine — don't skip ahead to "catch up."

Easy Means Easy

Zone 2 is the most underused zone in amateur athletes. Most people train their easy days too hard and their hard days too easy. If you can't hold a full conversation during an easy run, slow down.

Sleep Is Your Best Training Tool

8–9 hours per night is the target. A bad night of sleep affects your next session more than a missed session does.

Don't Chase Pace

Your GPS watch doesn't know how your legs feel. Run by effort, not by numbers. Consistency across months, not pace, is the measure of long run success.

Fuelling for 10K

For runs under 60 minutes, water is enough. For your longer runs (8–10 km), consider carrying water if it's warm. Practice your race-day nutrition in training — never try anything new on race day.

Warm Up, Always

Every running session starts with a walk warm-up. Cold muscles are injury-prone muscles. 5 minutes of walking before you run is the cheapest injury prevention available.

Hydrate Throughout the Day

Don't wait until your run to drink water. Arrive at every session hydrated. A simple check: your urine should be pale yellow, not dark.

Rest Days Are Training Days

Adaptation happens during rest, not during the session. Skipping rest days doesn't make you fitter — it makes you injured.

Prepare Kit the Night Before

Lay out shoes, clothes, and watch the night before. The fewer decisions between waking up and getting out the door, the more likely you'll go.

One Bad Session Is One Session

If a run feels terrible, walk home. Come back next session. Consistency over perfection. A 10-week plan has roughly 35 sessions — one bad one changes nothing.

Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints. Introduced in Week 5.
Tempo Run
A sustained effort at RPE 5–6 (comfortably hard). Improves lactate threshold and race-pace confidence. Introduced in Week 7.
Long Run
The anchor session of every week. Builds aerobic endurance, fat oxidation, and musculoskeletal durability. Always run at easy effort.
Active Recovery
Very light movement (walking, easy stretching) to promote blood flow without adding training stress.
Cool-down
5–10 minutes of easy walking after a session to gradually lower heart rate and begin recovery.
Warm-up
5–10 minutes of walking or very easy jogging before the main session. Non-negotiable injury prevention.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The ideal 10K pacing strategy for beginners.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity) week over week. How fitness is built safely.
Taper
A deliberate reduction in training volume before race day. Maintains intensity while reducing fatigue so you arrive fresh.
Race Day Basics

Your 10K Race Day Protocol

Night Before

Eat a normal meal with carbohydrates. Hydrate well. Prepare your kit — lay out everything you'll need. Pin your race number. Charge your watch. Get to bed early.

Race Morning (2–3 hrs before)

Light pre-race meal: toast with jam, banana, or porridge. Familiar foods only — nothing new on race day. Sip water, don't overdrink. Consider a gel or a few jelly lollies 15 minutes before the start for a small energy top-up.

Arrival (45–60 min early)

Collect race number if you haven't already. Use toilets. Walk the start/finish area. Give yourself time to settle nerves. Find your starting position — not the front.

Warm-up (15 min before start)

10 min easy walk/jog → 4 × 20-sec strides. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles and an elevated heart rate. This makes the first kilometre feel controlled rather than shocking.

Pacing Strategy

Start conservatively. The first 2 km should feel easy — almost too easy. Most beginners go out too fast because of adrenaline and the crowd. By km 6, those people are walking. Don't be one of them. Target even pace or slight negative split — run the second 5K faster than the first. Don't go out with the front pack. Seed yourself honestly.

Post-Race

Walk for 5–10 min. Rehydrate. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 5–7 days of easy movement before any hard training. Celebrate — you just ran 10K.
