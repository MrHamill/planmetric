You know the distance. You have crossed a 70.3 finish line before. Now the question is: how much faster can you go? The typical intermediate athlete finishes in 4:30–5:45 depending on the course. The swim takes 30–42 min, the bike 2:20–3:00, and the run 1:45–2:15. Marginal gains across all three disciplines and transitions compound into significant time savings. This plan targets those gains through structured threshold work, race-pace bricks, and disciplined pacing. The bike leg remains the most important discipline — it accounts for roughly half your total race time and dictates how fast you can run.

Plan Focus

This plan assumes you can already swim 1900m continuously, ride for 2+ hours, and run 15+ km. Weeks 1–4 (Base) re-establish aerobic rhythm with CSS swim intervals from day one, strides in every run, and cadence discipline on the bike. Week 4 is recovery. Weeks 5–8 (Build) introduce sweet spot bike work, tempo runs, and your first brick sessions — this is where the real performance gains begin. Week 8 is recovery. Weeks 9–12 (Peak) push to highest volume with race-pace bricks, open water swimming, and race simulation sessions. The long ride peaks at 3.5 hours. Week 12 is recovery. Weeks 13–16 (Taper) sharpen the blade — volume drops progressively while intensity stays sharp, delivering you to race day fresh, fast, and confident. Every session has specific targets. 80% of training remains aerobic — but the 20% that is not will redefine your race.

How To Use This Plan
  1. Read the full week before you start it. This plan has 5–6 sessions per week across three disciplines — you need to plan pool access, long ride routes, and brick session logistics in advance.
  2. Respect the recovery weeks. Weeks 4, 8, and 12 drop volume by 30–40%. Adaptation happens during rest. Skipping recovery is the fastest way to plateau or get injured — you know this from your first 70.3.
  3. Hit the key sessions. Every week has a marked key workout. If life gets in the way and you can only complete 3–4 sessions, prioritise the key session and one session from each of the other two disciplines.
  4. Intensity is structured, not improvised. When the plan says Z2, ride Z2 — not Z3. When it says CSS pace, hit CSS pace — not faster. The polarised approach works because the easy days are genuinely easy, which allows the hard days to be genuinely hard.
  5. Practise race nutrition from Week 1. Target 60–90 g carbs/hr on the bike during every long ride and brick session. Build gut tolerance progressively — start at the lower end and increase by 10 g/hr each fortnight.
  6. Nail your transitions. At intermediate level, a smooth T1 and T2 can save 2–4 minutes over a frantic one. Rehearse the full sequence in training so race day is automatic.

TuesdaySwim — CSS Baseline
SWIMRPE 3–52200m

300m easy warm-up (100 free / 100 back / 100 choice) → 4 × 50m catch-up drill with 15s rest → 6 × 100m at CSS pace on 1:50 send-off (focus: high elbow catch, exhale fully underwater) → 4 × 50m kick with board at easy effort → 200m easy cool-down.

Coach’s NoteCSS intervals from Week 1 because you already have the base. These 100m reps at threshold pace develop your sustainable swim speed — the pace you will hold for 1900m on race day. WednesdayBike — Endurance + Cadence
BIKERPE 3–475 min

10 min easy spin warm-up at Z1, raising cadence to 90+ RPM → 3 × 5 min high cadence spins at 95–105 RPM in Z2 with 3 min normal cadence between → 25 min steady Z2 on a flat to rolling route → 10 min easy spin cool-down at Z1. Cadence target for steady section: 85–95 RPM.

Coach’s NoteHigher cadence (90–100 RPM) is more cardiovascular than muscular. For triathlon, this matters because a higher cadence on the bike spares your leg muscles for the run. Many age-groupers default to 70–75 RPM because it feels powerful, but it costs them on the half marathon. Start building this habit now.
ThursdayRun — Easy + Strides
RUNRPE 3–440 min

5 min walk warm-up → 28 min easy run at T2 → 4 × 20-sec strides with 40-sec jog recovery (smooth accelerations, not sprints — fast but relaxed) → 5 min walk cool-down.

Coach’s NoteStrides are in every run from Week 1. They develop neuromuscular coordination and running economy at zero fatigue cost. Think of them as greasing the gears — quick, smooth, controlled accelerations that teach your legs to turn over faster when you need them to. Never strain on a stride. If it feels forced, slow it down.
FridaySwim — Technique + Endurance
SWIMRPE 3–42000m

Coach’s NoteTwo swims per week at intermediate level. This session is aerobic volume and technique — not threshold. The pull buoy isolates your upper body and forces you to maintain body position without kicking. It also gives your legs a break before the weekend. Every session includes a drill component because even experienced swimmers lose efficiency when they stop practising technique.

Race Day Protocol

Your 70.3 Race Day — Intermediate Pacing Strategy

Night Before

Eat a normal high-carb dinner — pasta, rice, potatoes. Familiar foods only, low fibre, low fat. Do not try new foods. Hydrate well but do not overdrink. Prepare all gear in transition order: swim kit (wetsuit, goggles, 2 caps), bike kit (helmet, shoes, sunglasses, nutrition taped to frame), run kit (shoes, race belt, hat, gels in belt). Check tyre pressure. Pin race number to race belt. Prepare morning nutrition. Set two alarms. Lay out race morning clothing. Get to bed early — the sleep 2 nights before matters more.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before start)

Pre-race meal: 1–2 g carbs per kg body weight. Toast with jam, banana, porridge, or bagel with honey — whatever you have used in training. Sip water with electrolytes. Caffeine: 3–6 mg/kg body weight 45–60 min before start improves performance by 2–4%. Take a gel or energy chew 15–20 min before the swim. Use the toilets before heading to transition.

Arrival (60–90 min early)

Set up transition: bike racked, helmet on bars (open and ready), shoes positioned, nutrition ready, run gear laid out. Walk the transition area — memorise your entry and exit points for T1 and T2. Use a landmark to find your rack position. Put on wetsuit 15–20 min before start. Double cap method: base cap, goggles, race cap.

Warm-Up (10–15 min before)

If warm-up swim is available, swim 300–400m easy to settle breathing and activate your stroke. Include 4 × 25m at CSS pace. If no warm-up is allowed: splash cold water on face and neck. Do arm swings and light jogging. Take 10 slow nasal inhales to calm the nervous system.

Swim — 1.9 km

Controlled start. Seed yourself appropriately — slightly back from the front if you are not a strong swimmer. Start at CSS + 3–5 sec/100m for the first 300m, then settle into CSS. Sight every 10 strokes using a fixed landmark. Draft off faster swimmers — 30–50 cm behind their feet saves 20–30% energy. Bilateral breathing. Even pace or slight negative split. Approach buoy turns wide to avoid contact — kick hard to rotate. Begin unzipping wetsuit as soon as you can stand. Run through the shallows with high knees.

T1 — Swim to Bike (target: under 90 sec)

Wetsuit already half-unzipped. At your rack: pull arms out, push suit to ankles, step out. Helmet on (buckle it!), sunglasses on, bike shoes on. Grab bike. Run to mount line. Mount smoothly. You have rehearsed this. Smooth is fast.

Bike — 90 km

Discipline wins the bike. Ride at Z2 upper to Z3 lower for the first 60 km. Begin eating and drinking within the first 10 min. Target: 60–90 g carbs/hr using your trained products. Drink 600–750 mL/hr. Electrolytes: 300–700 mg sodium/hr. Cadence 85–95 RPM. Hold steady effort on climbs — do not surge above Z3. Maintain legal drafting distance. If you feel strong at km 70, resist the urge to attack — save it for the run. Final 20 km: maintain Z3 or slightly lower. Take your last gel 15 min before T2.

T2 — Bike to Run (target: under 60 sec)

Dismount before the line. Run to your rack. Rack bike, remove helmet, change to run shoes, grab race belt and hat. Go. Stay calm. You have done this in training.

Run — 21.1 km

Start conservative, finish fast. First 3 km at T2 — let your legs adjust from cycling. Walk through the first aid station and take water. Take a gel at km 3. By km 5, settle into T3 — your race pace. Target: 30–60 g carbs/hr on the run (gel every 30–40 min + water from aid stations). Walk the aid stations if needed — it is smarter than losing 30 seconds than blowing up and losing 5 minutes. Km 5–16: hold T3 pace. Even effort on hills — do not chase pace uphill, recover on the descent. Km 17–19: maintain or slightly increase effort. Final 2 km: everything you have left. This is where the tempo runs and race-finish surges in training pay off. Cross the line strong.

Post-Race

Walk for 10–15 min. Rehydrate with electrolytes. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 10–14 days of easy movement before any structured training. Celebrate — you raced a faster 70.3.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. Used alongside zone targets in this plan.
Brick Session
A workout combining two disciplines back-to-back, typically bike then run. Trains the body to perform the second discipline on fatigued legs — simulating race conditions.
T1 (Transition 1)
The transition from swim to bike. Includes exiting the water, removing wetsuit, putting on helmet and bike shoes, and mounting the bike.
T2 (Transition 2)
The transition from bike to run. Includes dismounting, racking the bike, removing helmet, changing to run shoes, and heading out on the run course.
CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
Your sustainable threshold pace in the pool — the fastest pace you can hold for about 20 minutes. Calculated from a 400m + 200m time trial. The primary intensity measure for swim intervals.
FTP (Functional Threshold Power)
The highest power output you can sustain for approximately 1 hour on the bike. The gold standard for setting cycling training zones. Tested via a 20-min all-out effort (95% of average power = FTP).
Sweet Spot
A cycling training zone at 88–93% of FTP. Sits just below threshold and delivers the highest adaptation-per-unit-of-fatigue ratio. The most efficient bike interval for raising FTP.
Threshold
The intensity at which lactate production and clearance are in balance. On the bike: 95–105% FTP. On the run: T3 / tempo pace. Training at threshold raises the speed you can sustain before fatigue accelerates.
Over-Unders
Bike intervals alternating between above-threshold (overs) and below-threshold (unders). Train the body to clear lactate during sustained race efforts. Very taxing — once per week maximum.
Cadence
Pedal revolutions per minute (RPM) on the bike. For triathlon, 85–95 RPM is optimal — higher cadence spares leg muscles for the run.
Sighting
Lifting your eyes briefly while swimming to navigate in open water. Lift only the eyes (“crocodile sighting”) every 8–10 strokes to stay on course.
Zone 2 (T2 / Z2)
Aerobic base intensity. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training happens. Builds fat oxidation and endurance.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace during a run. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination without adding fatigue. Not sprints.
Negative Split
Completing the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The ideal pacing strategy for all three disciplines in triathlon.
Gut Training
Progressively increasing carbohydrate intake during training rides to improve the gut’s ability to absorb fuel during racing. Essential for 70.3 and Ironman distances.
Conversational Pace
A pace where you can speak in full sentences without gasping. The gold standard for easy training across all three disciplines.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity) week over week. How fitness is built safely. Maximum ~10% increase per week.
Taper
The planned reduction in training volume before race day (typically 2–3 weeks). Volume drops 20–50% but intensity is maintained. Allows the body to absorb training and arrive fresh.
Active Recovery
Very light movement (walking, easy swimming, easy spinning) to promote blood flow without adding training stress.
Coach Tips

Easy Days Must Be Easy

80% of your training is at conversational effort. The biggest mistake intermediate athletes make is training easy days too hard, which means hard days are never hard enough. Polarise your training: easy is genuinely easy, hard is genuinely hard.

Nutrition Is the Fourth Discipline

At 70.3 distance, 60–90 g carbs/hr on the bike is non-negotiable. Train your gut progressively: start at 60 g/hr and build by 10 g/hr each fortnight. Products combining 2:1 glucose:fructose allow 80–90 g/hr absorption. Test everything in training.

Race the Bike with Discipline

The bike determines the run. If you ride 5% too hard, you run 15% too slow. Hold Z2 upper to Z3 lower. Resist the urge to push on the bike — save it for the half marathon where it counts. Cadence 85–95 RPM preserves run legs.

Consistency Over Heroics

Missing one session is fine. Missing one week sets you back. Missing one month requires rebuilding. Consistent training over 16 weeks produces more improvement than any single heroic session. Show up, execute, recover, repeat.

Sleep Is Your Superpower

Sleep is the highest-ROI recovery tool available. 8–9 hours per night during training. HRV is suppressed with poor sleep — a suppressed morning HRV means reduce intensity that day. No supplement comes close to quality sleep.

Trust the Taper

You will feel restless, sluggish, or anxious during the taper. This is normal. Fitness peaks 8–14 days after the last hard session. No fitness is gained in the final 2 weeks — only lost through overtraining or injury. Rest is the final ingredient.
