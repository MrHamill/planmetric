Plan Focus

This is not a base-building plan. You arrive with an aerobic engine. This plan sharpens it. Weeks 1–2 reintroduce structured threshold and VO2max work. Week 3 is peak load. Week 4 absorbs the training. Weeks 5–6 strip volume while maintaining intensity, delivering you to the start line with full glycogen stores, fresh legs, and a neuromuscular system primed for race pace. Two key sessions per week. Everything else is recovery or aerobic maintenance. Protect the key sessions.

How To Use This Plan
  1. The 2 key sessions each week are non-negotiable. Schedule them first. Everything else supports those sessions.
  2. Recovery weeks are programmed deliberately. Do not add volume. If you feel good during a recovery week, that means it's working.
  3. RPE is your primary intensity guide. Cross-reference with HR or power if available, but on days where data and feel disagree, trust feel.
  4. Review each week's check-in question honestly. Self-awareness is the differentiator between athletes who peak and athletes who plateau.

Week 1 — Sharpen Baseexpand_more
Week 1 of 6

Re-establish structured training. Threshold work on Thursday, VO2max introduction on Tuesday. Volume is moderate — the goal is to lock in the weekly template and confirm pacing.

MondayEasy Run
RPE 3–48 km

Easy aerobic run. Flat route preferred. Conversational pace throughout.

Coach's NoteThis is recovery from the weekend and sets up Tuesday's key session. Do not run this hard. If your HR drifts above T2, slow down.
TuesdayVO2max Intervals
RPE 7–810 kmKEY

2 km warm-up → 5 × 1 km at 5K race pace, 90-sec jog recovery → 2 km cool-down.

Coach's NoteConsistency across reps matters more than the first being fast. Target even splits. If power or pace drops >10% on any rep, end the session. 5 good reps beats 7 deteriorating ones.
WednesdayEasy Run + Strides
RPE 3–47 km

6 km easy → 4 × 100 m strides at 5K pace with walk-back recovery.

Coach's NoteStrides maintain neuromuscular activation without adding fatigue. Smooth acceleration, relaxed shoulders, quick turnover.
ThursdayThreshold Run
RPE 5–610 kmKEY

2 km warm-up → 2 × 10 min at T3 (threshold), 3 min easy jog between → 2 km cool-down.

Coach's NoteThreshold pace should feel controlled — hard but not desperate. If you can't speak in short sentences, you're above threshold. Pull back.
FridayRest
REST

Complete rest. Pre-hydrate for Saturday's effort.

SaturdayParkrun Race Effort
RPE 7–85 km

Warm-up: 10 min easy jog → 4 × 100 m strides → Parkrun at 90–95% effort. Use it as a controlled time trial. Even splits or slight negative split.

Coach's NoteParkrun is a data point, not a peak performance. Run it controlled. Note your splits. This is baseline data for the block.
SundayLong Run
RPE 3–512 km

10 km easy → final 2 km at T3 (tempo finish). Negative split the session — start slow, finish controlled.

Coach's NoteThe tempo finish teaches you to run quality on fatigued legs. If the last 2 km feel unsustainable, you started the easy portion too fast.

star Key Workouts — Tuesday & Thursday

Tuesday introduces VO2max stimulus. Thursday develops lactate clearance at threshold. These are the sessions that move the needle. Everything else protects your ability to execute them.

nightlight Recovery

Sleep 8+ hours. Post-key-session nutrition within 30 minutes: 1.2g/kg carbs, 0.3g/kg protein. Foam roll or massage gun on calves and quads for 10 min on Wednesday and Friday.

psychology Weekly Check-In

"Were my VO2max reps consistent, or did I fade? If I faded, was the starting pace too aggressive, or is my aerobic base the limiter?"

◆ Important Notes & Tipsexpand_more

Protect the Key Sessions

Two key sessions per week. Everything else exists to support them. If life forces a choice, drop the easy run — never the interval or threshold session.

Easy Means Easy

Your easy runs should be genuinely easy — RPE 3–4, conversational, boring. If your easy pace feels hard, your body is telling you something. Listen.

The 10% Abandon Rule

If power or pace drops >10% on any VO2max rep compared to your first rep, end the session. Quality reps only. Junk volume at threshold or above is worse than no volume.

Sleep Is Your Best Ergogenic Aid

8–9 hours per night. A single night of poor sleep reduces VO2max output by 3–5%. Two consecutive bad nights compound. Prioritise sleep over early-morning sessions.

Nutrition Timing

Post-key-session: 1.2g/kg carbs + 0.3g/kg protein within 30 minutes. Pre-session: familiar foods, 2–3 hours before. Race week: increase carbohydrate intake by 20–30%.

Resting Heart Rate

Track it daily. A sustained elevation >5bpm above your baseline indicates accumulated fatigue. Replace the next quality session with easy running and reassess.

Strides Are Not Sprints

Smooth acceleration to ~90% effort for 15–20 seconds. Focus on cadence, posture, and relaxation. Walk-back recovery. These maintain neuromuscular sharpness without adding training stress.

Race the Clock, Not the Field

Parkruns and races are pacing exercises. Execute your plan. If someone passes you at km 1, let them. You'll likely pass them at km 4.

Recovery Weeks Are Sacred

Week 4 exists because Weeks 1–3 created a stimulus your body needs time to absorb. Adding volume during recovery weeks is the most common mistake competitive runners make.

Trust the Taper

You will feel restless during Weeks 5–6. You'll want to add sessions. Don't. Taper anxiety is universal. The fitness is already banked. Your job now is to not waste it.

◆ Glossaryexpand_more
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Lactate Threshold
The intensity above which lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared. Corresponds to T3 — the highest intensity sustainable for 20–40 minutes. Key limiter for 5K performance.
VO2max
Maximum rate of oxygen consumption during exercise. Corresponds to T4 effort. Trainable through intervals at 5K race pace. The primary physiological target of this plan.
Fartlek
Unstructured speed play — alternating fast and easy running by feel rather than fixed intervals. Useful for active recovery days or as an alternative to structured intervals when mentally fatigued.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The optimal 5K pacing strategy for experienced runners. Requires discipline in the first 2 km.
Zone 2 (T2)
Aerobic base. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your weekly volume should be. Builds mitochondrial density, fat oxidation, and capillary networks.
Zone 3 (T3)
Tempo / threshold. Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. Sustainable for 20–40 minutes. Develops lactate clearance capacity.
Zone 4 (T4)
VO2max intensity. Hard. A few words at most. Sustainable for 3–8 minutes per rep. Develops maximal aerobic power.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at 5K pace or faster. Develops running economy and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
Tempo Finish
Ending a long run with a threshold-pace segment. Develops the ability to run fast on pre-fatigued legs — directly simulating the final km of a 5K.
Taper
Planned reduction in training volume (20–50%) while maintaining intensity in the 1–2 weeks before a race. Allows full physiological adaptation and glycogen restoration.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress week over week followed by planned recovery. The fundamental principle behind all structured training.
Race Day Protocol

Your 5K Race Execution Plan

Night Before

Carbohydrate-rich dinner. Nothing new — familiar foods only. Hydrate well. Prepare race kit: flats, number, watch, gels. Get to bed early. Accept that pre-race sleep may be light — it doesn't affect performance.

Race Morning (3 hrs before)

Pre-race meal: 1–2g carbs per kg body weight. Toast with honey, banana, or porridge. Low fibre, low fat. Coffee if part of your routine. Optional: gel or energy chew 15 min before start for a small top-up.

Arrival (60 min early)

Collect number. Walk the start area. Use toilets. Begin mental preparation. Visualise your pacing plan km by km.

Warm-Up (15 min before start)

Thorough warm-up is non-negotiable at this level. 15 min easy jog → dynamic stretches (leg swings, high knees, A-skips) → 6 × 100 m strides at race pace, building to faster than race pace on the last 2. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles and elevated HR.

Pacing Strategy

Start at goal pace from the gun. Even splits through 3 km. At 3 km, assess — if you have capacity, begin to push. Km 4 is where races are won or lost. Final km: all-out. Negative split or dead-even is the target. If your first km is your fastest, you've blown the pacing plan.

Post-Race

10 min easy jog cool-down. Rehydrate immediately. Carbs + protein within 30 minutes. Review splits within 24 hours while memory is fresh. Allow 5–7 days of easy running or rest before resuming structured training.
