timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–2

Foundation building phase focusing purely on aerobic development through easy-pace running. No intensity work or racing - just building the cardiovascular engine and running efficiency that supports all future speed development. Includes highest volume of strength training.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 3–5

Introduction of race-specific intensities and threshold work. Mix of easy aerobic running with structured interval sessions targeting lactate threshold and VO2max. Training load increases as we add quality sessions while maintaining aerobic base. Optional lower-priority races allowed for experience.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 6–7

Highest training stress combining maximum volume with sharp race-pace intervals. Final fitness development through race simulations and speed work. Body reaches peak adaptation - highest fitness but also highest fatigue. Requires careful recovery management between sessions.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 8–8

Planned reduction in training volume while maintaining intensity touches. Body recovers from peak training load while preserving fitness gains. Focus shifts to race preparation, mental readiness, and arriving at start line feeling fresh, confident, and energized to perform.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Weeks 1–2

Week 1 — BASE
2.2h 13 Apr–19 2026
Welcome to your elite 5K training journey! This first week focuses on establishing your aerobic base with controlled intensities. At 55% of peak volume, we're building the foundation for harder work ahead. Keep all easy runs truly conversational - this discipline will pay dividends as training intensifies.

Tue - Base Building Easy Run (5:30am–5:50am)

RUN Z1-Z2 20min
Warm-up: 5 min walk/jog at very easy pace
Main Set: 12 min steady run at 5:30-5:45/km (Z1-Z2), fully conversational pace
Cool-down: 3 min walk/easy jog
Total: 20 min
Coach's Note:
Focus on establishing your easy pace rhythm - you should be able to hold a conversation throughout. This pace builds your aerobic engine without stress. Land with a slight forward lean, maintain 170-180 steps per minute cadence.

Wed - Tempo Introduction (5:30am–5:55am)

RUN Z3 25min
Warm-up: 8 min easy jog building to moderate effort
Main Set: 12 min tempo at comfortably hard effort (4:45-5:00/km), should feel controlled but challenging
Cool-down: 5 min easy jog/walk
Total: 25 min
Coach's Note:
Your first tempo session should feel 'comfortably hard' - harder than easy pace but sustainable for the duration. Focus on smooth breathing rhythm (3:2 or 2:2 pattern). This effort improves your lactate threshold, crucial for 5K performance.

Thu - Recovery Run + Strength (5:30am–5:50am)

RUN Z1-Z2 20min
Warm-up: 3 min walk building to easy jog
Main Set: 15 min easy recovery run at 5:45-6:00/km, focus on movement quality
Cool-down: 2 min walk
Total: 20 min
Coach's Note:
Keep this genuinely easy - slower than Tuesday's pace. Schedule strength training 3-6 hours after this run or on a separate day entirely. Focus on hip stability (single-leg squats, glute bridges) and core strength. Two sessions per week during base phase maximizes strength gains without interference.

Fri - Midweek Easy Run (5:30am–5:50am)

RUN Z1-Z2 20min
Warm-up: 4 min walk/easy jog
Main Set: 13 min steady easy running at 5:30-5:45/km, relaxed effort
Cool-down: 3 min walk
Total: 20 min
Coach's Note:
Another foundation session to accumulate easy aerobic volume. Check your form: relaxed shoulders, slight forward lean, midfoot landing. If you're breathing hard or can't chat, slow down - easy days must be genuinely easy for the system to work.

Sat - Foundation Long Run (5:30am–6:20am)

RUN Z1-Z2 50min
Warm-up: 8 min gradual build from walk to easy running pace
Main Set: 35 min continuous run at 5:30-5:45/km, maintain conversational effort throughout
Cool-down: 7 min walk/easy jog
Total: 50 min
Coach's Note:
Your most important session of the week! This builds aerobic capacity and mental resilience. Stay disciplined with pacing - the goal is time on feet, not speed. Practice your race day fueling strategy if the session approaches 60 minutes. Focus on running relaxed and enjoying the process.

flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb breakfast (oatmeal, banana, toast with honey) - aim for 1-4g carbs per kg bodyweight. Include familiar foods only.
2 hours before: Stop eating solid food. Begin hydration with 500-750ml sports drink or water. Complete gear check and mental preparation.
60-90 minutes before: Arrive at venue. Complete registration, familiarize with course, set up transition if applicable. Light dynamic stretching.
30-45 minutes before: Begin warm-up routine. 10 minutes easy jogging, building to moderate pace. Include 4-6 × 100m strides at race pace to activate fast-twitch fibers.
15 minutes before: Final preparation. Use bathroom, final hydration sips, put on race gear. Move to start line with 5 minutes to spare.
5 minutes before: Mental focus routine. Three deep breaths, visualize race start, trust your fitness. You're ready.
Your Race Strategy

Start Strategy: Position yourself appropriately in the field - not too far forward (you'll go out too fast) or too far back (you'll weave through slower runners). Aim for 5-10 seconds per km slower than goal pace for the first 800m while settling in.

Middle 3K (800m-3800m): This is your bread and butter section. Settle into goal race pace (4:45/km for elite 5K) and maintain steady, controlled effort. Focus on smooth breathing rhythm and efficient form. Pass conservatively and maintain position when possible.

Final 1200m: Begin gradual acceleration from 3800m mark. Increase effort progressively - you should reach 85% perceived effort by 4500m and 95% effort for the final 500m. Trust your speed training and finish strong.

Pacing Target: Negative split approach - aim for second half 10-15 seconds faster than first half through controlled acceleration rather than dramatic pace changes.

Mental Strategy

Pre-Race Mantras: 'I am fit, I am ready, I belong here' and 'Trust the training, trust the pace.' Use these during warm-up and at start line to build confidence and focus.

Race Chunking: Break the 5K into five 1K segments. Each kilometer gets a specific focus: K1 (settle and find rhythm), K2-3 (steady and strong), K4 (maintain as it gets harder), K5 (everything you have left).

Discomfort Management: When the pace feels hard around 3K, remind yourself 'This is supposed to feel challenging - I've trained for this feeling.' Welcome the discomfort as a sign you're racing properly.

Finishing Mental Cue: At 4K mark, shift to 'One kilometer of excellence' - give everything to the finish line. Your interval training has prepared you for this final push.

school Glossary

Z1-Z2 (Zone 1-2)

Easy aerobic training zones - conversational pace where you can speak in full sentences. Heart rate typically 60-75% of maximum.

Z4-Z5 (Zone 4-5)

High-intensity training zones - Z4 is lactate threshold/tempo pace, Z5 is VO2max/5K race pace. Heart rate 85-95% of maximum.

5K Race Pace

The sustainable pace you can maintain for a 5-kilometer race - typically corresponds to your VO2max pace or Zone 5 intensity.

VO2max

Maximum oxygen uptake - the highest rate your body can consume oxygen during exercise. Trained through high-intensity intervals.

Lactate Threshold

The exercise intensity where lactate begins accumulating faster than it can be cleared. Corresponds to tempo/threshold pace.

Easy Recovery

Very low-intensity running used between hard sessions to promote blood flow and adaptation without adding training stress.

Interval Training

Structured workout alternating periods of high-intensity effort with recovery periods. Develops speed and anaerobic capacity.

Negative Split

Racing strategy where the second half of a race is faster than the first half, achieved through controlled pacing and gradual acceleration.

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume before a race to allow the body to recover and peak for performance.

Aerobic Base

Foundation cardiovascular fitness built through easy-pace running. Supports all higher-intensity training and racing.

Strides

Short accelerations (50-150m) done at 85-90% effort to activate fast-twitch muscle fibers and improve running form.

Progressive Build

Gradual increase in pace or effort during a warm-up or training session, allowing the body to prepare for higher intensities.

Tempo Pace

Comfortably hard pace you could sustain for 45-60 minutes. Typically 15-20 seconds per km slower than 5K race pace.

Peak Training

Highest volume and intensity phase of training when fitness reaches maximum levels before tapering for race day.

Training Load

Measure of physiological stress from exercise, considering both volume (duration) and intensity of training sessions.

schedule

Timing Your Intervals

Start your interval sessions within 30 seconds of target pace from the first repeat. Consistent pacing builds better physiological adaptations than starting slow and speeding up.

directions_run

Easy Day Discipline

Your easy runs should be genuinely conversational. If you can't speak in full sentences, you're running too hard and compromising your hard day quality.

psychology

Race Simulation Practice

Use your interval sessions to practice race-day mental strategies. Rehearse mantras, practice staying relaxed under pressure, and build confidence at goal pace.

local_drink

Hydration Strategy

For runs over 60 minutes, carry water and take small sips every 15-20 minutes. Practice this during long runs to prepare for longer races.

fitness_center

Strength Training Integration

Schedule strength sessions immediately after easy runs, never before quality sessions. Focus on core stability and hip strength - these have the highest injury prevention value.

hotel

Recovery Week Importance

Recovery weeks aren't lost fitness - they're when your body adapts and gets stronger. Embrace the easier training and trust the supercompensation process.

trending_up

Progressive Training Load

Increase your weekly training volume by no more than 10% each week. Consistent, gradual progression prevents injury and ensures sustainable fitness gains.

psychology_alt

Taper Week Mindset

During taper week, your legs may feel heavy or sluggish - this is normal as your body processes training adaptations. Trust the process and focus on feeling fresh for race day.
