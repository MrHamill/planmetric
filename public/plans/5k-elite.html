timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–2

Foundation building phase focusing exclusively on aerobic development through Zone 1-2 running. All sessions are conversational pace with emphasis on consistent volume, injury prevention, and movement efficiency. No intensity work - pure aerobic base building.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 3–5

Introduction of race-specific intensity while maintaining aerobic base. Includes threshold runs, interval sessions, and tempo efforts. Training becomes more challenging as we develop lactate clearance and VO2max alongside continued aerobic development.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 6–7

Highest training load with maximum race-specific intensity. Frequent interval sessions, race simulations, and quality work to sharpen speed and mental toughness. This phase develops the specific fitness demands of 5K racing through targeted high-intensity training.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 8–8

Planned volume reduction while maintaining intensity to allow supercompensation and race-day freshness. Focus shifts to mental preparation, race logistics, and trusting the fitness built in previous phases. Recovery becomes the priority.

flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb breakfast (oats with banana, toast with honey, or familiar race-day fuel). Aim for 1-4g carbs per kg body weight. Include moderate caffeine if you normally consume it.
90-60 min before: Complete your warm-up routine. 10 minutes easy jogging, followed by dynamic stretches and 3 × 20 second strides at race pace. Stay warm but not fatigued.
45-30 min before: Final preparations - check race number, timing chip, and gear. Use the bathroom one final time. Begin mental rehearsal of your race plan and positive self-talk.
15-10 min before: Move to starting area. Take 3-4 deep breaths and remind yourself of your first kilometer target pace. You're prepared and ready to execute.
5 min before: Final mental check - race pace mantra, first kilometer strategy, and confidence affirmation. Trust your fitness and preparation.
Your Race Strategy

Start Strategy: Begin 5-10 seconds per kilometer slower than goal pace for the first kilometer to avoid going out too aggressively in race-day excitement. Your body needs time to settle into rhythm.

Middle Phase (1-4km): Gradually settle into goal race pace. Focus on smooth, efficient running with controlled breathing. Use other runners for pacing and motivation, but don't get drawn into early surges.

Final Kilometer: This is where your interval training pays off. Begin your kick with 800m to go, increasing effort gradually. Give everything you have in the final 200m - empty the tank completely.

Pacing Checkpoints: 1km (5-10 sec slow), 2km (on pace), 3km (on pace), 4km (ready to kick), 5km (maximum effort finish).

Mental Strategy

Race Mantras: 'Smooth and strong' for the middle phase, 'I've trained for this' when it gets tough, 'Kick now' for the final 800m. Practice these mantras during your hardest training sessions.

Chunking Strategy: Break the race into five 1-kilometer segments. Each kilometer has its own mini-goal and focus. Don't think about the whole race - just execute each segment perfectly.

Adversity Plan: If you go out too fast, settle back gradually rather than panicking. If you feel flat early, trust that your training will carry you through - fitness shows up when it matters. For the inevitable tough patch around 3-4km, remind yourself this is where the race really begins.

Confidence Anchors: Recall your best interval session, your strongest long run, and the consistent training you've completed. You've prepared specifically for the demands you'll face today.

school Glossary

5K Race Pace

The target pace per kilometer you aim to maintain throughout your 5K race. For elite athletes, this represents your fastest sustainable pace for the distance.

VO2max Intervals

High-intensity intervals run at or slightly faster than 5K pace, designed to improve your maximum oxygen uptake and aerobic power.

Zone 1-2 (Easy Pace)

Conversational running pace where you can speak in full sentences. Forms the foundation of endurance training at 5:00/km for your fitness level.

Zone 4-5 (Hard Effort)

High-intensity running at threshold to VO2max pace. Includes race pace intervals and faster repetitions for speed development.

Lactate Threshold

The exercise intensity at which lactate begins to accumulate faster than it can be cleared. Critical for sustained race pace efforts.

Negative Split

Running strategy where the second half of your race is faster than the first half, requiring disciplined early pacing.

Strides

Short accelerations (20-30 seconds) at race pace or slightly faster, used in warm-ups to activate your neuromuscular system.

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume (45-60%) while maintaining intensity, allowing your body to recover and peak for race day.

Peak Phase

Highest intensity training phase where race-specific fitness is sharpened through frequent quality sessions and race simulations.

Active Recovery

Easy-effort training that promotes blood flow and adaptation while allowing recovery from harder sessions.

Cadence

Steps per minute while running. Target 170-180 spm for efficiency and injury reduction.

Threshold Pace

Comfortably hard pace sustainable for 15-60 minutes, typically 10-30 seconds per kilometer slower than 5K pace.

Race Simulation

Training session that mimics race-day conditions, pace, and duration to practice execution and build confidence.

Supercompensation

The training adaptation process where fitness increases above baseline after recovery from training stress.
