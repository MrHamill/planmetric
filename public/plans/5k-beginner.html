timeline Your Training Phases

foundation

Base Building Weeks 1–2

Foundation building phase focused entirely on easy-paced running to develop your aerobic engine. No intensity work - just consistent, comfortable miles to build fitness safely. This phase teaches your body to use fat for fuel and strengthens tendons and ligaments.

trending_up

Strength & Endurance Weeks 3–5

Introduction of race-pace intervals and tempo runs while maintaining your aerobic base. You'll practice running faster while learning to recover between efforts. This phase develops your VO2max and teaches race-pace pacing skills through structured sessions.

bolt

Peak Fitness Weeks 6–7

Highest training loads with race-simulation sessions. Your longest runs and most challenging intervals happen here as your fitness reaches its peak. Focus on executing sessions perfectly and building confidence in your race-pace ability.

self_improvement

Taper & Race Prep Weeks 8–8

Strategic reduction in training volume to eliminate fatigue while maintaining fitness sharpness. Easy runs with occasional short race-pace efforts keep your legs fresh. Trust that your fitness is banked and focus on arriving at the start line healthy and motivated.

Phase 1: Foundation Build

Weeks 1–2

Week 1 — BASE
2.2h 13 Apr–19 2026
Welcome to your 5K training journey! This first week focuses on building your aerobic base with gentle, conversational-pace running. The tempo run introduces you to comfortably hard effort - you should be able to speak in short phrases but not hold a full conversation.

Tue - Easy Run (6:00am–6:30am)

RUN Z1-Z2 30min
Warm-up: 5 min walk, then 5 min easy jog to gradually elevate heart rate
Main Set: 20 min steady running at conversational pace (7:30/km) - you should be able to chat comfortably throughout
Cool-down: 5 min walk to bring heart rate down gradually
Total: 30 min
Coach's Note:
This is your foundation session - resist the urge to go faster than conversational pace. Focus on relaxed shoulders, midfoot landing, and steady breathing rhythm. If you can't maintain a conversation, slow down immediately. Remember, easy days build your aerobic engine without accumulating fatigue.

Thu - Tempo Run (6:00am–6:35am)

RUN Z3 35min
Warm-up: 10 min easy running to prepare muscles and joints for harder effort
Main Set: 20 min at comfortably hard pace (approximately 6:45-7:00/km) - this should feel like a controlled, sustained effort where you can speak in short phrases
Cool-down: 5 min easy jogging followed by gentle walking
Total: 35 min
Coach's Note:
Your first tempo session! This pace should feel challenging but controlled - think of it as 'comfortably hard' rather than all-out effort. Focus on maintaining steady rhythm and breathing throughout the 20-minute block. If you start breathing very heavily, ease back slightly. This effort teaches your body to clear lactate efficiently.

Sat - Long Run (7:00am–8:10am)

RUN Z1-Z2 70min
Warm-up: 10 min easy walking and gentle jogging to prepare for extended effort
Main Set: 55 min continuous running at easy, conversational pace (7:30/km) - this builds your aerobic base and mental endurance
Cool-down: 5 min walking to aid recovery
Total: 70 min
Coach's Note:
Your most important session of the week! Stay disciplined with easy pace throughout - this run should feel comfortable and sustainable. Bring water and take small sips every 15-20 minutes. Focus on running tall with relaxed shoulders and arms. The goal is time on your feet, not speed. If you feel tired, it's better to walk briefly than to push too hard.

3.2h 20 Apr–26 2026

This extended time on feet is building crucial aerobic adaptations and mental toughness for your 5K goal.

Same duration as last week but with optional short pickups if you're feeling good. These brief accelerations (not sprints) help prepare your legs for race-pace turnover. flag Race Day Protocol

Pre-Race Timeline
3-4 hours before: High-carb meal (oatmeal with banana, toast with honey). Aim for familiar foods totaling 1-2g carbs per kg body weight.
2 hours before: Begin hydrating with 500ml water or sports drink. Stop eating solid food. Check weather and adjust clothing plan.
1 hour before: Light dynamic warm-up at home. Use bathroom, apply sunscreen if needed. Final gear check - shoes, race number, timing chip.
30-45 minutes before: Arrive at race venue. Collect race number if needed, familiarize yourself with start/finish area.
15-20 minutes before: Complete warm-up routine - 5-8 minutes easy jogging, 4 x 20-second strides building to race pace, dynamic stretching.
5-10 minutes before: Final mental preparation. Find your start position, practice positive self-talk, take deep breaths and visualize a strong start.
Your Race Strategy

First kilometer: Start conservatively at target pace or 5-10 seconds slower. Resist the urge to go with faster runners - trust your training pace.

Middle kilometers (2-4): Settle into your trained pace rhythm. Focus on smooth form, efficient breathing, and positive self-talk. Break the race into 1km segments mentally.

Final kilometer: This is where your interval training pays off. Gradually increase effort - you should feel like you're 'shifting gears' rather than sprinting. Maintain form and pump your arms to drive through the finish line strong.

Throughout: Stay relaxed in your shoulders, maintain your trained cadence around 170-180 steps per minute, and use the mantra 'smooth and strong' when the effort feels challenging.

Mental Strategy

Pre-race nerves: Reframe butterflies as readiness - your body is preparing for performance. Use the mantra 'I've trained for this' when doubts creep in.

During the race: Practice chunking - focus only on getting to the next kilometer marker rather than the finish line. When it gets tough, remind yourself 'I can do hard things for 20-25 minutes.'

If things go wrong: Stay flexible and positive. Use 'Yes, and...' thinking - 'Yes, I started too fast, and I can still run a smart race from here.' Every kilometer is a fresh start.

Final push: Remember that everyone hurts in the final kilometer - your training has prepared you to hurt better than others. Focus on form cues and drive through the line with pride in your effort.

school Glossary

5K Pace

The speed you can sustain for a 5-kilometer race - challenging but controlled, about 10-15 seconds per kilometer faster than your easy run pace.

Easy Pace

Conversational running pace where you can speak in full sentences. For beginners, this is typically 7:30-8:30/km - slower than you think!

Zone 1-2

Low intensity training zones where you build aerobic fitness. Should feel comfortable and sustainable for extended periods.

Zone 4-5

High intensity zones used for race-pace and VO2max intervals. Zone 4 is threshold effort, Zone 5 is near-maximum effort.

Interval Training

Structured sessions alternating between hard efforts and recovery periods to improve speed and VO2max.

Strides

Short 20-30 second accelerations to race pace or slightly faster, used in warm-ups to prepare your legs for faster running.

Negative Split

Running the second half of a race faster than the first half - a smart pacing strategy that prevents early fatigue.

VO2max

Your body's maximum ability to use oxygen during exercise - the 'engine size' that determines your aerobic potential.

Cadence

Steps per minute while running. Optimal range is 170-180 spm for efficiency and injury prevention.

Taper

Planned reduction in training volume before a race to reduce fatigue while maintaining fitness.

Active Recovery

Easy movement like gentle jogging or walking that promotes blood flow and recovery between harder sessions.

Threshold Pace

The fastest pace you can maintain for about 1 hour - corresponds to lactate threshold and builds race endurance.

Race Pace

Your target speed for race day, practiced in training to develop pacing skills and race-specific fitness.

Long Run

Extended easy-paced runs that build aerobic capacity, mental toughness, and teach your body to use fat for fuel.

Chunking

Mental strategy of breaking races into smaller segments to make the distance feel manageable and maintain focus.

psychology Coach's Tips

pace

Master Easy Pace

Most beginners run too fast on easy days. Slow down to a truly conversational pace - you should be able to chat comfortably throughout your easy runs.

schedule

Consistency Beats Intensity

Three moderate runs per week will improve your fitness more than one heroic effort followed by days off. Show up consistently, even when motivation is low.

timer

Time Over Distance

Focus on running for time (30 minutes) rather than distance. This removes pace pressure and lets you focus on building endurance gradually.

water_drop

Hydrate Smart

For runs over 60 minutes, drink 400-500ml beforehand and consider carrying water. Practice your race-day hydration strategy during long training runs.

self_improvement

Form Focus Points

Keep shoulders relaxed, aim for 170-180 steps per minute, and land with feet under your body rather than out in front. Small improvements compound over time.

psychology

Positive Self-Talk

Develop mantras like 'smooth and strong' or 'I've trained for this' to use when running gets challenging. Mental skills are as trainable as physical fitness.

nights_stay

Prioritize Recovery

Sleep 8-9 hours per night and take at least one full rest day per week. Your body adapts and gets stronger during rest, not just during running.

restaurant

Fuel Your Training

Eat carbs within 30-60 minutes after harder sessions to support recovery. For long runs over 90 minutes, practice taking on sports drinks during the session.
