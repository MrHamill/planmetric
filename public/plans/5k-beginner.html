Starter Plan

5K Beginner.
Zero to finish line.

An 8-week walk-to-run programme built on progressive overload, 80/20 intensity distribution, and the principle that consistency beats volume every time.

Plan Focus

This plan takes you from zero running fitness to completing a 5K. The first four weeks use walk-run intervals to build your aerobic base safely. Weeks 5–8 transition to continuous running and sharpen your fitness for race day. Every session has a purpose. Rest days are training days. The long run is the anchor of every week. 80% of your aerobic development happens at easy intensity — that's why these sessions aren't optional, they're the engine.

Important Notes & Tips

The 10% Rule

Never increase weekly running volume by more than 10%. This plan follows this rule strictly. If you repeat a week because life got in the way, that's fine — don't skip ahead to "catch up."

Easy Means Easy

Zone 2 is the most underused zone in amateur athletes. Most people train their easy days too hard and their hard days too easy. If you can't hold a full conversation during an easy run, slow down.

Sleep Is Your Best Training Tool

8–9 hours per night is the target. A bad night of sleep affects your next session more than a missed session does.

Don't Chase Pace

"Consistency across months, not pace, is the measure of long run success." Your GPS watch doesn't know how your legs feel. Run by effort, not by numbers.

Soreness vs. Pain

Muscle soreness after your first few sessions is normal. Sharp, localised pain in joints (knees, ankles, shins) is a warning sign. Rest and reassess — never train through joint pain.

Warm Up, Always

Every running session starts with a walk warm-up. Cold muscles are injury-prone muscles. 5 minutes of walking before you run is the cheapest injury prevention available.

Hydrate Throughout the Day

Don't wait until your run to drink water. For runs under 45 minutes, you don't need to carry water — but you need to arrive hydrated.

Rest Days Are Training Days

Adaptation happens during rest, not during the session. Skipping rest days doesn't make you fitter — it makes you injured.

Prepare Kit the Night Before

Lay out shoes, clothes, and watch the night before. The fewer decisions between waking up and getting out the door, the more likely you'll go.

One Bad Session Is One Session

If a run feels terrible, walk home. Come back next session. Consistency over perfection.

Glossary
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion — a 1–10 scale of how hard a session feels. The primary intensity measure in this plan.
Zone 1 (T1)
Recovery effort. Very easy. Barely above walking. Used for warm-ups, cool-downs, and active recovery.
Zone 2 (T2)
Aerobic base. Easy and conversational. Where 80% of your training happens. Builds fat oxidation and endurance.
Zone 3 (T3)
Tempo/threshold. Comfortably hard. Short sentences only. Used sparingly in this plan via strides.
Conversational Pace
A pace where you can speak in full sentences without gasping. The gold standard for easy running.
Walk-Run Intervals
Alternating between walking and running for set periods. The foundation of beginner running programmes.
Strides
Short accelerations (15–20 seconds) at a faster pace. Develops running form and neuromuscular coordination. Not sprints.
Active Recovery
Very light movement (walking, easy stretching) to promote blood flow without adding training stress.
Cool-down
5–10 minutes of easy walking after a session to gradually lower heart rate and begin recovery.
Warm-up
5–10 minutes of walking or very easy jogging before the main session. Non-negotiable injury prevention.
Negative Split
Running the second half of a distance faster than the first half. The ideal 5K pacing strategy.
Progressive Overload
Gradually increasing training stress (duration, intensity) week over week. How fitness is built safely.
Race Day Basics

Your 5K Race Day Protocol

Night Before

Eat a normal meal with carbohydrates. Hydrate well. Prepare your kit — lay out everything you'll need. Get to bed early.

Race Morning (3–4 hrs before)

1–2g carbs per kg body weight. Familiar foods, low fibre, low fat. Toast with jam, banana, or porridge are reliable choices. Sip water — don't overdrink.

Arrival (45–60 min early)

Collect race number. Use toilets. Walk the start/finish area. Give yourself time to settle nerves.

Warm-up (15 min before start)

10 min easy walk/jog → 4 × 20-sec strides. Arrive at the start line with warm muscles and an elevated heart rate.

Pacing Strategy

Start conservatively. The first KM should feel easy. Most beginners go out too fast and blow up by KM 3. Target even pace or slight negative split — run the second half faster than the first. Don't go out with the front pack unless that's your level. Seed yourself honestly.

Post-Race

Walk for 5–10 min. Rehydrate. Eat carbs + protein within 60 min. Allow 3–5 days of easy movement before any hard training.
