# Recovery Research

## ⚡ PLAN GENERATION ESSENTIALS

**Race recovery timelines (minimum before next hard session):**
- Sprint: 3–5 days | Olympic: 5–7 days | 70.3: 10–14 days | Ironman: 3–4 weeks
- Easy volume can resume sooner — these are minimums for quality/intensity work

**Post-session recovery (0–60 min):**
- 0.3–0.5 g/kg carbs + 20–30 g protein within 30–60 min of hard sessions
- Rehydrate to pre-exercise weight + 150% of fluid lost

**Sleep:** 8–9 hrs/night is the single highest-ROI recovery tool; HRV suppressed with poor sleep — reduce intensity that day

**Active recovery:** Z1 only (swim, cycle, walk) the day after hard sessions; avoid Z3+ until HRV/perceived recovery normalise — 48–72 hrs short-course, 7–14 days long-course

**Recovery week protocol:**
- Every 4th week at ~50–60% of normal volume (every 3rd week for athletes 45+)
- Account for life stress (work, family) as training load — not just session volume

**Speed endurance (NHE1 acid-clearance adaptation):**
- 8–12 × 30-sec maximal sprints with 2–4.5 min full rest between; 2–3×/week for 6 weeks
- Improves repeat-effort capacity and recovery between hard intervals — include in build/peak phases
- Requires maximal effort — submaximal efforts produce a different (lesser) adaptation

**Cold water immersion:** 10–15°C, 10–15 min reduces acute soreness; avoid immediately after strength sessions (blunts hypertrophy signalling)

## Speed Endurance Training and Skeletal Muscle Recovery (PMC12203883)

**Context:** Study examining how speed endurance training (SET) affects the expression of NHE1 (sodium-hydrogen exchanger 1), a key protein responsible for clearing acid (H⁺) from muscle cells during and after high-intensity exercise. This is directly relevant to triathlon athletes targeting repeated high-intensity efforts.

---

### What is NHE1?
NHE1 (Sodium-Hydrogen Exchanger Isoform 1) is a membrane protein embedded in the sarcolemma of skeletal muscle cells. Its function is to exchange intracellular H⁺ (acid) for extracellular Na⁺, maintaining intracellular pH during and after intense exercise.

**Why it matters for performance:**
- Fatigue during high-intensity exercise is partially caused by intracellular acidosis (H⁺ accumulation)
- A better-trained NHE1 system clears acid faster, reducing fatigue and improving repeat-effort capacity
- NHE1 is also critical for post-exercise recovery — clearing residual acid from muscle tissue after intense sessions

---

### Speed Endurance Training (SET) Protocol
**Study Protocol:**
- **8–12 × 30-second maximal sprint efforts**
- Recovery: **2–4.5 minutes** complete rest between each sprint
- Sessions performed **2–3 times per week** over 6 weeks

This produces a very different stimulus to traditional interval training — the maximal intensity is critical. Submaximal efforts do not produce the same NHE1 response.

### Key Results
- NHE1 protein expression increased **30–75%** following a 6-week SET protocol
- The degree of upregulation was dose-dependent — athletes completing more sessions per week showed greater adaptation
- NHE1 upregulation was associated with **faster recovery between efforts** and reduced perceived fatigue at matched intensities

---

### Implications for Training Plan Design

**1. Sprint intervals are not optional for competitive athletes**
Even pure endurance athletes (triathletes, marathoners) benefit from periodic speed endurance work to develop acid-clearing capacity. This translates to:
- Better recovery between T1, T2 transitions and initial effort spikes
- Improved ability to sustain surges in open water swim starts and race-pace run finishes
- Faster in-session recovery between intervals, allowing more total quality work

**2. Recovery duration matters**
The 2–4.5 min recovery windows in SET are long relative to typical training intervals. This is intentional — allowing near-complete recovery ensures maximal effort on each sprint. Insufficient recovery converts SET into submaximal threshold work, which produces a different (lesser) NHE1 adaptation.

**3. 6-week adaptation cycle**
Meaningful NHE1 upregulation takes approximately 4–6 weeks of consistent SET. This is a base-level physiological remodelling — it cannot be rushed with a single week of hard training.

---

### Integrating Speed Endurance into a Triathlon Plan

| Phase | Recommended SET Volume | Notes |
|-------|------------------------|-------|
| Base | None / minimal | Focus on aerobic development; high-intensity work before base is built leads to injury and burnout |
| Build | 1 × SET session/week | Begin 6-week block; running or cycling sprints |
| Peak | 1–2 × SET session/week | Maintain NHE1 adaptation; race-specific effort simulation |
| Taper | Reduce volume, maintain intensity | 1 × reduced SET session; keep neuromuscular sharpness |

**Best discipline for SET:** Running (highest acid load per sprint) or cycling (controlled environment). Swim sprints also produce NHE1 response but are less studied.

### General Recovery Principles (Inferred from Research Across All Sources)

While the Ironman.com recovery article was inaccessible, the following principles are consistent across the published research in this collection:

**Acute Recovery (0–24 hrs post-race or hard session)**
- Prioritise carbohydrate + protein within 30–60 min (0.3–0.5 g/kg carbs, 20–30 g protein)
- Rehydrate to pre-exercise weight + 150% of fluid lost
- Passive rest and sleep — avoid additional training load in first 12–24 hrs after a race or very hard session

**Sleep**
- The most powerful recovery intervention available
- 8–10 hrs/night is optimal for athletes in heavy training
- HRV is suppressed with poor sleep — a suppressed morning HRV is a reliable signal that intensity should be reduced that day (from PMC9473708 individualized training data)

**Active Recovery**
- Z1 swimming, cycling, or walking the day after a race/hard session promotes blood flow and metabolic waste clearance
- Avoid Z3+ until HRV and perceived recovery normalise (typically 48–72 hrs post-race for short-course, 7–14 days for long-course)

**Race Recovery Timelines**
| Race Distance | Minimum Recovery Before Next Hard Session |
|--------------|------------------------------------------|
| Sprint | 3–5 days |
| Olympic | 5–7 days |
| 70.3 Half Iron | 10–14 days |
| Ironman Full | 3–4 weeks |

These are minimums for returning to quality training, not for returning to easy volume (which can often resume sooner).

**Cold Water Immersion**
- Ice baths (10–15°C, 10–15 min) reduce acute muscle soreness and perceived fatigue
- Evidence is strongest for immediate post-session use; less clear for long-term adaptation
- Avoid cold immersion immediately after strength training — it blunts hypertrophy signalling

## 4 Ways to Recover Better After a Race (Ironman)

**Context:** Post-race and off-season recovery habits that pay dividends when training ramps back up. Primarily addresses long-course athletes.

### 1. Sleep — Volume and Quality
- **8–9 hrs/night** is the target for athletes in recovery or heavy training
- Sleep is routinely sacrificed during peak training — the off-season and recovery weeks are the time to re-establish the habit
- Sleep hygiene protocol:
  - No screens 60 min before bed
  - Fixed sleep and wake times (even weekends)
  - Invest in sleeping environment — mattress, blackout, temperature
- Sleep quality, not just quantity, matters — tracking via Oura/Whoop/Garmin helps identify sleep disruption

### 2. Nutrition — Anti-Inflammatory Focus
- **Anti-inflammatory diet** reduces recovery time and systemic inflammation following race stress:
  - Emphasise: berries, leafy greens, fatty fish (omega-3), nuts, olive oil
  - Reduce: processed foods, saturated fats, alcohol, refined sugar
- **Real food over convenience products** — gels and bars have their place during training, but recovery nutrition should prioritise whole foods
- Off-season / recovery period is the right time to experiment with dietary changes (vegan, gluten-free, etc.) — gives the body time to adapt before big training returns
- **Recovery window (0–60 min post-race):** 0.3–0.5 g/kg carbohydrate + 20–30 g protein

### 3. Plan, Track and Structure Recovery
- Build recovery weeks into every training block — **every 4th week should be a cutback week at ~50–60% of normal volume**
- Track: sleep duration, nutrition quality, mood, perceived fatigue, and HRV alongside training load
- A training log that includes recovery data reveals the connection between load and outcome — essential for spotting early signs of overtraining
- Account for life stress (work deadlines, family demands) as load — not just training volume

### 4. Gear Organisation and Maintenance
- Practical but easily overlooked: keeping kit organised reduces friction on early-morning sessions
- Clean and service bike after every race — salt, sweat, and road grime accelerate wear
- Learn basic maintenance (flat tyre, brake adjustment) to avoid race-day mechanical DNFs
- Use recovery time to research and evaluate new equipment — decisions made during rest are better than impulsive purchases mid-season

### Additional Recovery Principles (Off-Season / Post-Race)
- **Connect with family and support network** — the people who sustain your training during peak periods need time and attention in the recovery phase
- **Avoid filling recovery weeks with secondary sports that carry injury risk** — hiking and cycling are appropriate; competitive team sports with contact are not
- Use off-season to develop skills, not fitness — technique work (swimming drills, running form, power meter data analysis) has low fatigue cost and high long-term return
