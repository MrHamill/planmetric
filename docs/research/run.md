## 4 Key Sessions Every Runner Needs (Strava)

Four session types that cover the full physiological spectrum when programmed weekly.

### 1. Interval Tuesday — VO2max Development
**Purpose:** Raise aerobic ceiling, improve economy at race pace.

**Protocol:**
- 6–10 × 400m at 5km race pace (or faster)
- Recovery: equal time jogging (e.g. 90s work → 90s jog)
- Alternative: 4–6 × 1km at 10km race pace, 2 min jog recovery

**Coaching Notes:**
- First rep should feel achievable — the session accumulates fatigue across reps
- Consistency across reps matters more than the first few being fast
- Do not run faster than 5km race pace — harder is not better here
- Warm-up: minimum 10–15 min easy jog + 4×100m strides

### 2. Hill/Tempo Thursday — Threshold & Strength
**Purpose:** Improve lactate threshold, build leg power and running-specific strength.

**Option A — Hill Repeats:**
- 6–10 × 60–90s hard uphill effort
- Recovery: walk back down (~2–3 min)
- Feel: hard but repeatable — 8/10 effort
- Hills reduce impact stress while increasing intensity

**Option B — Tempo Run:**
- 20–40 min at comfortably hard pace (~threshold, T3)
- Should be able to speak 3–5 words at a time
- Or: 3 × 10 min threshold with 2 min jog recovery

**Coaching Notes:**
- Hill repeats + tempo on the same week is too much — choose one
- Tempo pace is roughly 15–20 sec/km slower than 10km race pace

### 3. Parkrun Saturday — Race-Specific Effort
**Purpose:** Practice race intensity and pacing, psychological familiarity with discomfort.

**Protocol:**
- 5km at race effort (not max effort — practice controlled pacing)
- Aim: negative split or even pace
- Treat as a quality workout, not a race

**Coaching Notes:**
- Parkrun provides the social/competitive stimulus that treadmill and solo running cannot replicate
- Best used in build phase, not every week during base
- Recovery window before Sunday long run: this is intentional accumulated fatigue

### 4. Long Run Sunday — Aerobic Base
**Purpose:** Build aerobic volume, fat oxidation, musculoskeletal durability, and mental resilience.

**Protocol:**
- 70–90 min to 2.5–3 hrs depending on goal race distance
- Pace: fully conversational (Zone 1–2), HR < 75% max
- Last 20–30 min can include marathon-pace miles for advanced runners

**Coaching Notes:**
- The most important session of the week — do not sacrifice it to compensate for a bad interval session
- Run on tired legs (after Saturday Parkrun) to simulate race fatigue
- No GPS watching — run by feel or HR only
- Consistency across months, not pace, is the measure of long run success

### Weekly Template
| Day | Session | Type |
|-----|---------|------|
| Monday | Easy 30–45 min or rest | Recovery |
| Tuesday | Intervals (VO2max) | Quality |
| Wednesday | Easy 30–45 min | Aerobic |
| Thursday | Tempo or hills | Quality |
| Friday | Rest or easy jog | Recovery |
| Saturday | Parkrun / race effort | Quality |
| Sunday | Long run | Aerobic Volume |

## Marathon Weekly Volume Guidelines (Luke Humphrey Running)

### Volume by Goal Time

| Goal | Average Weekly Volume | Peak Week Volume |
|------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Sub-3:00 | 55–60 miles (88–97 km) | 65–70 miles (104–112 km) |
| 3:00–3:30 | 45–55 miles (72–88 km) | 55–65 miles (88–104 km) |
| 3:30–4:00 | 35–45 miles (56–72 km) | 45–55 miles (72–88 km) |
| 4:00+ / First-timer | 25–35 miles (40–56 km) | 35–45 miles (56–72 km) |

### Build Principles
- **10% rule:** Do not increase weekly volume by more than 10% from one week to the next
- Every **3rd or 4th week** should be a cutback week at 60–70% of previous week's volume
- Peak volume should be reached **3–4 weeks before race day**, then taper
- Long run should represent **25–35% of weekly volume** maximum

### Quality vs. Volume Balance
- 80% of runs should be easy (Zone 1–2) regardless of goal
- 2 quality sessions/week maximum for most athletes — 3 only for advanced runners
- Every run does not need a purpose beyond "easy aerobic accumulation"

### Training Paces (Based on Recent 5km or 10km Time)
- Easy run: 60–75 sec/km slower than marathon goal pace
- Long run: 30–60 sec/km slower than marathon goal pace
- Threshold: 10–15 sec/km slower than marathon goal pace
- Interval (VO2max): 5km race pace

### Taper Guidelines
- Final 3 weeks: reduce volume by 20% → 40% → 50% (peak to race week)
- Maintain intensity — do not reduce pace, only volume
- Some "race sharpness" sessions (short, fast strides) in race week keep legs responsive

## 20-Week Endurance + Strength Training Study (MDPI Applied Sciences, 2025)

**Context:** 18 nationally-trained long-distance runners (sub-35 min 10km for males) followed a 20-week combined endurance and strength programme with a pyramidal intensity distribution. Assessed for changes in running economy (RE), VO2max, and biomechanics.

### Training Structure Used
- ~80% of volume in Zone 1 (continuous running at ~60% MAS)
- ~14% in Zone 2 (80–90% MAS intervals)
- ~6% in Zone 3 (>100% MAS, VO2max intervals)
- Strength: 2–3× per week, medium loads (50–70% 1RM) + plyometrics + core + 50–100m sprint reps

### Results
| Metric | Change | Significance |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| VO2max | +4.6% | p < 0.05 |
| Maximal Aerobic Speed (MAS) | +2.8% | p = 0.004 |
| Speed at VT1 (aerobic threshold) | +9.4% | p < 0.001 |
| Speed at VT2 (anaerobic threshold) | +3.7% | p = 0.004 |
| Running Economy (oxygen cost) | −4% (worse) | p = 0.011 |
| Biomechanics (stride, contact time) | No change | n.s. |

### Key Finding: RE vs. VO2max Tradeoff
- Running economy (oxygen cost at fixed speed) **worsened by ~4%**, despite VO2max improving
- This is expected: as VO2max increases, the same absolute speed requires proportionally less of a higher VO2max — but the absolute oxygen cost at that fixed speed increases
- **Net effect is performance improvement** — faster at the same RPE, higher VT speeds
- The inverse RE/VO2max relationship is well-documented in elite cyclists and runners

### Implications for Plan Design
- Combining endurance + strength training for 20 weeks produces significant VO2max and threshold gains in trained runners — even those already at national level
- **Strength training (medium load + plyometrics)** appears to be an important component — not just aerobic volume
- Pyramidal intensity distribution (80/14/6) produced better outcomes than comparable polarized or threshold-only approaches in this population
- Biomechanical efficiency did not change, suggesting **physiological adaptation precedes mechanical adaptation** — economy improvements likely appear after 6+ months

### Practical Application
- Include 2–3 strength sessions/week during the base and build phases
- Focus: core strength, plyometrics, leg strength at 50–70% 1RM, short sprint reps (50–100m)
- This approach is compatible with triathlon training — bike and run volume can be maintained
- Do not expect running economy (and feel of running) to improve in the first 12–20 weeks — VO2max and threshold gains come first

## Running Cadence

Cadence = steps per minute (both feet). At a given pace, higher cadence with shorter stride length reduces forces at ankles, knees, and hips — widely believed to reduce injury risk.

### Key Numbers
- Often-cited target: **180 steps per minute**
- Taller runners tend to have somewhat slower cadence naturally
- Efficient range for most runners: **170–180 spm**
- Applies to: All

### Associated Benefits
- Lower **vertical oscillation** (less energy wasted bouncing)
- Shorter **ground contact time** (more efficient force application)
- Reduced impact forces throughout the lower body
- Applies to: All

### Coaching Notes
- Cadence can only be increased so far — focus on gradual improvement, not forcing 180 spm immediately
- Particularly beneficial for injury-prone runners as a form correction tool
- On a treadmill, runners tend to slightly reduce cadence and increase stride length — worth monitoring

## Running Form Cues

### Posture & Core
- **Run tall:** head up, looking straight ahead, chest open for better lung capacity
- **Relaxed shoulders:** keep down and away from ears to prevent tension buildup
- **Engage core:** maintain a neutral spine and stable hips to prevent lower back pain and maintain alignment
- Applies to: All

### Forward Lean
- Lean slightly forward (**8–10 degrees**) from the **ankles**, not the waist — uses gravity to drive momentum
- Bending at the waist leads to poor posture and inefficient, heavy strides
- Applies to: All

### Arm Drive
- Keep elbows bent at roughly **90 degrees** or slightly less
- Swing arms **forward and backward** — avoid excessive side-to-side rotation or crossing the midline
- Keep hands **loosely cupped** to avoid tension through arms and shoulders
- Applies to: All

### Foot Strike & Stride
- **Land underneath:** foot should strike directly underneath the hips, not in front of the body
- **Midfoot landing:** aim for a soft, quiet, midfoot strike to minimise impact — avoid heavy heel-striking
- **Avoid overstriding:** reaching too far forward acts as a brake and increases impact forces
- Applies to: All

## Interval Training Types

Three core interval formats that develop different physiological systems.

### 1. Track Sessions (Structured Intervals)
**Purpose:** Develop anaerobic capacity, speed, and efficiency.
- Highly structured, done on a track or flat surface for precise pacing
- Sample: 6–10 × 400m at 5km goal pace, 2–3 min easy jog/walk recovery
- **Key:** consistency across all reps — don't go too fast early and fade
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### 2. Tempo Runs (Threshold Work)
**Purpose:** Increase lactate threshold — hold a faster pace for longer.
- Sustained effort at **25–30 sec/km slower than 5km race pace** (roughly 1-hour race pace)
- Sample: 10 min warm-up → 20–30 min at threshold pace → 10 min cool-down
- Should be challenging but sustainable — not a race effort
- Applies to: All

### 3. Fartlek (Speed Play)
**Purpose:** Improve speed endurance and mental toughness via varied pace changes.
- Blends high-intensity work with light active recovery in a continuous run
- Sample (landmarks): run fast to a telephone pole, light jog to a tree, repeat for 20 min
- Sample (time-based): 10 × (1 min hard, 1 min easy)
- **Key:** no strict rules — vary pace and effort by feel, not by watch
- Applies to: All

> **Note:** These three interval types overlap with the "4 Key Sessions" section above. Track sessions ≈ Interval Tuesday, tempo runs ≈ Tempo Thursday. Fartlek is an additional unstructured format not covered there.

## Long Run Structure for Triathlon (Chris Hauth / Ironman)

### Aerobic Long Run
- Triathletes gain aerobic conditioning from swim and bike — the long run focuses on **time on feet**, mitochondrial density, capillary development, and muscle/tendon strength
- Typically once per week at **low-intensity conversational pace**
- Applies to: All

### Aerobic Long Run with Variation

**Workout 1 — Late tempo intervals:**
60–90 min easy running. Final 30 min: alternate 5 min at tempo (15–30 sec/km faster than race pace) with 5 min easy.

**Workout 2 — Short speed bursts:**
90 min to 2 hrs easy. Insert pyramid bursts: 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 50, 40, 30, 20, 10 sec faster with 1–2 min easy between each. Repeat 2×. Strong effort with great form, not all-out.

**Workout 3 — Fartlek-style long run:**
Alternate ~30 sec/km faster than race pace for 3–5 km with ~30 sec/km slower for 3–5 km. Repeat 2–3×.

- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### Higher Intensity Long Run

**Track + Race Pace Combo (Hauth's favourite):**
20 min easy warm-up → 10–20 × 400m slightly faster than goal race pace, 30 sec recovery → 5–13 km at race pace.
- Start with 10 × 400m + 5 km run; build to 20 × 400m + 13 km over several weeks
- **Purpose:** trains body to switch between anaerobic and aerobic systems, then settle into race pace after hard effort
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

## Running Off the Bike (Brick Run Workouts)

Running off the bike replicates race conditions — legs must adapt to running tired.

### Workout 1 — Progressive intervals off the bike
After a harder bike set: 12 × 2 min progressive (each 30 sec block gets faster).
Example at 4:58/km race pace: 30 sec at 5:35, 30 sec at 5:17, 30 sec at 4:58, 30 sec at 4:40. 2 min easy recovery between reps.
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### Workout 2 — Steady state off a long ride
After a longer ride: 30–40 min run. Hit race pace for the first 10 min, then settle into easier steady-state pace. Practises finding race-pace rhythm immediately off the bike.
- Applies to: All

### Workout 3 — Cruise intervals off a long ride (Hauth)
After a 130–145 km ride: 6 × 1.6 km repeats. Start each interval on goal pace split — if goal is 4:40/km and you run 1.6 km in 7:00, you get 30 sec rest before next rep.
- Applies to: Elite

### Coaching Notes
- Aim for 1–2 brick runs per week during build phase
- Some athletes naturally run well off the bike — often linked to good bike pacing and bike fit
- Good posture and form are critical regardless of speed or fatigue level

## Triathlon Run Pacing (The Brick Phase)

### First 1.6 km — The "Brick" Phase
- Start **6–12 sec/km slower** than goal average pace
- Allows legs to transition from cycling to running motion
- Focus on **high cadence (90+ steps per foot per minute / 180+ spm)** — fast turnover with less force
- Going too fast here leads to severe slowdown later
- Applies to: All

### Middle Section
- Settle into target pace based on **RPE, not watch pace**
- Long course (Ironman/70.3): RPE **5–6/10**
- Short course (Olympic/Sprint): RPE **8–9/10**
- Applies to: All

### Final 1.6 km
- If energy remains, increase pace for a negative split
- If paced correctly, you will be passing athletes who started too fast
- Applies to: All

## Race-Specific Pacing Strategies

### 5K Pacing

**First 2 km — Controlled Speed:**
- Start at target pace immediately. Natural to go slightly faster in first 500m due to adrenaline — stay within 1–2 sec/km of target
- If first 1 km is more than 3 sec/km faster than goal pace, pull back
- Applies to: All

**2–4 km — Dig Deep:**
- Expect discomfort — this is where the 5K gets tough
- Maintain or slightly increase pace. Stay mentally focused — many runners slow here without realising
- Applies to: All

**Final 1 km — All Out:**
- Empty the tank. Push pace in final 500m–1 km and sprint the finish
- Applies to: All

### 10K Pacing

**Target pace range:** allow 3–4 sec/km buffer either side of goal pace.

**First 3 km:** Start ~5 sec/km slower than target pace. First km may be quick from excitement — dial back. Effort should feel fast but relaxed, breathing controlled.

**4–7 km:** Gradually increase to target race pace. Avoid surging — use other runners to pull you along. Hold pace if unsure.

**Final 3 km:** Pick your moment to push. If feeling good, push from 7 km. If struggling, wait until closer to the finish. From 9 km, empty the tank.
- Applies to: All

### Half Marathon Pacing

**Target pace range:** 3–4 sec/km buffer either side of goal pace. Break into three 7 km sections.

**First 7 km (the "free" section):** Fresh legs — should feel relatively easy at target pace. Do not get ahead of yourself and run too fast.

**Second 7 km (the "hard" section):** Fatigue building. Aim to maintain pace. If holding back, allow 5 sec/km increase. At 14 km you're two-thirds done — use that mental boost.

**Final 7 km (the "fun" section):** Physically tired but mentally it's the final stretch. If anything left in the tank, increase pace for the full section, not just the last 1 km.

**Final 500m:** Reward yourself — push to the finish line.
- Applies to: All

### Marathon Pacing

**Strategy:** Negative split — run second half slightly faster than first half.

**0–21 km (first half):** Should feel comfortable, like cruising. Stick to the slower side of target pace. For a 3:30 marathon (4:58/km), don't run faster than ~5:03/km. Stay on top of fuelling from the start — gel every 30–35 min (60g carbs/hour).

**21–36 km:** The real start of the marathon. Break it down km by km. If feeling good, pick up pace by ~5 sec/km max. Keep assessing — if unsure, hold pace. Countdown to next gel as a mental boost.

**Final 6 km:** Push harder. Everyone feels heavy — so does the competition. Tick off each km. Use crowd energy. Pick off runners ahead. Final 1 km: give everything.
- Applies to: All

> **Note:** The marathon fuelling guidance (60g carbs/hour) aligns with current nutrition research — see nutrition.md for full fuelling protocols.

## Treadmill vs Outdoor Running

### Muscle Activation Differences
- Treadmill: flat, straight-line running activates **fewer stabiliser muscles** than outdoor running
- Outdoor: dodging obstacles, uneven surfaces, curbs, and turns condition more muscles and improve agility
- Consistent treadmill-only use can develop muscular imbalances and increase injury risk when returning outdoors
- Applies to: All

### Impact & Bone Health
- Outdoor running on firm surfaces (concrete, tarmac) creates high ground reaction forces that **strengthen bones** — research from University of Missouri shows outdoor running may be more beneficial for bone health than resistance training
- Treadmills absorb ground reaction forces — gentler on joints but less bone-strengthening stimulus
- For athletes with impact-related injuries (shin splints, stress fractures), treadmill running reduces joint stress
- Applies to: All

### Technique Differences
- Running form is nearly identical indoors and outdoors after 4–6 min of settling into natural gait
- Only difference: treadmill runners tend to **increase stride length and reduce cadence slightly** — nothing to respond to, so stride is uninterrupted
- Applies to: All

### Treadmill Limitations
- No downhill running capability on most treadmills — reduces quad/anterior leg conditioning
- No turns or terrain variation — limits agility development
- Can become monotonous on longer runs
- Applies to: All

### Practical Application
- Outdoor running is superior for race preparation (muscle conditioning, bone health, terrain adaptation)
- Treadmill is valuable for: controlled pace work, injury management, bad weather, and time-efficient sessions
- Rotate between treadmill, road, and soft terrain to distribute mechanical stress across tissues
- Applies to: All

## Running Injury Prevention for Triathletes (Scientific Triathlon, TTS684)

### Most Common Injuries
- Muscle strains, lower-limb tendinopathies (especially Achilles), and bone stress injuries
- Applies to: All

### Primary Risk Factors
- **Training load and intensity:** high-intensity sessions demand elevated physical readiness
- **Systemic stressors:** life stress, sleep deprivation, inadequate fuelling, hormonal disruptions — the athlete is "an entire ecosystem, not isolated variables"
- **Energy availability:** under-fuelling is a critical pathway to bone stress injuries, especially for athletes with demanding work + high training load
- Applies to: All

### Prevention Protocols
- **Strength training (minimal effective dose):** compound movements + plyometrics for bone health. Avoid programs with excessive metabolic cost relative to fuelling capacity
- **Training surface variability:** rotate between treadmill, road, soft terrain, and rolling courses to distribute mechanical stress
- **Deload weeks:** planned volume/intensity reductions allow skeletal tissue to adapt — bones remodel slower than soft tissue
- **Footwear rotation:** varying shoe types encourages robust musculoskeletal adaptation
- Applies to: All

### Pain Management Guidelines

**Tendon issues (no bone stress history):**
- May continue training through stable pain (1–5/10) provided symptoms settle by next morning and movement patterns remain normal

**Bone stress concerns:**
- Pain tolerance is extremely narrow — maintain **0–1/10 maximum**
- Bone-related symptoms are more volatile; early recognition prevents escalation
- Applies to: All

### Return-to-Running Protocol (Bone Stress Injuries)
**Prerequisites:** full pain-free daily activities + 45–60 min brisk walking (~5.1–5.6 km/h) on rolling terrain

**Progression:**
1. Begin: 1 min running + 4 min walking, repeated cycles
2. Repeat each level 2–3 times before advancing
3. Schedule non-consecutively (3 days/week with recovery days)
4. Progress deliberately — rushing increases recurrence risk
- Female athletes: verify menstrual function restoration, fuelling protocols, and appropriate blood work before progressing
- Applies to: All

### Recovery Misconceptions
- **Stretching shows no evidence** supporting injury prevention in endurance athletes — most hold stretches for insufficient duration (10–15 sec) for physiological change
- Recovery priorities: **sleep > nutrition > stress management > consistency** — these outperform modalities like foam rolling
- Applies to: All

### Readiness Framework
- High-intensity sessions require high readiness
- Under-fuelled, sleep-deprived, or fatigued states warrant pivoting to low-intensity work
- Protecting consistency is more important than forcing intensity
- Applies to: All
