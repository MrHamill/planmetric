# Running Training Research

## ⚡ PLAN GENERATION ESSENTIALS

**4 key session types (include all four in weekly structure):**
1. **Intervals (VO2max):** 6–10 × 400m at 5K pace, equal jog recovery — raises aerobic ceiling
2. **Tempo/hills (threshold):** 20–40 min at comfortably hard pace, OR 6–10 × 60–90 sec hill repeats
3. **Race-effort run:** controlled 5K effort (parkrun) — pacing practice, competitive stimulus
4. **Long run:** 70–90 min to 2.5–3 hrs, fully conversational Z1–Z2 — most important session of the week

**Long run peak distance targets:**
- Marathon: 30–35 km (cap at 3–3.5 hrs) | Half marathon: 18–21 km | 10K: 15–18 km | 5K: 12–15 km

**Marathon weekly volume by goal time:**
- Sub-3:00 → 88–97 km/wk peak | 3:00–3:30 → 72–88 km | 3:30–4:00 → 56–72 km | 4:00+ → 40–56 km

**Cadence:** 170–180 spm; reduces injury risk through lower ground contact forces and vertical oscillation

**Race pacing (build into coaching notes):**
- 5K: start at goal pace, push hard in final 1 km
- 10K: first 3 km at 5 sec/km slower; push from 7 km
- Half marathon: three 7 km sections — even effort, negative-split finish
- Marathon: run first half conservatively (5 sec/km slower than goal); fuel every 30–35 min; push from 36 km

**Injury prevention:** never back-to-back hard sessions; rotate surfaces; deload every 3–4 weeks; strength 2×/week in base phase; cadence correction for injury-prone runners

## 4 Key Sessions Every Runner Needs (Strava)

Four session types that cover the full physiological spectrum when programmed weekly.

### 1. Interval Tuesday — VO2max Development
**Purpose:** Raise aerobic ceiling, improve economy at race pace.

**Protocol:**
- 6–10 × 400m at 5km race pace (or faster)
- Recovery: equal time jogging (e.g. 90s work → 90s jog)
- Alternative: 4–6 × 1km at 10km race pace, 2 min jog recovery

**Coaching Notes:**
- First rep should feel achievable — the session accumulates fatigue across reps
- Consistency across reps matters more than the first few being fast
- Do not run faster than 5km race pace — harder is not better here
- Warm-up: minimum 10–15 min easy jog + 4×100m strides

### 2. Hill/Tempo Thursday — Threshold & Strength
**Purpose:** Improve lactate threshold, build leg power and running-specific strength.

**Option A — Hill Repeats:**
- 6–10 × 60–90s hard uphill effort
- Recovery: walk back down (~2–3 min)
- Feel: hard but repeatable — 8/10 effort
- Hills reduce impact stress while increasing intensity

**Option B — Tempo Run:**
- 20–40 min at comfortably hard pace (~threshold, T3)
- Should be able to speak 3–5 words at a time
- Or: 3 × 10 min threshold with 2 min jog recovery

**Coaching Notes:**
- Hill repeats + tempo on the same week is too much — choose one
- Tempo pace is roughly 15–20 sec/km slower than 10km race pace

### 3. Parkrun Saturday — Race-Specific Effort
**Purpose:** Practice race intensity and pacing, psychological familiarity with discomfort.

**Protocol:**
- 5km at race effort (not max effort — practice controlled pacing)
- Aim: negative split or even pace
- Treat as a quality workout, not a race

**Coaching Notes:**
- Parkrun provides the social/competitive stimulus that treadmill and solo running cannot replicate
- Best used in build phase, not every week during base
- Recovery window before Sunday long run: this is intentional accumulated fatigue

### 4. Long Run Sunday — Aerobic Base
**Purpose:** Build aerobic volume, fat oxidation, musculoskeletal durability, and mental resilience.

**Protocol:**
- 70–90 min to 2.5–3 hrs depending on goal race distance
- Pace: fully conversational (Zone 1–2), HR < 75% max
- Last 20–30 min can include marathon-pace miles for advanced runners

**Coaching Notes:**
- The most important session of the week — do not sacrifice it to compensate for a bad interval session
- Run on tired legs (after Saturday Parkrun) to simulate race fatigue
- No GPS watching — run by feel or HR only
- Consistency across months, not pace, is the measure of long run success

### Weekly Template
| Day | Session | Type |
|-----|---------|------|
| Monday | Easy 30–45 min or rest | Recovery |
| Tuesday | Intervals (VO2max) | Quality |
| Wednesday | Easy 30–45 min | Aerobic |
| Thursday | Tempo or hills | Quality |
| Friday | Rest or easy jog | Recovery |
| Saturday | Parkrun / race effort | Quality |
| Sunday | Long run | Aerobic Volume |

## Marathon Weekly Volume Guidelines (Luke Humphrey Running)

### Volume by Goal Time

| Goal | Average Weekly Volume | Peak Week Volume |
|------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Sub-3:00 | 55–60 miles (88–97 km) | 65–70 miles (104–112 km) |
| 3:00–3:30 | 45–55 miles (72–88 km) | 55–65 miles (88–104 km) |
| 3:30–4:00 | 35–45 miles (56–72 km) | 45–55 miles (72–88 km) |
| 4:00+ / First-timer | 25–35 miles (40–56 km) | 35–45 miles (56–72 km) |

### Build Principles
- **10% rule:** Do not increase weekly volume by more than 10% from one week to the next
- Every **3rd or 4th week** should be a cutback week at 60–70% of previous week's volume
- Peak volume should be reached **3–4 weeks before race day**, then taper
- Long run should represent **25–35% of weekly volume** maximum

### Quality vs. Volume Balance
- 80% of runs should be easy (Zone 1–2) regardless of goal
- 2 quality sessions/week maximum for most athletes — 3 only for advanced runners
- Every run does not need a purpose beyond "easy aerobic accumulation"

### Training Paces (Based on Recent 5km or 10km Time)
- Easy run: 60–75 sec/km slower than marathon goal pace
- Long run: 30–60 sec/km slower than marathon goal pace
- Threshold: 10–15 sec/km slower than marathon goal pace
- Interval (VO2max): 5km race pace

### Taper Guidelines
- Final 3 weeks: reduce volume by 20% → 40% → 50% (peak to race week)
- Maintain intensity — do not reduce pace, only volume
- Some "race sharpness" sessions (short, fast strides) in race week keep legs responsive

## 20-Week Endurance + Strength Training Study (MDPI Applied Sciences, 2025)

**Context:** 18 nationally-trained long-distance runners (sub-35 min 10km for males) followed a 20-week combined endurance and strength programme with a pyramidal intensity distribution. Assessed for changes in running economy (RE), VO2max, and biomechanics.

### Training Structure Used
- ~80% of volume in Zone 1 (continuous running at ~60% MAS)
- ~14% in Zone 2 (80–90% MAS intervals)
- ~6% in Zone 3 (>100% MAS, VO2max intervals)
- Strength: 2–3× per week, medium loads (50–70% 1RM) + plyometrics + core + 50–100m sprint reps

### Results
| Metric | Change | Significance |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| VO2max | +4.6% | p < 0.05 |
| Maximal Aerobic Speed (MAS) | +2.8% | p = 0.004 |
| Speed at VT1 (aerobic threshold) | +9.4% | p < 0.001 |
| Speed at VT2 (anaerobic threshold) | +3.7% | p = 0.004 |
| Running Economy (oxygen cost) | −4% (worse) | p = 0.011 |
| Biomechanics (stride, contact time) | No change | n.s. |

### Key Finding: RE vs. VO2max Tradeoff
- Running economy (oxygen cost at fixed speed) **worsened by ~4%**, despite VO2max improving
- This is expected: as VO2max increases, the same absolute speed requires proportionally less of a higher VO2max — but the absolute oxygen cost at that fixed speed increases
- **Net effect is performance improvement** — faster at the same RPE, higher VT speeds
- The inverse RE/VO2max relationship is well-documented in elite cyclists and runners

### Implications for Plan Design
- Combining endurance + strength training for 20 weeks produces significant VO2max and threshold gains in trained runners — even those already at national level
- **Strength training (medium load + plyometrics)** appears to be an important component — not just aerobic volume
- Pyramidal intensity distribution (80/14/6) produced better outcomes than comparable polarized or threshold-only approaches in this population
- Biomechanical efficiency did not change, suggesting **physiological adaptation precedes mechanical adaptation** — economy improvements likely appear after 6+ months

### Practical Application
- Include 2–3 strength sessions/week during the base and build phases
- Focus: core strength, plyometrics, leg strength at 50–70% 1RM, short sprint reps (50–100m)
- This approach is compatible with triathlon training — bike and run volume can be maintained
- Do not expect running economy (and feel of running) to improve in the first 12–20 weeks — VO2max and threshold gains come first

## Running Cadence

Cadence = steps per minute (both feet). At a given pace, higher cadence with shorter stride length reduces forces at ankles, knees, and hips — widely believed to reduce injury risk.

### Key Numbers
- Often-cited target: **180 steps per minute**
- Taller runners tend to have somewhat slower cadence naturally
- Efficient range for most runners: **170–180 spm**
- Applies to: All

### Associated Benefits
- Lower **vertical oscillation** (less energy wasted bouncing)
- Shorter **ground contact time** (more efficient force application)
- Reduced impact forces throughout the lower body
- Applies to: All

### Coaching Notes
- Cadence can only be increased so far — focus on gradual improvement, not forcing 180 spm immediately
- Particularly beneficial for injury-prone runners as a form correction tool
- On a treadmill, runners tend to slightly reduce cadence and increase stride length — worth monitoring

## Running Form Cues

### Posture & Core
- **Run tall:** head up, looking straight ahead, chest open for better lung capacity
- **Relaxed shoulders:** keep down and away from ears to prevent tension buildup
- **Engage core:** maintain a neutral spine and stable hips to prevent lower back pain and maintain alignment
- Applies to: All

### Forward Lean
- Lean slightly forward (**8–10 degrees**) from the **ankles**, not the waist — uses gravity to drive momentum
- Bending at the waist leads to poor posture and inefficient, heavy strides
- Applies to: All

### Arm Drive
- Keep elbows bent at roughly **90 degrees** or slightly less
- Swing arms **forward and backward** — avoid excessive side-to-side rotation or crossing the midline
- Keep hands **loosely cupped** to avoid tension through arms and shoulders
- Applies to: All

### Foot Strike & Stride
- **Land underneath:** foot should strike directly underneath the hips, not in front of the body
- **Midfoot landing:** aim for a soft, quiet, midfoot strike to minimise impact — avoid heavy heel-striking
- **Avoid overstriding:** reaching too far forward acts as a brake and increases impact forces
- Applies to: All

## Interval Training Types

Three core interval formats that develop different physiological systems.

### 1. Track Sessions (Structured Intervals)
**Purpose:** Develop anaerobic capacity, speed, and efficiency.
- Highly structured, done on a track or flat surface for precise pacing
- Sample: 6–10 × 400m at 5km goal pace, 2–3 min easy jog/walk recovery
- **Key:** consistency across all reps — don't go too fast early and fade
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### 2. Tempo Runs (Threshold Work)
**Purpose:** Increase lactate threshold — hold a faster pace for longer.
- Sustained effort at **25–30 sec/km slower than 5km race pace** (roughly 1-hour race pace)
- Sample: 10 min warm-up → 20–30 min at threshold pace → 10 min cool-down
- Should be challenging but sustainable — not a race effort
- Applies to: All

### 3. Fartlek (Speed Play)
**Purpose:** Improve speed endurance and mental toughness via varied pace changes.
- Blends high-intensity work with light active recovery in a continuous run
- Sample (landmarks): run fast to a telephone pole, light jog to a tree, repeat for 20 min
- Sample (time-based): 10 × (1 min hard, 1 min easy)
- **Key:** no strict rules — vary pace and effort by feel, not by watch
- Applies to: All

> **Note:** These three interval types overlap with the "4 Key Sessions" section above. Track sessions ≈ Interval Tuesday, tempo runs ≈ Tempo Thursday. Fartlek is an additional unstructured format not covered there.

## Long Run Structure for Triathlon (Chris Hauth / Ironman)

### Aerobic Long Run
- Triathletes gain aerobic conditioning from swim and bike — the long run focuses on **time on feet**, mitochondrial density, capillary development, and muscle/tendon strength
- Typically once per week at **low-intensity conversational pace**
- Applies to: All

### Aerobic Long Run with Variation

**Workout 1 — Late tempo intervals:**
60–90 min easy running. Final 30 min: alternate 5 min at tempo (15–30 sec/km faster than race pace) with 5 min easy.

**Workout 2 — Short speed bursts:**
90 min to 2 hrs easy. Insert pyramid bursts: 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 50, 40, 30, 20, 10 sec faster with 1–2 min easy between each. Repeat 2×. Strong effort with great form, not all-out.

**Workout 3 — Fartlek-style long run:**
Alternate ~30 sec/km faster than race pace for 3–5 km with ~30 sec/km slower for 3–5 km. Repeat 2–3×.

- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### Higher Intensity Long Run

**Track + Race Pace Combo (Hauth's favourite):**
20 min easy warm-up → 10–20 × 400m slightly faster than goal race pace, 30 sec recovery → 5–13 km at race pace.
- Start with 10 × 400m + 5 km run; build to 20 × 400m + 13 km over several weeks
- **Purpose:** trains body to switch between anaerobic and aerobic systems, then settle into race pace after hard effort
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

## Running Off the Bike (Brick Run Workouts)

Running off the bike replicates race conditions — legs must adapt to running tired.

### Workout 1 — Progressive intervals off the bike
After a harder bike set: 12 × 2 min progressive (each 30 sec block gets faster).
Example at 4:58/km race pace: 30 sec at 5:35, 30 sec at 5:17, 30 sec at 4:58, 30 sec at 4:40. 2 min easy recovery between reps.
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### Workout 2 — Steady state off a long ride
After a longer ride: 30–40 min run. Hit race pace for the first 10 min, then settle into easier steady-state pace. Practises finding race-pace rhythm immediately off the bike.
- Applies to: All

### Workout 3 — Cruise intervals off a long ride (Hauth)
After a 130–145 km ride: 6 × 1.6 km repeats. Start each interval on goal pace split — if goal is 4:40/km and you run 1.6 km in 7:00, you get 30 sec rest before next rep.
- Applies to: Elite

### Coaching Notes
- Aim for 1–2 brick runs per week during build phase
- Some athletes naturally run well off the bike — often linked to good bike pacing and bike fit
- Good posture and form are critical regardless of speed or fatigue level

## Triathlon Run Pacing (The Brick Phase)

### First 1.6 km — The "Brick" Phase
- Start **6–12 sec/km slower** than goal average pace
- Allows legs to transition from cycling to running motion
- Focus on **high cadence (90+ steps per foot per minute / 180+ spm)** — fast turnover with less force
- Going too fast here leads to severe slowdown later
- Applies to: All

### Middle Section
- Settle into target pace based on **RPE, not watch pace**
- Long course (Ironman/70.3): RPE **5–6/10**
- Short course (Olympic/Sprint): RPE **8–9/10**
- Applies to: All

### Final 1.6 km
- If energy remains, increase pace for a negative split
- If paced correctly, you will be passing athletes who started too fast
- Applies to: All

## Race-Specific Pacing Strategies

### 5K Pacing

**First 2 km — Controlled Speed:**
- Start at target pace immediately. Natural to go slightly faster in first 500m due to adrenaline — stay within 1–2 sec/km of target
- If first 1 km is more than 3 sec/km faster than goal pace, pull back
- Applies to: All

**2–4 km — Dig Deep:**
- Expect discomfort — this is where the 5K gets tough
- Maintain or slightly increase pace. Stay mentally focused — many runners slow here without realising
- Applies to: All

**Final 1 km — All Out:**
- Empty the tank. Push pace in final 500m–1 km and sprint the finish
- Applies to: All

### 10K Pacing

**Target pace range:** allow 3–4 sec/km buffer either side of goal pace.

**First 3 km:** Start ~5 sec/km slower than target pace. First km may be quick from excitement — dial back. Effort should feel fast but relaxed, breathing controlled.

**4–7 km:** Gradually increase to target race pace. Avoid surging — use other runners to pull you along. Hold pace if unsure.

**Final 3 km:** Pick your moment to push. If feeling good, push from 7 km. If struggling, wait until closer to the finish. From 9 km, empty the tank.
- Applies to: All

### Half Marathon Pacing

**Target pace range:** 3–4 sec/km buffer either side of goal pace. Break into three 7 km sections.

**First 7 km (the "free" section):** Fresh legs — should feel relatively easy at target pace. Do not get ahead of yourself and run too fast.

**Second 7 km (the "hard" section):** Fatigue building. Aim to maintain pace. If holding back, allow 5 sec/km increase. At 14 km you're two-thirds done — use that mental boost.

**Final 7 km (the "fun" section):** Physically tired but mentally it's the final stretch. If anything left in the tank, increase pace for the full section, not just the last 1 km.

**Final 500m:** Reward yourself — push to the finish line.
- Applies to: All

### Marathon Pacing

**Strategy:** Negative split — run second half slightly faster than first half.

**0–21 km (first half):** Should feel comfortable, like cruising. Stick to the slower side of target pace. For a 3:30 marathon (4:58/km), don't run faster than ~5:03/km. Stay on top of fuelling from the start — gel every 30–35 min (60g carbs/hour).

**21–36 km:** The real start of the marathon. Break it down km by km. If feeling good, pick up pace by ~5 sec/km max. Keep assessing — if unsure, hold pace. Countdown to next gel as a mental boost.

**Final 6 km:** Push harder. Everyone feels heavy — so does the competition. Tick off each km. Use crowd energy. Pick off runners ahead. Final 1 km: give everything.
- Applies to: All

> **Note:** The marathon fuelling guidance (60g carbs/hour) aligns with current nutrition research — see nutrition.md for full fuelling protocols.

## Treadmill vs Outdoor Running

### Muscle Activation Differences
- Treadmill: flat, straight-line running activates **fewer stabiliser muscles** than outdoor running
- Outdoor: dodging obstacles, uneven surfaces, curbs, and turns condition more muscles and improve agility
- Consistent treadmill-only use can develop muscular imbalances and increase injury risk when returning outdoors
- Applies to: All

### Impact & Bone Health
- Outdoor running on firm surfaces (concrete, tarmac) creates high ground reaction forces that **strengthen bones** — research from University of Missouri shows outdoor running may be more beneficial for bone health than resistance training
- Treadmills absorb ground reaction forces — gentler on joints but less bone-strengthening stimulus
- For athletes with impact-related injuries (shin splints, stress fractures), treadmill running reduces joint stress
- Applies to: All

### Technique Differences
- Running form is nearly identical indoors and outdoors after 4–6 min of settling into natural gait
- Only difference: treadmill runners tend to **increase stride length and reduce cadence slightly** — nothing to respond to, so stride is uninterrupted
- Applies to: All

### Treadmill Limitations
- No downhill running capability on most treadmills — reduces quad/anterior leg conditioning
- No turns or terrain variation — limits agility development
- Can become monotonous on longer runs
- Applies to: All

### Practical Application
- Outdoor running is superior for race preparation (muscle conditioning, bone health, terrain adaptation)
- Treadmill is valuable for: controlled pace work, injury management, bad weather, and time-efficient sessions
- Rotate between treadmill, road, and soft terrain to distribute mechanical stress across tissues
- Applies to: All

## Running Injury Prevention for Triathletes (Scientific Triathlon, TTS684)

### Most Common Injuries
- Muscle strains, lower-limb tendinopathies (especially Achilles), and bone stress injuries
- Applies to: All

### Primary Risk Factors
- **Training load and intensity:** high-intensity sessions demand elevated physical readiness
- **Systemic stressors:** life stress, sleep deprivation, inadequate fuelling, hormonal disruptions — the athlete is "an entire ecosystem, not isolated variables"
- **Energy availability:** under-fuelling is a critical pathway to bone stress injuries, especially for athletes with demanding work + high training load
- Applies to: All

### Prevention Protocols
- **Strength training (minimal effective dose):** compound movements + plyometrics for bone health. Avoid programs with excessive metabolic cost relative to fuelling capacity
- **Training surface variability:** rotate between treadmill, road, soft terrain, and rolling courses to distribute mechanical stress
- **Deload weeks:** planned volume/intensity reductions allow skeletal tissue to adapt — bones remodel slower than soft tissue
- **Footwear rotation:** varying shoe types encourages robust musculoskeletal adaptation
- Applies to: All

### Pain Management Guidelines

**Tendon issues (no bone stress history):**
- May continue training through stable pain (1–5/10) provided symptoms settle by next morning and movement patterns remain normal

**Bone stress concerns:**
- Pain tolerance is extremely narrow — maintain **0–1/10 maximum**
- Bone-related symptoms are more volatile; early recognition prevents escalation
- Applies to: All

### Return-to-Running Protocol (Bone Stress Injuries)
**Prerequisites:** full pain-free daily activities + 45–60 min brisk walking (~5.1–5.6 km/h) on rolling terrain

**Progression:**
1. Begin: 1 min running + 4 min walking, repeated cycles
2. Repeat each level 2–3 times before advancing
3. Schedule non-consecutively (3 days/week with recovery days)
4. Progress deliberately — rushing increases recurrence risk
- Female athletes: verify menstrual function restoration, fuelling protocols, and appropriate blood work before progressing
- Applies to: All

### Recovery Misconceptions
- **Stretching shows no evidence** supporting injury prevention in endurance athletes — most hold stretches for insufficient duration (10–15 sec) for physiological change
- Recovery priorities: **sleep > nutrition > stress management > consistency** — these outperform modalities like foam rolling
- Applies to: All

### Readiness Framework
- High-intensity sessions require high readiness
- Under-fuelled, sleep-deprived, or fatigued states warrant pivoting to low-intensity work
- Protecting consistency is more important than forcing intensity
- Applies to: All

## Half Marathon Training Volume Progression

### Beginner (Goal: Finish)
- Frequency: 3–4 days/week
- Starting volume: 15–20 km/week
- Peak volume: 30–40 km/week
- Peak long run: 17–18 km (reached around week 9–11)
- Taper: reduce volume by 50–60% in final week
- Applies to: Beginner

**Progression:**
- Weeks 1–4: build endurance by increasing long run by 1–2 km/week
- Weeks 5–8: introduce 1× tempo run (20–30 min) and short intervals (e.g. 400m reps)
- Weeks 9–11: peak long run reaches 17–18 km
- Week 12: taper week

**Key sessions:** long slow run (Sunday), 2–3 easy/recovery runs, 1 optional strength session.

### Intermediate (Goal: PB or Target Time)
- Frequency: 4–5 days/week
- Starting volume: 30–40 km/week
- Peak volume: 50–70 km/week
- Peak long run: 18–20+ km
- Taper: 2 weeks to reduce fatigue
- Applies to: Intermediate

**Progression:**
- Base phase (4–6 weeks): consistent running building to 40–50 km/week
- Speed/strength phase: introduce intervals and tempo runs 2×/week
- Peak phase: long runs reach 18–20+ km
- Taper: 2-week reduction

**Key sessions:** 1× speed interval session, 1× tempo/threshold run, 1× long run with race-pace segments, 1–2 recovery runs, 1–2 strength training sessions.

### Key Principles (All Levels)
- **80/20 rule:** ~80% easy conversational pace, ~20% high-intensity (intervals/tempo)
- **Long run is the most important session:** builds from 60 min to nearly 2 hours (15–18 km beginner, 18–21 km intermediate)
- **Deload/recovery weeks:** every 3–5 weeks, reduce volume by 20–30%
- **Rest days:** at least 1, ideally 2, full rest or very light active recovery days per week
- **10% rule:** maximum weekly volume increase of ~10%
- Applies to: All

## Half Marathon 12-Week Training Block Structure

### Phase 1: Base Building & Consistency (Weeks 1–4)
- Focus: establish routine with easy-paced running, introduce light strength training
- Workouts: mostly easy aerobic runs, short runs (3–5 km), long run building from 8–10 km
- Goal: adapt body to regular training without overtraining
- Applies to: All

### Phase 2: Build & Peak Intensity (Weeks 5–9)
- Focus: increase weekly volume, introduce speed work (intervals/tempo) to improve aerobic capacity
- Workouts: 1–2 speed sessions/week (tempo runs or 400m intervals), long run growing to 15–18 km
- Goal: build speed, endurance, and confidence at race pace
- Applies to: All

### Phase 3: Taper & Race Prep (Weeks 10–12)
- Focus: reduce weekly volume to allow muscles to recover, ensure freshness for race day
- Longest run (18–20 km) is often in week 10 or 11
- Final week: very light, short easy efforts, 2–3 rest days before race
- Goal: recover and sharpen for peak performance
- Applies to: All

> **Cross-reference:** See `periodisation.md` for how these phases map to the Friel model and how shorter plans compress phase durations.

## Half Marathon Lactate Threshold Speed Work

Six specific threshold workout protocols. All follow the same principle: training at lactate threshold pace (comfortably hard, ~7–8 RPE) teaches the body to clear lactate more efficiently and sustain faster paces for longer.

### 1. Five-Minute Interval Workout
- 15–20 min warm-up at easy pace
- 6 × 5 min at threshold pace, 60–90 sec recovery between reps
- 15–20 min cooldown at easy pace
- **Why:** breaks help runners new to threshold work reflect and adjust pace between reps; many beginners can't hold threshold for 20–30 min continuously
- Applies to: Beginner / Intermediate

### 2. Eight-Minute Interval Workout
- 15–20 min warm-up at easy pace
- 2–5 × 8 min at threshold pace, 60–90 sec recovery between reps
- 15–20 min cooldown at easy pace
- **Scaling:** 5K runners may only need 2 reps; marathon runners benefit from 4–5 reps
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### 3. Steady-State Tempo Run
- 15–20 min warm-up at easy pace
- 20–30 min continuous at threshold pace
- 15–20 min cooldown at easy pace
- **Why:** eliminating breaks forces runners to battle through minor fatigue and maintain comfortably-hard pace — teaches the low-grade discomfort tolerance that distance running demands
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### 4. Long Progression Run (Marathon-Focused)
- 5–6.5 km at easy pace (3–4 RPE)
- 5–6.5 km at marathon pace (4–5 RPE)
- 20 min at lactate threshold pace (7–8 RPE)
- 5–6.5 km at marathon pace (4–5 RPE)
- 5–6.5 km at easy pace (3–4 RPE)
- Total: ~24 km — build up to this over several weeks
- **Why:** changing paces through a long run trains the body to maintain pace during the later stages of a race
- Applies to: Elite

### 5. Half Marathon Pace Progression (~14.5 km)
- 1.6 km at easy pace (3–4 RPE)
- 1.6 km at marathon pace (4–5 RPE)
- 1.6 km at half marathon pace (5–6 RPE)
- 4.8 km at lactate threshold pace (7–8 RPE)
- 1.6 km at half marathon pace (5–6 RPE)
- 1.6 km at marathon pace (4–5 RPE)
- 1.6 km at easy pace (3–4 RPE)
- **Why:** gradual pace increase and decrease simulates mid-race fatigue while sustaining threshold effort — builds confidence
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### 6. Hill Threshold Workout
- 15–20 min warm-up at easy pace
- 10 × 2 min uphill running (7–8 RPE), light jog back down after each rep
- 15–20 min cooldown at easy pace
- **Why:** combines threshold endurance gains with hill-specific leg and core strength. Run at threshold effort (not pace) — you'll be slower uphill but giving the same physiological effort
- Total uphill effort: ~20 min (matches the recommended threshold stimulus)
- Good option for runners not yet confident enough for a full steady-state tempo
- Applies to: Beginner / Intermediate

## 5K Training Plan — 8-Week VO2max Block

An 8-week plan for intermediate runners with a base of 3–4 sessions/week. Follows a "general-to-specific" progression: higher-volume VO2max work early, transitioning to 5K-specific race pace efforts.

### Phase Breakdown
| Weeks | Focus | Interval Type |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| 1–2 | Foundation & pace adaptation | 400m–600m repeats |
| 3–4 | Strength & speed endurance | 600m–800m repeats + tempo |
| 5 | Peak VO2max intensity | 800m–1000m repeats |
| 6 | 5K-specific training | 800m–1000m at goal pace |
| 7 | Final peak & taper begins | Reduced volume |
| 8 | Taper & race week | Short, sharp efforts only |

### Weekly Structure
| Day | Session |
|-----|---------|
| Monday | Rest or light cross-training |
| Tuesday | VO2max interval session |
| Wednesday | Rest or easy recovery run (5–6 km) |
| Thursday | Tempo run (20 min at comfortably hard pace) |
| Friday | Rest day |
| Saturday | Easy run (5–8 km) or rest |
| Sunday | Long run (9–13 km) at relaxed pace |

### VO2max Interval Progression (Tuesday Sessions)
10–15 min warm-up with light jogging and strides before every session.

| Week | Session | Recovery |
|------|---------|----------|
| 1 | 8–10 × 400m at goal 5K pace | 90 sec–2 min jog |
| 2 | 10–12 × 400m at goal 5K pace | 90 sec jog |
| 3 | 6–8 × 600m at goal 5K pace | 2 min jog |
| 4 | 4–5 × 800m slightly faster than 5K pace | 2–3 min jog |
| 5 | 5–6 × 1000m at goal 5K pace | 2–3 min jog |
| 6 | 4–5 × 1000m at goal 5K pace | 1:1 work-to-rest ratio |
| 7 | 3–4 × 1000m at goal 5K pace (taper) | 2 min jog |
| 8 | 4–5 × 400m at goal 5K pace (race week) | Full recovery |

### Additional VO2max Session Options
- **4 × 4 min:** 4 min hard (90–95% max HR), 3 min easy recovery. Repeat 4 times
- **30/30s (Billat Method):** 30 sec hard (above 5K pace), 30 sec easy. 10–13 reps per set, 3 min rest, 2–3 sets
- **Pyramid:** 400m–800m–1200m–800m–400m at 5K pace with equal distance recovery

### Key Notes
- VO2max intervals should feel "hard" — the pace you could sustain for a 10–12 min race, not an all-out sprint
- For 800m–1000m repeats, recovery should allow HR to drop ~50 bpm before next rep
- Taper: reduce volume (not intensity) in the final 7–10 days
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

## 10K Training Plan — Weekly Structure

A 10K plan typically spans 8–10 weeks with 3–5 sessions per week, building aerobic capacity and pace management through a mix of easy, tempo, and long runs.

### Weekly Schedule (4-Day Structure)
| Day | Session |
|-----|---------|
| Monday | Rest or very light cross-training (yoga, walk) |
| Tuesday | Speed/interval session (e.g. 5–6 × 800m fast, or 20 min tempo) |
| Wednesday | Rest or active recovery (cycling, swimming) |
| Thursday | Easy run (~4–6 km at conversational pace) |
| Friday | Rest |
| Saturday/Sunday | Long run (gradually increasing to 8–12 km) |

### Key Session Types
- **Long runs:** done on weekends, comfortable pace, distance increasing weekly. Build endurance foundation
- **Speed work (intervals):** short, fast reps (200m–800m) with jogging recovery. Improve running speed and VO2max
- **Tempo runs:** 20–30 min at comfortably hard pace (~10K race pace). Boost lactate threshold and sustained endurance
- **Cross-training:** low-impact options (cycling, swimming, elliptical) on off-days. Cardiovascular benefit without running impact
- **Rest days:** at least 1–2 per week. Critical for injury prevention and muscle recovery

### Progression Principles
- Long run builds from ~6 km to 10–12 km over the training block
- Speed work progresses from shorter reps (200m–400m) to longer reps (800m–1000m)
- Tempo run duration increases from 15 min to 25–30 min
- Recovery week every 3rd or 4th week (reduce volume by 20–30%)
- Final 7–10 days: taper — reduce volume, maintain intensity
- Applies to: Beginner / Intermediate

## Couch to 5K (C25K) Program Structure

A 9-week beginner running program designed to progress from zero running to a continuous 30-minute run (approximately 5 km). The structure emphasises gradual adaptation through run/walk intervals.

### Program Overview
- **Frequency:** 3 days per week with at least one rest day between runs
- **Session structure:** 5-minute brisk walk warm-up → intervals → 5-minute cool-down walk
- **Total duration:** 9 weeks (can be extended by repeating difficult weeks)
- Applies to: Beginner

### Weekly Progression

| Week | Intervals | Total Active Time |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| 1 | 60-sec run / 90-sec walk, repeated for 20 min | 20 min |
| 2 | 90-sec run / 2-min walk, repeated for 20 min | 20 min |
| 3 | 90-sec run / 90-sec walk, then 3-min run / 3-min walk (x2) | 20 min |
| 4 | 3-min run, 90-sec walk, 5-min run, 2.5-min walk, 3-min run, 90-sec walk, 5-min run | ~25 min |
| 5 (pivot week) | Day 1: 5-min run / 3-min walk (x3). Day 2: 8-min run / 5-min walk (x2). Day 3: 20-min continuous run | 20–25 min |
| 6 | Similar to week 5, ending with 25-min continuous run | 25 min |
| 7 | 25-min continuous runs | 25 min |
| 8 | 28-min continuous runs | 28 min |
| 9 | 30-min continuous run (5K goal) | 30 min |

### Key Principles
- **Pace:** run at conversational pace — the goal is to finish the intervals, not to run fast
- **Flexibility:** repeat any week that feels too difficult before advancing
- **Rest days:** critical for recovery — muscles repair and joints recover between sessions
- **Warm-up/cool-down:** never skip the 5-min brisk walk — prepares the body for high-impact exercise
- Applies to: Beginner

## Beginner Running Gear Guide

### Essential Gear (Do Not Skip)
- **Running shoes:** the most important investment — visit a specialty running store for gait analysis to find shoes with the right support, cushioning, and stability. Avoid regular gym sneakers
- **Running socks:** never cotton — use synthetic fibre or wool (polyester, nylon) to wick sweat and prevent blisters
- **Moisture-wicking shirt:** technical fabrics (polyester or nylon blends) that move sweat away from the body. Anything but cotton
- **Running shorts or leggings:** lightweight, ideally with inner liner to prevent chafing
- **Sports bra:** high-support, moisture-wicking (for women)
- Applies to: Beginner

### Nice-to-Have
- **Running belt:** holds phone, keys, water — eliminates carrying items in hands
- **Anti-chafe balm:** apply to inner thighs, underarms as distances increase
- **Hat or headband:** blocks sun, keeps sweat from eyes
- **Reflective gear/headlamp:** essential for early morning or evening visibility
- **App:** free apps (Nike Running Club, Strava) are sufficient — no GPS watch needed initially
- Applies to: Beginner

### Shoe Selection for Beginners
- **Comfort first:** should feel comfortable immediately — no "break-in" period
- **Size:** running shoes should be half-size to full-size larger than street shoes (feet swell during runs)
- **Type:** neutral shoes for normal gait; stability shoes for overpronation (feet rolling inward)
- **Heel-to-toe drop:** 6–8 mm is a good versatile starting point for beginners
- **Fit test:** thumb's width of space between toes and end of shoe; try on with running socks in the afternoon when feet are largest
- Applies to: Beginner

## Common Beginner Running Mistakes

### Training Errors
- **Too much, too soon:** increasing mileage or intensity too quickly leads to shin splints, runner's knee, or stress fractures
- **Running too fast all the time:** running at a hard pace constantly prevents aerobic base building and causes burnout — most runs should be at conversational pace
- **Ignoring rest days:** running every day prevents muscles and joints from repairing
- **Skipping run/walk method:** beginners often feel they must run continuously, but alternating running and walking builds stamina safely
- Applies to: Beginner

### Gear and Form Mistakes
- **Inappropriate shoes:** running in old, worn-out, or non-running shoes causes injuries
- **Cotton socks/clothing:** cotton retains moisture, causing blisters and chafing
- **Overstriding:** landing with foot too far ahead of the body acts as a brake and increases impact forces
- **Skipping warm-ups/cool-downs:** putting cold muscles under impact increases injury risk
- Applies to: Beginner

### Mental Mistakes
- **Comparing to others:** comparing pace or distance to seasoned runners leads to discouragement
- **Expecting linear progress:** fitness improvements take time — building a base requires patience
- **Running through sharp pain:** distinguishing between normal discomfort and injury is crucial — sharp, persistent pain means stop
- Applies to: Beginner

## Building a Running Habit

### Consistency Strategies
- **Habit stacking:** anchor the run to an existing habit (e.g. "after I brush my teeth, I put on my running shoes")
- **Start small:** begin with low mileage and easy effort to prevent burnout
- **Schedule runs:** treat them like work meetings — put them in the calendar
- **Reduce friction:** set out gear the night before
- **Commit to a habit window:** focus on consistent running for two weeks, then a month, to build long-term routine
- Applies to: Beginner

### Motivation and Accountability
- **Sign up for a race:** a scheduled event provides a definitive goal and builds accountability
- **External accountability:** having a specific running partner or appointment increases adherence by up to 95%

- **Identify your "why":** focus on mental health, physical health, or social benefits rather than performance metrics
- Applies to: Beginner

- **80/20 rule:** 80% of runs at easy/conversational pace, 20% at high intensity
- **Gradual frequency increase:** do not increase training days by more than one day every 6 weeks
- **Rest days are non-negotiable:** particularly important for injury prevention when starting
- Applies to: Beginner

## Conversational Pace and Zone 2 Running

### What Is Conversational Pace?
- Run at an effort where breathing is relaxed and easy — could even breathe through the nose — and you can hold a full conversation without getting out of breath
- At the end of a conversational-pace run, you should feel like you could have continued for longer
- The "talk test" is the simplest way to gauge effort without a heart rate monitor
- Compare to tempo runs (only short sentences possible) or high-intensity intervals (no words possible)
- Applies to: All

### Why It Works
- At easy aerobic pace, the body uses fat as its primary fuel source rather than glycogen
- Zone 2 running produces the majority of aerobic adaptation:
  - Increases capillary numbers and density (more blood flow to muscles)
  - Boosts fat oxidation efficiency
  - Builds heart muscle strength and stroke volume
  - Increases mass and distribution of mitochondria
  - Reduces injury risk as the body adapts gradually to running loads
- Over time, the body adapts so you can run further and faster at the same effort
- Applies to: All

### Evidence
- Eliud Kipchoge: out of 13 training sessions per week, 10 are at easy pace (4:00–5:00/km) — "he gets better at running fast mostly by running slowly"
- 2021 study (Journal of Strength & Conditioning Research): amount of easy training was key to predicting elite-level marathon performance
- Applies to: All

### Practical Tips
- If struggling to stay at conversational pace, remove the watch and focus on breathing and perceived effort
- If speed creeps up, ask: "Can I still hold a conversation without getting out of breath?" — if not, slow down
- Easy runs are ideal for running with friends or groups — slower pace enables social catch-up
- For maximum benefit, combine ~80% easy volume with 1–2 weekly sessions at VO2max or lactate threshold
- Applies to: All

## Walk/Run Strategies for First 5K (Jeffing Method)

### Core Principle
Start walk breaks from the first minute — do not wait until fatigued. Planned walking conserves energy for the final stretch.

### Recommended Ratios

| Level | Run | Walk |
|-------|-----|------|
| Complete beginner | 15–30 sec | 45–90 sec |
| Beginner | 1 min | 1–2 min |
| Intermediate beginner | 2–3 min | 1 min |
| Conservative (just starting exercise) | 5–8 sec | 25–30 sec |

### 7-Week Beginner Walk/Run Plan
- **Weeks 1–2:** alternate 15–30 sec running with 45–90 sec walking for 30 min
- **Weeks 3–4:** increase to 2 min running / 2 min walking
- **Weeks 5–6:** build to 8–18 min continuous running with walk breaks
- **Week 7 (race week):** run 20–25 min continuously or use 1:1 ratio; rest day before race
- Applies to: Beginner

### Race Day Execution
- **Start slow:** excitement causes beginners to go too fast — start slower than training pace
- **Position at the back:** avoid being forced into a faster pace by seasoned runners
- **Break it down:** view the 5K as three segments of ~1.7 km each rather than one long distance
- **Hydrate strategically:** use water stations as natural walk-break opportunities
- **Nothing new on race day:** wear tested shoes and clothes only
- **Walking form:** head up, shoulders relaxed, midfoot landing, arms pumping to keep HR elevated
- Applies to: Beginner

## Nose vs Mouth Breathing While Running

### Key Research Findings (PMC5466403)
- **Performance:** no significant difference in power output between nasal and oral breathing during high-intensity exercise — preference of the athlete should guide choice
- **Metabolic efficiency:** nasal breathing kept respiratory exchange ratio (RER) below 1.0, indicating reduced hyperventilation and more efficient gas exchange
- **Heart rate:** nasal breathing produced higher heart rates during maximal effort (greater cardiovascular stress), suggesting it is harder to sustain at very high intensities
- **Ventilatory efficiency:** nasal breathing required less ventilation (lower VeqO2) to produce the same work output
- Applies to: All

### Practical Guidelines
- **Easy runs (Zone 1–2):** nasal breathing is ideal — encourages slower pace, better CO2 tolerance, and confirms conversational effort. If you cannot breathe through your nose, you are running too hard for an easy day
- **Moderate effort (Zone 3):** combination of nose and mouth breathing is natural — inhale through nose, exhale through mouth
- **High intensity (Zone 4–5):** mouth breathing is necessary — air demand exceeds nasal capacity. Do not force nasal breathing during intervals or threshold work
- **General rule:** let intensity dictate — nose at easy pace, mouth opens as effort increases
- Applies to: All

## Rhythmic Breathing Patterns for Runners

### The Principle
Rhythmic breathing synchronises inhale/exhale cycles with footstrikes. The key insight: using an odd-count pattern (e.g. 3:2) means the exhale — when the diaphragm relaxes and the core is least stable — alternates between left and right foot, distributing impact stress evenly and reducing injury risk on one side.

### Breathing Ratios by Intensity

| Intensity | Pattern | Inhale (steps) | Exhale (steps) | Use When |
|-----------|---------|---------------|----------------|----------|
| Easy / Z1–Z2 | 3:3 or 3:2 | 3 | 3 or 2 | Long runs, recovery runs |
| Moderate / Z3 | 2:2 | 2 | 2 | Tempo runs, steady state |
| Hard / Z4–Z5 | 2:1 or 1:1 | 2 or 1 | 1 | Intervals, race pace, sprints |

### How to Practice
1. Start lying down — breathe deeply into the belly (diaphragmatic), feeling the abdomen rise before the chest
2. Walk while counting steps per inhale/exhale — establish the 3:2 rhythm
3. Progress to easy jogging at 3:2 — the exhale will naturally alternate between left and right foot
4. At higher intensities, allow the pattern to shift to 2:1 — do not fight it
5. If gasping or losing rhythm, slow down until the pattern re-establishes
- Applies to: All

## Diaphragmatic vs Chest Breathing for Runners

### Key Research Findings (PMC8967998)
- **Upper-thoracic (chest) breathing** is associated with increased work of breathing, hyperventilation, flow limitation, and higher metabolic cost
- **Diaphragmatic (belly) breathing** correlates with reduced resting heart rate, better gas exchange, improved alveolar ventilation, enhanced postural stability, and lower metabolic cost of breathing
- Applies to: All

### Why Diaphragmatic Breathing Is Superior
- The diaphragm is the primary breathing muscle — when it contracts, it pulls the lungs downward, creating larger lung volume and better air exchange
- Chest breathing recruits accessory muscles (neck, shoulders, upper back) that fatigue faster and waste energy
- Diaphragmatic breathing improves core stability during running — the diaphragm works as both a breathing muscle and a postural stabiliser
- Applies to: All

### How to Train Diaphragmatic Breathing
1. **Lie on back:** place one hand on chest, one on belly. Breathe so only the belly hand rises — chest stays still
2. **Seated practice:** same technique sitting upright — feel the ribcage expand laterally and the belly push outward
3. **Walking:** maintain belly breathing while walking at a brisk pace
4. **Easy running:** focus on belly expansion during the inhale phase — this is easier at conversational pace
5. **Integration:** combine with rhythmic breathing (3:2 pattern) once the diaphragmatic pattern feels natural
- **Target:** reducing breathing rate by 10–20% from peak during submaximal running improves efficiency, particularly for recreational runners experiencing exercise-induced breathlessness
- Applies to: All

## Side Stitches (Exercise-Related Transient Abdominal Pain)

### Prevalence
- ~70% of runners report experiencing a side stitch in the past year
- More common in younger, less experienced athletes
- Typically occurs on the right side of the abdomen
- Applies to: Beginner / Intermediate

### Causes
- **Primary theory:** irritation of the abdominal and pelvic cavity lining from movement and torso friction during running
- **Secondary theory:** changes in blood flow to the diaphragm during exercise
- **Triggers:** eating large meals before running, sugary sports drinks, high-fat or high-fibre foods pre-run, poor posture (rounded spine)
- Applies to: All

### Prevention
- Avoid eating or drinking heavily 1–3 hours before running
- Maintain upright posture — rounded spines increase occurrence
- Build fitness gradually — side stitches decrease with experience
- Skip sugary beverages before workouts
- Warm up progressively — do not start at full pace
- Practise diaphragmatic breathing to reduce shallow, erratic breathing patterns
- Applies to: All

### Treatment During a Run
1. Slow to a walk or reduce pace significantly
2. Take deep, slow breaths — exhale fully through pursed lips
3. Stretch: reach the arm on the affected side overhead and bend away from the pain
4. Apply gentle finger pressure to the painful area while bending slightly forward
5. Resume running once pain subsides — if it returns, slow down again
- Applies to: All

## Breathing in Cold Weather

### Why Cold Air Causes Difficulty
- Cold, dry air irritates the airways — the body must warm and humidify inhaled air before it reaches the lungs
- This process can trigger bronchospasm (temporary airway narrowing), causing tightness, coughing, or wheezing
- Runners with exercise-induced asthma are particularly affected below 0°C
- Applies to: All

### Practical Techniques
- **Breathe through the nose** at easy intensities — nasal passages warm and humidify air more effectively than the mouth
- **Use a buff or scarf** over the mouth and nose — traps warm, humid exhaled air and pre-warms the next inhale
- **Warm up indoors** for 5–10 min before heading outside — gradually exposes airways to cold air
- **Start slow:** begin at a very easy pace for the first 5–10 min to allow airways to adapt
- **Avoid deep, gasping breaths** — breathe steadily and rhythmically; shallow, controlled breathing reduces airway shock
- **Exhale through pursed lips** — slows the exhale and maintains pressure in the airways, reducing bronchospasm
- Applies to: All

### When to Be Cautious
- Temperatures below −15°C carry genuine risk of airway damage — consider indoor alternatives
- If coughing persists for more than 30 min after finishing, consult a doctor — may indicate exercise-induced bronchoconstriction
- Athletes with asthma should carry an inhaler and consider a pre-exercise bronchodilator in cold conditions
- Applies to: All
