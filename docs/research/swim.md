# Swimming Training Research

## ⚡ PLAN GENERATION ESSENTIALS

**CSS (Critical Swim Speed — the primary swim metric):**
- Formula: (400m time − 200m time) / 2 = CSS per 100m (threshold pace)
- Zones: Recovery = CSS +15 sec | Aerobic = CSS +8–14 sec | Threshold = CSS ±3 sec | VO2max = CSS −5–10 sec

**Session frequency impact:**
- 1/week: maintenance only (no improvement) | 2/week: no regression, no improvement | 3/week: improvement begins | 4+/week: significant improvement
- Session length: beginners 20–30 min (stop when form breaks down); intermediate+ 45–60 min

**Every swim session must include a drill component** — technique matters more than volume for triathletes

**Open water coaching notes (include for all triathlon plans):**
- Sight every 10–12 strokes (crocodile technique — eyes only, not full head lift)
- Draft 30–50 cm behind feet → saves 20–30% energy expenditure
- First 200m: slow stroke rate deliberately; focus on full exhale underwater; if panicking — roll to back, float, resume
- Cold shock: splash face/neck before entry if no warm-up swim is permitted
- Minimum 2–3 open water sessions before race day — pool fitness does not fully transfer (+15–20% energy cost outdoors)

**Wetsuit rules:**
- Compulsory <14°C | Permitted 14–22°C | Banned >22°C (always check specific event rules)
- Advantage: 2–4 sec/100m from buoyancy; adjust stroke cadence and entry angle with wetsuit on

**Session structure:** varied interval sets consistently outperform continuous swimming for pace and technique

## 10 Tips for Open Water Triathlon Swimming (Sydney Tri Group)

### 1. Sight Regularly and Efficiently
- Sight every 10–12 strokes in open water (more frequently in chop or around buoys)
- Lift only your eyes above water (not your head) — crocodile sighting technique
- Over-lifting causes your hips/legs to sink, breaking body position and costing energy
- Practise sighting in pool by targeting a point on the wall every 10 strokes

### 2. Draft Off Faster Swimmers
- Swimming directly behind a faster swimmer saves approximately **20–30% of energy expenditure**
- Optimal draft position: 30–50 cm behind the lead swimmer's feet
- Side-drafting (off the hip) saves ~15% — useful when you can't find the feet
- Practise bilateral breathing to spot drafting opportunities on both sides

### 3. Start Position Strategy
- Do not line up at the front unless you can sustain the initial pace — getting swum over causes panic and wasted energy
- Starting at the side of the wave avoids the central "washing machine" effect
- Wide start adds distance but reduces contact — often a net time gain for mid-pack swimmers
- Seed yourself by pool CSS or recent open water 750m time

### 4. Warm-Up Before the Swim
- Cold water and sudden immersion causes involuntary gasping (cold shock response)
- If no warm-up swim is allowed: enter water knee-deep and splash face/neck pre-start to acclimate
- A 5–10 min warm-up swim dramatically reduces the panic response in the first 200m

### 5. Control Breathing in the First 200m
- The first 200m of a triathlon swim is the highest risk for panic — HR spikes, adrenaline peaks
- Deliberately slow your stroke rate for the first 30–50 strokes
- Focus on the exhale — breathing out fully underwater reduces CO2 build-up
- If panic occurs: roll to your back, float, breathe, resume when calm

### 6. Wetsuit Fit and Legality
- Wetsuit provides ~2–4 sec/100m advantage from buoyancy alone
- Wetsuit must fit at the shoulders — shoulder restriction causes fatigue within 500m
- FINA/WTS wetsuit rules (also used by most triathlon federations):
  - **Permitted below 20°C (68°F)**
  - **Compulsory below 14°C (57.2°F)**
  - **Banned above 22°C (71.6°F)** in elite/competitive categories (varies by event)
  - Age-group rules may differ — check specific race rules

### 7. Swim Straight to Buoys
- Failure to sight costs significant distance — a 1° error over 1500m adds ~25m of swimming
- Before the race: identify your first sighting target (buoy, landmark, building) and memorise it
- Use the sun angle and current conditions to pre-plan sighting targets
- Do not sight immediately after turning a buoy — you'll sight back at the buoy you just passed

### 8. Bilateral Breathing
- Breathing on both sides (every 3 strokes or alternating patterns) prevents stroke imbalance
- Prevents unilateral shoulder overuse injury
- Essential for open water — allows you to sight and draft on both sides
- Develop in training by alternating 100s breathing every 3 vs. every 5 strokes

### 9. Entry Angle at T1
- Approach the exit ramp with momentum — don't slow down prematurely
- Begin unzipping wetsuit as soon as you can stand (use the tether cord)
- Practise wetsuit removal in training — common time loss for beginners in T1

### 10. Open Water vs. Pool Fitness
- Open water swimming requires 15–20% more energy than pool swimming at the same pace (no walls, no black line, current, chop, wetsuit)
- Minimum 2–3 open water swims before race day — pool fitness does not fully transfer
- Include at least one open water time trial in race-day gear before the event

## Critical Swim Speed (CSS) and Zone System (triq.ai)

### What is CSS?
Critical Swim Speed is the maximum speed you can sustain aerobically — roughly analogous to threshold pace in running. It is the most useful single metric for structuring swim training.

### Calculating CSS
**Field Test:**
1. Swim a **400m time trial** (maximal effort, record time)
2. Rest 10 minutes
3. Swim a **200m time trial** (maximal effort, record time)

**Formula:**
```
CSS (sec/100m) = (400m time − 200m time) / 2
```

**Example:**
- 400m: 7:20 (440 sec)
- 200m: 3:30 (210 sec)
- CSS = (440 − 210) / 2 = **115 sec/100m = 1:55/100m**

### CSS-Based Training Zones

| Zone | % CSS Pace | Purpose |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Recovery | >CSS + 15 sec/100m | Technique, easy aerobic |
| Aerobic | CSS + 8–14 sec/100m | Base aerobic volume |
| CSS (Threshold) | CSS ±3 sec/100m | Lactate threshold development |
| VO2max | CSS − 5–10 sec/100m | Aerobic ceiling development |
| Anaerobic | CSS − 15+ sec/100m | Short sprints, pace range |

### Re-test CSS
- Every 8–10 weeks during a training block
- CSS should improve 2–5 sec/100m over a 12-week focused training block

## Open Water Strategies (triq.ai)

### Buoy Navigation
- Do not cut corners sharply — the inside line is crowded; a wide arc is often faster
- Approach buoys at an angle that keeps your sighting consistent
- Slow your stroke slightly before a turn buoy and kick hard to rotate — don't grab the buoy

### Current and Chop Management
- Cross-current: aim slightly upstream so drift brings you to the correct line
- Chop: shorten your stroke and increase stroke rate — a long stroke breaks down in rough water
- Headwind/headchop: keep breathing to the downwind side to avoid inhaling water

### Pace Distribution
- Swim even pace or slightly negative split
- Most age-groupers start too fast — first 200m adrenaline spike leads to blowing up by 600m
- Target first 500m at CSS + 3–5 sec/100m (conservatively), then settle to race pace

### Wetsuit Temperature Reference (triq.ai)
| Water Temperature | Wetsuit Status |
|-------------------|---------------|
| < 14°C (57.2°F) | Compulsory (hypothermia risk) |
| 14–20°C (57–68°F) | Permitted |
| 20–22°C (68–71.6°F) | Permitted for age-group, may be banned for elite |
| > 22°C (71.6°F) | Banned in most competitive categories |

*Always confirm with specific race rules — federations vary.*

## 5 Beginner Basics for Triathlon Swimming (Ironman / Lance Watson)

### 1. Breathing Is a Technique, Not an Instinct
- Runners and cyclists often struggle in the pool despite good fitness — the reason is CO2 build-up from holding breath
- **Common mistake:** holding breath underwater, then trying to exhale and inhale in one quick motion
- **Correct pattern:** begin exhaling through nose and mouth as soon as face enters water → by the time you rotate to breathe, lungs are already partially empty → responsive inhale
- Breathing rhythm: every 3 strokes for balance; under high effort, alternate 3-2-3-2 to increase frequency while maintaining bilateral balance

### 2. Pool Fitness Doesn't Fully Transfer to Open Water
- Fit athletes frequently feel out of breath in the pool — swimming is both technical and fitness-based
- Open water has no lane lines, black line, or wall for reference — sighting and navigation are separate skills
- Practice sighting in the pool by targeting a coloured marker at end of lane every 8–10 strokes
- Minimum 2–3 open water practice sessions before race day — always with a partner or group

### 3. Varied Sets Beat Continuous Swimming
- Long unbroken swims allow pace to drift and technique to deteriorate
- Break training into intervals with structured rest — better average velocity, better technique, stronger training stimulus
- Simple structure: pyramid sets (25-50-100-50-25), descending/ascending, or drill-swim alternation
- Every session should include a technical drill component (fingertip drag, single-arm, catch-up)

### 4. Managing the Start and Anxiety
- The first 200m of a triathlon swim is highest-risk for panic — adrenaline spike + cold shock + body contact
- **Cold shock response:** if no warm-up swim is allowed, splash face and neck at water's edge before start
- If panicking mid-swim: roll to back, float, breathe slowly, resume when calm
- Breaststroke is acceptable mid-race — allows vision ahead and breathing recovery without stopping

### 5. Sighting is the Most Valuable Open Water Skill
- Even a small sighting error compounds over 750m–1500m into significant added distance
- Lift only the eyes above water ("crocodile sighting") — full head lift drops hips, increases drag
- Practice: swim 10 strokes, sight, swim 10 strokes → ingrain the rhythm
- Use a permanent landmark (building, tree, tower) when buoys are hard to see

## 7 Steps to Improving Your Triathlon Swim (Sunsational / Wendy Highland)

### 1. Conquer Open Water Nerves
- Schedule **weekly open water swims** starting with short distances — familiarity reduces anxiety
- Always swim with a buddy or club for safety
- Treading water: practise 15–30 sec intervals to build confidence for mid-race pause without panic
- Breathing reset: 10 slow nasal inhales before entry calms the nervous system

### 2. Entry and Exit Efficiency
- Wade in with normal stride, then lift knees high once shin-deep, then dolphin dive
- **Dolphin dive:** hands plant on bottom → feet plant close to hands → push up and dive over the water
- Repeat dolphin dives until deep enough to stroke without touching bottom
- Most beginners waste 10–20 sec in shallows relative to efficient entries

### 3. Race-Ready Sighting Protocol
- Sight every 8–10 strokes; more frequently in currents or near buoys
- Pool drill: swim towards orange cone, sight mid-length without breaking stroke rhythm
- Open water drill: use a friend in a kayak 100m ahead as your sighting target

### 4. Contact Practice
- The start of a triathlon involves significant body contact — it is not a malfunction, it is expected
- Group swim drill: intentionally swim close, bump arms, tangle legs — desensitises the contact response
- **Double cap method:** base cap → goggles → race cap to protect goggles from being knocked off
- Practice swimming 25m without goggles so a knock-off doesn't cause panic

### 5. Pacing by Feel and HR
- Race excitement causes most beginners to start the swim too fast — a mistake that costs the bike
- Use HR monitor to find race-pace HR in training; stay within that range in the swim
- Do a time trial of the race distance to establish a baseline pace; glance at watch occasionally to check
- Track stroke cadence (strokes/minute) at race pace — use it as a pacing reference on race day

### 6. Equipment Testing
- **Goggles:** test multiple types (padded, unpadded, mirrored) — find one pair that fits perfectly and use it exclusively for the race
- **Triathlon suit:** allows seamless swim-to-bike-to-run without changing; test it in training in all three disciplines
- **Nothing new on race day** — every piece of equipment should have been worn in training

### 7. Build Your Support Network
- Triathlon clubs provide: training partners, coaching input, open water opportunities, and accountability
- Family and friends who understand your training schedule become active supporters, not obstacles
- A coach removes guesswork and reduces the most common training error: doing too much too soon in the swim

## Common Freestyle Technique Mistakes and Fixes (USMS)

### Body Position & Alignment
- Swimming with head up and feet low creates significant drag. Maintain a horizontal line at the water's surface — head neutral, hips high.
- Lateral misalignment (head/shoulders moving side-to-side) causes hips and legs to snake. Focus on moving through the water in a straight line without excessive lateral motion.
- **Applies to:** All

### Pull Technique
- Using only the hand for propulsion results in a "dropped elbow" where only the hand pushes backward. Engage the entire forearm and hand as a single paddle surface.
- Excessive side-to-side pulling wastes energy on lateral movement. Direct the pull backward as much as possible.
- Maintaining consistent pull speed through the stroke fails to maximise propulsion. Accelerate the arm through the pull phase — slow entry, fast finish.
- **Applies to:** All

### Breathing
- Lifting the head up and to the side causes hips to sink, shoulders to move laterally, and pulls arms out of effective pulling position. Breathe by rotating the body — the head follows the shoulder roll, not the other way around.
- **Applies to:** All

## Wetsuit Swimming — How Buoyancy Changes Stroke Mechanics (Total Immersion)

### Buoyancy & Balance Changes
- Wetsuits use thicker neoprene in the pelvic region and upper legs, elevating the pelvis and legs higher in the water. This can cause technically proficient swimmers to lose "bite" in their kick — the traction needed for thrust and rotational torque.
- The suit provides increased glide speed from buoyancy and reduced drag.
- **Applies to:** All

### Stroke Adjustments for Wetsuit
- Use a shallower angle of entry and extension to accommodate the increased glide speed and higher body position in water.
- Maintain a consistent deep grip position (high elbow, near-vertical forearm) before each torso rotation, regardless of entry angle changes.
- Practise the "slip" movement from extension to grip without causing turbulence or dropping into vertical grip too early.
- Train hand/forearm sensitivity to detect streamline vs turbulence — harder without tactile feedback from water on skin.
- **Applies to:** Intermediate / Elite

### Kick Modifications
- Slightly decrease knee flexion to prevent the foot from kicking at or above the water surface.
- Increase whip speed from flexion to extension to match accelerated glide speed.
- 2-beat kick should stay within the body's hydrodynamic shadow for efficiency.
- **Applies to:** All

### Cadence and Recovery
- Increase overall stroke cadence to adapt to faster glide speeds. Use a Tempo Trainer in pool training to practise faster turnover rates.
- The hand/arm drift phase from entry to grip must happen faster in a wetsuit. Accelerate the recovery phase while preserving lead elbow and relaxed forearm positioning.
- Keep the entry clean and silent while developing speed without tension.
- **Applies to:** Intermediate / Elite

### Wetsuit Type Selection
- **Full-sleeve** with open-cell neoprene on forearms — reduces arm fatigue and eases entry/extension
- **Sleeveless** — preserves shoulder mobility and flow sensitivity but requires careful fit to prevent water entry
- **Long John** — suits technique-focused swimmers particularly well
- **Applies to:** All

## Legal Drafting in Triathlon Swim — Positioning and Benefit

- The ideal draft position is directly behind someone's feet — give just enough room for their kick to operate properly. Side-drafting (off the hip) is an alternative when the feet position isn't available.
- Drafting in the water saves significant energy and helps you make it into a swim group. Even advanced swimmers need to practise being comfortable in congested water.
- **Applies to:** All (critical for draft-legal racing)

## Mass Swim Start Strategy — Positioning, Feet to Follow, Managing Contact

### Start Positioning
- Athletes start side by side — choice of position on the line can make or break your swim. Understanding the swim venue is critical as starts can be from beach, in the water, or from a pontoon (dive start).
- **Applies to:** All

### Start Type Skills
- **Beach starts:** practise dolphin diving and aggressively running in and out of the water to gain time in the shallows.
- **Pontoon starts:** the force from all athletes pressing off simultaneously can push the dock backwards — be ready to dive immediately on the horn.
- **Applies to:** All

### First Leg Tactics
- Start speed to the first turn buoy is critical — the beginning of draft-legal swims is aggressive, rough, and fast. Swim with a higher stroke cadence due to wind, waves, and other competitors.
- Sighting needs only a quick glance — buoys are large yellow or orange.
- **Applies to:** Intermediate / Elite

### Congested Water Drill
- **Drill:** put 3–4 people in one lane and swim to the other end simultaneously — practises swimming in choppy water and staying relaxed around other swimmers.
- Athletes have the most success in open water when they are relaxed. For many athletes, draft-legal racing is their first time swimming in large packs for an extended period.
- **Applies to:** All

## Bilateral Breathing — Why It Matters and How to Train It (Pete Jacobs, IRONMAN World Champion)

### Why Bilateral Breathing
- Allows you to change breathing side during a race to accommodate wind and chop. In training, breathing every 3 strokes allows more focus on stroke technique and develops a more even stroke on both sides.
- **Applies to:** All

### How to Train It
- **Catch-up drills:** leave one arm out in front until the other hand hits the water. Keep arms moving at normal pace, just pause in front longer. This builds rhythm without slowing down.
- **Applies to:** All

### Breathing Timing Protocol
- Tilt head to the side as shoulders roll — breathe under the armpit as the arm comes over in recovery.
- As the hand hits the water and shoulders roll back, take the head with you.
- Look back at a **45° angle** under the arm — get the mouth out of the water, not the eyes.
- Crown of the head should point forward, not up.
- **Applies to:** All

## Weekly Swim Volume by Level

### Session Frequency
| Frequency | Expected Outcome |
|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 session/week | Maintains swimming memory only — no improvement |
| 2 sessions/week | Maintains fitness — no real improvement, no regression |
| 3 sessions/week | Will see improvement in ability and fitness |
| 4+ sessions/week | Will see significant improvement in ability and fitness |

### Session Duration
- **Most triathlon swimmers:** 45–60 min per session is adequate, even for long-course athletes. High frequency coupled with 45–60 min enables good progression session to session.
- **Beginners/learners:** shorter sessions of **20–30 min** are more appropriate. Swim to the point of fatigue where technique breaks down, then stop. Short sessions with good technique focus are more valuable than longer sessions with deteriorating form.
- **Applies to:** Beginner (20–30 min) / Intermediate–Elite (45–60 min)
