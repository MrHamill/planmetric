## ⚡ PLAN GENERATION ESSENTIALS

**When to programme S&C:**
- Base phase: 2 sessions/week — this is when strength gains are made
- Build phase: 1–2 sessions/week | Peak phase: 1 session/week (maintenance) | Race week: none

**Scheduling (interference effect):**
- Separate strength and endurance by at least 3–6 hours, or on different days
- If same day: always lift BEFORE running or cycling (not after)
- Never heavy strength the day before a key quality endurance session
- Stop sets 2–3 reps before failure — the goal is to get stronger, not create debilitating soreness

**What to prescribe (meta-analysis backed):**
- **Best combination:** heavy compound movements (≥80% 1RM) + plyometrics — produces largest running economy gains
- Submaximal circuits alone (40–79% 1RM) produce no significant improvement — avoid as the sole S&C method
- Plyometrics alone: 2–8% running economy improvement, most effective at lower speeds (≤12 km/h)

**Key exercises:** single-leg squats, Romanian deadlifts, glute bridges/hip thrusts, rows, planks, dead bugs, clamshells with band (hip stability = highest injury prevention ROI)

**Quick home option (no equipment, 15 min):** single-leg squats + push-ups + single-leg glute bridges + dead bugs — 2–3 sets × 6–10 reps; viable 1–2×/week as a supplement

## Why Include S&C in Endurance Plans?

Strength training improves three things that directly affect race performance:

1. **Power output** — more force per pedal stroke, stride, or pull
2. **Economy** — less oxygen used at a given pace/power (2–8% improvement in running economy from plyometrics alone)
3. **Injury resistance** — stronger tendons, bones, and stabiliser muscles reduce overuse injury risk

The research is clear that endurance athletes who add 1–2 strength sessions per week improve performance more than those who only do endurance work. This is not optional for serious athletes — it's a key component.

- Applies to: All

## Concurrent Training Principles

When combining strength and endurance training in the same programme, the "interference effect" can reduce gains from both. These guidelines minimise that interference.

### Scheduling
- Separate strength and endurance sessions by **at least 3–6 hours**, or do them on different days
- If doing both in one day, **lift before running or cycling** — this ensures high-quality strength work when fresh
- Never schedule heavy strength work the day before a key quality endurance session
- Applies to: All

### Loading
- Focus on **moderate-to-heavy loads with lower reps** (3–6 sets of 4–8 reps) to build strength without excessive hypertrophy or fatigue
- **End sets 2–3 reps before failure** — going to failure creates debilitating soreness that disrupts sport-specific training
- The goal is to get stronger, not to get sore
- Applies to: All

### Fuelling
- Eating enough calories and protein is essential to minimise interference effect
- Under-fuelled athletes who add strength training risk both poor adaptation and increased injury
- Applies to: All

### Phase-Specific Frequency
| Training Phase | Suggested Frequency | Focus |
|---------------|-------------------|-------|
| Off-season / Base | 2 sessions/week | Build strength — this is when gains are made |
| Pre-season / Build | 1–2 sessions/week | Transition to power and sport-specific movements |
| In-season / Peak | 1 session/week (maintenance) | Maintain strength, prioritise injury prevention |
| Race week | 0 sessions | Full recovery |

- **Never sacrifice a hard run or bike session to make up for a missed lifting session** — endurance training always takes priority in-season
- Applies to: All

## Suggested Exercises for Endurance Athletes

These are general-purpose, functional movements that support swim, bike, and run performance. They can be done at home, in a gym, or with minimal equipment.

### Lower Body (Run & Bike Power)
- **Single-leg squats / split squats** — running stability, cycling power, corrects imbalances between legs
- **Romanian deadlifts** — posterior chain strength, supports posture during long rides and runs
- **Glute bridges / hip thrusts** — targets glutes and hamstrings, addresses common weakness in endurance athletes
- **Reverse lunges** — mimics running mechanics, builds strength through full hip range of motion

### Upper Body (Swim & Posture)
- **Push-ups** — shoulder and core stability for swimming
- **Pull-ups / lat pulldowns** — back strength for swim endurance and catch power
- **Rows** — upper back strength to maintain posture on long rides

### Core (All Disciplines)
- **Planks** (front and side) — maintains form when fatigued across all three disciplines
- **Dead bugs** — core stability with controlled movement, low injury risk
- **Side plank with hip abduction** — combined core and hip stability

- Applies to: All

## Quick 15-Minute Session (No Equipment Needed)

For athletes short on time or without gym access. This can be done at home before or after an easy session.

1. **Warm-up:** leg swings, arm swings, cat-cow (2 min)
2. **Single-leg squats:** 2–3 sets × 6–10 reps per side
3. **Push-ups:** 2–3 sets × 6–10 reps (knees or toes)
4. **Single-leg glute bridges:** 2–3 sets × 6–10 reps per side
5. **Dead bugs:** 2–3 sets × 8–12 reps

Total time: ~15 min. Suggested 1–2×/week during base phase, 1×/week in build phase.

- Applies to: All

## Plyometrics for Running Economy

Plyometric training (explosive jumping/bounding movements) improves running economy by 2–8% through three mechanisms:

1. **Increased tendon stiffness** — better elastic energy storage and return (the "tendon spring" effect)
2. **Reduced ground contact time** — faster, more powerful strides without extra energy cost
3. **Improved neuromuscular coordination** — more efficient muscle recruitment patterns

### Implementation Guidelines
- **Duration:** programmes lasting 7–15 weeks yield the best results
- **Frequency:** 1–3 times/week, often paired with strength sessions
- **Progression:** start with low volume (e.g. 3 sets × 10 reps), gradually increase — avoid high-intensity plyometrics when pre-fatigued
- **Recovery:** tendons need time to adapt; if soreness lasts 48+ hours, volume is too high
- Applies to: All

### Suggested Exercises (Beginner → Advanced)
1. **Pogos** (ankle hops in place) — beginner, teaches reactive stiffness
2. **Squat jumps** — beginner/intermediate, builds explosive leg power
3. **Bounding** — intermediate, exaggerated running strides for stride power
4. **Depth jumps** (step off box, land and immediately jump) — advanced, maximum reactive strength

### Key Research Finding
- An acute plyometric warm-up (e.g. squat jumps, bounding before a run) can **immediately improve running economy** in that session
- Combining plyometrics with heavy strength training (≥80% 1RM) produces the maximum gains
- Plyometric training is more effective for running economy at **lower speeds** (≤12 km/h), while heavy strength training is more effective at **higher speeds** — suggesting both should be included
- Applies to: All

## Hip Stability & Injury Prevention

Weak hips are one of the most common contributors to overuse injuries in endurance athletes. The gluteus medius, gluteus maximus, and hip flexors are the key targets.

### Why It Matters by Discipline
- **Running:** weak glutes increase load on knee joints → runner's knee, IT band syndrome
- **Cycling:** tight hip flexors from hours in the saddle → lower back pain, reduced power
- **Swimming:** weak core/hips → poor body position in water, increased drag

### Suggested Exercises
- **Clamshells (with resistance band)** — gluteus medius activation, lateral stability. 2–3 sets × 30–45 sec
- **Single-leg bridge** — glute strength, prevents hip drop during running
- **Lateral band walks / monster walks** — strengthens adductors and gluteus medius, stabilises pelvis
- **Single-leg squats / single-leg deadlifts** — balance, corrects left-right imbalances
- **Hip thrusts** — glute power for cycling and running
- **Side plank with hip abduction** — combined core and hip stability

### When to Programme
- **Base phase:** build capacity with 1–2 sessions/week
- **Build and Race phases:** maintain with 1 session/week, focusing on the exercises that address the athlete's specific weaknesses
- Can be used as a warm-up before key sessions (5–10 min of band walks + clamshells + glute bridges)
- Applies to: All

> **Reminder:** if an athlete is experiencing pain, they should consult a physiotherapist. These exercises are suggestions for general injury prevention, not treatment for existing conditions.

## Meta-Analysis: Strength Training Methods & Running Economy (Llanos-Lagos et al., 2024)

**Context:** Systematic review with meta-analysis (Sports Medicine, PMID: 38165636) examining 652 middle- and long-distance runners across multiple studies. Compared high-load, submaximal, plyometric, isometric, and combined strength training methods.

### Results by Method

| Method | Effect on Running Economy | Evidence Certainty |
|--------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| High-load (≥80% 1RM) | Small improvement (ES = −0.27) | Moderate |
| Combined methods (2+ types) | Moderate improvement (ES = −0.43) | Low |
| Plyometric | Small improvement (ES = −0.31), but only at speeds ≤12 km/h | Moderate |
| Submaximal (40–79% 1RM) | No significant improvement | Moderate |
| Isometric | No significant improvement | Moderate |

### Key Findings
- **High-load strength training** is most effective for runners with high VO2max and at higher running speeds
- **Plyometric training** is most effective at lower speeds (≤12 km/h) — more relevant for beginner/intermediate runners
- **Combining methods** (e.g. heavy lifting + plyometrics) produces the largest overall effect
- **Submaximal and isometric training alone are not effective** for improving running economy
- Programmes ranged from 6–24 weeks, 1–4 sessions/week

### Implications for Plan Design
- Suggested S&C sessions should include **heavy compound movements + plyometrics** — not just bodyweight circuits
- For beginners running at slower paces, plyometric emphasis may be more beneficial
- For faster/fitter runners, heavy strength work becomes increasingly important
- Neither submaximal circuits nor isometric holds alone justify inclusion as the primary S&C method
- Applies to: All
