## Why Masters Athletes Need Different Plans

Peak endurance performance is maintained until approximately **age 35**, followed by modest decreases until **50–60**, with progressively steeper declines thereafter. However, the rate of decline is heavily influenced by training — athletes who maintain intensity and volume age far slower athletically than those who reduce training.

The three physiological determinants of endurance performance change differently with age:

| Factor | Change with Age | Implication |
|--------|----------------|-------------|
| VO2max | Declines ~10% per decade from 30s; accelerates to ~15% per decade between 50–75 | Primary driver of performance decline — must be actively defended |
| Lactate threshold (as % of VO2max) | Declines, but partly secondary to VO2max reduction | Can be maintained well with threshold training |
| Exercise economy | **Does not change** with age in trained adults | Technique and efficiency gains are preserved — high-value investment |

**Key finding:** over 50% of the variance in VO2max decline is associated with **reduced training volume and intensity**, not ageing itself. Athletes who drop to "long slow distance" only see VO2max decline of 1–1.5%/year vs 0.5%/year for those who maintain HIIT.

- Applies to: All masters athletes (40+)

## Training Adaptations for 40+

### Intensity Over Volume
- Focus on **1–2 high-quality sessions per discipline per week** rather than piling on volume
- Quality sessions (intervals, threshold work) provide better performance returns than extra easy volume, with lower injury risk
- The "train smarter, not harder" principle becomes genuinely critical after 40
- Applies to: All (40+)

### Strength Training Is Non-Negotiable
- Muscle mass loss (sarcopenia) accelerates after 40 — approximately 3–8% per decade, increasing further after 50
- **Lift heavy, 2–3 times per week** — sets of 4–6 reps at challenging loads to maintain strength and bone density
- This directly combats the power loss that drives performance decline
- Also improves exercise economy and reduces injury risk
- Applies to: All (40+)

### VO2max Interval Training
- To defend against VO2max decline, regularly include **VO2max intervals: 3–6 min efforts at RPE 9–10/10**
- These are the most effective tool for slowing the age-related aerobic ceiling drop
- Athletes who neglect HIIT and only do endurance work see dramatically faster VO2max decline
- Applies to: All (40+)

### Technique Emphasis
- Efficiency declines more slowly than raw speed — regular technique sessions in swim, bike, and run help maintain performance even as physiological capacity decreases
- Technique is the highest-ROI training investment for masters athletes
- Applies to: All (40+)

### Threshold Focus
- As the absolute VO2max ceiling lowers, improving **power/pace at lactate threshold** (as a percentage of VO2max) becomes the primary lever for maintaining race performance
- Threshold sessions (tempo runs, sweet-spot bike work, CSS swim sets) should remain a weekly staple
- Applies to: All (50+)

## Recovery Adaptations

Recovery is where the biggest adjustments are needed. Masters athletes experience greater muscle damage and delayed recovery compared to younger athletes.

### Rest Days
- Masters athletes often require **2 rest days per week** rather than the 1 typical for younger athletes
- Over-50 athletes may need to separate hard interval days by **2–3 days** instead of 1
- Applies to: All (40+)

### Training Cycles
- Consider a **2-weeks-on / 1-week-off** cycle rather than the standard 3-on/1-off
- This shorter loading cycle prevents the cumulative fatigue that leads to injury and burnout in older athletes
- Applies to: All (45+), strongly recommended for 50+

### Active Recovery
- Use active recovery days (light swimming, easy cycling) rather than complete rest
- Movement promotes circulation, reduces muscle stiffness, and maintains training consistency
- Applies to: All (40+)

### Intensity Ceiling by Age
| Age Range | Max High-Intensity Sessions/Week | Notes |
|-----------|--------------------------------|-------|
| 40–49 | 2–3 (possibly more for well-trained) | Prioritise recovery over adding extra sessions |
| 50–59 | 2–3 (hard ceiling) | Separate hard days by at least 48 hrs |
| 60+ | 1–2 | Quality over everything; consistency is the goal |

- Applies to: All (40+)

### Over-Training Warning
- Back-to-back high-intensity days are the primary cause of fatigue and injury in masters athletes
- A slightly lower volume that allows for **consistent training** is always better than a high-volume approach that leads to forced time off
- Applies to: All (40+)

## Nutrition Adjustments

### Protein
- Increase protein intake to **1.6–2.0 g/kg body weight per day** to counteract age-related muscle loss
- Distribute protein intake across 4+ meals/day (muscle protein synthesis response is blunted in older adults — more frequent doses help)
- Applies to: All (40+)

### Mobility & Recovery Aids
- Increased focus on flexibility and mobility work (dynamic stretching, yoga, foam rolling)
- Compression garments and massage can support recovery between sessions
- Applies to: All (40+)

## Female Masters Athletes

Female athletes in their late 40s and beyond face additional considerations due to perimenopause and menopause:

- **Significantly higher recovery needs** than men of the same age due to hormonal changes
- Oestrogen decline affects bone density, recovery rate, body composition, and thermoregulation
- Strength training and adequate protein become even more critical for bone health
- Sleep disruption from hormonal changes can further impair recovery — training load may need to be adjusted during this transition
- Applies to: Female athletes (45+)

## Implications for Planmetric Plan Generation

When generating plans for athletes over 40, the following adjustments should be applied:

- **Recovery weeks:** use 2-on/1-off cycle instead of 3-on/1-off for athletes 45+
- **Rest days:** programme 2 rest days/week minimum (vs 1 for younger athletes)
- **Hard session spacing:** never schedule back-to-back high-intensity days; space by 48+ hrs for 50+
- **Strength training:** include 2 sessions/week in base phase, 1–2 in build phase (framed as suggestions — see `strength-conditioning.md`)
- **VO2max work:** maintain at least 1 VO2max interval session/week year-round to defend aerobic capacity
- **Technique sessions:** include regular drill/technique focus across all disciplines
- **Volume:** scale down total weekly hours by ~10–15% compared to equivalent younger athlete plans
- **Female athletes 45+:** further reduce intensity ceiling, increase recovery days, flag the importance of bone-loading strength work
