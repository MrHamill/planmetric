## Mental Skills for Race Day

### Process-Oriented Focus
- Focus on **controllables** — pacing, form, nutrition, breathing — not outcomes (time, podium)
- Outcome goals create anxiety; process goals create action
- During the race, the only question is: "Am I executing my plan right now?"
- Applies to: All

### Visualisation (Mental Rehearsal)
- Visualise each segment of the race in advance — swim, bike, run, and transitions
- **Include adversity scenarios:** flat tyre, lost goggles, cramping, bad weather — mentally rehearse your response to each
- Visualisation primes the brain for both success and problem-solving under pressure
- Do this in training (before key sessions) and in the days before the race
- Applies to: All

### Positive Self-Talk & Mantras
- Use short, rehearsed phrases to reframe discomfort or negative thoughts
- Examples: "Strong and steady", "I've trained for this", "Smooth is fast", "I can do hard things"
- Research shows positive self-talk **significantly improves performance** compared to passive thinking
- Mantras should be personal and practised in training — not invented on race day
- Applies to: All

### Breathing Techniques
- **Box breathing** (inhale 4 sec, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4) — calms the nervous system in the start corral or during moments of panic
- **4-7-8 breathing** (inhale 4, hold 7, exhale 8) — deeper calming effect for pre-race anxiety
- Practise during training so it becomes automatic under race stress
- Applies to: All

### Discomfort Management
- Reframe pain as **data** — it tells you you're working hard, not that you should stop
- Accept that pain and fatigue are part of the process — fighting them wastes mental energy, accepting them frees it
- Intentionally train in challenging conditions (heat, rain, fatigue, hunger) to build pain tolerance and mental resilience
- Applies to: All

### Chunking the Race
- Break the race into smaller, manageable pieces rather than focusing on the total distance
- Chunk by: aid station to aid station, km markers, landmarks, or discipline segments
- A marathon becomes three 14 km sections. A 70.3 becomes six distinct tasks (swim, T1, bike, T2, run first half, run second half)
- Each chunk gets its own mini-goal and mental cue
- Applies to: All

## Pre-Race Routine (Race Morning)

A consistent pre-race routine reduces anxiety and decision fatigue. Build this in training and repeat it identically on race day.

### The Checklist Approach
- **Stay methodical:** follow a step-by-step checklist for race morning (wake time, meals, kit check, warm-up, mental prep)
- **Visualise the flow:** while setting up in transition, mentally rehearse the layout — rack position, entry/exit routes, gear placement
- **Stop tinkering:** once equipment is set up, leave it alone. Last-minute adjustments create doubt
- Applies to: All

### Managing Pre-Race Nerves
- **Reframe nervousness as readiness:** change the internal narrative from "I'm nervous" to "I'm ready" — jitters are adrenaline the body is providing for performance
- **Control your environment:** stay away from negative or anxious people, listen to calming or energising music, stick to your routine
- **Mental warm-up:** treat mental preparation as a "brain warm-up" — just as you warm up your body, warm up your focus and intention
- Applies to: All

## Transition as a Mental Reset

Transitions are not just physical changeovers — they are opportunities to mentally reset between disciplines.

### The "3 Rs" Rule
1. **Rest** — let go of the previous leg (good or bad, it's done)
2. **Regroup** — prepare mentally for the next discipline
3. **Refocus** — narrow attention to the immediate task ahead

### The "Yes, And..." Technique
- If a segment was difficult, acknowledge it without dwelling: "Yes, the swim was rough, **and** I still have two legs left to make up time"
- This prevents a bad segment from contaminating the rest of the race
- Applies to: All

## Mental Toughness (Trainable, Not Genetic)

Mental toughness is a skill that improves with deliberate practice — it is not a fixed trait.

### Building Resilience
- **Grit** = knowing something will be hard and doing it anyway
- Develop through consistent training, not through occasional heroic efforts
- Persevere through setbacks by reframing them as learning opportunities, not failures
- Applies to: All

### Adversity Training
- Practise handling race-day problems in training: change a flat tyre while exhausted, swim without goggles, run the last interval when you want to stop
- This builds **procedural confidence** — you've solved the problem before, so it won't panic you on race day
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### "Why" Awareness
- Connect with your deeper purpose for competing — this maintains long-term motivation when training gets difficult
- Athletes who can articulate *why* they train are more resilient than those focused only on *what* they want to achieve
- Applies to: All

## Goal Setting for Endurance Athletes

### Goal Hierarchy
| Level | Timeframe | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Long-term | 2–5 years | Complete an Ironman, qualify for age-group worlds |
| Intermediate | This season | Run a sub-1:45 half marathon, finish an Olympic tri |
| Short-term | This week / this session | Hit 5 × 1000m at threshold pace, complete a 2-hour long ride |

### Process Over Outcome
- **Outcome goals** (finish time, podium) are motivating but uncontrollable — weather, competition, and illness can undermine them
- **Process goals** (stick to nutrition plan, maintain cadence, execute pacing strategy) are fully within the athlete's control
- Set 2–3 process goals for every race alongside any outcome target
- Applies to: All

### Actionable Milestones
- Break long events into segments with specific mini-goals at each
- Example for a marathon: "First 14 km — stay at goal pace, fuel every 30 min. Middle 14 km — maintain effort by feel, not watch. Final 14 km — empty the tank from 35 km"
- Prevents the psychological overwhelm of focusing on the total distance
- Applies to: All

## Pacing Psychology

### Energy Management
- Pacing is a **decision-making process**, not just a physical output — it's about managing energy to optimise performance across the full distance
- The most common pacing error: starting too fast on adrenaline, then crashing in the second half
- Applies to: All

### Negative Splitting
- Starting at a controlled pace and gradually increasing speed in the later stages is consistently more effective than positive splitting (fast start, slow finish)
- Requires discipline in the first third of the race when everything feels easy
- Applies to: All

### Internal vs External Focus
- **Interoceptive awareness** — monitoring heart rate, breathing, perceived exertion — allows athletes to pace based on internal energy levels rather than external competition
- Chasing another athlete's pace early in the race is one of the most common causes of blowing up
- Applies to: All

### Mental Fatigue and Perceived Exertion
- Mental fatigue causes athletes to **perceive the same effort as harder** — leading to premature pace reduction
- This is a real physiological effect, not weakness
- Countermeasures: music, positive self-talk, crowd energy, chunking, changing mental focus
- Pre-race mental fatigue (poor sleep, stress, long travel) can impair performance even when physically rested — manage the mental load in race week
- Applies to: All

## Implications for Planmetric Coaching Notes

When writing coaching notes in plans, mental skills can be woven into the training context:

- **Pre-race week:** include visualisation prompts and checklist reminders
- **Long sessions:** suggest mantra practice and chunking strategies
- **Hard interval sessions:** frame as mental toughness training, not just physical stress
- **Recovery weeks:** remind athletes that rest is part of the process, not a sign of weakness
- **Race-week notes:** include the 3 Rs for transitions, pacing reminders, and process goal prompts
- Mental preparation should feel like a natural extension of the training plan, not a separate programme
