# Nutrition for Endurance Athletes

## ⚡ PLAN GENERATION ESSENTIALS

**Carb targets during exercise (by duration):**
- <60 min: 0–30 g/hr (optional) | 60–90 min: 30–60 g/hr | 90 min–2.5 hrs: 60–80 g/hr | >2.5 hrs: 80–90 g/hr

**Fuelling rules:**
- Glucose saturates at ~60 g/hr (SGLT1 ceiling); fructose uses a separate transporter — combining at 2:1 ratio allows up to 90 g/hr total without GI distress
- Start fuelling within the first 20–30 min of any session >60 min — do not wait until depleted
- Gut training: build from 40 g/hr → 90 g/hr over 6–7 weeks; always train with race-day products
- Sodium: 300–700 mg/hr for sessions >90 min; deficiency is the leading cause of cramping

**Daily carb targets by training load:**
- Rest/light: 3–5 g/kg | Moderate: 5–7 g/kg | Hard (1–3 hrs): 6–10 g/kg | Very hard (>4 hrs): 8–12 g/kg

**Protein:** 1.4–2.0 g/kg/day; post-session: 20–30 g within 60 min alongside 0.3–0.5 g/kg carbs

**Hydration:** 500–750 mL/hr mild; 750–1,000 mL/hr hot conditions

**Pre-race:** high-carb meal 3–4 hrs before; optional 20–30 g fast carbs 30–60 min before for events >90 min

**Race day by distance:**
- Sprint: pre-race load only; gel 5 min before if desired
- Olympic: 1–2 gels on bike; no fuelling needed on run
- 70.3: 60–80 g/hr on bike + run
- Ironman: 80–90 g/hr; 750–1,000 mL/hr fluid; 500–700 mg/hr sodium

## No-Nonsense Endurance Nutrition (Strava)

### Carbohydrate Intake During Exercise

Carbohydrates are the primary fuel source above ~70% VO2max. Under-fuelling is the leading cause of "bonking" or hitting the wall.

| Effort Duration | Carbohydrate Target |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| < 60 min | 0–30 g/hr (optional) |
| 60–90 min | 30–60 g/hr |
| 90 min – 2.5 hrs | 60–80 g/hr |
| > 2.5 hrs | 80–90 g/hr (trained gut can reach 120 g/hr) |

### Multiple Transportable Carbohydrates
- The gut can absorb **~60 g/hr of glucose** and a further **~30 g/hr of fructose** via separate intestinal transporters
- Products combining **2:1 glucose:fructose** (e.g. most sports gels, Maurten) allow 80–90 g/hr absorption without GI distress
- Gut training: tolerance to high carbohydrate intake improves with consistent training — start at lower end and build over weeks

### Hydration Guidelines

| Condition | Fluid Target |
|-----------|-------------|
| Mild conditions (<20°C) | 500–600 mL/hr |
| Moderate conditions (20–25°C) | 600–750 mL/hr |
| Hot conditions (>25°C) | 750–1,000 mL/hr |

- **Sweat rate varies significantly between athletes** — the above is a starting point
- Sweat test: weigh before and after a 1-hr session (no fluid); 1 kg weight loss ≈ 1L sweat loss
- Electrolytes (sodium) are required for sessions >90 min to prevent hyponatremia and muscle cramping

### Sodium Replacement
- Average sweat sodium concentration: 500–1,500 mg/L (highly individual)
- Target: 300–700 mg sodium per hour for sessions >90 min in heat
- Sources: electrolyte tabs, sports drink, salt capsules
- Low sodium intake during long races is a leading cause of GI cramping and heat illness

### Pre-Race Fuelling
- **Night before:** High-carbohydrate meal (~8–10 g carbs/kg body weight across the day)
- **Morning of race (3–4 hrs before):** 1–2 g carbs/kg body weight; familiar foods, low fibre, low fat
- **Final 30–60 min before:** 20–30 g fast-release carbs (gel, banana) if desired; avoid this for <90 min events

### Race Day Nutrition by Distance

**Sprint (750m / 20km / 5km):**
- Little to no fuelling required during race
- Strong pre-race carbohydrate load is more important
- Consider a gel 5 min before swim start

**Olympic (1.5km / 40km / 10km):**
- 1–2 gels on the bike (30–60 g carbs)
- Begin fuelling within the first 20–30 min of the bike
- No need to fuel on the run (duration too short)

**70.3 Half Iron:**
- 60–80 g carbs/hr on the bike (2–3 hrs)
- 30–60 g carbs/hr on the run (1–2 hrs)
- Practise race nutrition in training — GI issues in a 70.3 are almost always preventable

**Ironman Full:**
- 80–90 g carbs/hr target (gut-trained athletes may reach 120 g/hr)
- Fluid: 750–1,000 mL/hr depending on conditions
- Sodium: 500–700 mg/hr
- Nutrition strategy must be race-rehearsed at least 2–3 times in long training days

### Common Mistakes
1. **Starting fuelling too late** — glycogen depletion is hard to reverse once it occurs; fuel proactively from the first 20 min
2. **Relying on race-day nutrition without training the gut** — novel gels/drinks in a race frequently cause GI distress
3. **Over-relying on water without electrolytes** in long events → dilutes sodium → nausea, cramping
4. **Under-eating on heavy training days** — total daily energy availability must match training load to prevent RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)

### Daily Training Nutrition Principles
- **Carbohydrate periodisation:** High carbohydrate on high-intensity and long training days; moderate on easy days
- **Protein:** 1.6–2.0 g/kg/day for endurance athletes (lean muscle maintenance and repair)
- **Recovery window:** Consume 0.3–0.5 g/kg carbohydrate + 20–30 g protein within 30–60 min of hard sessions
- **Caffeine:** 3–6 mg/kg body weight 45–60 min before exercise improves time-trial performance by 2–4%

### Gut Training Protocol
For athletes targeting 80+ g/hr during long events:
1. Week 1–2: 40 g/hr with water
2. Week 3–4: 60 g/hr
3. Week 5–6: 80 g/hr
4. Week 7+: 80–90 g/hr, testing specific race-day products

Train the gut on the bike — this is where the majority of race-day fuelling occurs and where GI issues most commonly arise.

## Carbohydrate Transport Science (Jeukendrup)

This is the foundational research behind modern endurance fuelling. Asker Jeukendrup's work explains *why* glucose:fructose blends work and how to choose the right ratio.

### The Transport Bottleneck
- Glucose is absorbed via **SGLT1** transporter in the intestine — this saturates at ~60 g/hr regardless of how much more glucose is ingested
- Fructose uses a separate transporter (**GLUT5**) — adding fructose allows additional carbohydrate absorption on top of the glucose ceiling
- When glucose and fructose are combined, **oxidation rates are 50% higher** than glucose alone
- The highest oxidation rates were seen at intakes of 144 g/hr, but the best efficiency (least residual volume in the gut) was at **108 g/hr**
- Higher residual volume = higher risk of GI distress
- Applies to: All

### The 90 g/hr Recommendation
- 90 g/hr was established as a practical safe ceiling — very few athletes experience GI problems at this rate
- Above 90 g/hr, an increasing number of athletes develop GI issues
- This is a population-level recommendation — individual tolerance varies and can be trained
- Applies to: All

### Glucose:Fructose Ratio — There Is No Single "Best" Ratio
The optimal ratio depends on total intake:

| Total Intake | Suggested Glucose | Suggested Fructose | Ratio |
|-------------|------------------|-------------------|-------|
| 80 g/hr | 60 g/hr | 20 g/hr | 3:1 |
| 90 g/hr | 60 g/hr | 30 g/hr | 2:1 |
| 100 g/hr | 60 g/hr | 40 g/hr | 3:2 |
| 110 g/hr | 60 g/hr | 50 g/hr | ~1:0.8 |
| 120 g/hr | 60 g/hr | 60 g/hr | 1:1 |

**Key rule:** glucose intake should always be ~60–70 g/hr (enough to saturate SGLT1) with fructose added on top based on what the athlete can tolerate.

### Commercial Products
- Most gels/drinks use **2:1** or **1:0.8** glucose(maltodextrin):fructose
- 2:1 is best suited for ~90 g/hr intake
- 1:0.8 is better suited for ~110 g/hr intake
- Claims of a single "optimal ratio" are misleading — it depends on the total amount consumed
- Applies to: All

### Current Debate: Is Higher Always Better?
- Increasing evidence suggests elite athletes may benefit from >90 g/hr
- However, **performance effects for intakes above 90 g/hr have not been conclusively demonstrated** in controlled studies
- The key variable is gut tolerance, which can be improved through consistent gut training
- Intake should always be individualised based on what the athlete tolerates without GI distress
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

## Carbohydrate Periodisation ("Fuel for the Work Required")

Rather than eating the same amount of carbohydrate every day, intake is matched to the demands of each session. This optimises both metabolic adaptation and performance.

### Core Principle
- **Low-intensity / easy days → lower carbohydrate intake** (enhances fat oxidation adaptations)
- **High-intensity / long days → high carbohydrate intake** (fuels performance and recovery)
- **Competition → always high carbohydrate** (maximise glycogen stores)
- Applies to: All

### Train-Low Strategies
These deliberately restrict carbohydrate to enhance endurance adaptations. **Only use for low-intensity sessions** — never for quality/key sessions.

| Strategy | Protocol |
|----------|----------|
| Fasted training | Morning session before eating (easy pace only) |
| Sleep low / train low | Hard evening session → no post-exercise carbs → fasted easy morning session |
| Twice-a-day training | Don't replenish carbs between two sessions in the same day |

**Caution:** train-low sessions must be genuinely low-intensity. Attempting high-intensity work in a glycogen-depleted state impairs performance and increases injury risk.

### Train-High
- Consume high carbohydrate (6–10 g/kg/day) on days with high-intensity or long-duration sessions
- Always train-high during race preparation to rehearse fuelling strategy
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### Daily Carbohydrate Targets by Training Load

| Training Load | Carbohydrate Target |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Rest day / very light | 3–5 g/kg body weight |
| Moderate (1 hr easy) | 5–7 g/kg body weight |
| High (1–3 hrs, includes intensity) | 6–10 g/kg body weight |
| Very high (>4 hrs or multiple sessions) | 8–12 g/kg body weight |

- Applies to: All

## Daily Nutrition Guidelines for Endurance Athletes

### Macronutrient Targets

| Nutrient | Target | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Carbohydrate | 5–10 g/kg/day (varies by training load) | Primary fuel source — see periodisation section above |
| Protein | 1.4–1.8 g/kg/day (up to 2.0 g/kg for heavy training) | Muscle repair, immune function, recovery |
| Fat | Remainder of caloric needs | Include nuts, seeds, avocados, oils — limit high-fat foods immediately before training |

### Fuelling Long Sessions (60+ min)
- **Start early:** begin fuelling within the first 30 min of the session
- **Frequency:** consume small amounts every 30–45 min
- **Target:** 30–60 g carbs/hr for sessions 60–150 min; up to 90+ g/hr for sessions >2.5 hrs (as tolerated)
- **Options:** gels, energy chews, bananas, sports drinks, easy-to-digest bars
- **Hydration:** 120–240 mL of fluid every 15–20 min, including electrolytes for sessions >90 min
- Applies to: All

### Pre-Session Nutrition
- **2–3 hours before:** balanced meal rich in complex carbs, light on fat and fibre
- **Examples:** porridge with banana, toast with honey, rice with lean protein
- Applies to: All

### Post-Session Recovery
- **Within 30–60 min:** consume carbohydrate (0.3–0.5 g/kg) + protein (20–30 g)
- **Rapidly replenish glycogen** within 1–2 hours after intense sessions — this is especially important when training again within 24 hours
- **Examples:** protein shake with banana, Greek yoghurt with granola, chicken and rice
- Applies to: All

### Under-Fuelling Warning Signs
- Persistent heavy legs or fatigue despite adequate sleep
- Increased injury frequency (especially bone stress injuries)
- Declining performance despite consistent training
- Loss of menstrual function (female athletes)
- These are signs of RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport) — caloric intake must match training demands
- Applies to: All

## Carbohydrate Intake Scaled by Event Duration (IOC/Jeukendrup Consensus)

A summary framework for how much carbohydrate to consume during competition, based on event duration.

| Event Duration | Carbohydrate Strategy | Mechanism |
|---------------|----------------------|-----------|
| < 45 min | Not needed | Existing glycogen is sufficient |
| 45–75 min | Mouth rinse or small amounts | Central nervous system effect — signals fuel availability to the brain, improves performance even without significant absorption |
| 1–2 hrs | 30–60 g/hr | Single carbohydrate source (glucose or maltodextrin) is sufficient |
| 2–2.5 hrs | 60–80 g/hr | Dual-source (glucose + fructose) recommended to avoid GI issues |
| > 2.5 hrs | Up to 90 g/hr (trained gut may tolerate more) | Dual-source essential; gut training required; higher intakes only if individually tolerated |

- **Key insight:** for events 45–75 min, even a carbohydrate mouth rinse (swish and spit) improves performance through central nervous system activation — the carbs don't need to be absorbed
- Applies to: All

### Practice Strategy
- Use training long runs and rides to test what your stomach can handle
- Keep a fuelling journal to develop a personalised race-day strategy
- Build a detailed race-day plan pairing specific products with specific time points
- Never try a new product or strategy on race day
- Applies to: All

## Pre-Run Meal Timing

### By Time Window

| Window | Goal | What to Eat | Examples |
|--------|------|-------------|---------|
| 2–3 hrs before | Sustained energy without feeling heavy | High carbs, moderate protein, low fat, low fibre | Oatmeal with banana, toast with eggs, bagel with cream cheese and fruit |
| 1 hr before | Top up glycogen, maintain blood sugar | Simple, quickly digestible carbs | Toast with honey or jam, banana, applesauce |
| 30 min before | Fast energy boost | Light, easily digestible, liquid or simple carbs | Banana, handful of pretzels, sports drink, gel |

### Key Considerations
- **Run intensity matters:** longer, higher-intensity runs require more fuel (higher carbs) than easy runs
- **Gut training:** experiment with timing and food types during training — never test on race day
- **Hydration:** drink 400–800 mL/hr, using sports drinks for longer efforts
- **Avoid:** high-fibre, high-fat, and very spicy foods in the pre-run window
- Applies to: All

## Pre-Run Foods by Run Type

### Easy Runs (< 60 min)
- Minimal fuel needed — a small snack or fasted running is fine
- **Best options:** banana, toast with jam, granola bar, few crackers
- **Timing:** 15–30 min before (or skip entirely)
- Applies to: All

### Hard Sessions / Speed Work (Intervals, Tempo)
- High-carbohydrate, fast-acting, easily digestible foods for immediate energy
- **Best options:** white toast with jam, energy gel, oatmeal, banana
- **Timing:** 60–90 min before for optimal digestion
- Applies to: All

### Long Runs (> 90 min)
- Sustained-release energy — moderate carbs with some protein
- **Best options:** oatmeal with fruit, bagel with peanut butter, oatmeal with nut butter
- **Timing:** 90–120 min before
- Applies to: All

## Fasted Running

### What It Is
Running on an empty stomach, typically in the morning after 6–12 hours of not eating. A safe and effective tool for specific run types when used strategically.

### Benefits
- **Fat adaptation:** depleted glycogen forces greater fat oxidation — beneficial for endurance athletes aiming to "push back the wall" in long races
- **Reduced GI distress:** ideal for runners prone to cramping, side stitches, nausea, or runner's trots
- **Convenience:** enables early morning training without waiting to digest
- **Metabolic markers:** some studies suggest improved insulin sensitivity
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### Risks
- **Reduced performance at high intensity:** maintaining fast pace or high intensity is difficult without carbs
- **Muscle breakdown (catabolism):** body may break down muscle protein for fuel
- **Elevated cortisol:** excessive fasted running can impair immune function
- **Low energy availability (LEA):** chronic under-fuelling can cause hormonal imbalances, bone density loss, and increased injury risk — particularly in women
- **Fatigue/dizziness:** low blood sugar can cause lightheadedness
- Applies to: All

### Suitability by Run Type

| Run Type | Fasted? | Reasoning |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Easy/recovery (< 60 min) | Yes | Ideal — encourages fat metabolism without risking fatigue |
| Long run | Sometimes | Strategic use by experienced runners only — risk of bonking |
| Intervals/speed | No | Requires readily available carbs — fasted = poor performance |
| Tempo/threshold | No | Requires sustained energy — fasted efforts feel excessively hard |

### Guidelines
- Start with short, easy runs (30–45 min) to test tolerance
- Limit to 1–2 times per week — it is a tool, not a daily requirement
- Prioritise post-run nutrition: protein + carbs immediately after
- Hydrate before fasted runs — water is still essential
- **Do not fast if:** prone to low blood sugar, pregnant, or recovering from injury
- Applies to: All

## Foods to Avoid Before Running

### High-Fibre Foods
Slow to digest, produce gas, cause bloating and urgent bathroom stops:
- Legumes: beans, lentils, chickpeas, peas
- Cruciferous vegetables: broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, kale, cabbage
- Whole grains: high-fibre bran cereals, bran muffins, whole-wheat pasta
- Raw fruits/vegetables: apples with skin, pears, berries, raw vegetables
- Dried fruit: prunes and apricots (laxative effect)
- Applies to: All

### High-Fat Foods
Delay digestion, cause sluggishness or nausea:
- Fried foods: chips, fried chicken, greasy takeaway
- Fatty meats/dairy: bacon, sausage, heavy cream, full-fat cheese
- Excessive healthy fats: large amounts of nuts, seeds, or avocado
- Applies to: All

### Common GI Triggers
Irritate the gut lining or produce excess gas:
- **Dairy:** milk, yoghurt, soft cheese (especially if lactose-sensitive)
- **Spicy foods:** hot sauce, chilli, curry, peppers
- **Sugar alcohols/artificial sweeteners:** sorbitol, xylitol, erythritol (found in "sugar-free" products, gum, some protein bars)
- **Carbonated drinks:** soft drinks, seltzer, fizzy energy drinks
- **Excessive caffeine:** strong coffee or heavily caffeinated energy drinks — can cause cramping and accelerate bowel movements
- **Alcohol:** causes dehydration, especially the night before a long run
- Applies to: All

## Pre-Run Hydration

### Timing Protocol

| Window | Amount | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| 2–4 hrs before | 500–600 mL water or electrolyte drink | Allows time for absorption and urination |
| 30–60 min before | 250–300 mL | Final top-up |
| Long/intense runs | Electrolyte preload ~500 mL with strong electrolyte mix, 90 min before | Finish at least 45 min prior for absorption |

### Hydration Cues
- **Urine colour:** aim for pale yellow — dark urine means increase intake gradually
- **Avoid overhydration:** do not force excessive water — can cause hyponatraemia (diluted sodium)
- **Hot weather:** be more aggressive with early hydration and electrolyte consumption
- Applies to: All

## Morning Runner Fuelling

### Quick-Reference (30–60 min before)
- **Target:** 30–50 g of carbohydrate from quick-digesting sources
- **Best for quick energy:** banana (high in carbs and potassium)
- **Best for sensitive stomachs:** toast, bagel, or rice cake with honey or jam
- **Convenience:** energy gel, chews, or fig bar
- **Slightly more substantial (60–90 min before):** small bowl of oatmeal, porridge, or overnight oats
- **Before hard sessions:** raisins, dates, or small pretzel serving for fast-acting glucose
- Applies to: All

### Morning-Specific Tips
- **Easy runs (< 60 min):** fasted is fine for many runners, but a small carb snack often improves performance
- **Long or intense runs:** eat 90 min before — do not attempt fasted
- **Avoid:** high fat, high fibre, or high protein immediately before — these slow digestion
- **Hydration:** 500–600 mL water 2–3 hrs before, or 200–300 mL just before if waking close to run time
- **Coffee:** many runners pair coffee with their pre-run meal — caffeine boosts energy and can aid digestion
- Applies to: All
