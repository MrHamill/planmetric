## No-Nonsense Endurance Nutrition (Strava)

### Carbohydrate Intake During Exercise

Carbohydrates are the primary fuel source above ~70% VO2max. Under-fuelling is the leading cause of "bonking" or hitting the wall.

| Effort Duration | Carbohydrate Target |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| < 60 min | 0–30 g/hr (optional) |
| 60–90 min | 30–60 g/hr |
| 90 min – 2.5 hrs | 60–80 g/hr |
| > 2.5 hrs | 80–90 g/hr (trained gut can reach 120 g/hr) |

### Multiple Transportable Carbohydrates
- The gut can absorb **~60 g/hr of glucose** and a further **~30 g/hr of fructose** via separate intestinal transporters
- Products combining **2:1 glucose:fructose** (e.g. most sports gels, Maurten) allow 80–90 g/hr absorption without GI distress
- Gut training: tolerance to high carbohydrate intake improves with consistent training — start at lower end and build over weeks

### Hydration Guidelines

| Condition | Fluid Target |
|-----------|-------------|
| Mild conditions (<20°C) | 500–600 mL/hr |
| Moderate conditions (20–25°C) | 600–750 mL/hr |
| Hot conditions (>25°C) | 750–1,000 mL/hr |

- **Sweat rate varies significantly between athletes** — the above is a starting point
- Sweat test: weigh before and after a 1-hr session (no fluid); 1 kg weight loss ≈ 1L sweat loss
- Electrolytes (sodium) are required for sessions >90 min to prevent hyponatremia and muscle cramping

### Sodium Replacement
- Average sweat sodium concentration: 500–1,500 mg/L (highly individual)
- Target: 300–700 mg sodium per hour for sessions >90 min in heat
- Sources: electrolyte tabs, sports drink, salt capsules
- Low sodium intake during long races is a leading cause of GI cramping and heat illness

### Pre-Race Fuelling
- **Night before:** High-carbohydrate meal (~8–10 g carbs/kg body weight across the day)
- **Morning of race (3–4 hrs before):** 1–2 g carbs/kg body weight; familiar foods, low fibre, low fat
- **Final 30–60 min before:** 20–30 g fast-release carbs (gel, banana) if desired; avoid this for <90 min events

### Race Day Nutrition by Distance

**Sprint (750m / 20km / 5km):**
- Little to no fuelling required during race
- Strong pre-race carbohydrate load is more important
- Consider a gel 5 min before swim start

**Olympic (1.5km / 40km / 10km):**
- 1–2 gels on the bike (30–60 g carbs)
- Begin fuelling within the first 20–30 min of the bike
- No need to fuel on the run (duration too short)

**70.3 Half Iron:**
- 60–80 g carbs/hr on the bike (2–3 hrs)
- 30–60 g carbs/hr on the run (1–2 hrs)
- Practise race nutrition in training — GI issues in a 70.3 are almost always preventable

**Ironman Full:**
- 80–90 g carbs/hr target (gut-trained athletes may reach 120 g/hr)
- Fluid: 750–1,000 mL/hr depending on conditions
- Sodium: 500–700 mg/hr
- Nutrition strategy must be race-rehearsed at least 2–3 times in long training days

### Common Mistakes
1. **Starting fuelling too late** — glycogen depletion is hard to reverse once it occurs; fuel proactively from the first 20 min
2. **Relying on race-day nutrition without training the gut** — novel gels/drinks in a race frequently cause GI distress
3. **Over-relying on water without electrolytes** in long events → dilutes sodium → nausea, cramping
4. **Under-eating on heavy training days** — total daily energy availability must match training load to prevent RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)

### Daily Training Nutrition Principles
- **Carbohydrate periodisation:** High carbohydrate on high-intensity and long training days; moderate on easy days
- **Protein:** 1.6–2.0 g/kg/day for endurance athletes (lean muscle maintenance and repair)
- **Recovery window:** Consume 0.3–0.5 g/kg carbohydrate + 20–30 g protein within 30–60 min of hard sessions
- **Caffeine:** 3–6 mg/kg body weight 45–60 min before exercise improves time-trial performance by 2–4%

### Gut Training Protocol
For athletes targeting 80+ g/hr during long events:
1. Week 1–2: 40 g/hr with water
2. Week 3–4: 60 g/hr
3. Week 5–6: 80 g/hr
4. Week 7+: 80–90 g/hr, testing specific race-day products

Train the gut on the bike — this is where the majority of race-day fuelling occurs and where GI issues most commonly arise.
