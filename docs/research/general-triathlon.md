## Norwegian Coaches Best-Practice (PMC12031707)

**Context:** Elite endurance coaches from Norway — one of the most successful nations per capita — describe their training principles across cycling, cross-country skiing, rowing, and triathlon.

### Volume
- Elite triathletes: ~1,300–1,500 hrs/year
- Age-groupers targeting performance: typically 10–15 hrs/week

### Intensity Distribution
- **80–90% of training at low intensity** (Zone 1–2, conversational pace, below VT1)
- **5–10% at threshold** (Zone 3–4, comfortably hard)
- **5% at high intensity** (Zone 5, VO2max intervals)
- This "polarised" or "pyramidal" approach is consistently observed in elite endurance athletes across disciplines

### Weekly Structure
- **2–3 key quality sessions per week** (intervals, threshold work, race-specific efforts)
- Remaining sessions are low-intensity aerobic work
- Recovery days should be genuinely easy — not moderate

### Periodisation
- Build aerobic base heavily in off-season
- Introduce threshold work in build phase
- Peak with race-specific intervals and taper

### Key Coaching Principles
- Aerobic base is the foundation — never sacrifice it for shortcuts
- High-intensity work requires full recovery before the next quality session
- Consistency over weeks and months matters more than any single session
- Athletes should train to improve, not to prove fitness in training

## Physiology of Endurance Champions (PMC2375555)

**Context:** Review of physiological determinants in world-class endurance athletes.

### Aerobic Capacity
- Elite male endurance athletes: VO2max typically **70–85 mL/kg/min**
- Elite female endurance athletes: **60–75 mL/kg/min**
- VO2max alone does not differentiate champions — threshold and economy are equally predictive

### Lactate Threshold
- Elite athletes sustain **75–90% of VO2max** at lactate threshold (LT2)
- Training at and around threshold is the key driver of performance improvement in trained athletes
- LT1 (aerobic threshold) should be kept comfortable for long aerobic sessions

### Running Economy
- Champions use less oxygen at a given pace — improved through strength work, plyometrics, high mileage
- Economy improvements take 1–2 years of consistent training to manifest

### Marathon-Specific
- Elite marathoners race at **75–85% of VO2max**
- Pace at LT2 correlates strongly with marathon performance
- Glycogen depletion ("hitting the wall") occurs when carbohydrate stores are insufficient at high % VO2max

## Individualized vs. Standardized Training (PMC9473708)

**Context:** 15-week RCT comparing individualized to standardized endurance training programs.

### Results
- Individualized group: **−6.2% improvement** in 10km time
- Standardized group: **−2.9% improvement**
- Effect size is practically significant — individualization roughly doubles improvement rate

### Monitoring Used
- **HRV (Heart Rate Variability)** — morning HRV guided session intensity decisions
- **Perceived recovery** — subjective readiness rating each morning
- When HRV was suppressed and/or perceived recovery was low, intensity was reduced

### Key Implications for Plan Design
- Training load must respond to the individual's recovery status, not a fixed weekly script
- Monitoring tools (Garmin Body Battery, Whoop, Oura) can substitute or supplement HRV
- Rigid plans without adjustment mechanisms underperform flexible, data-responsive plans

## T1–T5 Zone System (highperformancetri.com)

| Zone | Name | % Max HR | Feel | Typical Use |
|------|------|----------|------|-------------|
| T1 | Active Recovery | <65% | Very easy, could hold a conversation effortlessly | Recovery rides/runs |
| T2 | Aerobic Base | 65–75% | Easy, comfortable, sustainable for hours | Long rides/runs, base building |
| T3 | Tempo/Threshold | 75–85% | Comfortably hard, can speak in short sentences | Tempo intervals, sustained efforts |
| T4 | VO2max | 85–92% | Hard, breathing laboured, short sentences only | VO2max intervals (3–8 min) |
| T5 | Anaerobic | >92% | Very hard, unsustainable beyond ~2 min | Sprint intervals, neuromuscular development |

### Coaching Notes
- Zone 2 (T2) is the most underused zone in amateur athletes — most train "junk miles" in T3
- 80% of aerobic adaptation occurs in T1–T2
- T4/T5 sessions require 48–72 hrs recovery before next quality session

## Heart Rate Zone Calibration Across Disciplines (vic.tri-alliance.com)

### Karvonen Formula (Heart Rate Reserve Method)
```
Training HR = Resting HR + (% × (Max HR − Resting HR))
```
Example: Resting HR 55, Max HR 185, Zone 2 = 70%:
→ 55 + (0.70 × 130) = **146 BPM**

### Discipline Adjustments
Running produces the **highest HR** of the three disciplines due to total-body muscle recruitment and gravitational loading.

| Discipline | HR Adjustment vs. Running |
|------------|--------------------------|
| Running | Baseline |
| Cycling | −5 to −10 BPM (less gravitational loading, seated position) |
| Swimming | −10 to −15 BPM (horizontal position, water cooling, no gravitational load) |

### Practical Application
- Do not use the same zone numbers across all three disciplines
- A Zone 2 run at 145 BPM should correspond to ~135–140 BPM on the bike and ~130–135 BPM in the pool
- Each athlete should establish zones independently for each discipline via a field test

## Training Zone Calibration Methods

### Field Tests by Discipline
- **Run:** 30-min all-out time trial; average HR of final 20 min = Functional Threshold HR (FTHR)
- **Bike:** 20-min FTP test; 95% of average power = FTP; average HR = cycling FTHR
- **Swim:** 400m + 200m time trial; CSS formula (see swim.md)

### Re-test Frequency
- Every 6–8 weeks during build phase
- After a significant training block or illness
- As fitness improves, zones shift upward — zones based on stale data lead to undertrained athletes

## Plan Structure: Building a Triathlon Season

> **See also:** `periodisation.md` for detailed phase-by-phase protocols, distance-specific breakdowns (Olympic/70.3/Ironman), and the full Joe Friel six-phase model.

### Phase Overview
| Phase | Duration | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Base | 8–12 weeks | Aerobic volume, technique, Z1–Z2 only |
| Build | 8–10 weeks | Introduce threshold, sport-specific work, brick sessions |
| Peak | 4–6 weeks | Race-specific intensity, volume reduction begins |
| Taper | 2–3 weeks | Volume drops significantly, intensity maintained |
| Race | 1 week | Sharpening, rest, race execution |

### Discipline Priority for Age-Groupers
Most age-group triathlon time is lost on the run (not the swim or bike). Training allocation should reflect this:
- Swim: technique work is high-value; volume matters less than in pure swimming
- Bike: highest total volume (longest discipline in most formats); FTP is the key variable
- Run: highest injury risk; conservative volume build, never sacrifice recovery here

### Brick Sessions (Key for Triathlon)
- At least **2–4 brick sessions** (bike→run immediately) in the 6 weeks before race day
- Purpose: neuromuscular adaptation to the discipline transition
- Protocol: 60–90 min bike at race power → 15–30 min run at race pace, no break
- The "brick legs" sensation diminishes with repeated practice

## How to Build Your Own Triathlon Training Plan (220 Triathlon / Phil Mosley, MyProCoach)

### The Three Guiding Principles (Joe Friel, The Triathlete's Training Bible)
1. **Least amount** — small gains over a long time beat quick gains. Bodies absorb changes of ~10% per week; beyond that leads to breakdown.
2. **Continual improvement** — gradual week-to-week changes produce fitness that stays longer and reaches higher levels.
3. **Most specific** — every session should have a clear purpose (improve fitness, maintain fitness, or recover).

### Minimum Training to Start Racing by Distance
| Distance | Swim | Bike | Run | Readiness Benchmark |
|----------|------|------|-----|---------------------|
| Supersprint (400/10km/2.5km) | 20–30 min | 20–30 min | 20–30 min | Can complete each individually |
| Olympic (1.5km/40km/10km) | 1.125km | 30km | 7.5km | 75% of race distance in each |
| 70.3 / Ironman | Multiple sessions/week | 4–7 hr long rides | Multiple sessions/week | Well-established base required |

### Progression Rules (Phil Mosley)
- Swim and run: increase duration by **max 5 min/week**
- Bike: increase duration by **max 10 min/week**
- Do NOT do back-to-back hard sessions (hard = high intensity OR high duration)
- **Active recovery week every 4th week** at ~50% of normal load — physical and mental reset
- At least one complete rest day per week

### Intensity Distribution by Season
| Season Phase | % Below 75% Max HR | % Above 75% Max HR |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Off-season / Base | ~80% | ~20% |
| Build / Pre-race | ~65% | ~35% |

**Why:** Low-intensity base builds fat-burning capacity and aerobic engine; repeated speed efforts require a strong aerobic foundation; lower intensity preserves immune function across a long season.

### Discipline Guidance
- **Swim:** Technique is more important than volume. Every session needs a drill component. A 30% stronger swimmer saves ~2 min in an Olympic race — less than fixing T1 or pacing the run.
- **Bike:** Best discipline for aerobic volume accumulation. Long weekend ride is the anchor of every triathlete's week.
- **Run:** Highest injury risk. Run off-road when possible. Include one quality (intensity) run session per week maximum. Never follow a hard run with another hard session.
- Training background doesn't determine discipline balance — a strong runner still benefits from 2–3 sessions per discipline

### Race-Specific Preparation (Final 6–8 Weeks)
- Include **one race-workout per week**: ride in tri suit on race bike, with efforts at race power/pace
- **Brick sessions weekly** (bike→run), plus occasional swim→bike for T1 confidence
- Include 1–2 build-up races (shorter tri or single-discipline events) to sharpen confidence
- Begin **open water swimming** at minimum 4–6 weeks before race day
- Taper: optimal taper duration = **8–14 days**; reduce volume 20–50%, maintain intensity

## How to Make Time for Triathlon Training (Ironman Community Advice)

### Structural Principles
- **Assess your week before planning** — identify time blocks that can be dedicated to training before layering in sessions
- **Schedule workouts as appointments** — treat them the same as work meetings (don't cancel on yourself)
- **Morning training (4:30–6:00am) is the most reliable window** — life hasn't started yet, distractions are minimal
- Prepare kit the night before — reduces friction that kills early-morning training

### Efficiency Approaches
- **Commute training:** cycling or running to/from work combines transport and training
- **Brick during the week:** ride home → immediate 20-min run replicates race conditions and saves time
- **Indoor trainer:** all cycling training for a 70.3/Ironman can be done indoors — no transit time, weather-independent, family-compatible during early hours
- Short sessions still count: 30 min of structured intervals is a valid training session

### Consistency Over Volume
- "If it's important enough to you, you will make the time for it" — frequency matters more than any single long session
- Quality over quantity: every session should have a purpose; unsupervised junk miles don't move the needle
- Missing one session is fine; missing one week sets you back; missing one month requires rebuilding
- Hire a coach to eliminate the planning burden and prevent the most common error: doing too much

### Family Integration
- Communicate your training schedule to family in advance — buy-in reduces friction
- Co-training where possible (cycling alongside kids, running with a partner)
- Recovery weeks serve double duty: physical rest + family time

## 50 Ways to Be a Better Triathlete — Key Principles (Ironman / Marni Sumbal)

Selected principles most applicable to Planmetric plan design and coaching context:

**Training Philosophy**
- Quality over quantity — don't equate more hours with more improvement
- Keep easy days easy — most athletes train easy days too hard and hard days too easy
- You cannot buy speed — equipment upgrades have a ceiling; fitness does not
- Stay process-driven, not outcome-focused — daily habits compound

**Health and Longevity**
- Never let performance come before health — training through illness/injury destroys fitness faster than a rest week
- Maintain a healthy relationship with food and body — RED-S is a performance-killer
- Never underestimate the value of restful sleep — it is the highest-ROI recovery tool

**Mindset**
- Accept adversity and setbacks as part of training — they are data, not failures
- Don't be too hard on yourself — a bad session is one session, not a trend
- Fall in love with the process of self-improvement — if you only train for race day, the other 364 days feel like punishment
- Anything is possible — the athletes who achieve most are not always the most talented
