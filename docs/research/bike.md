## 15 Key Cycling Training Sessions (Roberto Vukovic)

### Zone Reference (FTP-based)
| Zone | Name | % FTP | RPE |
|------|------|-------|-----|
| Z1 | Active Recovery | <55% | 1–2 |
| Z2 | Endurance | 56–75% | 3–4 |
| Z3 | Tempo | 76–90% | 5–6 |
| Z4 | Threshold | 91–105% | 7–8 |
| Z5 | VO2max | 106–120% | 9 |
| Z6 | Anaerobic | >121% | 10 |

---

### Session 1 — Endurance Ride
**Purpose:** Aerobic base development, fat oxidation, musculoskeletal conditioning.
- Duration: 2–5 hrs
- Intensity: Z2 (56–75% FTP), HR 65–75% max
- Steady state — no surges, no chasing
- Most important session for triathletes; chronically under-utilised

### Session 2 — Sweet Spot Training
**Purpose:** High-efficiency threshold development; best watts-per-hour-of-fatigue ratio.
- Protocol: 2–3 × 20 min at 88–93% FTP
- Recovery: 5 min Z1 between efforts
- Sweet spot sits just below threshold — high adaptation stimulus with manageable fatigue

### Session 3 — Threshold Intervals (FTP Blocks)
**Purpose:** Raise functional threshold power.
- Protocol: 2 × 20 min or 3 × 15 min at 95–105% FTP
- Recovery: 5–10 min easy spin
- HR should stabilise ~90–95% of threshold HR by end of interval
- Do not chase power from the first minute — let it build

### Session 4 — Tempo Blocks
**Purpose:** Aerobic threshold development, fat-burning efficiency.
- Protocol: 1 × 45–60 min or 2 × 30 min at 76–88% FTP
- Recovery: 10 min Z1
- Suitable for early build phase; less taxing than threshold but still productive

### Session 5 — VO2max Intervals
**Purpose:** Raise aerobic ceiling, improve power at VO2max.
- Protocol: 5–8 × 3–5 min at 110–120% FTP
- Recovery: equal time spinning Z1–Z2
- First interval should feel achievable — fatigue accumulates
- Stop if power drops >10% below target

### Session 6 — Over-Unders (Threshold Tolerance)
**Purpose:** Train the body to clear lactate during sustained race efforts.
- Protocol: 3 × 10 min alternating: 2 min at 115% FTP / 3 min at 95% FTP
- This simulates the surges and recovery in a race or group ride
- Very taxing — typically only once per week

### Session 7 — Sprint Intervals
**Purpose:** Neuromuscular power, sprint finish capability.
- Protocol: 8–12 × 10–15 sec maximal sprints
- Recovery: 3–5 min complete rest between each
- Seated or standing — both have value; standing produces higher peak power
- Total sprint work volume is low (<4 min); quality over quantity

### Session 8 — Race Simulation
**Purpose:** Specificity for target race pace and duration.
- Protocol: Ride at planned race power/HR for 80–100% of race distance
- Include target nutrition strategy (carbs/hr, fluid/hr)
- Best done in build phase, 4–6 weeks before race day

### Session 9 — Recovery Ride
**Purpose:** Active recovery, promote blood flow without adding training stress.
- Duration: 30–60 min
- Intensity: Z1 only (<55% FTP, HR <65% max)
- If HR drifts above Z1, slow down — the purpose is recovery, not fitness

### Session 10 — Hill Repeats
**Purpose:** Power development, force application, sport-specific strength.
- Protocol: 6–10 × 3–5 min at 105–120% FTP on a 5–8% gradient
- Recovery: freewheel back down (~3–5 min)
- Develops seated climbing power; increases average power on flat courses too

### Session 11 — Cadence Pyramid
**Purpose:** Improve pedalling efficiency across a range of cadences.
- Protocol: 5 min at 60 rpm → 5 min at 70 rpm → 5 min at 80 rpm → 5 min at 90 rpm → 5 min at 100 rpm → reverse
- Maintain constant power output through all cadence ranges
- Identifies each athlete's optimal cadence range

### Session 12 — Isolated Leg Training (ILT)
**Purpose:** Eliminate dead spot in pedal stroke, improve single-leg power.
- Protocol: 6 × 30 sec single-leg pedalling, each leg
- Recovery: 30 sec both legs between each
- Best on a trainer; most athletes initially struggle above 60 rpm single-leg

### Session 13 — Motor Pacing / Group Ride
**Purpose:** Simulate race demands — surges, sustained high power, tactical pacing.
- 2–3 hr ride with structured surges at race intensity
- Teaches power management in variable-pace scenarios
- Only relevant if triathlete uses drafting events; otherwise use controlled efforts

### Session 14 — Brick Session (Bike + Run)
**Purpose:** Overcome "brick legs" — the neuromuscular transition from cycling to running.
- Protocol: 60–90 min at race power → immediate 15–30 min run at race pace
- No break between disciplines — transition as in race conditions
- Do at least 2–3 bricks in the 6 weeks before race day

### Session 15 — Long Aerobic Ride + Blocks
**Purpose:** Combine aerobic volume with race-pace quality.
- Protocol: 3–4 hr ride with 3 × 20 min sweet spot blocks embedded (at 2hr, 2.5hr, 3hr marks)
- Remaining time in Z2
- Simulates the fatigue-under-load that defines Ironman pacing

## Cadence Optimisation (TrainerRoad)

### Optimal RPM Range
- **80–100 RPM** is optimal for most cyclists and triathletes
- Higher cadence (90–100 RPM) reduces muscular fatigue on the bike and preserves run legs in triathlon
- Lower cadence (70–80 RPM) develops more muscular force but increases fatigue

### Why Cadence Matters for Triathletes
- Triathlon bike legs require glycogen sparing — higher cadence is more cardiovascular, less muscular
- Arriving at T2 with fresh legs requires a cadence discipline that is often higher than "natural" preference
- Many age-groupers default to 70–75 RPM because it feels powerful — but costs them on the run

### 4 Cadence Drill Types

**1. High Cadence Spins**
- 5–10 min at 100–110 RPM in Z1–Z2
- Purpose: train smooth, efficient pedal stroke at high RPM
- Cue: eliminate "bouncing" in the saddle — smooth is fast

**2. Low Cadence Force Reps**
- 4–6 × 5 min at 55–65 RPM at Z3–Z4 power
- Purpose: develop force production, simulate climbing or headwind
- Cue: full pedal circle engagement — avoid stomping

**3. Single-Leg Drills**
- 6 × 30 sec each leg, Z1 resistance
- Purpose: identify and correct dead spots in pedal stroke
- Cue: "scrape mud off shoe" at the bottom of the stroke

**4. Cadence Ramps**
- Start at 80 RPM, increase 5 RPM every 2 min to 110+ RPM
- Purpose: challenge neuromuscular ceiling; find "spin-out" threshold
- Most athletes plateau at 100–105 RPM before losing control

## Indoor Trainer Setup (Strava)

### Session Structure
Indoor sessions should be structured to maintain focus — unstructured Z2 riding indoors is mentally difficult and often produces poor pacing compliance.

**Recommended structure:**
1. **5–10 min warm-up** at Z1, gradually raising cadence to 90+ RPM
2. **Main set** — interval-based or structured tempo (see sessions above)
3. **5–10 min cool-down** at Z1

### Setup Checklist
- Fan: mandatory — core temperature rises faster indoors due to no airflow
- Hydration: 750–1,000 mL/hr is appropriate indoors (higher sweat rate than outdoors)
- Screen: ERG mode on smart trainer removes pacing discipline — use resistance mode for threshold+ work
- Towel: protect drivetrain and components from sweat corrosion

### ERG Mode Notes
- ERG mode appropriate for endurance/recovery sessions
- For intervals above threshold: disable ERG and ride to power — teaches real pacing
- ERG "death spiral" at VO2max: if cadence drops, resistance increases, causing further slowdown — avoid by maintaining 85+ RPM

### Session Timing
- Indoor sessions shorter than outdoor equivalents produce similar training stress (no coasting, no traffic lights)
- 60-min indoor interval session ≈ 90-min outdoor ride for training adaptation

## 9 Ways to Become a Better Cyclist — Triathlon Application (Ironman / Mike Ricci, D3 Multisport)

### 1. Race Bike Specificity
- Use your triathlon bike for all race-specific and brick sessions — not just race day
- Road bike is fine for easy recovery rides (gives a break from the aero position)
- The more comfortable you are on your tri bike in training, the better you'll race on it
- Never ride your tri bike for the first time at a race

### 2. Hit All Energy Zones in a 10-Day Block
- Endurance, strength, and speed are the three pillars of cycling fitness
- Within a 10-day block, include: VO2max work, threshold, tempo, hills, and long endurance
- Example: VO2max early in the week → threshold mid-week → long hilly ride with tempo finish on weekend
- Build up to this schedule gradually; don't add multiple hard sessions at once

### 3. Vary Brick Run Speeds
- Don't always run at the same pace off the bike
- Rotate: one run at easy pace, one with strides inserted, every other week a race-pace run
- Goal: your run pace should not be a surprise to your legs on race day

### 4. Establish Heart Rate Zones Correctly Before Training "By Zone"
- Protocol: after proper warm-up, all-out 10-mile or 30-min time trial → record average HR
- Use that HR to set zones using an online calculator
- Zone 2 (endurance) is not "easy" once zones are correctly set — it requires discipline
- Incorrect zones are the #1 reason athletes train ineffectively by HR

### 5. Cadence Is Strategic, Not Fixed
- Self-selected cadence varies by athlete (75–100 RPM is a wide normal range)
- To ride well across terrain (climbs, false flats, rollers), learn to use all cadences
- Drill: 4 × 5 min at 65, 70, 75, and 80 RPM at constant power — develops full cadence range
- Higher cadence (90–100 RPM) spares muscular glycogen on long triathlon bike legs

### 6. Quality Over Quantity for Long-Course Build
- 6+ months out from an Ironman: focus on weakness correction, not volume
- If hills are your weakness, prioritize climbing quality over mileage
- 100–150 miles/week is fine if time allows, but quality (FTP, weakness work) matters more
- Do not try to build peak volume too far out — save that for the specific build phase

### 7. Aero Helmet ROI
- Wind tunnel data: aero helmet saves as much time as more expensive aero wheelsets
- Cost per minute saved: aero helmet offers the best value of any aerodynamic upgrade
- If racing competitively in your age group without one, you're giving others a measurable advantage

### 8. Sweat Rate Testing
- Significant performance decline is caused by dehydration — but correction requires knowing your individual sweat rate
- Protocol: weigh nude before 1-hr session → towel off and reweigh after → account for fluids consumed → 1 kg loss = 1L sweat
- Work with a nutritionist to match electrolyte intake (especially sodium) to sweat composition
- Individual sodium concentration in sweat varies enormously — generic advice often under-replaces

### 9. Power Meter as Gold Standard
- Power meter is the most accurate measure of cycling output — not affected by terrain or HR lag
- If no power meter: HR monitor + known lactate threshold is the best alternative
- Speedometer is only useful on flat terrain — useless for gauging effort on variable courses
- Recommended progression: speedometer → HR → power meter as athlete investment grows

## Power Zones vs Heart Rate Zones — Which to Use and When (Stages Cycling)

### Heart Rate Zones (5-Zone Model)
| Zone | Name | % HR Max | Use |
|------|------|----------|-----|
| 1 | Very Light | 50–60% | Recovery, warmups, cool-downs |
| 2 | Light | 60–70% | Endurance building, fat oxidation |
| 3 | Moderate | 70–80% | Cardiovascular efficiency, lactate starts accumulating |
| 4 | Hard | 80–90% | Speed and endurance capacity |
| 5 | Maximum | 90–100% | Interval training, unsustainable for extended duration |

- Max HR estimated as 220 − age (rough guide, individual testing preferred)
- **Applies to:** All

### Power Zones (7-Zone Coggan Model)
| Zone | Name | % FTP | Use |
|------|------|-------|-----|
| 1 | Active Recovery | 0–59% | Circulation, movement reinforcement |
| 2 | Endurance | 60–79% | Long-duration aerobic, mitochondrial density |
| 3 | Tempo | 80–90% | Type IIa fibre recruitment, glycogen storage |
| 4 | Threshold | 91–104% | Muscular endurance, ~20–30 min sustainable |
| 5 | VO2max | 105–120% | Aerobic ceiling, cardiac remodelling |
| 6 | Anaerobic | 121–150% | Short high-force efforts, lactate tolerance |
| 7 | Neuromuscular | 151%+ | All-out sprints, neuromuscular recruitment |

- FTP = sustainable power for ~1 hour
- **Note:** These Coggan zones differ slightly from the 6-zone model in the sessions section above (Z2 upper range 79% vs 75%, Z3 starts at 80% vs 76%). Both are widely used — be consistent within a plan.
- **Applies to:** All

### When to Use Power vs Heart Rate
- Power measures how hard you are actually working; heart rate measures the body's response to that work. Power is immediate; HR lags by 30–90 seconds.
- HR is easily influenced by temperature, hydration, fatigue, stress, and caffeine. Power is objective — a watt is a watt regardless of conditions.
- For indoor cycling with temperature elevation and short intervals, power should be the primary metric.
- Optimal training uses all three: power, heart rate, and RPE together. No single metric gives a complete picture.
- **Applies to:** All (power meter required for power-based training)

## Cadence for Triathletes — Optimal Range and Why

- Higher cadence requires less force per pedal stroke and places less stress on joints and muscles, reducing overuse injury risk. However, too high a cadence becomes less effective as muscles must fire faster, requiring more fibre recruitment.
- **Power formula:** Force × Cadence = Power. When cadence increases, force per pedal stroke decreases, and vice versa.
- The optimal cadence occurs where the body balances the best combination of muscle force and muscle shortening speed. Too low recruits extra fibres (high force); too high also recruits extra fibres (high speed demand).
- **Recommended range: 85–100 RPM** for triathletes. Ride at the highest cadence you're comfortable with without giving up any power. Either end of this range is equally effective.
- Higher cadence is more cardiovascular, less muscular — spares glycogen and preserves run legs for T2.
- Many age-groupers default to 70–75 RPM because it feels powerful but costs them on the run.
- **Applies to:** All

## Nutrition on the Bike — IRONMAN Fueling Plan (Jesse Kropelnicki)

### Carb-Loading Protocol
- Begin carbohydrate loading at lunchtime, two days before the race. Incorporate grains not typically consumed (e.g. white bread), without finishing meals uncomfortably full.
- Main carb load at breakfast the day before — finish eating by 9am, then taper food for the rest of the day with frequent carb-rich snacks (pretzels). Early, light dinner. Foods very low in fat and fibre.
- **Target: ~10g of carbohydrate per kg of body weight** across the loading period.
- **Applies to:** All (Ironman / 70.3)

### Race Morning
- Eat **3.5 hours before start** (yes, ~3:30am for most Ironman starts).
- **110–180g of carbohydrate** depending on size, with very little fat or fibre.
- Example: 2.5 cups unsweetened apple sauce, 1 scoop whey protein, 1 bottle sports drink, 1 banana.
- **Applies to:** All (Ironman)

### On-Bike Fueling
- **Minimum 1.3g carbohydrate per hour per kg of body weight** on the bike. Half that on the run.
- Sodium content of fuels should be at least **8mg per gram of carbohydrate**.
- Fat, protein, and fibre content should be absolutely minimal.
- **Applies to:** All (Ironman / 70.3)

### Caffeine Strategy
- Avoid caffeine during race week and early on race day to maintain sensitivity.
- Don't consume caffeine until at least **2–3 hours into the race** — it may wear off if taken too early.
- Caffeine helps maintain elevated heart rate and proper pacing in the later stages.
- General rule: keep intake below **200mg per day / 1,000mg per week**.
- **Applies to:** All

### Sodium Management
- Most athletes underestimate race-day sodium needs. Athletes lose **800–4,000mg of sodium per hour**.
- Estimate: **500–800mg sodium per 500mL of sweat lost**. If you've experienced cramping, err to the higher side.
- Always carry extra sodium capsules (at least **200mg per capsule**). If you feel a muscle twinge or stomach bloating, take one immediately.
- **Applies to:** All (Ironman / 70.3)

### Hydration
- Avoid plain water during an Ironman — it increases probability of sloshy stomach due to lack of sodium and disrupts blood sugar response. Stick to sports drinks with appropriate sodium concentration.
- **Pee check: aim for at least 2 urinations during the bike** (Ironman) or at least 1 (70.3). Athletes who fail to do this typically won't run to their potential.
- **Applies to:** All (Ironman / 70.3)

### Pacing and Gut Integrity
- Even the best nutrition plan backfires if the race is mispaced. Start steady — no harder than you're able to finish with.
- Too much stress too early shuts down the gut and renders fueling useless. Bloating and sloshing generally occur from not enough sodium or too much stress.
- If gut issues creep up, slow down and back off pace to let things clear. Few athletes have the courage to do this before it's too late.
- **Applies to:** All

### Heart Rate as Fueling Indicator
- Mistakes in fueling and pacing show up as a lower heart rate toward the end of the run. If you're a geek about one thing, make it this — the ability to keep HR elevated during the later run portions defines your day.
- **Applies to:** Intermediate / Elite

## Ironman Bike Pacing Strategy

- Most athletes go too hard on the bike. The bike is not where you win an Ironman — it's where you set up (or destroy) your run.
- Start with a steady pace no harder than you can finish with. Too much early stress shuts down the gut and causes fueling failure.
- Monitor heart rate throughout — any mistakes made on the bike will be visible as declining HR on the run.
- **Applies to:** All (Ironman / 70.3)

## How to Structure a Long Ride

### Standard Long Ride Structure
1. **Warm-up (15–20 min):** Easy pedaling in Zones 1–2 to raise heart rate and prepare muscles
2. **Main body:** Steady, low-intensity, conversational-pace riding in Zone 2
3. **Race-pace intervals (mid-ride):** 30–60 min blocks at target race wattage or heart rate, followed by 10–20 min easy spinning
4. **Final hour:** Challenging section at the end to simulate building fatigue while maintaining speed
5. **Cool-down (10–15 min):** Very easy spin to flush legs

### Session Types
- **Aerobic long ride (base phase):** Focus on comfort and fat-burning in Zone 1–2
- **Simulation ride (race prep):** Ride in aero bars for over 80% of the time, practising race-day nutrition and pacing
- **Back-half intervals:** Ride the first half easy, then perform 10 × 5 min at above-race effort with 2 min easy recovery
- **Big gear intervals (strength):** Within a 5–6 hour ride, perform 5 × 8 min in a heavy gear at 50–60 RPM
- **Applies to:** All

### Key Principles
- Use long rides to test race-day fuel (gels, bottles) to ensure your gut handles it.
- If you can't stay in aero bars for at least 80% of the ride, adjust your bike fit — comfort over long distances is vital.
- **10% rule:** Don't increase long ride distance or duration by more than 10% per week.
- Brick training: occasionally add a short 15–30 min run immediately after to get used to "jelly legs."
- **Applies to:** All

### Scheduling
- Long ride usually best on weekends. If the week is busy, protect the long ride and long run above all other sessions.
- **Recovery weeks:** Every 3rd or 4th week, shorten the long ride to allow the body to adapt.
- **Applies to:** All

## Indoor Trainer vs Outdoor Riding (TrainerRoad)

- Indoor rides produce steady, controlled power (within ~1% of target in ERG mode). Outdoor rides have more variability due to terrain, traffic, and environment.
- Both produce comparable training stimulus — TSS and Intensity Factor align closely when properly executed.
- Indoor workouts are more time-efficient: **60 min indoor ≈ 90 min outdoor** for equivalent training adaptation (no coasting, no traffic lights).
- Outdoor sessions typically require 1.5–2× longer duration due to travel and route logistics.
- Heat and humidity rise faster indoors due to no airflow — fan is mandatory, hydration needs are higher indoors (**750–1,000 mL/hr**).
- Route selection should match workout demands — flat routes for threshold work, hilly routes for climbing sessions.
- For intervals above threshold: disable ERG mode and ride to power — teaches real pacing. ERG mode is fine for endurance/recovery sessions.
- **Applies to:** All

## Brick Sessions — How Often, What Duration, What Purpose

### What Is a Brick
- A brick workout performs two triathlon disciplines consecutively, most often bike → run. The neuromuscular system must completely reorganise from the confined pedal motion of cycling to the elastic, eccentric force of running within seconds.
- When done consistently, brick sessions teach the nervous system to anticipate the change. Blood flow adjusts faster, muscle recruitment patterns streamline, and the feeling of "heavy legs" diminishes.
- **Applies to:** All

### Why Bricks Matter
- T2 (bike to run) is the most punishing transition — heart rate spikes, blood flow shifts, posture changes, and pacing sense often collapses.
- Cycling primarily uses concentric muscle contractions; running demands eccentric control and elastic recoil. Without practice, the switch feels like chaos.
- Benefits: circulatory adaptation (redirecting oxygenated blood from cycling to running muscles), motor-pattern conditioning, metabolic efficiency, thermoregulation, and mental confidence.
- **Applies to:** All

### When to Incorporate
| Phase | Frequency | Focus |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Base (early season) | 1 short brick every 10–14 days | Frequency, not duration — adaptation without draining reserves |
| Build (mid-season) | Weekly, alternating aerobic and race-pace | Where most adaptation occurs |
| Peak (late season) | Simulation brick every 2–3 weeks | Full race pacing, nutrition, transitions |
| Taper | Reduce volume by 40%, retain short sessions | Keep the body "connected" |

- **Sprint/Olympic distance:** 1 weekly brick
- **70.3/Ironman:** 1 long endurance brick + 1 shorter technical/cadence session mid-week
- Never schedule two consecutive high-load bricks — recovery is non-negotiable.
- **Applies to:** All

### Brick Workout Types
- **Short transition brick:** 45–60 min ride + 10–15 min run. Focus: rhythm, familiarity, form under low fatigue.
- **Threshold brick:** 90 min ride (3 × 10 min at 90–95% FTP) + 30 min run at race pace. Focus: pacing, lactate tolerance, control.
- **Endurance brick:** 3–4 hr ride + 40–60 min steady run. Focus: nutrition, hydration, mental consistency.
- **Simulation brick:** Full race-distance pacing with all kit, transitions, and fueling tested. Focus: realism and confidence.
- **Applies to:** All (scale duration to race distance)

## Climbing and Descending Technique for Triathlon Courses

### Train on Undulating Terrain
- Train on hills as often as possible — not just hill repeats, but undulating loops that force constant gear selection, climbing, descending, and cornering. This trains all key skill areas every ride.
- **Rule of thumb:** For a typical 2-hour ride, aim to climb over **600m**. A tough 3-hour ride should climb **1,000m**.
- **Applies to:** All

### Gear Selection and Cadence on Climbs
- When developing bike fitness, spend the majority of time in the small ring. Focus on riding gears that give a cadence you can tap along at (~90 RPM) with a smooth pedalling action. Power development comes later, once pedalling efficiency is established.
- As strength develops, it is often quicker to stand on the pedals and power over shorter undulations rather than shift to a smaller gear. This maintains speed and momentum.
- **Applies to:** Beginner / Intermediate

### Sitting vs Standing on Climbs
- Constantly sitting tends to tire the quad muscles, impacting the ability to run off the bike. Standing in a slightly larger gear and powering through climbs uses the glutes more than the quads.
- When standing: hold the hoods, body weight forward with chest over the front hub. Keep the bike in a straight line but lean it away from you at the bottom of each pedal stroke. The tip of the seat should tap the back of the very top of your hamstrings.
- If sitting to climb, you'll generally ride a smaller gear. Fine for riding with the group or biding time, but any aggressive attack on a hill requires standing.
- **Applies to:** All

### Descending Technique
- Keep inside pedal up and push the bike into the ground with the outside leg through bends.
- Holding the drops gives a more aerodynamic position for fast descents; hoods for slower descending.
- Look where you want to go — the bike follows your eyes. Keep focus on the task, don't consider what could go wrong.
- Descending well is free time gained by rolling faster. Practice is essential.
- **Applies to:** All
