# Training Load & Monitoring Research

## ⚡ PLAN GENERATION ESSENTIALS

**80/20 intensity distribution (Seiler — the fundamental rule):**
- ~80% of sessions in Zone 1 (easy, fully conversational, <2 mmol/L lactate)
- ~20% of sessions in Zone 3 (hard, >4 mmol/L, 90%+ max HR)
- Minimise Zone 2 (moderate/tempo) — high fatigue relative to adaptation produced
- In a 5-session week: 4 easy + 1 hard; in 7 sessions: 5–6 easy + 1–2 hard
- **Easy days must be genuinely easy** — most athletes train easy days too hard and hard days too easy

**HRV guidance:**
- Track 7-day rolling average, not single-day readings (trends matter more than daily fluctuations)
- Suppressed HRV trend below personal baseline → switch planned intensity session to easy/recovery
- Most useful: during heavy build phases, post-illness or travel, and for all masters athletes (40+)

**TSS benchmarks (single session load):**
- <150 TSS: recovered by next day | 150–300: some residual fatigue | 300+: 2+ days recovery needed

**Weekly load management:**
- Max 10% weekly TSS increase from week to week
- Recovery weeks: TSB should return above zero before the next loading block
- Race-day TSB target: +10 to +25 (achieved through taper — ATL drops while CTL holds)

**For athletes without devices (RPE-based):**
- Session RPE (1–10) × duration in minutes = simple weekly load unit
- Track totals; apply 10% weekly increase rule; subjective morning readiness (1–10) as HRV substitute

## Training Stress Metrics (TSS / CTL / ATL / TSB)

These metrics quantify training load and help manage the balance between fitness and fatigue. Originally developed by Andrew Coggan for cycling (power-based), they are now widely applied across endurance sports.

### TSS (Training Stress Score)
- Measures the overall physical stress of a **single session** based on intensity and duration
- **100 TSS ≈ 1 hour at Functional Threshold Power (FTP)** — this is the reference point
- Higher intensity or longer duration = higher TSS
- Applies to: All

**TSS Benchmarks for a Single Session:**

| TSS | Recovery Impact |
|-----|----------------|
| < 150 | Low — recovered by next day |
| 150–300 | Medium — some residual fatigue next day |
| 300–450 | High — requires 2+ days recovery |
| > 450 | Very high — may need several days to fully recover |

### CTL (Chronic Training Load) — "Fitness"
- Weighted rolling average of daily TSS over the **past 42 days**
- Represents your accumulated fitness — a rising CTL indicates increasing fitness
- Takes weeks of consistent training to build; drops quickly during rest or illness
- Applies to: All

### ATL (Acute Training Load) — "Fatigue"
- Weighted rolling average of daily TSS over the **past 7 days**
- Represents short-term fatigue — rises quickly with hard training blocks
- A high ATL means the body is carrying significant recent training stress
- Applies to: All

### TSB (Training Stress Balance) — "Form" / "Freshness"
- Calculated as: **Yesterday's CTL − Yesterday's ATL**
- **Positive TSB** = rested, fresh — ready to perform (or potentially undertrained if consistently high)
- **Negative TSB** = fatigued — normal during build phases, problematic if sustained too long
- **TSB near zero** = balanced — fit but not overly fatigued
- Applies to: All

**TSB Guidelines:**

| TSB Range | Status | Action |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| +15 to +25 | Race-ready freshness | Ideal for race day (after taper) |
| +5 to +15 | Fresh | Good for key sessions, testing |
| −10 to +5 | Balanced | Normal productive training range |
| −10 to −30 | Fatigued | Acceptable during build phases; monitor recovery |
| < −30 | Overreaching | Risk of overtraining — reduce load, increase recovery |

### Practical Application
- During **base phase**, CTL should rise steadily with TSB staying between −10 and +5
- During **build phase**, deeper TSB dips (−20 to −30) are acceptable for short periods, followed by recovery weeks
- During **taper**, ATL drops while CTL stays relatively stable — TSB rises toward +10 to +25 for race day
- **Recovery weeks** should bring TSB back above zero before the next loading block
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

### Limitations
- TSS is most accurate with **power data** (cycling) — HR-based and pace-based TSS for running and swimming are approximations
- Athletes without power meters can use **RPE-based training load** (session RPE × duration in minutes) as a simpler alternative
- CTL/ATL/TSB are population-level models — individual response varies; use alongside subjective feel and HRV
- Applies to: All

## HRV-Guided Training

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) measures the variation in time between heartbeats, reflecting the balance between the sympathetic (stress) and parasympathetic (recovery) nervous systems. Daily HRV monitoring can guide training intensity decisions.

### How It Works
- **Higher HRV** (more variation between beats) = parasympathetic dominance = recovered, ready for intensity
- **Lower HRV** (less variation) = sympathetic dominance = stressed, fatigued, or under-recovered
- Measured first thing in the morning, lying down, for 60–120 seconds using a chest strap or validated app

### HRV-Guided vs Traditional Training
- Research protocols compare HRV-guided groups (daily HRV determines whether to train hard or easy) against traditional groups (fixed training plan regardless of readiness)
- The individualised PMC9473708 study (see `general-triathlon.md`) found HRV-guided training produced **−6.2% improvement in 10K time** vs **−2.9%** for standardised training
- HRV-guided training essentially prevents athletes from doing hard work when they're not recovered — the gains come from **better timing of intensity**, not more of it
- Applies to: All

### Practical Implementation
- Use a validated app (HRV4Training, Elite HRV) or device (Garmin Body Battery, Whoop, Oura ring) for daily morning readings
- Track a **7-day rolling average** rather than reacting to single-day readings — trends matter more than daily fluctuations
- When HRV trend is **suppressed** (below personal baseline): switch planned intensity session to easy/recovery
- When HRV trend is **elevated or normal**: proceed with planned training
- Applies to: All

### When HRV Is Most Useful
- During heavy build phases (to detect when recovery is insufficient)
- After illness, travel, or life stress (to determine readiness to resume intensity)
- For masters athletes (40+) who have reduced recovery capacity
- Less useful for athletes who already train intuitively and respond well to RPE-based decisions
- Applies to: All

## Polarised Training (Stephen Seiler)

Seiler's polarised model is the most well-researched intensity distribution for endurance athletes. Based on decades of observing elite rowers, cyclists, runners, and cross-country skiers, it defines how training time should be distributed across intensity zones.

### The Three-Zone Model

| Zone | Name | Physiological Marker | Blood Lactate | Feel |
|------|------|---------------------|---------------|------|
| Zone 1 | Low intensity | Below aerobic threshold (VT1) | < 2 mmol/L | Easy, fully conversational |
| Zone 2 | Moderate intensity | Between aerobic and anaerobic thresholds | 2–4 mmol/L | "Comfortably hard" — tempo/sweet spot |
| Zone 3 | High intensity | Above anaerobic threshold (VT2) | > 4 mmol/L | Hard to very hard, laboured breathing |

### The 80/20 Distribution
- **~80% of training** in Zone 1 (low intensity, easy)
- **~20% of training** in Zone 3 (high intensity, hard intervals)
- **Minimise Zone 2** — the "moderate-intensity black hole"
- Applies to: All

### Why Avoid the Middle?
- Zone 2 (moderate/tempo) generates **high fatigue relative to the adaptation it produces**
- Athletes who spend too much time in Zone 2 are "too hard on easy days, too easy on hard days"
- Zone 1 builds aerobic base with minimal fatigue cost
- Zone 3 produces the highest-value physiological adaptations (VO2max, lactate clearance) but requires full recovery
- The combination of lots of Zone 1 + a little Zone 3 produces better results than lots of Zone 2
- Applies to: All

### How to Apply the 80/20 Split
- The split can be measured by **number of sessions** or **time in zone** — session-based counting is simpler and more practical
- In a 5-session week: 4 easy sessions + 1 hard session = 80/20
- In a 7-session week: 5–6 easy sessions + 1–2 hard sessions = ~80/20
- The 80/20 is a **guideline ("guardrails"), not a rigid formula** — some weeks will be 85/15, others 75/25
- Applies to: All

### Scientific Evidence
- Research across elite rowers, cyclists, runners, and cross-country skiers consistently shows successful athletes spend the majority of training time in Zone 1
- Athletes who train "polarised" outperform those who train "threshold-heavy" (lots of Zone 2) in studies of 6–16 weeks duration
- This finding holds for both elite and amateur athletes training 5–7+ hours per week
- Applies to: All

### Key Coaching Principles
- **"Easy should be easy"** — most athletes train their easy days too hard, which compromises recovery and makes hard days less effective
- Hard days must be genuinely hard — Zone 3 intervals at 90%+ max HR, not moderate efforts
- The polarised approach works because it **maximises recovery** between hard sessions, allowing better quality when it matters
- Applies to: All

## Combining These Tools

### For Athletes Without Devices
- Use **session RPE × duration** as a simple training load metric (e.g. RPE 7 × 60 min = 420 load units)
- Track weekly load totals and apply the 10% weekly increase rule
- Use subjective recovery ratings (1–10 each morning) instead of HRV
- The polarised 80/20 framework requires no technology — just honest self-assessment of effort
- Applies to: Beginner

### For Athletes With HR Monitors
- HR-based TSS (hrTSS) provides reasonable training load estimates for running and cycling
- HR zones can enforce the polarised distribution — easy runs should stay in Zone 1 HR
- Morning resting HR trends (rising = fatigue accumulating) offer a basic recovery signal
- Applies to: Beginner / Intermediate

### For Athletes With Power Meters / Advanced Devices
- Power-based TSS is the gold standard for cycling load management
- CTL/ATL/TSB tracking via TrainingPeaks or similar platforms provides precise periodisation feedback
- Daily HRV monitoring adds an individualisation layer on top of planned load
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

---

## Implications for Planmetric Plan Generation

- Plans should follow the **polarised 80/20 intensity distribution** — most sessions easy, 1–2 hard sessions per discipline per week
- Easy sessions must be prescribed at genuinely easy intensity (Zone 1, conversational pace) — not moderate
- Hard sessions should be clearly marked and spaced with adequate recovery (48+ hrs between hard sessions, 72+ hrs for masters athletes)
- Recovery weeks should target a TSB return above zero before the next loading block
- Weekly load progression should respect the 10% rule
- When athlete provides HRV or readiness data, plans should adapt intensity accordingly
- For athletes without monitoring devices, include subjective readiness check-ins (RPE, sleep quality, motivation) as part of the coaching notes
