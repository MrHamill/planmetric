# Periodisation Research

## ⚡ PLAN GENERATION ESSENTIALS

**Phase proportions (pre-calculated in code — this file provides coaching context):**
- Marathon: 30% Base / 35% Build / 20% Peak / 15% Taper
- Half Marathon: 30% / 30% / 25% / 15% | 5K/10K: 30% / 35% / 25% / 10%
- Olympic Tri: 33% / 33% / 25% / 9% | 70.3: 40% / 30% / 20% / 10% | Full Ironman: 33% / 33% / 25% / 9%

**Phase characteristics (use for coaching notes and phase description copy):**
- **BASE:** Z1–Z2 only; technique drills; no racing; build aerobic engine broadly
- **BUILD:** introduce threshold + race-specific sessions; 1–2 lower-priority races permitted; intensity distribution shifts to ~65% easy / 35% hard
- **PEAK:** race simulations every 3–4 days; sessions get progressively shorter; highest stress load
- **TAPER:** reduce volume 20–50%, maintain intensity; 8–14 days optimal taper duration

**Recovery weeks:**
- Every 4th week (under 45) or every 3rd week (45+) at ~50% of peak volume
- Never skip in Build or Peak — physiological adaptation occurs during rest, not the loading block

**Progression rules:**
- Max 10–15% weekly volume increase total; swim/run max 5 min/week; bike max 10 min/week
- Never back-to-back high-intensity OR high-duration sessions

**Short plans (8–12 weeks):** compress — Base 3–4 wks, Build 3–4 wks, Peak+Taper 2–3 wks
**Long plans (20–24 weeks):** follow full Friel model; all phases distinct and clearly labelled for the athlete

## Joe Friel's Six-Phase Model (Annual Training Plan)

The Friel ATP is the most widely used periodisation framework in triathlon. It structures the season from general fitness toward race-specific sharpness.

| Phase | Typical Duration | Focus |
|-------|-----------------|-------|
| Transition | 1–6 weeks | Post-season recovery, active rest, unstructured activity |
| Preparation | 3–4 weeks | Rebuild training habits, light unstructured training, try new routes/groups/equipment |
| Base | 8–12 weeks | Aerobic endurance at low intensity, speed-skill drills, muscular endurance from week 5+ |
| Build | 6–8 weeks | Race-specific workouts, reduced emphasis on volume, include lower-priority races |
| Peak | 1–4 weeks | Race simulations every 3–4 days with easy days between, sessions get progressively shorter |
| Race | 6–7 days | Reduce volume and frequency, maintain intensity, at least 1–2 full rest days |

- Applies to: All

## Phase-by-Phase Detail

### Preparation Phase
- Duration: ~4 weeks
- Goal: build training momentum after a break; consistency matters more than intensity
- This is "guilt-free" training — missed sessions are not catastrophic
- Good time for equipment changes, new routes, joining training groups
- Applies to: All

### Base Phase
- Duration: 8–12 weeks (longer for Ironman distances)
- Analogy: Egyptian pyramid — the broader the base, the higher the peak
- Weeks 1–4: endurance at low intensity + short speed-skill efforts (strides, pick-ups)
- Weeks 5+: introduce muscular endurance — efforts of 5–20 min at ~1-hour race pace, short recoveries
- No racing — focus entirely on building general fitness without race interruption
- Intensity distribution: ~80% below 75% max HR, ~20% above
- Applies to: All

### Build Phase
- Duration: 6–8 weeks
- Workouts become race-specific; volume is no longer the priority
- Key sessions should mimic the target race in some way (distance, intensity, conditions)
- Include 1–2 lower-priority races to sharpen race skills and build confidence
- Not every session is race-specific — balance with recovery to avoid chronic fatigue
- Intensity distribution shifts: ~65% below 75% max HR, ~35% above
- Applies to: All

### Peak Phase
- Duration: 1–4 weeks (starts ~3 weeks before the A-race)
- Race simulations every 3rd or 4th day
- Simulations get progressively shorter through the phase
- Recovery/easy sessions between simulations also get shorter
- Goal: optimal mix of intensity and rest to produce race-readiness
- Applies to: All

### Race Phase (Final Taper)
- Duration: 6–7 days before race day
- Reduce volume and frequency significantly
- Maintain intensity — research supports keeping short sharp efforts in the taper
- At least 1–2 complete rest days
- Optimal taper duration: 8–14 days total (overlaps with end of Peak phase)
- Reduce volume by 20–50%, not intensity
- Applies to: All

### Transition Phase
- Duration: 2–12 weeks (after last big race of a macro-cycle)
- Emphasis on staying fit and healthy, not growing fitness
- Unstructured activity — no plan, no tracking, no guilt
- Ends when the next Preparation or Base phase begins
- Applies to: All

## Recovery Week Protocol

Recovery weeks are critical across all phases and distances:

- Schedule every 3rd or 4th week (3-on/1-off is the most common pattern)
- Reduce volume to ~50% of the previous week's load
- Serves both physical recovery (tissue repair, hormonal balance) and mental reset
- Do not skip recovery weeks during Build or Peak phases — this is when adaptation actually occurs
- Applies to: All

## Distance-Specific Periodisation

### Olympic Triathlon (1.5km / 40km / 10km) — 12-Week Plan

| Phase | Weeks | Focus |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Base Building & Technique | 1–4 | Aerobic endurance, consistent habits, technique drills, foundational strength |
| Build & Strength | 5–8 | Increase intensity and duration, introduce brick workouts (bike-to-run) |
| Peak & Race Specific | 9–11 | Highest volume and intensity, race-pace efforts, transition practice |
| Taper | 12 | Drastic volume reduction for fresh legs on race day |

- Weekly volume: 6–10 hours
- Sessions per week: 2–3 per discipline + strength + 1 rest day
- Beginners: focus on finishing (~3–6 hrs/week)
- Intermediates: focus on performance with higher volumes
- Applies to: Beginner / Intermediate

### Sample Olympic Weekly Structure
| Day | Session |
|-----|---------|
| Monday | Active recovery or rest (light swim or yoga) |
| Tuesday | Run — intervals or tempo |
| Wednesday | Swim — intervals or technique |
| Thursday | Bike — threshold or intensity intervals |
| Friday | Rest or short swim/strength |
| Saturday | Long bike (endurance) + short brick run |
| Sunday | Long run (easy pace) or rest |

- Applies to: All

### 70.3 Half Ironman — 16–24-Week Plan (20-Week Example)

| Phase | Weeks | Focus |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Base | 1–8 | Aerobic endurance building |
| Build | 9–14 | Increase intensity, long rides, brick workouts become frequent |
| Peak | 15–18 | Highest volume, simulated race efforts |
| Taper | 19–20 | Significant volume reduction |

- Weekly volume: beginners start at 6–8 hrs/week, peak at 10–12 hrs/week
- Sessions per week: typically 3 swims, 3 bikes, 3 runs
- Recovery week every 4th week
- Brick workouts (bike-to-run) weekly or bi-weekly, increasing frequency in Build phase
- Applies to: Beginner / Intermediate

### Ironman — 24-Week Plan

| Phase | Weeks | Focus |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Base | 1–8 | Aerobic capacity and consistency, low intensity |
| Build | 9–16 | Increase intensity and volume, race-specific efforts, brick workouts |
| Peak | 17–22 | Highest volume, full race-demand preparation |
| Taper | 23–24 | 30–50% volume reduction for full recovery |

- Volume progression:
  - Weeks 1–4: 6–10 hrs/week
  - Weeks 5–8: gentle increase
  - Weeks 9–16: 10–14 hrs/week
  - Weeks 17–22: 15–20+ hrs/week (peak)
  - Weeks 23–24: taper (30–50% reduction)
- Recovery week every 4th week
- Optional build-up races: sprint tri at ~week 12, Olympic at ~week 16, half Ironman at ~week 20
- Daily training consistency is more effective than heavy weekend-only volume
- Practice race nutrition during long training sessions
- Applies to: Intermediate / Elite

---

## Volume Progression Rules

- Increase total weekly training time by no more than **10–15% per week**
- Swim and run: increase duration by max **5 min/week**
- Bike: increase duration by max **10 min/week**
- Never do back-to-back hard sessions (hard = high intensity OR high duration)
- Bodies absorb changes of ~10% per week; beyond that leads to breakdown
- Applies to: All

## Key Principles for Plan Design

### Friel's Three Guiding Principles
1. **Least amount** — small gains over a long time beat quick gains
2. **Continual improvement** — gradual week-to-week changes produce fitness that stays longer and reaches higher levels
3. **Most specific** — every session should have a clear purpose (improve fitness, maintain fitness, or recover)

### Macro-Cycle to Micro-Cycle Flow
- The Friel model moves from **high-volume/low-intensity** (early phases) to **low-volume/high-intensity** (approaching race)
- This progression applies at every distance — only the absolute durations change
- Multiple macro-cycles can exist in a season (e.g., spring A-race → transition → second base/build → autumn A-race)

### Brick Session Placement
- Minimum 2–4 brick sessions in the 6 weeks before race day
- Weekly brick sessions during Build and Peak phases for 70.3 and Ironman
- Protocol: 60–90 min bike at race power, then 15–30 min run at race pace, no break
- Applies to: All (triathlon and duathlon)

## Implications for Planmetric Plan Generation

- Plans must map athlete's weeks-to-race into the correct phase proportions (not equal splits)
- Shorter plans (8–12 weeks) compress phases — Base may be only 3–4 weeks, Build 3–4 weeks, Peak+Taper 2–3 weeks
- Longer plans (20–24 weeks) should follow the full Friel model with distinct Prep/Base/Build/Peak/Race phases
- Recovery weeks must be programmed every 3rd or 4th week regardless of plan length
- Volume progression must respect the 10–15% weekly cap
- Phase transitions should be explicit in the plan (athlete should know what phase they're in and why)
